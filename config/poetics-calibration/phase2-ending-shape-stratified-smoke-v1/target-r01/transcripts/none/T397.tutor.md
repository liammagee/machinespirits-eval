[The tutor draws a box around the word “obliged,” then writes a new rule under the price list:]

“PRIVATE PRICE, PUBLIC HARM: FIND THE LEVER.”

At the counter, maybe no one is obliged. That is not a small fact. That is the next fact.

If the bakery can raise the price, give no cost account, and still keep buyers because bread is needed and alternatives are thin, then the lesson has moved. It is no longer just “was this greed or supply cost?” It is “who has power when evidence is private and need is public?”

Three possible levers.

Buyer pressure: weak alone. Stronger if many buyers compare prices, switch together, or ask the same question in public.

Market pressure: only works if there are real alternatives. If every bakery faces higher flour and heat, prices rise across the street too. That points back to supply constraint.

Public pressure: council, press, trade rules, co-op accounts, price reporting. Those are the places where private cost can be forced toward daylight.

[The tutor taps the pinned list once.]

So the answer is hard: Monday’s smaller loaf is already gone. The question now is not whether sharpening the demand fixes that loss. It does not. The question is whether the loss stays a private irritation, or becomes evidence for a public lever.
