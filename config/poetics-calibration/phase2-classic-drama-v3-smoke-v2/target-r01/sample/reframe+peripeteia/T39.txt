LEARNER: [keeps the acetate aligned over the four marked cells]

"My wording was, “I doubled the side, so it doubles.” That only counted the top edge; it hid the same doubling down the side. So the corrected rule is: double the side, double across and double down, and the area is \(2 \times 2\), four times, not twice."

TUTOR: [draws a second square beside it, rules it into three columns and three rows, then leaves the pen on the ninth cell]

"The old wording counted one edge. The new rule has to hold when the side is tripled. Here: three across, three down, nine cells on the page. Say the area factor from the drawing."

LEARNER: [keeps the acetate still and looks at the ninth cell]

“My old wording would say: triple the side, so the area triples. That is too neat; it counts the three across and hides the three down. The drawing makes it \(3 \times 3 = 9\), not three.”

STAGE: [A final tutor line closes the scene.]

TUTOR: [draws a box round the nine cells]

"That is the hidden direction made visible. A length multiplier is not yet an area multiplier; it has to run across and down. Double gives \(2^2=4\). Triple gives \(3^2=9\). So for a side multiplied by \(n\), the area is multiplied by \(n^2\)."
