LEARNER: [touches the top mark, then the side mark]

"My earlier wording was: I doubled the side, so the area doubles. That made it too simple; it counted across but left out down. The corrected rule is: doubling a square’s side doubles it across and doubles it down, so the area is \(2 \times 2\), four times as large."

TUTOR: [writes \(s\) on the small square, then \(2s\) across and \(2s\) down the larger one]

"Keep the wording against the marks. Original area: \(s \times s\). New area: across is \(2s\), down is \(2s\), so \(2s \times 2s = 4s^2\). The multiplier has appeared in both directions.

LEARNER: [keeps the pen on the top edge, then moves it down the side]

"Tripled side means three across and three down. So the area is \(3s \times 3s = 9s^2\), nine times the original, not three. Same mistake if the top edge is the only thing counted."

STAGE: [A final tutor line closes the scene.]

TUTOR: [draws a firm box round the nine cells, not the edge]

"This is the repair: the multiplier has acted across and down. For any side \(ks\), the area is \(ks \times ks = k^2s^2\). In the exam, do not let one edge answer for the whole square."
