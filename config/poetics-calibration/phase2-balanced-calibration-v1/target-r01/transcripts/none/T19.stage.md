The arithmetic gets teeth when it is column arithmetic, not room-percentage arithmetic.

That meter says 420 ppm in this air, but the relevant path is from the wet floor, through the whole atmosphere, out to space. Infrared leaving Earth does not ask, “Is CO2 rare?” It asks, “How many absorbing molecules are in the way at the wavelengths I use to escape?”

Add CO2, and two things happen. First, more infrared photons get intercepted in the absorption bands. Second, the level where heat can finally escape to space shifts a bit higher. Higher air is colder, and colder air radiates less energy. That leaves a small energy shortfall until the surface and lower atmosphere warm enough to push the outgoing heat back up.

The scale is not guessed from 0.04%. It is measured as radiative forcing: raising CO2 from preindustrial 280 ppm to about 420 ppm adds roughly 2 watts per square meter of trapped energy before feedbacks. Two watts sounds small until it is spread over every square meter of Earth, day and night. That is why the trace-gas argument is too thin by itself: rarity is not the same as irrelevance when the gas sits in the escape route.
