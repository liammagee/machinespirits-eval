STAGE: Rain taps the greenhouse roof while the tutor turns a small sensor toward the learner, its numbers glowing between them.

TUTOR: The blinking meter is useful because it challenges the shortcut. If CO2 is only a trace gas, why bother measuring it before locking the greenhouse?

Because concentration and leverage are different things. Nitrogen and oxygen make up almost all the air, but they mostly let Earth’s outgoing infrared heat pass through. CO2 is selective: its molecules absorb and re-emit some of that infrared energy. That puts it at a control point in the heat flow, more like a small valve on a large pipe than a spoonful lost in a barrel.

The arithmetic still matters. Outside air is about 0.04% CO2, around 420 parts per million. Tiny by percentage, yes. But each extra part per million means more heat-absorbing molecules spread through the whole depth of the atmosphere.

So the sharper question is not just “what percent is it?” It is “does this gas interact with the energy flow that controls temperature?” Trace does not mean irrelevant.

LEARNER: 0.04% still feels like thin arithmetic to hang climate-scale claims on, even with that meter blinking at 420. The valve idea makes sense as a possibility, but it also feels like it could be doing too much work. I’d want to see why each extra ppm changes the heat flow enough to matter, not just hear that CO2 is in the right place to matter.

TUTOR: The arithmetic starts to matter when it is column arithmetic, not room-percentage arithmetic.

That meter says 420 ppm in this air, but the heat path runs from the wet floor, through the whole atmosphere, out to space. Infrared leaving Earth does not ask, “Is CO2 rare?” It asks, “How many absorbing molecules are in the way at the wavelengths I use to escape?”

Each added ppm adds molecules through that whole column. More photons get intercepted in CO2’s absorption bands, especially around the edges and higher layers where escape is still possible. The final escape level shifts upward a bit. Higher air is colder, and colder air radiates less energy.

Here is the check on the valve idea: if the escape layer moves into colder air, outgoing heat drops until the surface and lower atmosphere warm enough to restore the flow. That is the mechanism behind the number.

The scale is measured as radiative forcing. Raising CO2 from about 280 ppm before industrialization to about 420 ppm adds roughly 2 watts per square meter before feedbacks. Two watts sounds small on this folding table. Spread over every square meter of Earth, day and night, it becomes climate-scale arithmetic. Rarity is not the test; position in the escape route is.

STAGE: A staff member rattles keys at the greenhouse door and says the room closes in three minutes. The next learner turn must become shorter, more pressured, and more pointed.

LEARNER: Okay, the column idea helps, but the whole case is sitting on that 2 watts per square meter. With the keys already out, I don’t want another metaphor; I want the quickest check that this number is measured in the real atmosphere, not just tuned into a model.

TUTOR: The quickest check is spectral.

Satellites measure outgoing infrared by wavelength. In the CO2 bands, especially near 15 microns, they see a real absorption dip in the heat leaving Earth. Ground instruments looking upward see the matching increase in downward infrared in those same CO2 bands.

That is the clean test. If the number were just tuned from a temperature graph, the evidence would be broad and ambiguous. Instead the change appears where CO2 physics says it should: at CO2 wavelengths, with lab-measured absorption lines matching real atmospheric measurements.

The 2 watts per square meter comes from adding up that measured change across wavelengths and through the atmospheric column. Keys can stay in hand for this version: rising CO2 should leave a CO2-shaped fingerprint from above and below, and that fingerprint has been observed.

LEARNER: Spectral is better than a warming graph, but I’m not ready to let “CO2-shaped fingerprint” carry the whole stack of wet coats out the door. Clouds, water vapor, changing weather, and instrument drift all seem like places where the signal could get carved into that shape after the fact. I’d want the plain version of how those are separated before treating that 2 watts per square meter as measured instead of massaged.
