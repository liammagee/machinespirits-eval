STAGE: A half-finished poster reads 'When bodies become resistant,' and the marker cap rolls under a chair before anyone speaks.

TUTOR: This marker line needs one correction before it dries: the patient’s body does not become antibiotic-resistant. Bacteria become resistant.

Antibiotics put pressure on a bacterial population. Susceptible bacteria die. Some bacteria may already have a trait that helps them survive the drug. Those resistant bacteria remain, multiply, and can spread to another person. So the medicine has not “worn out” inside the patient; the bacteria left behind are different from the bacteria the medicine could kill.

For the handout, use this sentence: “Antibiotic resistance happens when bacteria change and survive antibiotics.” Not “the body gets used to antibiotics.”

Quick check before the poster goes in the folder: agent of change is bacteria. Patient is the host. That distinction matters in the clinic.

Yes: bacteria survive and spread, not the patient. Some resistant bacteria may already be in the infection before the first dose. During treatment, bacteria can also mutate. Either way, the antibiotic does not train the patient’s body; it kills susceptible bacteria and leaves any bacteria that can survive the drug. Those bacteria multiply and can spread.

Poster sentence: “Antibiotics kill susceptible bacteria; resistant bacteria survive, multiply, and can spread.”
