STAGE: A rolling cart rattles to a stop beside the canyon model while rain ticks at the high windows and the next group gathers beyond the door.

TUTOR: Set a finger on the canyon model here. Now drag one grain-width of sand from this tray edge. Hardly a canyon. Do that again each time water runs, carries sediment, and wears the channel lower, and the small loss starts to add up.

The clock is fast by five minutes; one glance hides the error, repeated glances expose it. A river rate can feel like that: little change in a short watch, real deepening across many returns.

A flood can cut, shift, and carry a lot at once. Keep that on the label. But before telling visitors the canyon needed one dramatic making-event, point to the steady work first: water returns, sediment leaves, the channel deepens.

LEARNER: I thought the label should say a canyon came from one huge flood, or something like that. But this one-grain bit and the clock make me think the small wearing and carrying matters if it keeps happening for a very long time, though I’m still not sure how to say that without getting the timescale wrong. Could the label mention floods but lead with the river deepening by repeated small changes?

TUTOR: Yes. Put the river first, and make the grain move do some of the work:

“Rivers wear, carry, and deepen a canyon by small changes repeated over long time. Floods can reshape the channel in bursts, but they do not have to be the whole making story.”

That first line should not sound like ten grains make a canyon. Read it once with the sand tray in hand: does “repeated over long time” keep the clock from shrinking the timescale too far?

STAGE: A child outside the door points through the glass at the canyon model and says, "That must have cracked all at once." The next learner turn must react to the overheard claim in a short, unfinished way.

LEARNER: I thought that too at first, that it cracked in one big moment. The label helps, I think, because the river keeps wearing and carrying bits away, but “long time” still feels hard to picture. Maybe I’d say to the child, “Floods can change it fast, but the river...”

TUTOR: Keep that start: “Floods can change it fast, but the river...”

Move one grain in the tray, then glance at the fast clock. A small rate barely shows in a short watch; over enough returns, wearing and carrying add up to deepening.

Finish the line so a child hears both parts: floods can matter, and the river’s slow work has time to shape the canyon.

LEARNER: Maybe, “Floods can change it fast, but the river wears and carries small bits away again and again...” I think that gets the slow part in, but I’m not sure it makes the long time clear enough. Should it say “over a very long time” before “shapes the canyon”?

STAGE: End the scene with one concise tutor line. Do not ask the learner to continue unless the scene genuinely needs it.

TUTOR: Yes. Let the label hold both: “Floods can change it fast, but over a very long time the river wears and carries small bits away again and again, deepening the canyon.”
