TUTOR: The phrase on those criminal procedure materials carries a legal rule, not a verdict about history. “Innocent until proven guilty” means the prosecution bears the burden of proving guilt to the required standard, usually proof beyond reasonable doubt, before the court may convict. It does not mean the law has already determined that the accused did not act.

That distinction matters. Evidence may create suspicion, even serious suspicion, but suspicion is not proof beyond the required standard. The presumption protects against treating an accusation as enough for conviction.

A useful check is this: if the court says, “guilt has not been proved,” has it necessarily answered the historical question, “the accused did not do it”? Not necessarily. The historical question is, “What happened?” The legal question at trial is, “Has guilt been proved well enough for conviction?”

TUTOR: The wording in those criminal procedure materials does two different jobs. The presumption of innocence is a legal rule for trial: it places the burden of proof on the prosecution and bars conviction unless guilt is proved to the required standard. It does not announce the historical fact that the accused did not act.

A verdict of **not guilty** therefore means, in legal terms, “guilt has not been proved sufficiently for conviction.” It may coincide with “the accused did not do it,” but it does not by itself establish that. Evidence can support suspicion, even serious suspicion, and still fall short of proof beyond reasonable doubt.

So the questions remain separate: **what actually happened** is the historical question; **whether guilt has been proved strongly enough for the court to convict** is the legal one. In the workshop example, which of those two questions does “not guilty” answer directly?
