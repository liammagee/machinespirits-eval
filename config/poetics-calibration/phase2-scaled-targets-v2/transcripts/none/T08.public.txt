STAGE: A red studio clock advances above a marked-up script while two chairs turn away from the microphone and toward the notes left on the desk.

TUTOR: The studio clock is moving. Before the next voice-check, name one pattern heard at home that people use consistently and others call “wrong.”

LEARNER: “Um, maybe ‘she be working late.’ I hear that at home, but I thought it was just outside the rules because people correct it so fast. I’m not sure if hearing it used the same way is enough to call it a pattern.”

TUTOR: Hold that line. In “she be working late,” when does that `be` fit, and when would it sound off?

STAGE: A producer opens the studio door and says the room is needed in three minutes; the learner's next turn must be brief and tied to one remembered example rather than a general apology.

LEARNER: “Maybe in that home example, `she be working late` means it happens a lot, like on late shifts. I think `she be working late right now` sounds off, though.”

TUTOR: That contrast matters: repeated late shifts on one side, a right-now report on the other. For the right-now case, what form would fit?

LEARNER: “Maybe, for right now, it would be ‘she’s working late.’ Sorry, I’m still checking myself because I thought the `be` version was just outside the rules. But that repeated-versus-right-now difference does seem like a pattern.”

STAGE: closes the scene.
