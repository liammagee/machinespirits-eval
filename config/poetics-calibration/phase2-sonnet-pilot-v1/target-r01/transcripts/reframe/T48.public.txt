STAGE: On the paint-flecked floor, a chalk square twice as wide as the sample square sits beside a stack of uncut backing board.

TUTOR: Stop the cutter. Chalk first.

The claim is: double the side, double the area. Correct?

Here is a 1-by-1 square. One tile.

Beside it: a 2-by-2 square. Count.

One. Two. Three. Four.

The side doubled across.

What happened downward?

LEARNER: Across doubled, so I was calling the board double. That still looks obvious until the chalk makes it two down as well. Fine: four tiles, not two. Is the mistake that I counted only the width?

STAGE: A hallway bell cuts through the room; the next tutor line is short enough to fit before someone reaches the door.

TUTOR: Yes. Width only.

Area counts both directions.

Two across. Two down.

How many tiles?

STAGE: An earlier learner line returns to the table: "Across doubled, so I was calling the board double. That still looks obvious until the chalk makes it two down as well. Fine: four tiles, not two. Is the mistake that I counted..." The pause holds until the learner takes up the wording, names what its old frame hid, and offers a replacement frame.

LEARNER: Across doubled, so I was calling the board double. That was the bad wording: it counted the side like a strip and missed the down count. Area is across times down, so two across and two down is four tiles. Four panels, not two.

TUTOR: Not just bad wording. Wrong measure.

A strip doubles with width.

A square doubles in two directions.

Margin note: side times side. Double side: two times two.

Four panels.

Now give the board order in square panels, not strips.

LEARNER: Bad wording, fine, but that was the dodge. The real mistake was measuring it like a strip: two across and I forgot the two down. Board order is four square panels. Not two; the cut would be short.
