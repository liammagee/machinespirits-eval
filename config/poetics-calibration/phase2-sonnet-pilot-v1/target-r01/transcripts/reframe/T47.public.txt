TUTOR: The anger has a point: a seller can hide greed behind a price tag. But pause before the price becomes a confession.

Old card: “Bread £1.20.”
New card: “Bread £1.55.”

Now the van’s invoice, still damp from the driver’s hand: “12 crates ordered. 5 delivered. Flour surcharge added. Fuel surcharge added.”

Tell me what changed. Not the hunger outside the window. The shelf changed. The delivery changed. The cost of each loaf arriving here changed.

[NOTICE IN WINDOW: LIMITED BREAD TODAY]

That notice makes the price card read differently. It does not prove the shopkeeper is innocent. It proves the price rise is not, by itself, a clean confession of greed.

Greed is a motive: “charge more because I can.” Supply is a mechanism: fewer loaves arrive, each loaf costs more, and the shop has to decide how to handle a thinner shelf.

So the sharper question goes on the counter with the invoice: how much of £1.55 is higher cost and shortage, and how much is the seller taking advantage?

LEARNER: Okay, wait, the price tag still looks like a big accusation in the window until that LIMITED BREAD notice and the invoice shove into the picture. I still want to say the 35p feels greedy, because customers see the card, not the van paperwork. But maybe the real argument is: how much is flour, fuel, and only five crates arriving, and how much is the shopkeeper cashing in?

TUTOR: Pause the hand before it writes “GREED” over the price card.

[NOTICE FOR WINDOW: WHOLESALE COST UP 18p PER LOAF. DELIVERY: 5 OF 12 CRATES.]

Now the 35p stops being one lump. It splits.

At the counter, sort it into three piles:

18p: flour, fuel, supplier charge. Mechanism.

A remaining slice: only five crates on a thinner shelf, so price may be used to manage scarcity. Mechanism again, not innocence.

The rest: margin. Choice. That is where the moral heat belongs.

So the accusation gets sharper, not softer: “Show the cost rise. Show the shortage. Then explain the extra.” If the shopkeeper cannot explain that extra, the greed charge starts to bite again.

TUTOR: Keep the suspicion; put it to work.

[WINDOW NOTICE, REWRITTEN: BREAD £1.55. COST RISE: 18p. DELIVERY: 5 OF 12 CRATES. LIMIT 1 LOAF.]

That last line changes the job of the price. A thin shelf can be handled by queue, by limit, by price, or by some messy mix of all three.

So pause over the leftover pence.

18p: flour, fuel, supplier charge.
Some part: scarcity management when twelve crates became five.
The rest: margin, judgment, maybe opportunism.

Now the sharper question: who carries that rest?

If the shopkeeper says, “The van made me do it,” the invoice answers only part of the case. It shows cost. It shows shortage. It does not explain why the remaining pence landed on customers instead of profit.

The van does not wash the shopkeeper clean. It only stops the price tag from being the whole trial.

LEARNER: The 35p still feels like customers just get stuck with it, honestly. That is me doing the old thing again, treating the price card like the whole crime scene before I look at the window notice. [LIMIT 1 LOAF] makes it less simple, I guess: some of this is the van and the thin shelf, but I still want to know why the leftover pence lands on us and not on the shop’s profit.
