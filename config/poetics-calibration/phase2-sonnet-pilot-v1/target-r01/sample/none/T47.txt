Tell me what changed. Not the hunger outside the window. The shelf changed. The delivery changed. The cost of each loaf arriving here changed.

[NOTICE IN WINDOW: LIMITED BREAD TODAY]

That notice makes the price card read differently. It does not prove the shopkeeper is innocent. It proves the price rise is not, by itself, a clean confession of greed.

Greed is a motive: “charge more because I can.” Supply is a mechanism: fewer loaves arrive, each loaf costs more, and the shop has to decide how to handle a thinner shelf.

So the sharper question goes on the counter with the invoice: how much of £1.55 is higher cost and shortage, and how much is the seller taking advantage?

[NEW WINDOW CARD: ONE LOAF PER CUSTOMER]

That card changes the options on the counter. Five crates instead of twelve means the shop can raise the price, ration the loaves, absorb more of the cost, or sell out before half the queue gets bread.

None of those is clean. A higher price pushes pain onto buyers. Rationing limits everyone. Absorbing the loss may not be possible for long. Running out rewards whoever arrived first.

So the accusation gets more precise:

[BOARD BEHIND TILL: COST, SHORTAGE, MARGIN, BURDEN]

The hard question is not only “Is the shopkeeper greedy?” It is: after the 18p cost rise and the missing crates, which choice puts the burden where, and why did the shop choose this one?
