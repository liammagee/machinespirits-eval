STAGE: A fluorescent tube hums over three open boxes; the exhibit label for 'The Official Account' lies face-down beside a stack of mismatched records.

TUTOR: This ledger gives us the official verdict. That clipping gives us the editor’s version by breakfast. The transcript over there gives us a witness’s voice with half the tape chewed off.

“History is written by the winners” belongs on a sticky note while we sort, not on the wall label yet. It catches something real: courts, newspapers, and archives often preserve the voices with power first. But a slogan that neat can make missing names, dissenting columns, and later evidence-work disappear.

Before we print anything bold, test it on this table. Pick one item where the slogan fits cleanly. Then pick one where it fails, or where the evidence makes it messier: a crossed-out name, a damaged interview, a clerk’s margin note. That second case is where the exhibit starts getting honest.

The crossed-out name and chewed-up tape are where I want to push the label harder, and I’m not sure how to weigh them without either bowing to the ledger or just picking the messiest thing because it looks more honest.

TUTOR: The chewed tape does not become truer because it is damaged. The ledger does not become false because it is official. That is the trap on both sides of the table.

We weigh them by asking what each object can safely carry. For each piece, write two quick notes: one claim it can support, and one claim it cannot support without backup. The ledger can show what the court recorded. The clipping can show what the editor pushed into public view. The transcript can preserve a witness’s voice, but where the tape breaks, the gap has to stay visible.

So put two pins in before the label hardens. First: one case where “written by the winners” fits cleanly. Second: one case where it fails, or turns incomplete. The honest exhibit label will come from that contrast, not from bowing to the ledger or worshiping the mess.

TUTOR: The gap can be loud without being allowed to testify alone.

Five minutes means no grand theory. Use two pins and two notes. On the ledger pin, write one claim it can carry: maybe “this is what the court made official.” Then add what it cannot carry without backup.

On the transcript pin, do the reverse carefully. It can carry the fact of a damaged surviving voice. What can it not carry? Write that limit in plain ink, even if the silence feels louder than the words.

Then test a label frame, not a verdict: “Power shaped the record; historians read both the record and its silences.” Put one clean fit under it and one place where the slogan leaks.
