STAGE: A noticeboard trembles as the seminar-room door shuts, and a tutor pauses beside a learner who is rereading a marked exercise on irrational numbers.

LEARNER: My first instinct is that √2 cannot be a fraction because the decimal just keeps going. But that feels a bit thin for a proof, and I am not quite seeing where the contradiction comes in.

TUTOR: Quite. The decimal hunch points at the claim, but it does not yet give an assumption that can contradict itself. So take the fraction claim seriously for a moment: suppose \(\sqrt{2}=a/b\), with \(a/b\) in lowest terms. After squaring, \(a^2=2b^2\). What does that force one to notice about \(a^2\)?

LEARNER: Then \(a^2\) is even, since it is twice \(b^2\). I think that should force \(a\) itself to be even, but that is the step I would want pinned down cleanly before the contradiction runs on.

TUTOR: That is the hinge. Suppose \(a\) were odd, \(a=2k+1\). When that is squared, what sort of integer appears: even or odd?

LEARNER: Right: \(a\) has to be even. With \(a=2m\), \(4m^2=2b^2\), so \(b^2=2m^2\): another even square. Then the same parity step ought to make \(b\) even as well, though I would check that hinge once more before calling the contradiction.
