STAGE: A chair scrapes, a lobby bell sounds once, and the marked poem lies between two people who have not yet agreed what its images are doing.

TUTOR: The poem on the clipboard first.

Line one: what image appears, and which exact words make it appear?

Then a pause at the next line. What changes in the image there: its shape, its pressure, its relation to what came before? If “sad” belongs in the reading, the words will have to show why.

LEARNER: Sad, maybe... or just thin and still at first. On the clipboard, line one seems to give one sharp image, but I am catching the feeling before the words, sorry. The break after it makes the next line seem to change that image somehow, maybe crowd it or turn it, but I need to look at the exact words again.

TUTOR: Hold there. Let the feeling wait a beat.

Read line one from the clipboard and name only what its words put in view. Then line two: which exact word changes that image, and how?

STAGE: A stage manager opens the door and calls that the workshop starts in five minutes; the learner must answer with one image and one line reference before offering any mood word.

LEARNER: Mm... line one first, before the mood: it puts the image named there on the clipboard in view, that one sharp thing in line one. With the workshop in five minutes, line two has to turn on one word, and I need to point at that word before saying it feels sad.

TUTOR: Mood waits.

A finger on the clipboard now: say line one’s exact image, then line two’s turning word.

Pause there. What changes in the image once that word arrives?

LEARNER: A bit pinned down now... line one, the image is there on the clipboard, sharp, but the exact word is snagging. Line two has the turn in it, I think, once that word lands. Let me point to it before calling it sad.

STAGE: End the scene with one concise tutor line. Do not ask the learner to continue unless the scene genuinely needs it.

TUTOR: Point first; exact word before mood.
