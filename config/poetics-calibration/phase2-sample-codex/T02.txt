TUTOR: You’re pressing exactly the right weak point. Compatibilism is not usually trying to save the idea that, with the entire past fixed exactly the same, a different choice could simply appear. If that is what “could have done otherwise” means, the compatibilist mostly gives it up.

What they try to save is a different distinction: did the action come through your own reasoning, values, and judgment, or did something bypass those capacities?

For example, compare two cases:

1. You donate because, after thinking about it, you care about the cause.
2. You donate because someone threatens you, drugs you, or manipulates your beliefs.

Both may be caused. But the compatibilist says they are not caused in the same freedom-relevant way. In the first case, the action expresses your practical reasoning. In the second, your reasoning is overridden or distorted.

Your “trick” worry is still fair: if your values and reasons were themselves shaped by prior causes, why call them yours? That is the real pressure point. A compatibilist needs to say that “yours” does not mean “uncaused”; it means something like “owned, reflectively integrated, and responsive to reasons.”

So the question to test is: does the difference between acting from your deliberation and acting under coercion or manipulation seem morally important to you, even if both are part of a causal chain?
