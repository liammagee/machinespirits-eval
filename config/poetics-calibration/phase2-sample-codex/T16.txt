A simple version is:

`next year’s deer = current deer + births - predator deaths - starvation/disease/winter deaths`

Removing predators lowers one subtraction. So at first, the population may rise.

But imagine the land can feed about 100 deer through winter. With predators, maybe there are 80 deer. Remove predators, and the herd rises to 120. Now the “food per deer” drops. That changes the other terms: fewer healthy births, weaker fawns, more disease, and more starvation or winter deaths.

So the turning point is not “predators gone = immediately worse.” It is:

`more survivors -> too many deer for the habitat -> food per deer falls -> birth rates fall and non-predator deaths rise`

The key question is: once the herd passes what the habitat can support, which subtraction gets bigger? Not predator deaths anymore, but starvation, disease, and winter deaths. Predators can reduce deer numbers, but they can also prevent the herd from overshooting the food supply.

TUTOR: You’re asking exactly the right question. The crossover is not “the herd passes 100, so now predators would have helped.” And the 80/100/120 numbers were just illustrative, not typical values.

The crossover happens when the losses caused by crowding become bigger than the predator deaths that were removed.

A simple comparison would be:

`benefit of removing predators = fewer deer killed by predators`

But as the herd grows, the hidden costs increase:

`cost of overcrowding = more starvation/disease/winter deaths + fewer surviving fawns`

So the crossover is roughly:

`extra crowding losses + lost reproduction > predator deaths removed`

For example, suppose predators had been killing 10 deer per year.

If removing predators leads to only 4 extra starvation deaths and 2 fewer surviving fawns, then the herd is still ahead:

`6 crowding cost < 10 predator deaths removed`

But if the herd grows enough that food shortage causes 8 extra winter deaths, 5 extra disease deaths, and 4 fewer surviving fawns, then the tradeoff has flipped:

`17 crowding cost > 10 predator deaths removed`

That is the crossover.

The tricky part is that carrying capacity is not a hard line. The habitat does not support 100 deer perfectly and then fail at 101. Instead, food per deer declines gradually. Deer may get lighter, fawns may survive less often, disease may spread more easily, and a harsh winter can make the crossover happen much sooner.

So your skepticism is right: “remove predators, therefore fewer/worse deer” is too simple. The better claim is: removing predators can increase deer at first, but if the herd overshoots the food supply enough, the main losses shift from predation to starvation, disease, weak reproduction, and winter mortality.

A good way to test the idea is: if predators removed 10 deaths per year, what overcrowding effects would have to appear before the predator removal stops being a net benefit?

Stronger evidence would be a coordinated pattern like:

2. **Do predators reduce overbrowsing and boom-crash dynamics enough to improve herd/habitat stability?** Harder to show.

So the predator-stability claim depends on assumptions like these:

1. **Similar pre-trends:** before the predator change, the predator-change area and control areas were already moving similarly in deer density, body condition, fawn survival, browsing pressure, and crash frequency.

2. **No unequal outside shock:** after the predator change, one area was not also hit by something different, like a harsher winter, new hunting rules, disease outbreak, drought, or habitat restoration.

3. **Same measurement methods:** body condition, fawn recruitment, browsing, disease, and predation are measured consistently across sites and years.

4. **Correct causal timing:** predator pressure changes first; then deer density, movement, or browsing changes; then forage recovery, body condition, fawn survival, or crash frequency changes.

5. **Mechanism-specific pattern:** the improvement tracks the expected mechanism. For example, stronger predator pressure should be associated with less overbrowsing or fewer extreme boom-crash cycles, not just “fewer deer.”

`predators present -> deer healthier`

`predator pressure changed in one area but not the other`

`the size of the change tracks predator pressure, browsing reduction, or density`
