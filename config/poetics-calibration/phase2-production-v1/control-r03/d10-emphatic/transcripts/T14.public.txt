TUTOR: The slogan has bite. It catches something real about power. But before it hardens into a master key, put it against the table.

Accession slips. Memoir extracts copied years later. Medals labelled by a regiment. These are not all “written” in the same sense, and not all by the same sort of winner. One record survives because an office filed it. Another because a family kept it. The gap beside them matters too.

With ten minutes left, test the slogan twice: pick one item here that fits “written by the winners,” and one that makes the phrase creak. For each, ask: who made this record, who kept it, what is absent, and what other source would have to confirm it?

LEARNER: Oh, I get it now, or I think I do: the medal label feels like the regiment getting to pin its version right onto the thing. So the slogan still works, but it starts wobbling once the accession slip and the missing papers are sitting right there on the table.

STAGE: A staff member rolls a returns cart past the table; one folder slides loose, showing a receipt for destroyed records. The tutor must respond to the object before giving any abstract explanation.

TUTOR: Hold that thought. That folder just added a new kind of evidence: a receipt for destroyed records. It records an absence, not the event itself.

The medal label has a regimental voice. The copied memoir has a later hand between the witness and the page. The destruction receipt has an institutional voice, and it tells us that some questions have been made harder to ask.

Put the three traces side by side for a moment: label, copy, receipt. Which one most supports “history is written by the winners”? Which one forces the phrase to stretch or change?

The historian’s question may be less about who wrote the final story, and more about who had the power to label, file, copy, preserve, weed, or destroy the traces. Before the archive closes, make one revised version of the slogan that can survive all three objects on the table.

LEARNER: Oh, I get it now, or I’m running ahead again: the “winner” is not just the person writing the grand story, it is the regiment with the label, the office with the file, and maybe the hand near the shredder. The medal label fits the slogan most cleanly, but that destruction receipt is stranger, because it is almost history being written by leaving a hole in the folder. So maybe the slogan becomes something like: history is made from the traces powerful people label, keep, copy, or lose, and the gaps are part of the evidence too.

TUTOR: That version will hold more weight than the original slogan.

One caution: “powerful people” may still be a little too neat. The regiment and the office have power, yes. But the copied memoir might survive because of a family habit, a clerk’s bundle, or plain accident.

So leave a pencil mark beside the thought, not a finished maxim. Label, copy, receipt: each trace has a maker, a keeper, and a path to the table. The gap is evidence too, but evidence with a provenance.

Before the archive closes, test the hole itself: what would count as corroboration for it: another file list, a disposal policy, a catalogue note, or a second memoir noticing the same missing papers?

LEARNER: Oh, I get it now, the hole is almost like a labelled object too, except the label says “something used to be here.” The destruction receipt makes the gap feel active, like the archive has left a footprint where the papers vanished. I want to say a disposal policy plus another file list would steady it, but maybe a second memoir noticing the same missing papers would make the silence feel less like one office’s story.
