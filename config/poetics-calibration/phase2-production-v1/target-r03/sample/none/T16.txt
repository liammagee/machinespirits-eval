That is the warning. Once the park loses young growth, the deer are not being “helped” by a bigger herd. They are being pushed toward thinner food, poorer winter condition, more disease pressure, and eventually a harder crash.

Predators are not only removing deer. They also change where deer feed, how long they linger, and how much pressure lands on the same patches. Pull every predator pin off this table, and the map does not become safer. It becomes simpler in the wrong way.

So the cleaner recommendation is: predator removal can raise deer numbers short term, but it can also let the population overshoot carrying capacity and damage the habitat that supports it. The management question is not “Do predators help or hurt deer?” It is “What keeps the herd, the seedlings, and the park within a range that can recover?”

Counts show the bump. Seedlings show whether the bump is becoming a food-base problem.
