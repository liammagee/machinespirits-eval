TUTOR: Look at the browse map first. If predators disappear, the first line in the public note can’t just be “more deer survive.” For a season or two, yes, survival may rise. Then the herd meets the food line.

Now set the seedling sheets beside the winter counts. If deer numbers climb but forage does not, what do these browse marks predict for next spring’s saplings? Fewer stems getting past deer height, especially along the same ravines and edges already marked heavy.

That is the warning. Once the park loses young growth, the deer are not being “helped” by a bigger herd. They are being pushed toward thinner food, poorer winter condition, more disease pressure, and eventually a harder crash.

Predators are not only removing deer. They also change where deer feed, how long they linger, and how much pressure lands on the same patches. Pull every predator pin off this table, and the map does not become safer. It becomes simpler in the wrong way.

So the cleaner recommendation is: predator removal can raise deer numbers short term, but it can also let the population overshoot carrying capacity and damage the habitat that supports it. The management question is not “Do predators help or hurt deer?” It is “What keeps the herd, the seedlings, and the park within a range that can recover?”

TUTOR: Slide the seedling sheets over the browse map and run one test: what pattern separates “more deer made it through winter” from “the habitat is failing to replace itself”?

The line is not the deer count by itself. It is recruitment through the browse zone. Look for high browsing on terminal leaders across several plots, few seedlings making it above deer height, and the same ravines and edges showing pressure year after year. If winter counts rise while those recruitment numbers stay flat or fall, that is strong enough for a carrying-capacity warning, not a crash prediction.

So the public note should stay careful:

“Predator removal may increase short-term deer survival, but current browse and seedling data suggest a risk of overshooting the park’s carrying capacity and reducing habitat recovery.”

Counts show the bump. Seedlings show whether the bump is becoming a food-base problem.
