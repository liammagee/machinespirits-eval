Take a sentence someone marked “proper?” or “wrong.” Don’t defend it. Map it.

By rule, I mean pattern: what has to stay the same for the sentence to sound right in that speech setting? What can move? What cannot?

Where can the home form appear? Where would it sound off, even at home?

A correction mark may catch a slip, a context mismatch, or a workplace-standard issue. A dialect form has patterns, limits, and settings. The mark may be naming the workplace standard. It has not yet proved carelessness.

The ear counts. Then run the contrast test.

A rule is the pattern that survives three checks:

1. What part has to stay fixed?
2. What part can change and still sound right at home?
3. What change breaks it?

Write those three beside the “proper?” mark. Then the mark gets sorted. It does not get to be judge and evidence at the same time.
