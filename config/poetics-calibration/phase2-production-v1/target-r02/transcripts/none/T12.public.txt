STAGE: A half-lit canyon model sits on a trolley, its painted river thread no wider than a shoelace.

TUTOR: Set the label draft on the cart. Look at the river line cut into the model.

A flood can move boulders. A storm can tear a bank. That part of the guess is not wrong. But this canyon is asking a rate question: one thin scrape, then another, then another.

Here is the held scale: a fingernail’s width of lowering over a long span. If that much is taken, then that much again, then the same thin loss laid down like transparent sheets, what does the stack become?

Not one dramatic day. Repeated small work, with water and grains carrying pieces away past counting.

For the school label: “The river did not blast the canyon open at once; it carried grains away, year after year, until small losses became a deep valley.”

LEARNER: So, sorry, the label should not say one flood opened it, even if floods can tear bits loose. Looking at this river line, I think it is the small scrape repeated, a fingernail-width and then another, but I still do not quite trust that becoming all this depth. Could it say the river carried grains away year after year, or is that too plain?

TUTOR: Plain is allowed. The school label has to hold.

Set a finger here, at the river line. A flood tears loose pieces; it does not explain the whole cut by itself. Rate plus deep time does the slow work: a thin lowering, then the same thin lowering again, stacked past ordinary counting.

Test this on the card:

“The canyon was not opened by one flood. Floods helped break and move rock, but the river carried grains away year after year. Over deep time, many small losses cut the valley deeper.”

Now check the weak spot: is “year after year” enough for the school group, or does “over deep time” need to stay so the scale does not shrink back into one storm?

TUTOR: Keep that corner. The wording is starting to carry the rate.

Test this line on the card:

“Floods sometimes broke and moved rock, but the canyon was not cut by one flood. Grain by grain, the river carried material away over deep time, and repeated small losses deepened the valley.”

Hold it against the river line. “Sometimes” keeps floods from taking over. “Repeated” keeps grain-by-grain from shrinking into a few wet seasons.

Does that still block the one-big-flood reading when a school group sees the cut?

LEARNER: I think it blocks the one-big-flood reading, sorry, because “sometimes” keeps the flood from taking over the label. “Over deep time” and “repeated small losses” make me look back at the river line instead of picturing one huge day.
