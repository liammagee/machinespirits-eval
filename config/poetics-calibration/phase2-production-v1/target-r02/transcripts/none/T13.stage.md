Pause over the intake note before the printer starts again: “innocent until proven guilty” is not the file announcing what really happened. It is the court’s starting rule for handling an accusation.

The accused may be factually innocent, factually guilty, or the facts may be unclear. The presumption does not settle that question. It tells the court who carries the burden: the prosecution must prove guilt, and must do so to the required standard. Until that happens, the law treats the accused as not legally guilty.

So in your intake note, keep two columns separate. One column is allegation: “The prosecution alleges X.” The other is procedural posture: “At this stage, guilt has not been proved.” Do not write as if the court has found the accused did not do it. Write as if the court has not yet been given proof strong enough to convict.

Leave the packet open there. The useful distinction is this: “not legally guilty yet” is a courtroom status; “probably innocent” is a factual guess. The presumption of innocence gives the accused the first status without requiring the court to make the second guess.

The court can say, at the arraignment stage, “No conviction exists, and the prosecution has not yet met its burden.” That statement is compatible with several factual possibilities: the accused did it, did not do it, or the evidence is still too thin to know. The rule is deliberately about burden and proof, not probability.

“Presumed innocent” = procedural posture.

“Not guilty unless proved beyond a reasonable doubt” = legal standard for conviction.
