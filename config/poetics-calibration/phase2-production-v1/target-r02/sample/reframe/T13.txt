STAGE: A charge sheet lies between two coffee cups while someone in the hallway calls for the next file and the word "presumed" sits circled three times in pencil.

TUTOR: Pause over the intake note before the printer starts again: “innocent until proven guilty” is not the file announcing what really happened. It is the court’s starting rule for handling an accusation.

The accused may be factually innocent, factually guilty, or the facts may be unclear. The presumption does not settle that question. It assigns the burden: the prosecution must prove guilt, and in a criminal case must prove it beyond a reasonable doubt. Until that standard is met, the law treats the accused as not legally guilty.

So keep two columns in the note. Allegation: “The prosecution alleges X.” Procedural posture: “At this stage, guilt has not been proved.” The file should not say the court has found the accused did not do it. It should say the court has not yet been given proof strong enough to convict.

Try this sentence before the next packet prints: “The accused is presumed innocent” belongs in which column: allegation, factual finding, or procedural posture?

TUTOR: Leave the packet open there. The useful distinction is this: “not legally guilty yet” is a courtroom status; “probably innocent” is a factual guess. The presumption of innocence gives the accused the first status without requiring the court to make the second guess.

At arraignment, the court can say: “No conviction exists, and the prosecution has not yet met its burden.” That fits several factual possibilities: the accused did it, did not do it, or the evidence is still too thin to know. The rule is about burden and proof, not probability.

3. “The prosecution must prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.”
