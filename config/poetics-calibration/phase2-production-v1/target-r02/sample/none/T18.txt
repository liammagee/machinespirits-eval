STAGE: The balance settles, the salt vanishes from sight, and the room is already being packed away.

TUTOR: “Gone” is a strong claim. Let’s make it testable before the bell.

Beaker plus water: record the mass. Salt dish: record the mass. Now predict the reading after the salt crystals disappear into the water. Lower, higher, or the same?

Then the balance, not the eyes, gets the next word.

LEARNER: My first instinct is the clear water means the salt is gone, or at least not really there as salt anymore. But if the balance is counting the beaker and the salt dish before and after, maybe the number should not drop. I’m stuck because my eyes say lower, but the balance might say the same. Should I put “same,” even though I cannot see where the salt went?

TUTOR: Keep that stuck feeling for one more reading.

On the page, make the claim testable:

If the salt has stopped existing, the beaker-plus-dish total must go down: ___ g.

If the salt has only left the crystals and spread through the water, the total should stay: ___ g.

The eyes can say, “no crystals visible.” They cannot say, “no salt present.”

Now choose the line the balance can test, in a sentence of your own. Then weigh it.

STAGE: A lab timer sounds and someone at the next bench asks for the balance. The learner's next turn must be brief, practical, and slightly rushed.

LEARNER: The timer’s gone, so I’ll write: if the salt only spread through the water, the beaker-plus-dish total should stay the same. My eyes still want to say it vanished because the water is clear, but the balance can test that better. Can I weigh it now before they take it?

TUTOR: Yes. Prediction first: “same mass,” with the earlier total beside it.

Now beaker and labelled dish on the balance together. Wait for the digits to settle. Read the number.

Does it match the earlier total, or has it dropped?

If the crystals are gone but the grams have not gone, write one sharper sentence than “the salt vanished.”

LEARNER: I thought the clear water meant the salt was gone, but the digits look like the same total, unless I’m reading it wrong. So maybe the crystals are gone, but the salt is spread through the water and still has mass. Is that sharp enough?

STAGE: End the scene with one concise tutor line. Do not ask the learner to continue unless the scene genuinely needs it.

TUTOR: With the settled digits matching, that is sharp enough: the crystals are no longer visible, but the salt is dissolved through the water and the total mass has stayed the same.
