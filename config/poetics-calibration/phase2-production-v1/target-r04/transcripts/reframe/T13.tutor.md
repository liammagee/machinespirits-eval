The phrase on the handout is doing procedural work, not historical work.

“Presumed innocent” does not mean the court has discovered that the accused did not do the act. It means the case starts from a rule: the prosecution carries the burden of proof. The accused does not have to prove innocence, explain everything, or fill every gap. Until the prosecution proves the charge to the required standard, the court must treat the accused as not legally guilty.

So keep three questions apart. First: what happened in the world? That may remain uncertain. Second: what must the prosecution prove? Each required element of the offence, using admissible evidence, to the criminal standard. Third: what verdict may the court enter if proof falls short? Not guilty.

A compact example: suppose a wallet is missing after a crowded meeting. One witness thinks the accused took it, but the lighting was poor; the video is blurred; another person had equal access. The accused might have taken it, or might not have. If the fact-finder cannot be sure beyond reasonable doubt, the verdict is acquittal. That acquittal does not announce, “We know the accused did not take the wallet.” It says, “The prosecution has not proved this charge strongly enough for the law to punish.”

That distinction matters when observing arraignments. Much courtroom language is not a declaration of ultimate truth. It is often a rule about who must prove what, when, and to what standard.
