Pause there. Same acres, same winter food, more mouths. What happens to the next flush of seedlings? What condition is the herd in after two browse-heavy seasons?

That is where the tidy argument starts to bend. More deer browse young trees harder, seedlings fail, understory plants disappear, and the herd starts living above the park’s carrying capacity. The muddy boots came back from a transect where that food limit is already visible.

Then the deaths return by a rougher route: hunger, parasites, disease, winter stress, road collisions. Predators are not just subtracting animals. They can keep the herd moving, keep browsing patchier, and keep numbers closer to what the land can actually feed.

So the briefing question is not “Do predators reduce deer?” They do. The sharper question is: “Does removing predators leave a healthier deer population and a recovering forest two or three seasons later?” The browse map is already warning that the answer may be no.

The line is not one deer count. It is whether browsing stays a short pulse or starts changing what the park can regrow.

Temporary overbrowsing: heavy feeding in a patch, seedlings still return next season, deer body condition holds, and the damage stays local.

Trophic cascade: predator loss changes deer numbers or movement enough that young trees fail across plots, understory plants thin out, nesting cover drops, and the food base shrinks. Then the herd is not just eating leaves. It is eating the park’s future carrying capacity.

Sort the evidence this way: are the browse marks scattered damage after a hard season, or are fenced plots, open plots, sapling counts, and deer condition all pointing in the same direction?

A crash is the delayed bill: poorer deer, lower fawn survival, more disease, more winter loss. The herd can peak by count while the food limit is already being damaged, so the crash looks sudden only if the plants were not being watched.
