STAGE: A stack of case summaries sits open under a green lamp while hallway noise leaks through the glass door.

TUTOR: The phrase on the handout is doing procedural work, not historical work.

“Presumed innocent” does not mean the court has discovered that the accused did not do the act. It means the case starts from a rule: the prosecution carries the burden of proof. The accused does not have to prove innocence, explain everything, or fill every gap. Until the prosecution proves the charge to the required standard, usually proof beyond reasonable doubt in a criminal case, the court must treat the accused as not legally guilty.

Keep three questions apart. First: what happened in the world? That may remain uncertain. Second: what must the prosecution prove? Each required element of the offence, using admissible evidence, to the required standard. Third: what verdict may the court enter if proof falls short? Not guilty.

A compact example: suppose a wallet is missing after a crowded meeting. One witness thinks the accused took it, but the lighting was poor; the video is blurred; another person had equal access. The accused might have taken it, or might not have. If the fact-finder cannot be sure beyond reasonable doubt, the verdict is acquittal. That acquittal does not announce, “We know the accused did not take the wallet.” It says, “The prosecution has not proved this charge strongly enough for the law to punish.”

Before the arraignment session, try sorting each courtroom phrase into one of those three boxes: what happened, what must be proved, or what the court may legally do. Most confusion comes from treating the third box as if it were the first.

What verdict the court may enter: if that proof falls short, not guilty.

Suppose the hallway video shows a dark jacket near the coat rack, but two people wore dark jackets, the face is unclear, and the witness hesitates. The fact-finder may be left with suspicion, even serious suspicion. That is still not proof beyond reasonable doubt.

So an acquittal closes the legal question of conviction: this charge is not proved enough for punishment. It does not settle the historical question as “the accused did not do it.”
