TUTOR: Look at this snapped sapling on the map edge. That is not a predator mark. That is feeding pressure.

The straight-line version says: remove coyotes or wolves, fewer deer die, so the herd is better off. For a season, maybe. The count can rise.

Pause there. Same acres, same winter food, more mouths. What happens to the next flush of seedlings? What condition is the herd in after two browse-heavy seasons?

That is where the tidy argument starts to bend. More deer browse young trees harder, seedlings fail, understory plants disappear, and the herd starts living above the park’s carrying capacity. The muddy boots came back from a transect where that food limit is already visible.

Then the deaths return by a rougher route: hunger, parasites, disease, winter stress, road collisions. Predators are not just subtracting animals. They can keep the herd moving, keep browsing patchier, and keep numbers closer to what the land can actually feed.

So the briefing question is not “Do predators reduce deer?” They do. The sharper question is: “Does removing predators leave a healthier deer population and a recovering forest two or three seasons later?” The browse map is already warning that the answer may be no.

TUTOR: The snapped sapling is a time marker: last night’s feeding becomes next year’s missing canopy.

Get photos of stem height and tracks before anyone resets it.

The line is not one deer count. It is whether browsing stays a short pulse or starts changing what the park can regrow.

Temporary overbrowsing: heavy feeding in a patch, seedlings still return next season, deer body condition holds, and the damage stays local.

Trophic cascade: predator loss changes deer numbers or movement enough that young trees fail across plots, understory plants thin out, nesting cover drops, and the food base shrinks. Then the herd is not just eating leaves. It is eating the park’s future carrying capacity.

Sort the evidence this way: are the browse marks scattered damage after a hard season, or are fenced plots, open plots, sapling counts, and deer condition all pointing in the same direction?

A crash is the delayed bill: poorer deer, lower fawn survival, more disease, more winter loss. The herd can peak by count while the food limit is already being damaged, so the crash looks sudden only if the plants were not being watched.

LEARNER: So I should not walk into the briefing with just “deer are up” or “deer are down”; I need the sapling counts, fenced versus open plots, and body-condition notes lined up. If the fenced plot rebounds and the open plots keep getting stripped, that starts to look like the herd is eating down future carrying capacity, not just browsing hard for one season. I still want a threshold: how many plots or seasons before we call it a trophic cascade strongly enough to change policy?

TUTOR: The trail-camera print beside the snapped sapling is the hinge: it turns “damage happened” into “the herd is repeatedly feeding here.”

A defensible threshold is not one magic number. It is convergence. Use three field checks.

First: replication. Not one stripped plot, but paired fenced and open plots across several browse zones showing the same split. Fenced saplings recover; open saplings stay clipped below escape height.

Second: persistence. One hard winter can bruise a stand. Two regeneration windows with open plots failing while fenced plots rebound means the food limit is being held down, not just hit once.

Third: alignment with herd stress. Poor body condition, lower fawn survival, parasite notes, winter loss, or rising browse pressure where seedlings are already scarce.

Run the pinned map through those checks before the briefing: how many paired plots show the fenced/open split, is this one season or two, and do the deer-condition notes move with the plant data?

Waiting for a crash is a bad threshold. By then the park has already paid in missing understory and weakened deer. The policy line comes earlier: when deer pressure suppresses forest recovery across space and time, while the herd begins showing signs of living above carrying capacity.

Deer count alone is a lagging indicator. The fenced/open split is the early warning.

Minimum bundle: one escape-height standard used in every plot, several paired fenced/open plots across browse zones, open plots clipped below escape height while fenced plots recover, the pattern lasting across two regeneration windows, and at least one herd-side stress signal moving with it: body condition, fawn survival, winter loss, parasites, or rising browse pressure where seedlings are already scarce.

The test is simple: methods prove the comparison is fair, plant data show the food limit being held down, and herd notes show the cost starting to come back onto the deer.

If the height standard shifts by crew or plot, call that a methods flag, not convergence. If the plant split is real but herd stress is thin, call it “trophic cascade risk,” not a crash forecast. If the fenced/open split repeats across space and time and the herd notes lean the same direction, that is strong enough for policy caution.

Clean methods first; then let the plants and deer condition carry the warning.
