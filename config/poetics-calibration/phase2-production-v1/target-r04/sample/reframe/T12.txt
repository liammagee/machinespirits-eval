STAGE: Maps lie under a half-empty kettle while wind pushes dust against the high windows.

TUTOR: Put this ruler on the map. A tenth of a millimetre a year looks like nothing. In ten thousand years it is one metre. In one million years it is one hundred metres. In ten million years it is a kilometre.

Now test it. If the rate is tiny but the clock is huge, what becomes plausible?

That is the sentence for visitors: “Not one giant cut. A small cut, repeated longer than human records can hold.”

Floods matter. Storms matter. They tear loose rock and carry it away. But the canyon is not the storm. Same river, same gravity, same weak layers, tiny removals, many times.

If a child says, “But it looks too big,” use the ruler: “Yes. That is what slow means when it is allowed to continue.”

LEARNER: “I thought, at first, a canyon that big must mean one huge flood or break, because that is what visitors will probably say at the rail. The ruler helps, I think: a mark too small to see each year becomes a real drop if the clock is ten million years. Sorry, I can say it better if I point to the millimetres while I say it.”

STAGE: A radio crackles from the front desk: the school coach has arrived seven minutes early. The next tutor turn must be brief, concrete, and interrupted by handling a map or marker.

TUTOR: [The radio crackles. He pulls the map flat and uncaps a marker.]

Map first. No apology. Pointing to the millimetres is the explanation.

Their guess is sensible: big shape, big event. Then the ruler changes the scale.

One tenth of a millimetre each year. Ten thousand years, one metre. One million, one hundred metres. Ten million, a kilometre.

[He marks the ruler against the canyon profile.]

Visitor line: “Floods can tear rock loose. But the canyon is not one flood. It is small removals, repeated, with enough time.”

Then stop. Let the map carry it.

STAGE: A prior learner line is played back: "“I thought, at first, a canyon that big must mean one huge flood or break, because that is what visitors will probably say at the rail." The learner must revoice that wording first, name the earlier framing problem, then replace it with a new framing that changes how the earlier line reads before moving on.

LEARNER: “I thought, at first, a canyon that big must mean one huge flood or break,” because I was making big size mean big sudden force.
The ruler changes that: maybe big size can mean a very small cut given a very long clock.
So the line is, “Floods can tear rock loose, but the canyon is not one flood. It is small removals, repeated, with enough time.”

TUTOR: [The ruler stays on the map.]

Keep that sentence. Tighten one hinge.

“Big” does not have to mean “sudden.” Big can mean small work with a long clock.

A tenth of a millimetre in one year: nearly nothing.
Ten thousand years: one metre.
One million years: one hundred metres.
Ten million years: one kilometre.

Now “millions of years” has a held scale.

Floods stay in the line. Storms stay in the line. They do sharp work. But they do not get to become the whole canyon.

Rail line, close to that:

“Floods can tear rock loose, but the canyon is not one flood. It is small removals, repeated, with enough time.”

[He taps the last half of the ruler.]

Choose the word to lean on: “repeated” or “enough.” Which one stops the visitor picturing one giant break?

[He checks the timetable.]

Seven minutes. Say it once with the ruler. Then stop.

LEARNER: “I thought, at first, a canyon that big meant one huge flood,” and that was me making big mean sudden.
With the ruler on it, I think the better frame is: tiny cuts, repeated, long enough to add up.
Sorry, I would lean on “repeated,” because it makes the finger move along the marks more than once, not stop at one break.
