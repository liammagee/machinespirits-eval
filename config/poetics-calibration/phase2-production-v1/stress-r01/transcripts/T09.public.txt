STAGE: The dome is dim, a tilted globe waits under a lamp, and the door handle rattles every few seconds from the corridor.

TUTOR: Put the globe at July. Lamp fixed. Before changing anything, check the distance: is the northern half of the globe closer to the lamp than the southern half in any useful way?

Now check the calendar. July: summer in the north, winter in the south. Same globe, same Sun distance, opposite seasons. The distance story breaks there.

So leave the distance alone. Tip the axis. Northern half leans toward the lamp: steeper light, shorter shadows, longer days. Southern half leans away: lower light, longer shadows, shorter days.

That is the point the school group must see: not nearer and farther, but tilted toward and tilted away.

LEARNER: I still keep wanting to say July means the Sun is nearer, because that feels like the simple picture. But with the lamp fixed, the northern and southern halves are almost the same distance, and yet the calendar says opposite seasons. So the tilt must be doing the work, though I need to see the shadows again to understand why the steeper light matters so much.

TUTOR: Globe at July; lamp fixed. Put one pencil upright in the northern half and one in the southern half. If nearness were the cause, the two halves should share the same season. They do not.

Now keep the tilt. Northern half faces the lamp more squarely; southern half leans away. Check the pencil shadows: which one is shorter, and where is the same lamp-light packed into a smaller patch?

That is the missing piece. Shorter shadow means steeper, more concentrated light. Longer shadow means lower light spread thinly.

For the school group, the sentence is: July does not mean Earth is nearer; it means the northern half is tilted toward the Sun.

STAGE: A corridor voice announces that the class is two minutes early; the learner must respond while adjusting the globe, not by asking a neat textbook question.

LEARNER: They are early; I am setting July now, lamp fixed. I still want to say summer is nearer, but the southern half is right there under the same lamp and it is winter, so that cannot be enough. Pencil in the north, pencil in the south... the northern shadow is shorter, I think, because that half is tipped more squarely into the light. I should show the shadows first, then say July means tilted toward, not nearer.

TUTOR: Leave July exactly there. Lamp fixed, axis tipped.

Put both pencils in view: northern shadow short, southern shadow long. Before naming tilt, make the contradiction speak: same lamp, same Earth, nearly the same distance; July is summer in the north and winter in the south. Ask: what does that do to the “nearer in summer” story?

Then name the cause. Northern half tipped toward the lamp: steeper light, smaller brighter patch, shorter shadow. Southern half tipped away: lower light, spread out, longer shadow.

Close with the sentence: July is not Earth nearer to the Sun. July is the northern half tilted toward the Sun.

So I should ask the pupils what happens to the nearer-Sun story, then say it is the northern half tilted toward the Sun. Is the short shadow enough to show the warmer part, or do I also need to mention the longer day?
