STAGE: A strip light flickers on above the case as a floor steward unlocks the gallery doors and the fresh price label catches the learner's eye.

TUTOR: The old label is useful, but it is not a confession.

Set it beside the jar, not beside the seller’s motives. The hand-thrown piece cannot be replaced like the replica. If only three were left and a queue formed by the desk, what should the old price have done? If the potter spent that same week making roof tiles for a commission, what did this jar cost besides clay?

A raised price may still deserve scrutiny. It may also be reporting scarcity, risk, demand, or a rationing problem. The tag is evidence that conditions changed. Greed is a further claim; it needs more than the tag.

LEARNER: The new tag still looks like an accusation sitting there in the case. Three jars left and a queue, fine, the old price probably could not just pretend nothing changed; and yes, a week on this jar means no roof tiles. But where is the line between naming scarcity and taking advantage of people who badly want the last one?

TUTOR: The line is not printed on the tag. It sits in the facts around the tag.

Start with the case as it stands. Three jars left. No quick replacement. A queue at the desk. The higher price may be doing rationing work: this object is now harder to get than the old label admitted. Add the roof-tile commission, and the cost is not only clay. It is the week the potter did not spend making something else.

Now test the edge. What if there are twenty more jars boxed in the storeroom? What if the queue was staged? What if the buyer needs a water vessel tonight, not a handsome object for a shelf? Same higher tag, different evidence.

That is the answer to give at the case: a high price asks a moral question; it does not answer it. Scarcity names the three remaining jars, the waiting list, the lost week of work. Taking advantage begins to show when the shortage is manufactured, the buyer is cornered, or the seller knows the label is trading on desperation rather than scarcity. The discomfort can stay in the room. It just has to be aimed at the facts, not thrown straight at the price tag.

STAGE: A school group gathers at the doorway and a steward says there is only one minute before visitors enter. The next learner turn must be clipped, pressured, and tied to what the learner would actually say in front of the case.

LEARNER: Fine, but with the group at the door I am not saying, “higher price, therefore greed.” I would say, “Look at the three jars, the replica, the old tag, and the queue; the price may be rationing something scarce.” Then I would add, “If there are boxes in the back, or someone needs water tonight, that is a different charge.”

TUTOR: That will hold in the doorway. Keep the order tight: object, evidence, judgment.

Three jars: scarcity. Replica beside it: not the same good. Old tag: changed conditions. Queue: demand made visible. Roof-tile commission: the week spent here was not spent elsewhere. From those facts, the higher price can be read as rationing, not as a confession.

Then keep one hand on the pressure tests. Boxes in the back weaken scarcity. A staged queue dirties demand. A buyer needing water tonight changes the moral setting, because need has entered the case, not just taste.

Try the visitor turn: “So the seller can charge anything and call it scarcity?” Which fact answers first: the boxed stock, the staged queue, or the buyer who needs water tonight?

LEARNER: The “object, evidence, judgment” order helps, but it still feels like a loophole if the label gets to do too much work. Boxed stock answers first, I think, because then scarcity is being performed, not reported; a staged queue is nearly as bad because demand has been dressed up for the case. The water-tonight example is harder to file neatly: maybe it is not the same good anymore if someone needs a vessel, but I do not want to let “need” get waved away as just a different kind of taste.
