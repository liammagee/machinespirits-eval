On the handout, the key word is “proven.” The rule is not a declaration that the accused in fact did nothing. The court is not claiming private knowledge of what happened.

It is a rule about legal position. Until the prosecution proves the charge, the accused must be treated as not guilty in law. That means the burden stays with the prosecution; the accused does not have to prove non-involvement, explain every suspicion, or earn innocence back.

So the distinction for the summary is this: factual reality is whatever actually occurred. The legal presumption is how the court must treat the accused before proof. The evidentiary standard tells the jury how strong the prosecution’s proof must be before a guilty verdict is permitted.

A “not guilty” verdict therefore means: the prosecution did not meet the required standard. It does not necessarily mean the jury has found, as a factual certainty, that the act did not occur or that the accused could not have done it. That precision matters, especially in jury-orientation material.
