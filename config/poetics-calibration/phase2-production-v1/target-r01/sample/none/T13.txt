STAGE: A badge lanyard lies beside a half-erased whiteboard while the last handout on 'presumption of innocence' curls at one corner.

TUTOR: On the handout, the key word is “proven.” The rule is not a declaration that the accused in fact did nothing. The court is not claiming private knowledge of what happened.

It is a rule about legal position. Until the prosecution proves the charge, the accused must be treated as not guilty in law. The burden stays with the prosecution; the accused does not have to prove non-involvement or explain away every suspicion.

So the summary should keep four things separate: factual reality, legal presumption, burden of proof, and evidentiary standard. What happened is one question. What the court may treat as established is another.

A “not guilty” verdict means the prosecution did not meet the required standard. It does not necessarily mean the jury has found, as a factual certainty, that the act did not occur or that the accused could not have done it.

Before the handout is put back on the sign-in table, try phrasing it this way: “The presumption of innocence means the prosecution must prove guilt to the required standard before the accused may be convicted.” That sentence avoids the mistaken claim that the law has declared what actually happened.
