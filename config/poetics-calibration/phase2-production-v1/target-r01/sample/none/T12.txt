TUTOR: One millimeter: the edge of a coin. At that rate in ten years, a hundred years makes one centimeter. A thousand years makes ten centimeters. Ten thousand years makes one meter.

Same rate. No trick. A million years makes about a hundred meters. Ten million makes about a kilometer. The arithmetic is plain; the years are the hard part.

A flood can tear banks, move boulders, leave a sharp layer. This canyon also keeps quieter records: grit in water, frost in cracks, rock dissolving grain by grain.

So the question at the railing is still narrow: which process left the whole shape, not just one scar?

TUTOR: That ledge, boot-high below the rail.

A huge flood should leave one hard signature: jumbled blocks, torn banks, one sharp high mark.

Look instead for repeats. Gravel bench. Lower gravel bench. Side gully cut down to meet the river. Same pattern, different heights.

The eye catches drama first. The map catches spacing. Rates catch years.

So use the wall as a test: one scar, or nested work? If the same cutting shows up level after level, size is not the clue. Repetition is.

LEARNER: I think I see the test a little better: not just one big violent mark, but the same kind of cut showing up again and again at different heights. I’m still nervous I’d call the wrong thing a gravel bench and be fooling myself. But if the side gullies and benches line up in repeats, then maybe the years are doing more of the work than the size makes me want to believe.

TUTOR: The boot mark in dust is one test. A bench is a shape test.

Flat tread. Rounded river gravel on top. A cut edge facing the canyon. Then another at a lower height. One loose pile can fool the eye. A line of treads, matched to side gullies, is harder to fake.

Take that ledge below the gate. Test it for the three signs: flat tread, river gravel, cut edge.

That caution is good field sense. Don’t trust one mark. Stack witnesses.

A flood can leave order, and a river can make a mess. So the test is not neat versus wild. The test is repetition: the same clue stepping down the wall, beyond one season, beyond one lifetime, beyond many.

LEARNER: I think I can try that, though I’m already not sure whether that ledge is actually flat or just looks flat from here. The rounded gravel part worries me too; I might call any loose stones river gravel by mistake. But if the tread, gravel, and cut edge show up again lower down, then maybe it is less one flood mark and more the river coming back over a lot of time.
