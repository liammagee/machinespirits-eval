STAGE: A wall clock clicks loudly as the tutor places two labeled cards, 'stretching' and 'inherited variation,' beneath the giraffe neck bones.

TUTOR: Stand by the neck bones and put two cards on the whiteboard: DURING LIFE and BORN WITH.

The stretched-neck story sounds tidy: one giraffe reaches, the neck gets longer, the young inherit that longer neck. Test it here before the tour arrives: if one animal stretches muscles during life, would its calves be born with longer neck bones because of that stretching?

That is the weak point. Acquired change during life is not the same as inherited variation.

Check one: during-life change is not inherited variation.

Now start with the group. Some young giraffes are born with slightly different neck lengths. If higher leaves matter, those born with helpful inherited differences may survive and leave more offspring. Across many generations, the population shifts.

Check two: natural selection changes populations, not one animal by effort.

At the display, say: “Giraffes did not stretch their necks longer and pass that on. They varied at birth, and inherited traits that helped some feed led to more descendants.” Then point to the bones and ask visitors: one animal changing, or a population changing?

LEARNER: Okay, stretching goes on the DURING LIFE card, but the calf’s neck bones would have to be BORN WITH it. I can say that much, but I still don’t get where the slightly longer baby necks come from if stretching did not make them. If a visitor asks before I’m ready, do I just say some are born different?

TUTOR: Keep the two-card split. Stretching stays under DURING LIFE. The source of slightly longer baby neck bones goes under BORN WITH.

Point to the bones and say: “Parents pass on inherited instructions. Reproduction copies and mixes those instructions, and sometimes the copy changes a little. If that affects bone growth, a calf can start life with a slightly longer neck.”

Quick check before the door opens: copying, mixing, small copying changes—which card? Not the stretching card. The calf has to start with it.

Then add the second step: if higher leaves matter, calves born with helpful neck differences may feed better, survive, and have more calves. Over many generations, the population shifts.

So do not stop at “some are born different.” Say: “They are born different because inheritance is copied, mixed, and sometimes slightly changed. If a born difference helps feeding, those giraffes can leave more offspring.”

STAGE: A tour coordinator opens the door and says the group is three minutes early. The learner must answer in a short display-label style sentence, not a long question.

LEARNER: Okay, by the bones I’ll say: “Stretching is a during-life change, not what calves inherit. Calves are born with copied and mixed instructions, sometimes slightly changed; if longer neck bones help feeding, those giraffes leave more calves over generations.”

TUTOR: That version is ready for the bones. Tighten one phrase before the families come in: do not make the change sound as if it happened because the animal needed leaves.

Card check: stretching after birth goes under DURING LIFE. Copied, mixed, slightly changed instructions before birth go under BORN WITH.

Now run the order at the skeleton: born difference first, feeding advantage second, more offspring third. Which step can stretching never explain? The born difference.

If a visitor presses, say: “The longer neck did not come from reaching. Some calves already had inherited differences in neck growth; if those helped feeding, those giraffes left more offspring.”

LEARNER: Okay, the step stretching can never explain is the born difference, because the calf has it before it reaches. I can point to the BORN WITH card, but I’m still a bit shaky on the “copy changes” part. If someone asks, do I say those changes are random, or just say “copied, mixed, sometimes changed” and keep moving?

STAGE: End the scene with one concise tutor line. Do not ask the learner to continue unless the scene genuinely needs it.

TUTOR: At the BORN WITH card, say: “The copy changes are random: they do not happen because leaves are high; the non-random part is that calves born with helpful neck growth may feed better and leave more offspring.”
