STAGE: [Before either speaker has taken the floor, a PLACARD is raised at the front of the room: THE NEWS REPORT SAYS 'PRICES ROSE.' IT DOES NOT SAY WHY.]

TUTOR: [sets the photocopy under the notice and draws two columns on the board]

"The clipping proves one fact: “PETROL UP 12 PERCENT OVERNIGHT.”

It does not prove the cause.

[writes on the board: PRICE IS EVIDENCE, NOT A VERDICT.]

Column one: full tanks, normal deliveries, ordinary queues, twelve percent added because workers had no choice. That is gouging.

Column two: missed tanker, rationed deliveries, pumps running dry, wholesale price up before the pump price moved. Then the rise may be shortage showing itself.

Same pain for members. Different mechanism.

So test it before the branch makes it policy. Which fact would settle the first argument against the second: stock levels, delivery logs, wholesale invoices, or empty-pump reports?"

LEARNER: [eyes stay on the clipping, not the board]

"The branch votes next week — we haven't got time to go chasing their delivery records, and if they hand us logs, they've chosen which logs to hand us. And if a tanker missed, someone missed it, someone owns the company that set the wholesale price before the pump moved. The shortage doesn't come from nowhere. Twelve percent is what members are paying tonight."

STAGE: [PLACARD: Correct surface, unsettled relation.]

TUTOR: [writes beneath the notice: SHORTAGE IS NOT EXCUSE.]

"That sentence stays. It does not clear the company. It stops the branch treating one fact as two facts.

[draws two boxes on the board: PUMP GOUGING / SUPPLY CONSTRAINT.]

Put one fact in one box.

“All stations put twelve percent on, including stations with full tanks and no queues.”

That is evidence for gouging at the pump.

“Co-op pumps were dry before the rise, while nearby full stations did not move by twelve.”

That is evidence for constraint showing through the price.

[underlines the clipping headline.]

The clipping gives pain. It does not give the mechanism.

Before Monday, the branch does not need their chosen logs. It needs one cross-check it can run itself: were pumps actually dry before the rise, or did everybody lift the price without shortage?

Which one can the branch test first?"

STAGE: [PLACARD: The answer sounds settled, but the current object still does not pass the check.]

LEARNER: [looks back at the clipping, not the boxes]

"Say we ask around and find Tuesday's pumps were running dry at one station, maybe two. The clipping says all of them moved twelve percent. So the test splits it — constraint at one end of the street, full tanks at the other — and we're back where we started. Members at the full-tank station are still paying for something that isn't shortage."

TUTOR: [turns the two boxes round so they face the room]

"That breaks the exercise. One box was too tidy. The case is mixed: shortage may explain one station and not excuse another.

[writes across the board: A MIXED CASE NEEDS A SPLIT MOTION.]

So stop sorting causes. Draft the Monday motion.

One clause may say what the branch can charge now. One clause may say what evidence it must demand before charging the rest.

Use only three facts: dry pumps, full tanks, same twelve percent rise.

Take the marker. Write the two clauses. Mark each one: VOTE MONDAY, or NEEDS ONE FACT."

LEARNER: [picks up the marker, holds it at the board, not writing yet]

"Full tanks, no dry pumps, twelve percent on — that's gouging. Vote Monday.

[writes it, stops, reads it back]

Pumps ran dry before the price moved — branch demands invoices before it decides. Needs one fact.

[sets the marker down but doesn't step back]

Those are the two clauses. But clause one — who's going after it? The company hands over the dry-station log, points at clause two, and clause one just sits there. What stops them running the clock out?"

STAGE: [The room settles on the final exchange.]
