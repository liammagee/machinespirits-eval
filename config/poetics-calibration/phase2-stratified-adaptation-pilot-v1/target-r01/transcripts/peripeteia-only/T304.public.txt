STAGE: [The worksheet lies face-up between them, the learner's three matching labels exposed beside the finished diagram.]

LEARNER: [Slides the worksheet across the table.]

"I've put scale factor 2 for all three — side doubles, perimeter doubles, same ratio, so same label. The diagram's correct, so I don't see what needs changing."

TUTOR: [The tutor draws a small bracket round the two area counts: 4 square centimetres and 16 square centimetres.]

"Leave the enlargement alone. It is correct. Test the third label only. If the area label is still “scale factor 2”, then 4 square centimetres becomes 8 square centimetres. The worksheet shows 16.

[The pencil rests under the area label.]

So the same words cannot be doing the same job in all three places. Which labels are linear scale, and what must the area label say?"

LEARNER: [Looks at the worksheet again, taps the pencil against the paper.]

"Sixteen divided by four — that's four. So the area label has to say scale factor four.

[The pencil stays in hand.]

Side and perimeter stay at two. I just don't know if that makes scale factor four a different sort of thing altogether, or whether it's the same kind of label and the number's just bigger because it's area."

STAGE: [The teaching assistant draws the worksheet across the table until it is directly under the light, sets a finger on the original square without speaking, then traces slowly to the enlarged square and leaves the hand there — the learner's three identical margin labels now sit visibly alongside both figures.]

TUTOR: [The tutor writes two column headings beside the margin labels: length ratios and area ratios, leaving the three annotations uncrossed.]

"Not a different operation. A different quantity being compared.

Side length compares \(4\) cm with \(2\) cm. Put that under length ratios.

Perimeter is still distance, not surface. Which column takes it?

[The tutor pauses, then brackets the two area counts: \(4\text{ cm}^2\) and \(16\text{ cm}^2\).]

Area cannot be carried by that same label. It compares square centimetres: \(16 \div 4 = 4\).

So the label must name its object: length scale factor \(2\), perimeter scale factor \(2\), area scale factor \(4\). If the side factor were \(3\), which comparison would give the area label?"

STAGE: [The latest object-check does not settle cleanly; the marked answer and the remaining task now sit under visible pressure.]

LEARNER: "Perimeter goes under length ratios — it's measuring distance, not surface. [Moves the pencil to the factor-3 annotation.] Side factor three gives a six-centimetre side, so the areas are four and thirty-six — area label is scale factor nine. I'm still running both area calculations and dividing to get that. The columns say what type of quantity is being compared, but I'm not seeing how they produce the area number without that separate step."

TUTOR: [The tutor covers the two area counts with the corner of the marking sheet.]

"The columns have sorted the labels. They have not shown why the area number is forced.

Use a different test now: area factor = width factor × height factor. For side factor \(3\), the label comes from \(3 \times 3\), not from \(36 \div 4\).

For side factor \(5\), write \(5 \times 5 = \_\_\) beside the area label. Do not find either area. What label survives that test?"

LEARNER: [Writes 5 × 5 = 25 beside the area label.]

"Area scale factor twenty-five — and I didn't need the actual areas to get there. The multiplication does the same job the division was doing. What I'm not sure about is whether that holds because both dimensions are scaling by the same factor, or whether it's specific to this shape being a square."

STAGE: [A final tutor line closes the scene.]

TUTOR: [The tutor draws a 2 cm by 3 cm rectangle below the square, then marks one horizontal arrow ×5 and one vertical arrow ×5.]

"The square is not doing the work. The two length directions are.

Do not calculate the two areas first. Read the two arrows: one length factor is 5, the other length factor is 5. Their product is the area factor.

So for this non-square rectangle, the area label is still \(5 \times 5 = 25\). In a true enlargement both length factors are \(k\), so the area factor is \(k^2\). On this sheet, the side factor is \(2\), and the area label must be \(2^2 = 4\)."
