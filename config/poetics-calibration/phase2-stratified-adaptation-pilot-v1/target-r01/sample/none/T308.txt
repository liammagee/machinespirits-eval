STAGE: [One student from the queue has already left; the sheet and the prototype are both on the bench.]

LEARNER: [slides the sheet forward]

"Everything's ticked — all twelve points. The first-year group collects it tomorrow morning, so the form should be ready to sign."

TUTOR: [sets the checklist beside the prototype and glances toward the door]

"The twelve ticks make the form complete. They do not yet make tomorrow’s transfer safe. With two groups still waiting, take the tick most dependent on user action, bench condition, or interpretation. State its hidden assumption, put the prototype under tomorrow’s first-year use condition, and show what evidence answers it. Then the signature has a basis."

LEARNER: [glances at the sheet, then toward the door]

"So — the form being complete isn't the same as... right. [pause] The calibration step, maybe. Or the power-on sequence — a first-year might read that differently from how it was written. Though it's hard to say which one carries the most assumption without — [quieter] — which tick would it be?"

TUTOR: [places the pencil between “calibration” and “power-on sequence”]

"Compare the two ticks by order, first user action, and damage if misread. Calibration can be checked later; power-on can make later calibration evidence useless. So power-on is the working tick. Read that line with the board cold on the bench: what must be visible before power is applied, what sign means stop, and what bench evidence proves the instruction survives a first-year reading?"

STAGE: [The queue moves; the explanation now has to become usable, not longer.]

LEARNER: "Power-on, maybe — [glances at the instruction sheet] — the line says to apply power once the board is seated, but whether it says to check the polarity marking first... [quieter] ...it might not actually say that. And there's the stop condition — if something looks wrong before power goes on, the instruction might not say what to do. [pause] That part — whether it tells a first-year to stop — [trails off] — is that where the bench evidence would have to come from?"

TUTOR: [sets the supply switch to off]

"The stop condition is the hinge, not an appendix. Before current enters the board, set the cold board as tomorrow’s group will receive it. Point to the polarity evidence visible without memory, then write the stop rule in one line: if the mark is absent, reversed, or uncertain, the supply stays off and supervision is called. If that cannot be done from the sheet and the bench alone, the power-on tick is still carrying an assumption."

LEARNER: [looks back at the sheet]

"The polarity mark — that would be the visible thing, before anything goes on. But the power-on line — [quieter] — it doesn't seem to say *if the mark is wrong or missing, stop*. Maybe there's a line earlier that covers it and — [pause] — is that condition somewhere here that I'm not finding, or is it actually not written?"

STAGE: [A final tutor line closes the scene.]

TUTOR: [draws one line under “power-on” and leaves the supply switched off]

"The failed search now counts as bench evidence. If the stop condition sits elsewhere, this line must point to it; if no such line can be found, the power-on tick is not a release condition. Polarity visible and correct, or the supply remains off and supervision is called. Until that can be read at the action point and shown on the cold board, the signature waits."
