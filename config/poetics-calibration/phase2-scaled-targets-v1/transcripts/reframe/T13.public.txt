TUTOR: That reading is understandable, but the handout is doing something more precise. “Innocent until proven guilty” does not settle the event in advance; it sets the legal posture before proof is tested.

The accused may have done the act, or may not have done it. At the outset, suspicion does not fill the evidence box. The prosecution must offer evidence and must persuade the court beyond the criminal standard. If the evidence leaves the required level of doubt, what should the verdict form record: that the accused certainly did not do it, or that guilt was not proved?

That is why a criminal verdict may say “not guilty” while uncertainty remains. It means the charge was not proved to the required standard; it is not, by itself, a factual declaration of innocence.

So the distinction to keep beside these notes is this: **what happened** is one question; **what has been proved well enough for the law to condemn** is another.
