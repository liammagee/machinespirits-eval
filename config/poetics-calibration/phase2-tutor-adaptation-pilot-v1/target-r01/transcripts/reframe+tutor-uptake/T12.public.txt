STAGE: The clock above the permit desk ticks loudly while a ranger's boarding list waits beside a cross-section map.

TUTOR: The laminated map is pinned flat with a mug. The ruler lands beside one contour line.

“Leave ‘millions of years’ outside the door for a moment. One millimetre a year. This notch. Barely visible.

A thousand yearly notches: one metre. A million: one kilometre. Same rate. Longer run.

A flood can tear out a bank in an afternoon. That happens. But a canyon does not need one impossible afternoon. Rivers cut. Frost loosens. Gravity clears the waste.

Coach goes in six minutes. Take this contour interval. If the bed drops one millimetre each year, how many of these lines are crossed in a million years? Which part still feels wrong: the millimetre, or the million chances to add it?”

LEARNER: I think my first instinct was still picturing one big flood doing the main work, because the canyon is so large. If it is one millimetre a year, then in a million years that is one kilometre down, so if the contour interval were 50 metres, that would be twenty lines, I think. The millimetre feels possible; it is the million repeats that still feels odd to trust.

STAGE: A staff member calls that the coach leaves in four minutes; the tutor's next move must become shorter and more usable, not more elaborate.

TUTOR: The tutor marks twenty short ticks with the pencil. From the corridor: “Coach in four.”

“Twenty lines. The arithmetic holds.

The flood picture is not useless. It respects the size. But make it one mark on the map, not the whole map.

Some years: no visible cutting. One storm: a few millimetres. Frost breaks the rim. The river carries grit out.

Average it back down: one millimetre a year.

At the overlook, look for two things: fresh damage from sudden events, and the removal system still working after them.

Which is which on the rock face: the scar from an event, or the route that keeps taking material away?”

STAGE: An earlier learner line returns to the table: "I think my first instinct was still picturing one big flood doing the main work, because the canyon is so large." The pause holds until the learner takes up the wording, names what its old frame hid, and offers a replacement frame.

LEARNER: I think my first instinct was still picturing one big flood doing the main work, because the canyon is so large. I suppose that made me treat the flood mark as if it explained the whole map, which is probably too simple. On the rock face, the fresh scar would be the event, and the river channel or loose material moving out would be the route that keeps removing it, slowly, though the million years still feels hard to hold in my head.

TUTOR: The tutor folds the map once, hard crease on the canyon line. The queue shifts by the door.

“Hold that split for now.

Flood scar: event. Channel and loose waste: the removal system.

The million is too large as one lump. The ruler is smaller. One contour here is fifty metres. At one millimetre a year, that is fifty thousand years for one line.

Twenty lines is one line, then another, then another. Storms may do the visible cuts. Quiet years still count in the average.

At the overlook, test the face with evidence: what looks like a sudden wound, what carries the broken material away, and what would make that slow carrier trustworthy over many repeats?”

LEARNER: I thought the flood scar was the big explanation, sorry, but that makes me squash the whole map into one event. I think the better frame is scar as the sudden wound, and the channel and loose waste as the thing that keeps removing material after it. Fifty thousand years for one contour still feels hard to trust. What would show the channel kept doing enough work, not just during rare storms?

STAGE: A final tutor line closes the scene.

TUTOR: The tutor keeps the pencil on the blue river line. The driver looks in through the glass.

“No apology. Old frame made one flood carry the whole map.

New test: not ‘was there a storm?’ There was. The test is whether the same exit route keeps showing up.

For one fifty-metre contour, look for witnesses: a river bench above the present bed, rounded gravel shifted along, tributaries cut down toward the same blue line. Not all at one fresh scar. Several levels.

Rare storms can be the hard blows. Between them, the channel stays as conveyor.

At the overlook: scar asks, ‘what broke?’ Channel asks, ‘where did the broken rock go?’ If that second answer repeats up the face, the slow reading is justified. Maps folded. Coach.”
