TUTOR: [Reopened comment · 4:18 PM · edited]

> “The court assumes the accused didn’t do it.”

Correction: not quite.

That sentence treats the presumption as a factual claim. It is not. “Innocent until proven guilty” is a procedural rule: the accused does not have to prove innocence, and the state may not treat the accused as guilty unless the prosecution proves the charge.

For observation notes, keep the distinction tight:

Presumption of innocence: starting legal position, not a finding about what happened.

Burden of proof: the prosecution must prove guilt; the accused does not have to disprove it.

Criminal standard: the prosecution’s proof must meet the criminal standard, usually beyond a reasonable doubt.

Use:

> “The accused is presumed innocent unless and until the prosecution proves guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.”

Do not write:

> “The court believes the accused is innocent.”

Check the sentence before saving: if it sounds like the court is deciding what actually happened before proof is heard, revise it. The rule controls who must prove what, and how strongly; it does not announce factual innocence.

That word is doing the misleading work. Here, “innocent” does not mean “factually did not do it.” It means “not legally guilty unless the prosecution proves guilt.”

Margin note:

- **Factual innocence:** the accused did not commit the act.
- **Presumption of innocence:** the accused must be treated as not guilty before proof.
- **Burden/standard:** the prosecution must prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.

So it is not a verdict before the verdict. It is a rule that blocks the court from treating accusation as proof.
