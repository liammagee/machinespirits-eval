STAGE: On the lab table, five paper deer stand inside a chalk circle with twelve green paper leaves, while two red binder clips labeled PREDATORS sit just outside the circle.

TUTOR: [The tutor moves the predator cards aside, then slides the paper deer into the chalk boundary and bunches the leaf cutouts into one small corner.]

Let the straight-line answer run first: predators gone, more deer survive, so the herd grows. That part is not wrong; it is just unfinished.

Hold it there for a second. Same chalk boundary, same small leaf pile, more paper deer inside it. If all of them eat from this patch, what is left for the next round?

That is the missing step in the marked answer. The food has not grown with the herd. Once the deer pass what the habitat can replace, they overbrowse the plants, weaken next year’s food supply, and the “helped” population can end up thinner, sicker, and headed for a crash.

So the revision can say: removing predators may increase prey numbers briefly, but it can push the prey past carrying capacity. Then the effects spread through the plants and other species too; that wider chain reaction is the trophic cascade.

That is the right place to pause: the cascade does not have to become “everything affects everything.” It just needs one honest step past the plants.

Then keep the chain tight: predators removed, deer increase, deer exceed carrying capacity, overbrowsing reduces plant recovery, and that habitat change affects another species group. That is far enough to show a trophic cascade without turning the answer into a full ecosystem inventory.
