> “The court assumes the accused didn’t do it.”

Correction: not quite.

“Innocent until proven guilty” is a procedural rule, not a factual claim. The court is not saying, “This person actually did not commit the act.” The rule does something narrower and important: it prevents the state from treating the accused as guilty unless the prosecution proves the charge.

So separate three points in the notes:

Presumption of innocence: the accused starts the case without having to prove anything.

Burden of proof: the prosecution must prove guilt.

Standard of evidence: in a criminal case, that proof must meet the level required by law, usually “beyond a reasonable doubt.”

Better observation-note phrasing:

“The accused is presumed innocent unless and until the prosecution proves guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.”

Avoid writing:

“The court believes the accused is innocent.”

That second sentence turns a legal safeguard into a factual conclusion about what happened.
