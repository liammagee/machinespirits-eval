TUTOR: Red pen on the judgement, not on the speaker. We have nine minutes.

Write one ordinary sentence from the dialect that was called “wrong”. Not a comic version. The real form.

Now test it. Where does that form appear? Where would speakers of that dialect reject it? Try a contrast: same verb, different tense; same sentence, made negative; same speaker, different situation. If there are conditions, there is grammar.

Descriptive grammar records the pattern. Prescriptive grammar says which pattern the university expects in assessed Standard English.

Those are different questions. “Will this pass in the essay?” is one. “Is this broken speech?” is another. Give me the sentence; we’ll find the rule before we correct the register.

LEARNER: Sorry, right, an actual sentence: “She were late yesterday.” I’d always thought that was just me being sloppy, but people at home would say that, and probably “she weren’t late” too, while some other versions sound off. I’m not sure I’m testing it properly, but I think I want to find the rule before I change it for the essay.
