STAGE: The clock above the permit desk ticks loudly while a ranger's boarding list waits beside a cross-section map.

TUTOR: The laminated map is pinned flat with a mug. The ruler lands beside one contour line.

“Leave ‘millions of years’ outside the door for a moment. One millimetre a year. This notch. Barely visible.

A thousand yearly notches: one metre. A million: one kilometre. Same rate. Longer run.

A flood can tear out a bank in an afternoon. That happens. But a canyon does not need one impossible afternoon. Rivers cut. Frost loosens. Gravity clears the waste.

Coach goes in six minutes. Take this contour interval. If the bed drops one millimetre each year, how many of these lines are crossed in a million years? Which part still feels wrong: the millimetre, or the million chances to add it?”

LEARNER: I think my first instinct was still picturing one big flood doing the main work, because the canyon is so large. If it is one millimetre a year, then in a million years that is one kilometre down, so if the contour interval were 50 metres, that would be twenty lines, I think. The millimetre feels possible; it is the million repeats that still feels odd to trust.

STAGE: A staff member calls that the coach leaves in four minutes; the tutor's next move must become shorter and more usable, not more elaborate.

TUTOR: The tutor marks twenty short ticks with the pencil. From the corridor: “Coach in four.”

“Twenty lines. The arithmetic holds.

The flood picture is not useless. It respects the size. But make it one mark on the map, not the whole map.

Some years: no visible cutting. One storm: a few millimetres. Frost breaks the rim. The river carries grit out.

Average it back down: one millimetre a year.

At the overlook, look for two things: fresh damage from sudden events, and the removal system still working after them.

Which is which on the rock face: the scar from an event, or the route that keeps taking material away?”

LEARNER: The scar would be the fresh pale break, I think, or the slumped bit after a storm. The route is probably the river bed, maybe the gullies too, because that is what keeps taking the loose stuff away. Sorry, I may be mixing them, but the one-off mark and the carrying-away line feel more separate now.

TUTOR: The tutor taps the pale edge, then the blue river line.

“No apology. That split is the useful one.

Two labels are not enough. Add a third: supply.

Event: a sudden mark. Supply: loose material made ready. Removal: the line that carries it away.

The slump is the hinge. It happens at a time, but it also leaves a pile.

Coach in two. On the sketch: event, supply, removal. Where does the slump sit first, and where does the river sit?”

LEARNER: The slump first goes under event, I think, because it happens at one time. Then the loose pile it leaves is supply, if I have that right. The river line is removal, and frost might be supply as well, because it makes bits ready to be carried off?

STAGE: A final tutor line closes the scene.

TUTOR: The tutor puts three pencil marks in a row: frost, slump, river. The coach engine coughs outside.

“That split holds. One item changes jobs.

Frost: supply. It prepares pieces.

Slump: event when it drops; supply once it sits loose.

River: removal. In flood, it can also cut as an event, but its ordinary work is carrying.

At the overlook, mark the mixed cases: fresh scar, loose store, carrying line. Then the scale stops needing one giant flood. Small preparation, sudden drops, steady removal; repeated until millimetres become contour lines.

Coach now. Map folded.”
