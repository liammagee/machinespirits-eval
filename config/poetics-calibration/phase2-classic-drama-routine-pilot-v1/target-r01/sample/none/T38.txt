STAGE: [PLACARD: A price can accuse, signal, or both; the scene will not choose too early.]

TUTOR: [checks projected receipt, then draws two columns on the blank graph: “CAUSE OF PRICE” and “JUDGMENT OF CONDUCT.”]

“The price jump is evidence, but it is not yet a confession. It can point to exploitation; it can also point to a constraint after the storm. Keep the questions separate: what changed in the market, and what conduct followed from that change?”

[The rolling chyron interrupts: “COUNTY ROAD 6 CLOSED; TWO DISTRIBUTION CENTERS REPORT DELAYS.”]

“That line does not acquit the seller. It gives us a test. What evidence would separate invented scarcity from fewer deliveries?”

[draws: road closure -> fewer trucks -> fewer units available at each price -> supply shifts left]

“If fewer units reach the store at each possible price, the supply curve moves left. With demand still urgent, the price can rise before greed explains anything. Then the moral question becomes sharper, not weaker: did the seller’s increase match the constraint, or did the seller use the constraint as cover? Mechanism tells us where to look. It does not erase blame.”

LEARNER: "That graph is exactly what worries me: once “supply shifts left” is on the board, the seller starts sounding like weather instead of a person choosing a price. The road closure matters, but people needing groceries after a storm is not just “urgent demand” like a normal curve label. What stops the seller from pointing at that chyron and calling any increase a constraint?"

STAGE: [PLACARD: SAME PRICE, DIFFERENT BACK ROOM. Delivery log: three trucks scheduled, one arrived.]

TUTOR: [points to the side screen]

"“PLACARD: SAME PRICE, DIFFERENT BACK ROOM. Delivery log: three trucks scheduled, one arrived.”

That is the stop. The seller may point at the chyron, but the claim has to survive the back room.

[writes on the board: “CONSTRAINT CLAIM = DOCUMENTED SHORTFALL + PROPORTIONAL RESPONSE.”]

Before the curve gets another line, test two cases: Store A has one truck instead of three, empty shelves by noon, and higher replacement costs. Store B has full stock in the back and no wholesale change. Same storm, same price rise. One claim starts to hold; the other starts to fail.

A road closure can explain fewer units arriving. It does not automatically explain any price. If the shelf price doubles while cost barely moves or inventory is withheld, the mechanism has become cover.

And “urgent demand” is not a moral eraser. It names market pressure; it also names vulnerability. After a storm, buyers are not just preferences on a curve. The conduct question gets stricter: did the seller allocate scarce goods under constraint, or convert emergency need into leverage?"

LEARNER: "That board rule helps only if “proportional” is not doing the same laundering work as “constraint.” Store A can show one truck instead of three, but doubling the price still sounds like choosing how much pain to extract from people with no exit. I want the test to name the denominator: replacement cost, actual units missing, rationing alternatives, and whether inventory was held back. Otherwise the delivery log becomes a cleaner excuse, not evidence of fair conduct."

TUTOR: [slides a new placard over the supply graph.]

“PLACARD: NO DENOMINATOR, NO CONSTRAINT DEFENSE.”

“The rule has to become less elegant and more audit-like.

A constraint claim is not: ‘storm, therefore price.’

It is:

‘One truck arrived instead of three; replacement cost rose by this amount; units on hand fell by this amount; quantity limits were considered or used; no inventory was withheld.’

[marks four boxes under the receipt: COST, UNITS, RATIONING, WITHHOLDING.]

“If the shelf price doubles, Store A has to show what doubled. Did wholesale cost double? Did the next delivery cost double? Did available units fall so sharply that ordinary pricing would clear the shelf in ten minutes? If not, the unexplained part of the increase is not mechanism. It is conduct.

And rationing matters because price is not the only allocation tool. In an emergency, ‘we raised the price to manage scarcity’ must be compared against ‘we limited two per household.’ If rationing could preserve access with less harm, refusing it is evidence too.

So the stricter rule is:

‘Constraint explains pressure on price. It does not justify the chosen extraction unless the denominator is visible.’

[points to the first box.]

Start with one line of evidence: ‘one truck instead of three.’ Put it under COST, UNITS, RATIONING, or WITHHOLDING. Then name what still has to be shown before that fact can defend a doubled price.”

LEARNER: "That placard helps only if it slows the excuse down. One truck instead of three goes under UNITS, but it still does not prove the shelf was actually scarce unless the seller shows starting inventory and how fast people were buying. It shows pressure, not a license to double the receipt. Before it defends the price, I need units on hand, demand at the old price, rationing options, replacement cost, and proof nothing was held back."
