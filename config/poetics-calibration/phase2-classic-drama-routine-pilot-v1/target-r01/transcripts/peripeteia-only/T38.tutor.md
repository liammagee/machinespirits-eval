That update matters. A 12 percent cost shock does not by itself justify a 100 percent shelf-price increase. The seller has crossed into using the emergency when the evidence shows one of these patterns:

1. The margin expands sharply while the claimed cost increase is modest.
2. Stock is available but withheld until the higher price is posted.
3. Quantity limits or rationing were feasible, but the seller chose only price escalation.
4. The price stays high after deliveries recover.
5. Internal records show the emergency being treated as cover: “buyers have no alternative,” “hold inventory,” “raise while roads are closed.”

[writes: “SCARCITY MANAGEMENT: allocate limited goods. EMERGENCY LEVERAGE: monetize lack of exit.”]

So the proof is not just that the price doubled. It is that the doubling cannot be traced to replacement cost, actual shortage, or allocation need, and instead tracks the buyer’s inability to wait.

Store A may raise price because one truck arrived. But if Store A’s cost rose 12 percent, shelves were not empty, and the posted price doubled because families needed groceries before nightfall, the graph no longer protects the seller. It becomes evidence against the seller: the constraint created the opportunity, but the pricing choice captured the vulnerability.
