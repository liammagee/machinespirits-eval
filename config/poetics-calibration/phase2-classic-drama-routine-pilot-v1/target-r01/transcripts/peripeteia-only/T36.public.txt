STAGE: [4:55 p.m. A clipboard with every box ticked sits beside a small powered prototype; beyond the glass, a volunteer waits with a staff badge in hand.]

LEARNER: “I’m sorry, I thought the sign-off sheet was the thing that made it okay to take out there. I did each item, so I’m not sure what else I was supposed to judge before someone else handles the prototype.”

TUTOR: [sets the checklist next to the prototype]

“The sheet says each required check was done. It is evidence, not permission by itself. The next person will not have the build in their hands the way the maker does. Before this leaves the bench, look at the prototype, not the clipboard: what is the first reasonable thing a new handler might do that could make it unsafe?”

LEARNER: “I think maybe the sheet only says I did those items, not that this is ready for anyone to pick up. I’m worried I was leaning on the clipboard because it felt safer than making the call myself. If someone in the demo room grabs it by that loose side panel, it might shift or come off, maybe that is the first unsafe thing?”

STAGE: [The volunteer at the glass door raises the prototype request form, then lowers it when no one signs.]

TUTOR: [keeps the pen off the signature line and turns the prototype so the loose panel faces up]

“The loose panel is a serious candidate for the safety decision, not a box the sheet can settle. Test it out loud before I sign: a new handler lifts here, the panel shifts, then what moves, who is exposed, and what control changes that today?”

LEARNER: "I think if the new handler lifts from that loose panel, it could slide and pull the casing, and their fingers might end up near the edge or the inside part, or they might drop it. Sorry, I’m not completely sure I’m naming the exposure right, but it is the handler first, and maybe anyone close to the table if it slips. Today I would fasten that side before it leaves, or at least add a clear safe grip point and tell the person, but fastening feels safer."

TUTOR: [sets the clipboard aside and angles the prototype toward the exit]

“The exposure name can stay rough for now. Before this crosses that door, set the test: where will an unbriefed handler naturally grip, what movement counts as failure, and what control is in place today? A warning is not enough if a normal first grip can make the part move. If fastening is the control, fasten it, lift-test that side without coaching, then call the result.”

LEARNER: "Okay, I think I can do that, though I’m still nervous about being the one to call it. The test is maybe: if someone just picks it up near that side without being warned, and the panel shifts or opens, then it has failed. I should fasten it first, then try that lift, not just write a warning and hope they use the right grip."

STAGE: [A final tutor line closes the scene.]

TUTOR: [glances toward the demo-room door, where the next group is waiting, then places the pen beside the sheet]

“The nervous part is the real handoff: the builder has to make a defensible safety call. Fasten the panel, run the unbriefed lift from that side, and call it: pass if the panel stays put, fail if it shifts or opens. The sheet records that judgment; it does not make it.”
