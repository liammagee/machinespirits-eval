STAGE: [PLACARD ON COUNTER: SAME WATER, HIGHER PRICE, EMPTY DELIVERY BAY.]

TUTOR: [turns yesterday's price placard towards the counter]

“Yesterday: £1.20.”

[points to the taped shop note beside it: “DELIVERY MISSED. TWO CRATES ONLY.”]

“Start with the public facts, not the verdict.

What is visible? A missed delivery, two crates, a half-filled shelf.

What is not visible yet? The margin, the alternatives, whether a per-customer limit was possible.

That note does not clear the seller. It changes what has to be proved.

If stock was ordinary and buyers were trapped, the price jump looks like exploitation. If stock collapsed and demand stayed high, the price may also be doing another job: rationing scarce goods through price instead of queues, limits, or favouritism.

So not ‘greed or no greed’. Too smooth.

The questions are: what constraint is visible, who bears the cost, and what alternatives were available?

Use the placard, the shelf, and the missed delivery to test the charge. Mechanism is not an excuse. It is evidence that makes the moral claim sharper.”

LEARNER: That note is doing a lot of work for the shop, though. “Delivery missed” explains less stock, but it also gives the price rise a respectable costume. If the answer is just “pay more or go without,” then the rationing is still by money, and I am not ready to call that a mechanism instead of exploitation until the limits or other options are actually on the counter.

STAGE: [A second placard is flipped over on the counter: PRICE CAN ACCUSE; PRICE CAN ALSO REPORT.]

TUTOR: [flips the second placard]

“PRICE CAN ACCUSE; PRICE CAN ALSO REPORT.”

Then do not give the shop the conclusion. Give it the burden.

“Delivery missed” proves only this: stock fell at the shelf. It does not prove the chosen response was fair.

Put the facts in two columns.

[points to the note]

“DELIVERY MISSED. TWO CRATES ONLY.”

That belongs under scarcity.

[points to the new price]

“£1.80.”

That belongs under access.

Now the question bites: did the shop add any rule besides price? Two per customer? A queue? A student lunch hold-back? A smaller rise? A posted delivery time?

If not, the mechanism becomes part of the accusation. The price is not only reporting “two crates.” It is deciding that the two crates go to whoever can absorb the rise.

That is where the economics and the moral charge meet.

LEARNER: [looks from the “£1.80” placard to the half-filled shelf]

The missed delivery explains the two crates, but the price sign still feels like the shop choosing richer buyers first. Calling it “reporting” makes me wary, because the report is also a shove at anyone short of cash. So the question is why £1.80 came before a limit, a queue, or some posted plan.

TUTOR: [turns the £1.80 placard face-down and writes on the board]

“RULE NOT POSTED = PRICE IS THE RULE.”

“That is the break in the case.

If there is no limit, no queue, no hold-back, no delivery-time notice, £1.80 is not just reporting scarcity. It is allocating the two crates.

Scarcity explains why yesterday’s arrangement broke. It does not justify the new one.

[sets four paper slips on the counter]

‘PRICE RISE’
‘TWO PER CUSTOMER’
‘QUEUE’
‘HOLD-BACK FOR STUDENTS’

Pick the least unfair rule under two crates only.

Not the pure rule. There is no pure rule on this counter.

For that rule, name three things:

Who gets served first.
Who gets pushed out.
What extra fact would defend it, or condemn it.

Price rise serves buyers who can absorb £1.80 first. It pushes out the tighter budget. It becomes harder to defend if the shop had staff available to enforce a two-per-customer limit.

So the question is no longer whether the note gives the shop a respectable costume.

The question is which rationing rule can survive being posted in public.”

LEARNER: [looks at the four slips, then back at the face-down £1.80 placard]

“Least unfair” already sounds like the shop’s choice has been cleaned up into a neat exercise, when the actual sign made poorer buyers disappear first. I can test two per customer, but if staff could enforce it and chose price instead, that choice needs defending in public.

STAGE: [The room settles on the final exchange.]
