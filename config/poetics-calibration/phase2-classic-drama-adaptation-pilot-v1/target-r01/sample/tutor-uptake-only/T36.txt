STAGE: [A transport case waits open beside a completed sign-off sheet; two technicians in the corridor keep checking the wall clock.]

LEARNER: I checked every item on the sign-off sheet, so I’m not sure what else I’m supposed to decide before it leaves. Is there a specific risk in the prototype you think I missed?

TUTOR: [sets the sign-off sheet beside the cart]

“The sheet shows the required checks were completed. The open decision is whether this prototype stays safe during its first off-script use in a public building.

Look at the cart, not the form. In that maintenance hallway, with a worker turning it near people outside the trial, what is the credible movement or contact hazard? Point to the feature, the evidence that bounds it, and the condition that stops the trial. Without that release judgment, it does not leave on checklist compliance alone.”

LEARNER: Okay, I think I was treating the sheet like it made the decision for me. I’m not completely sure, but the hazard might be the cart swinging wide or clipping someone at the hallway turn, especially at the front edge or handle. The evidence would have to be the turn test or clearance limit that shows it stays controllable, and if it drifts outside that clearance or a bystander comes inside it, the trial stops. Is that the kind of judgment I’m supposed to make before release?

TUTOR: [glances at the waiting technician, then back to the cart]

“The hazard named is the one this release has to bound: front edge or handle contact during the hallway turn.

Now make the release condition concrete. Use the actual values, not placeholders: the cart may leave only if the turn test shows the front edge and handle stay inside ___ clearance at ___ speed, with ___ as the operator control limit.

Then name the first stop condition. Is it crossing the clearance line, a bystander entering the marked zone, or the operator having to recover it?

Point to the measured clearance and the stop line. If those are not real yet, the prototype waits here.”

STAGE: [The corridor clock clicks over; one technician lifts the transport case handle, then sets it down again.]

LEARNER: I’m sorry, I don’t have those numbers in front of me, and I shouldn’t fill the blanks just because the cart is due out. I need to pull the turn-test sheet and point to the measured clearance line, the test speed, and the operator limit. If those are not real, the cart waits here. I think the first stop condition is crossing the clearance line; if a bystander enters the marked zone or the operator has to recover it, that stops it too.

TUTOR: [keeps the signature line blank]

“Then the decision is not ready until the turn-test sheet is on this counter.

Bring the measured clearance line, the test speed, and the operator control limit. On the release note, sort the three stop conditions: which one is the design boundary, which one is the operating condition, and which one is the control failure. Any one stops the trial, but they do not mean the same thing.

If those records do not exist, write ‘hold for bounded turn test’ on the release line. The schedule does not supply the missing evidence.”

LEARNER: [glances toward the waiting technician]

I’m sorry, I can’t sign this out without the turn-test sheet here. I think the clearance line is the design boundary, the marked zone is the operating condition, and needing to recover it is the control failure, but I need the actual numbers before I put that on the release note. If the records aren’t real, I’ll write “hold for bounded turn test” instead of trying to clear it on the schedule.

STAGE: [A final tutor line closes the scene.]

TUTOR: [slides the pen away from the signature line]

“Then write it: hold for bounded turn test.

Use that sorting on the release note: clearance line as design boundary, marked zone as operating condition, recovery as control failure. The actual values go beside them before any release signature.

Tell the technician the cart stays in the lab. A completed checklist records work done; it does not supply the release judgment.”
