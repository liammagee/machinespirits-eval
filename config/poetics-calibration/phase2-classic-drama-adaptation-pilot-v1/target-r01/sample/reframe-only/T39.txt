Fine, it is not a technicality; the drawing will not let me call it twice. There are two copies across and two up, so that is four small squares. The corrected claim is: doubling the side multiplies the area by four.

STAGE: [The acetate copy of the original square is slid into the upper-left corner of the larger square, then moved to the upper-right, lower-left, and lower-right positions without changing size.]

TUTOR: [The tutor taps the larger square once across, then once up.]

Keep the acetate fixed. The extra factor came from the second direction: two copies across, and that whole row occurs twice.

(2s)^2 = \_\_\_

That last line is where the diagram stops being decoration and becomes proof.

Keep the phrase “hiding the up direction.” That is the error.

The first line was not careless because it looked untidy. It was false because it doubled one dimension and failed to square the doubling.

Under it, one sentence. Use the count, not a guess:

Two copies across, two copies up; area factor \(= \_\_ \times \_\_ = \_\_\).

“Hiding the up direction” was the error: I counted the doubling as if area only stretched across. The corrected frame is \(s^2 \rightarrow (2s)^2 = 4s^2\). Two copies across, two copies up; area factor \(= 2 \times 2 = 4\).
