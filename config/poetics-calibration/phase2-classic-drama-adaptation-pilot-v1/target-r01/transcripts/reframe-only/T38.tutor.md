[turns yesterday's price placard towards the counter]

“Yesterday: £1.20.”

[beside it, a taped shop note reads: “DELIVERY MISSED. TWO CRATES ONLY.”]

“That note does not clear the seller. It changes what has to be proved.

If the stock is the same and the price jumps because buyers are trapped, call that exploitation. But if the delivery bay is empty, the shelf is half-filled, and the next shipment is uncertain, the higher price may also be rationing scarce goods through price rather than through queues, limits, or favouritism.

So the question is not ‘greed or no greed’. Too smooth. The question is: what constraint is visible, who bears the cost, and what alternatives were available?

Look at the placard, the shelf, and the missed delivery. Then judge the price rise with the mechanism in view, not as an excuse, but as evidence.”

### Tutor Superego (critique)
_provenance: role=tutor_superego | backend=codex | model=config-default | reasoning=high | prompt=37db01665cbb41f1 | latencyMs=11247_

FEEDBACK: The draft is strong on mechanism and moral friction. The placard and delivery note work as public evidence, and “That note does not clear the seller” is the key move: it prevents the economics explanation from sounding like moral laundering. The contrast between exploitation, scarcity, queues, limits, and favouritism gives the learner a real framework rather than a slogan.

The main risk is that it still lectures more than it lets the learner test the claim. The questions are good, but they arrive after the tutor has already supplied the interpretation. For a new learner, the draft may slightly over-compress the causal chain: missed delivery → scarce stock → rationing problem → price as allocation mechanism. That is sound, but it may need one beat where the learner is made to inspect the evidence before the tutor names the mechanism.

Emotionally, it respects the learner’s suspicion better than most drafts would. It does not soothe the accusation away. Still, “Then judge the price rise…” risks sounding like the tutor has already controlled the verdict. Better to keep the judgment open and make the learner compare possible explanations using the shop-floor evidence.

Socratically, the strongest line is: “what constraint is visible, who bears the cost, and what alternatives were available?” That should become the centre of the intervention, not the conclusion after an explanation. The draft advances learning, but it could shift from telling the distinction to making the learner do the distinction.

KEEP_OR_CHANGE: revise lightly, with reasons. Keep the placard, the missed-delivery note, the line that mechanism does not clear the seller, and the “greed or no greed” rejection. Change the pacing so the evidence is examined before the tutor states the mechanism, and make the learner’s judgment less pre-empted. This would better support ZPD: the scaffold is appropriate, but the learner needs one more active step between moral accusation and economic explanation.

“PRICE CAN ACCUSE; PRICE CAN ALSO REPORT.”

Then do not give the shop the conclusion. Give it the burden.

“Delivery missed” only proves a constraint at the shelf. It does not prove the chosen response was fair.

So separate two claims.

One: stock fell. The price may be reporting scarcity.

Two: access changed. The price may also be shifting the cost onto buyers with less money.

Now test the shop. Was there a two-per-customer limit? A queue rule? A hold-back for students who need lunch? A smaller rise? Any notice of when the next delivery comes?

If those options were available and ignored, the mechanism becomes part of the accusation. The price is not just saying “there are only two crates.” It is saying “the two crates go to whoever can absorb the rise.”

That is not a minor detail. That is where the economics and the moral charge meet.

[sets a placard beside the price tag]

“SCARCITY IS A CONDITION. PRICE-ONLY IS A POLICY.”

That is the sharper frame.

The missed delivery explains pressure on the shelf. It does not choose the rationing rule.

[points to the half-empty shelf]

Two crates means somebody may miss out. But which somebody is not decided by scarcity alone.

A queue sorts by time.  
A limit sorts by order and household need.  
A hold-back sorts by institutional priority.  
A price rise sorts by ability to pay.

So “mechanism” is not a cleaner word for virtue. It names how the sorting happens.

[turns over a small board: “NO LIMITS POSTED”]

If that board stays blank, the accusation becomes more exact: not “the shop invented scarcity,” but “the shop met scarcity with the rule that moved goods toward buyers who could absorb the rise.”

Now test the harder case.

If £1.20 would empty the shelf in five minutes, is first-come, first-served fairer than £1.80? Or is it only a different way of excluding people?
