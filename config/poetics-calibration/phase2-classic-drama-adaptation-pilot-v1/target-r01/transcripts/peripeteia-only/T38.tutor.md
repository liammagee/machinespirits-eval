### Tutor Superego (critique)

FEEDBACK: The draft is strong on mechanism and moral friction. The placard and delivery note work as public evidence, and “That note does not clear the seller” is the key move: it prevents the economics explanation from sounding like moral laundering. The contrast between exploitation, scarcity, queues, limits, and favouritism gives the learner a real framework rather than a slogan.

The main risk is that it still lectures more than it lets the learner test the claim. The questions are good, but they arrive after the tutor has already supplied the interpretation. For a new learner, the draft may slightly over-compress the causal chain: missed delivery → scarce stock → rationing problem → price as allocation mechanism. That is sound, but it may need one beat where the learner is made to inspect the evidence before the tutor names the mechanism.

Emotionally, it respects the learner’s suspicion better than most drafts would. It does not soothe the accusation away. Still, “Then judge the price rise…” risks sounding like the tutor has already controlled the verdict. Better to keep the judgment open and make the learner compare possible explanations using the shop-floor evidence.

Socratically, the strongest line is: “what constraint is visible, who bears the cost, and what alternatives were available?” That should become the centre of the intervention, not the conclusion after an explanation. The draft advances learning, but it could shift from telling the distinction to making the learner do the distinction.

KEEP_OR_CHANGE: revise lightly, with reasons. Keep the placard, the missed-delivery note, the line that mechanism does not clear the seller, and the “greed or no greed” rejection. Change the pacing so the evidence is examined before the tutor states the mechanism, and make the learner’s judgment less pre-empted. This would better support ZPD: the scaffold is appropriate, but the learner needs one more active step between moral accusation and economic explanation.

“RULE NOT POSTED = PRICE IS THE RULE.”

“Then call it what it is.

If there is no limit, no queue, no hold-back, no delivery-time notice, £1.80 is not just reporting scarcity. It is allocating the two crates.

Scarcity explains why yesterday’s arrangement broke. It does not justify the new one.

Now compare the rules on the counter:

‘PRICE RISE’
‘TWO PER CUSTOMER’
‘QUEUE’
‘HOLD-BACK FOR STUDENTS’

Each one rations. None is innocent.

Price rations by ability and willingness to pay. A limit rations by household size and timing. A queue rations by time. A hold-back rations by a stated priority.

So the charge becomes sharper: not ‘price rise means greed’, but ‘why did the shop choose money as the rationing rule, and why was that rule not posted as a rule?’”

FEEDBACK: The draft is pedagogically strong in one respect: it stops treating price as either “greed” or “neutral signal” and reframes it as one rationing rule among several. “Scarcity explains why yesterday’s arrangement broke. The tutor repeats and sharpens that insight, but does not make the learner do the next analytic move. It advances the argument, but not enough of the learner’s own reasoning.

The board list helps lower cognitive load because it makes alternatives visible. But it could become more generative if the learner had to test one rule against concrete costs: who benefits, who is excluded, what evidence would make it defensible. As written, the tutor supplies the whole comparison.

But the adaptive mechanism should go further: shift from explanation to an evidence-standard task. Make the learner judge which posted rule would be least unfair under the two-crate constraint, and what fact would change that judgment.

REGISTER_CHECK: The register mostly serves the scene.
