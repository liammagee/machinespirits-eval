STAGE: [The learner has just drawn a larger square around the first one, each side twice as long; the ruler still pins the lower edge in place.]

LEARNER: [slides the acetate square across the larger outline, corner to corner, then pauses] I thought doubling the side meant it was just twice as much, but it fits once, twice, three, four times in the bigger square. So the drawing is not backing my claim; it is making it wrong, and I need to see where the extra factor came from.

TUTOR: [draws one vertical split and one horizontal split through the larger outline.]

Stop there. The old claim was: double the side, double the area. The acetate has made a counterexample: two copies across and two copies up.

Count the grid now. Not two copies. A two-by-two square of copies.

Now state the corrected claim from the drawing, not from the guess.

LEARNER: [keeps the acetate pinned inside one grid square]

Fine, it is not a technicality; the drawing will not let me call it twice. There are two copies across and two up, so that is four small squares. The corrected claim is: doubling the side multiplies the area by four.

STAGE: [The acetate copy of the original square is slid into the upper-left corner of the larger square, then moved to the upper-right, lower-left, and lower-right positions without changing size.]

TUTOR: [The tutor taps the larger square once across, then once up.]

Keep the acetate fixed. The extra factor came from the second direction: two copies across, and that whole row occurs twice.

So the area factor is not 2. It is \(2 \times 2\).

Now make the exam line match the drawing. Old side: \(s\). Old area: \(s^2\). New side: \(2s\).

Do not skip the square. Write it first:

\[
(2s)^2 = \_\_\_
\]

That last line is where the diagram stops being decoration and becomes proof.

LEARNER: [keeps the acetate in the lower-left grid square and writes \((2s)^2=4s^2\) under the larger square.]

\((2s)^2\) is \(2s\) times \(2s\), so the 2 is squared as well: \(4s^2\), not \(2s^2\). For the exam answer, I should state “two copies across and two copies up, so \(2\times2\)” before the algebra, or the same mistake can hide in the notation.

TUTOR: [sets the ruler along the bottom edge, then turns it up the left edge.]

That is the missing factor.

Now make it exam-ready in order: diagram first, algebra second, conclusion last.

Write three lines, not a paragraph:

Line 1: count across and up.

Line 2: convert that count into \(2 \times 2 = 4\) copies.

Line 3: finish with \((2s)^2 = 4s^2\), and state the area change.

LEARNER: [sets the acetate into one grid square.] Across: two copies; up: two copies.
So the larger square contains \(2 \times 2 = 4\) copies of the original.
\((2s)^2 = 4s^2\), so doubling the side makes the area four times as large, not twice.

STAGE: [A final tutor line closes the scene.]

TUTOR: [The tutor lifts the acetate and lays it once over each of the four positions in the larger square.]

“Now the drawing and the algebra say the same thing. This three-line answer is exam-ready: two across, two up; \(2 \times 2 = 4\); \((2s)^2 = 4s^2\). Stop there.”
