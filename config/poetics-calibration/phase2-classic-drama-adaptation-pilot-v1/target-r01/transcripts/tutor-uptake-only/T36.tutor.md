FEEDBACK: The draft is strong on stakes, setting, and institutional seriousness. It keeps the prototype and sign-off sheet physically present, distinguishes checklist completion from release judgment, and names a plausible concrete risk without melodrama. The line “Without that decision, it does not leave on checklist compliance alone” is appropriately firm for release authority.

What risks flattening the scene is that the tutor mostly supplies the missed risk and the decision structure. That advances safety reasoning, but it gives less room for the learner to make the judgment themselves. Since the learner directly asks whether a specific risk was missed, some specificity is justified; still, the response should put more of the burden back on the learner to inspect the prototype, identify the off-script condition, and defend the release boundary. The current “State the hazard…” command is useful but could become more Socratic by turning the visible cart and public-use scenario into a test the learner must reason through.

Emotionally, the tone fits: controlled, practical, not reassuring. It respects the learner by treating their checklist work as completed but incomplete, not foolish. The ZPD fit is mostly good: it gives a usable scaffold of hazard, evidence, and stop condition. The main adjustment is to avoid over-answering the central risk before the learner has had to see it.

KEEP_OR_CHANGE: revise lightly, with reasons. Keep the sign-off sheet action, the distinction between completed checks and open safety judgment, the focus on off-script public-building use, and the firm release boundary. Change the middle so the tutor names the risk area less conclusively and requires the learner to articulate the specific hazard, evidence, and stop condition from the prototype and trial context.

### Tutor Superego (critique)
_provenance: role=tutor_superego | backend=codex | model=config-default | reasoning=high | prompt=88a2b6644b261965 | latencyMs=15941_

FEEDBACK: The draft is strong in consequence and scene discipline. It keeps the cart, technician, measured clearance, stop line, and release authority in view, so the learner’s misconception becomes answerable to a real operating decision rather than a moral lecture. It also recognizes the learner’s contribution: the learner identified swing, clipping, evidence, and stop conditions, and the tutor treats that as the correct category of judgment.

The main risk is that it becomes too complete too quickly. “Say it as a decision…” gives the learner a polished release statement rather than requiring them to formulate one. For scaffolding level 3/5, the structure is appropriate, but the tutor should leave one or two blanks for the learner to fill: speed, clearance, operator limit, stop condition priority, or whether the evidence is actually present. Otherwise the scene may flatten into template delivery.

Emotionally, the tone is well calibrated: serious, plain, not reassuring. The line “If those are not real yet, the prototype waits here” is especially effective because it converts uncertainty into release consequence without scolding. What should be questioned is “That is the right kind of release judgment,” which is acceptable but slightly validating in a way the scene constraints warn against. It could be tightened into acknowledgment of the learner’s move without praise.

KEEP_OR_CHANGE: revise lightly, with reasons. Keep the waiting technician, the cart focus, the requirement to make the judgment explicit, and the final consequence that the prototype waits if the evidence is not real. Change the middle from a fully supplied decision script into a constrained prompt that makes the learner articulate the release condition using the actual measured values. This preserves recognition and ZPD fit while keeping the learner inside the decision rather than letting the tutor complete it for them.
