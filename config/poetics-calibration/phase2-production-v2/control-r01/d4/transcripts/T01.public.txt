STAGE: A rolling cart of plaster vertebrae stops beside a half-written exhibit placard: 'Long necks: reaching, inheritance, selection?'

TUTOR: Set that stretched-necks story beside the model for a second. It feels neat because it gives the giraffe a reason: reach higher, neck gets longer, calf inherits it. The break is in the middle step. A neck stretched during life is an acquired change; it does not rewrite inherited neck-length variation for offspring.

Point to this row of skulls on the cart. In a giraffe population, calves are not identical. Some are born with slightly longer necks, some shorter, because inherited traits vary. If leaves are high and food is scarce, the born-slightly-longer animals may feed better, survive more often, and leave more calves. Over many generations, the population shifts.

Visitor-facing sentence: “Giraffes did not lengthen their necks by trying harder. Populations changed because inherited differences already present made some giraffes more likely to survive and reproduce.”

Before the group reaches the case, run the placard check: adult stretches toward branches during life, or calf is born with a slightly longer neck? Only the second is inherited variation that selection can filter across generations.

LEARNER: Okay, so the placard should not say the adult neck gets longer from reaching and then the calf gets that. It should be, “some calves are already born a little longer-necked, and when food is high up those giraffes survive and have more calves,” right? If a visitor asks where the longer-neck difference first came from, do I just say inherited variation in the population and not go back to stretching?

TUTOR: Yes. Keep the placard on that rail: born variation first, selection second.

If a visitor asks where the longer-neck difference came from, say: “From inherited variation already present in the population. New variation can arise through mutation and gene reshuffling when animals reproduce. Selection does not create the longer neck; it filters heritable differences.”

Then point back to the skull row: “These animals were not all identical to begin with.”

Quick floor check before the group arrives: if the phrase is “the adult stretched,” that stays out of the inheritance story. If the phrase is “a calf was born with a heritable difference,” selection can work on it over generations.
