The exhibit needs the second answer. Giraffes in a population already vary a little in neck length. Some of that variation is inherited. In a setting where higher leaves matter, giraffes with slightly longer inherited necks are more likely to survive and reproduce. Over many generations, those longer-neck traits become more common.

Key label contrast: **stretching during life is acquired; selection works on inherited variation.** A summer tan does not make a baby born tanned. In the same way, a giraffe’s reaching does not rewrite what its offspring inherit.

TUTOR: Set the two vertebrae side by side: not identical, not stretched.

Three gallery beats:

First: giraffes are born with small inherited differences.
Second: high leaves do not create the differences; they make some differences matter.
Third: giraffes with inherited longer necks leave more offspring, so that trait becomes more common.

For visitors, that is enough on where the differences come from: inheritance is not photocopying. Offspring get shuffled genetic material, and tiny copying changes sometimes appear.

If the neat wrong story starts to rise, switch the verb:

Not: “They stretched and passed it on.”
Say: “They varied, and selection favored the inherited longer necks.”

Doors in ten. Rehearse the visitor version once: one sentence, no stretching as the cause.
