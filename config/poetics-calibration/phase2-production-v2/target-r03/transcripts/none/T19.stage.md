This readout says **CO2: about 0.04%**. Leave that number on the label. The arithmetic is not the embarrassment; it is the starting point.

The useful split for visitors is this: **amount** is one question, **effect per molecule** is another. Nitrogen and oxygen make up almost all the air, but they barely interact with the infrared radiation Earth is trying to send back to space. Carbon dioxide is scarce, yes, but it absorbs in important infrared bands. So the relevant question is not “Is it most of the atmosphere?” but “Does it sit in the path of outgoing heat, and does adding more change that path?”

At 420 parts per million, CO2 means 420 molecules in every million air molecules. That is tiny as a percentage, but across the full height of the atmosphere it is an enormous absorbing column. And the increase matters: before heavy industrialisation it was about 280 parts per million, so the active gas has risen by roughly half. The school visitor’s arithmetic should compare **420 to 280**, not only **0.04 to 100**.

A concise floor answer would be: “A trace gas can matter if it interacts strongly with the kind of energy being trapped. Most air does not. CO2 does. Small share, large radiative leverage.”
