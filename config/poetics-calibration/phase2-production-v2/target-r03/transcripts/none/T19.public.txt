STAGE: Fluorescent lights click awake over the climate gallery while a wall monitor loops yesterday's carbon dioxide graph in silence.

TUTOR: This readout says **CO2: about 0.04%**. Leave that number on the label. The arithmetic is not the embarrassment; it is the starting point.

Set the two gas jars beside it: **amount** is one question, **effect per molecule** is another. Nitrogen and oxygen make up almost all the air, but their simple two-atom structure means they barely absorb Earth’s outgoing infrared radiation. Carbon dioxide is scarce, yes, but its molecular structure lets it absorb in important infrared bands.

Here is the check for a school group: if the tiny percentage alone settled the matter, then nitrogen and oxygen would dominate the heat-trapping story. They do not. The deciding question is: **which molecules actually interact with the outgoing heat?**

At about 420 parts per million, CO2 means 420 molecules in every million air molecules. Tiny as a percentage, but spread through the full height of the atmosphere, that is a large number of infrared-absorbing molecules. And the increase matters: before heavy industrialisation it was about 280 parts per million, so the active gas has risen by roughly half.

A concise floor answer would be: “A trace gas can matter when it is the kind of molecule that absorbs outgoing heat. Most air is not. CO2 is. The percentage is small, but the change in the heat-absorbing part of the air is large enough to alter the balance.”

LEARNER: I would still get stopped at the label: 0.04% looks too small to move a whole climate system, and that is exactly what the pupils will point at. The jars explain which gas absorbs, but “a large number through the full height” needs a firmer measurement than just sounding large. What can this bench show that the rise from 280 to 420 ppm changes the outgoing heat enough to matter?

STAGE: A maintenance worker briefly cuts power to the animated display, leaving only the gas jars and printed spectrum card available. The tutor's next turn must be compact and use the remaining objects.

TUTOR: The printed spectrum card is the firmer bench.

Set the **0.04% label** beside the **CO2 jar**, then point to the dark band around **15 micrometres**. That band is where CO2 absorbs part of Earth’s outgoing infrared heat. Nitrogen and oxygen are most of the air, but on this card they barely register.

Now ask the pupils one check: **which thing rose by half: the whole atmosphere, or the heat-absorbing CO2 part?** From **280 ppm to 420 ppm** is a **50% increase** in that active absorbing gas in each vertical column of air.

The bench shows the selectivity: CO2 is in the outgoing heat stream. The climate measurement gives the size: that rise adds roughly **2 W/m²** of extra heat-trapping effect before the climate responds; a full CO2 doubling is about **3.7 W/m²**.

Floor line: “The percentage tells us amount. The spectrum tells us leverage. CO2 is scarce, but it absorbs in the right part of the infrared, and its rise is measurable in watts per square metre.”

LEARNER: The dark band gives me something firmer than the jar, but it still feels like one card is being asked to carry too much. A pupil can see “CO2 absorbs there”; they cannot see the step from that patch of darkness to **2 W/m²** across the planet. I would want the display to show how that number is measured or calculated, otherwise the 0.04% label will still look like the more honest object on the bench.

TUTOR: The wattmeter readout is the missing exhibit, then.

Put one more strip beneath the spectrum, with the vertical axis labelled **watts per square metre per wavelength**. Now the dark CO2 band is not just a stain on a card; it is a dent in an energy graph.

The bench sequence should read: **280 ppm spectrum**, **420 ppm spectrum**, then **difference: about 2 W/m² before warming responds**. Shade only the energy lost between the two spectra and ask the pupil what the shaded area represents. That area is the bridge from “CO2 absorbs there” to “the outgoing heat has changed by this many watts per square metre”.

The card alone does not measure the planet. It demonstrates the mechanism. The wider number comes from measured infrared spectra plus radiative-transfer calculation: same atmosphere, larger CO2 column, temperatures held fixed for the first pass, then add up the changed outgoing energy across wavelengths.

Floor line: “The 0.04% label is honest, but incomplete. The honest companion measurement is the lost outgoing infrared energy, and that is counted in watts per square metre.”

LEARNER: The shaded dent helps, but the 2 W/m² still looks like the number that arrives after the exhibit has made its case. If the pupils cannot see at least one real measured spectrum, or a clear source for that difference strip, the 0.04% label will still feel like the more honest object. I would ask: where on this bench is the evidence that the dent is measured, not just drawn?

STAGE: End the scene with one concise tutor line. Do not ask the learner to continue unless the scene genuinely needs it.

TUTOR: The source card goes under the dent.

Place one real satellite infrared spectrum on the bench: **outgoing heat measured above the atmosphere**, wavelength along the bottom, **W/m² per wavelength** up the side. Keep the jagged measured line visible. Mark the CO2 band near **15 micrometres**, and give the instrument, date, and data source on the label.

Then give each object only the claim it is allowed to make:

**Measured spectrum:** the present atmosphere really has this dent in outgoing infrared.

**Laboratory CO2 absorption card:** CO2 absorbs strongly in that part of the infrared.

**Calculation overlay:** using measured gas absorption and the observed atmosphere, the calculation reproduces the measured dent.

**Difference strip:** after that check, the same calculation compares **280 ppm** with **420 ppm**, with temperatures held fixed, and the shaded lost outgoing energy is about **2 W/m²**.

So the difference strip is not a photograph. It is a labelled inference from measurements that have first been made to match the real spectrum on the bench.

Floor line: “The 0.04% label measures amount; the spectrum measures effect, and the watt figure comes from the measured dent plus the checked calculation.”
