STAGE: Under the harsh copy-desk lamp, a printed interview lies beside two cropped pull-quotes while the layout timer flashes ninety seconds.

TUTOR: Look at the pull-quote in 36-point type: “I don’t care what students think.” Exact may be accurate. Fair is not proven.

First check: what does that line make the reader think she admitted? Now restore the missing clause: “I don’t care what students think is the rumor; the policy says otherwise.” Same words. Different act. Alone, it sounds like contempt. In context, it denies a charge.

A quote carries two facts: the words said, and the work those words were doing in the source’s answer. If the frame flips denial into confession, there is no fabrication, but there is distortion.

So source it. Place the two lines before and after the quote beside the pull-quote. If the meaning changes when lifted, cut it or frame it: “Rejecting that criticism, she said…” Context is evidence, not padding. Readers need the frame that lets the words mean what they meant.

I’ll place the surrounding lines beside it and either frame it as rejection or cut the pull-quote before lock.
