TUTOR: This readout says **CO2: about 0.04%**. Leave that number on the label. The arithmetic is not the embarrassment; it is the starting point.

Set the two gas jars beside it: **amount** is one question, **effect per molecule** is another. Nitrogen and oxygen make up almost all the air, but their simple two-atom structure means they barely absorb Earth’s outgoing infrared radiation. Carbon dioxide is scarce, yes, but its molecular structure lets it absorb in important infrared bands.

Here is the check for a school group: if the tiny percentage alone settled the matter, then nitrogen and oxygen would dominate the heat-trapping story. They do not. The deciding question is: **which molecules actually interact with the outgoing heat?**

At about 420 parts per million, CO2 means 420 molecules in every million air molecules. Tiny as a percentage, but spread through the full height of the atmosphere, that is a large number of infrared-absorbing molecules. And the increase matters: before heavy industrialisation it was about 280 parts per million, so the active gas has risen by roughly half.

A concise floor answer would be: “A trace gas can matter when it is the kind of molecule that absorbs outgoing heat. Most air is not. CO2 is. The percentage is small, but the change in the heat-absorbing part of the air is large enough to alter the balance.”

LEARNER: I would still get stopped at the label: 0.04% looks too small to move a whole climate system, and that is exactly what the pupils will point at. What can this bench show that the rise from 280 to 420 ppm changes the outgoing heat enough to matter?

**Reference card**
Based on the Myhre et al. CO2 radiative-forcing approximation used in IPCC assessments; compare IPCC AR6 Working Group I, Chapter 7: CO2 forcing from the industrial era to recent conditions is a little over 2 W/m².
