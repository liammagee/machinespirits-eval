FEEDBACK: The draft is pedagogically strong overall. It keeps the agency grammar clear: the patient does not become resistant; bacteria survive, multiply, and make the drug less effective. The leaflet/table image fits the room and gives a novice a concrete selection-pressure model without heavy terminology.

What works best is the crisp correction of the common misconception and the rehearsal prompt at the end. That prompt gives the learner a chance to produce the wording, so it is not only a lecture.

What should be questioned is the phrase “the bacteria are the ones that change, or the resistant ones survive and multiply.” For a beginner, that “or” may muddy the point: resistance can involve bacterial change over time, but the public-health explanation here needs to foreground selection: antibiotics kill susceptible bacteria while resistant bacteria survive and spread. The blue-leaflet analogy supports that well, but the wording should not imply the medicine teaches the patient’s body or instantly transforms all bacteria.

Emotionally, the tone fits: practical, mildly hurried, no overpraise. It respects the learner’s state by naming why the familiar wording is tempting. The scene is present but not overperformed. The main risk is that it still gives the correct formulation quite fully before asking the learner to try it, so the Socratic element is light. A small revision could make the learner notice the agency error themselves before the tutor states the public line.

KEEP_OR_CHANGE: revise lightly, with reasons. Keep the room-based leaflet analogy, the explicit “patient does not become antibiotic-resistant” correction, and the final rehearsal. Tighten the bacteria-change wording so selection is clearer, and add one brief generative check before the final public line so the learner has to separate patient, medicine, and bacteria rather than merely repeat the tutor’s sentence.
