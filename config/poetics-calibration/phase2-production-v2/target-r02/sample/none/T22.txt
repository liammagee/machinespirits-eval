STAGE: The projector hums over a chart of neighborhood averages while a paper sign for tonight's talk curls at one corner.

TUTOR: That ethical worry is doing useful work here. Averages can be valuable, but only if their boundaries stay visible; the chart must not be made to speak beyond its evidence.

The projected average describes a group pattern, not a private truth about any one visitor. The chart may say: “In this survey, younger visitors reported shorter average dwell time at this exhibit.” It does not say: “This young visitor is impatient,” or even “this young visitor spent little time here.” That second claim would need individual evidence.

Before the chairs fill, try one risky sentence aloud and test it: does the chart say this about the group, or would this sentence quietly attach a label to a person? The careful speaker lets the chart remain a chart, not a verdict on someone walking through the room.

TUTOR: That revision keeps the chart on the screen, not on a person in the chair.

The nervousness is not a flaw in the talk; it is the part of the speaker that notices when a statistic might become a label. “Probably” is the hinge. If it refers to the surveyed group, it can belong to a careful statistical claim. If it leans toward one visitor, it begins to smuggle in an individual judgment.

For rehearsal, try this small test before the room fills: after the sentence, ask, “Who is the subject here?” If the subject is “the chart,” “the survey,” or “the group,” the evidence is being kept in its proper place. If the subject has quietly become “this young visitor,” then the sentence needs evidence from that visitor, not only from the projected average.
