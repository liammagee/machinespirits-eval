TUTOR: The percentage is not being swept under the carpet. Carbon dioxide is about 0.04 per cent of the air: roughly four molecules in ten thousand. That is small as an amount.

Now look at the infrared camera before the gallery closes. It is not measuring ordinary visible light; it is detecting heat radiation from warm objects. So the useful question is not only, “How many molecules are present?” It is, “Which molecules interact with this outgoing infrared?”

Nitrogen and oxygen are almost all of the air, but for much of this infrared they are rather poor catchers. Carbon dioxide is rare, but it absorbs strongly in particular infrared bands. A small concentration can matter if it sits in the part of the process that controls how easily heat escapes.

Try the arithmetic and the mechanism together: tiny percentage, yes. But not every molecule has the same job. The test is whether adding more of that infrared-absorbing gas changes the rate at which Earth loses heat to space. That is where the climate effect comes from.

LEARNER: The four-in-ten-thousand figure is still the bit that makes this feel slippery. The infrared camera shows that some gases catch heat better than others, but that does not yet prove that adding a little more CO2 changes much if those bands are already crowded. I want the missing step: how much extra blockage comes from the next bit, not just that CO2 has the right kind of job.

TUTOR: The missing step is marginal opacity, not mere presence.

The CO2 band is not one doorway that becomes simply shut. It is more like this infrared display split into many neighbouring colours. At the centre of the main CO2 band, the air near the surface is already a poor escape route; adding more CO2 there changes little. At the edges, though, the route is only partly narrowed. Add CO2, and those edges thicken.

Then comes the altitude step. If a wavelength can no longer escape from lower, warmer air, the effective escape point shifts upward. Higher air is colder; colder air radiates less heat to space. That is extra blockage from the next bit.

The arithmetic follows that mechanism. Pre-industrial CO2 was about 280 parts per million; the present atmosphere is above 420. A full doubling gives roughly 3.7 watts per square metre of extra heat retention before feedbacks. The rise from 280 to 420 gives about two watts per square metre: not because four in ten thousand is secretly large, but because the added molecules widen the partly open edges and lift the heat-leak point to colder air.

TUTOR: Then the label needs its tag attached.

The extra trapping is calculated wavelength by wavelength, using laboratory CO2 absorption spectra, atmospheric temperature profiles, and radiative-transfer calculations. The compressed museum version is:

`ΔF ≈ 5.35 × ln(C/C₀) W/m²`

Put in 280 ppm and 420 ppm:

`5.35 × ln(420/280) = 5.35 × ln(1.5) ≈ 2.2 W/m²`

The `5.35` is the fitted shortcut from the detailed physics. The logarithm is the crowded-band correction: the band centre is already crowded, so the next CO2 does not block in a simple straight line. Its main effect is at the partly open edges and by shifting the effective heat-leak level upward into colder air.

So the two watts is not a vote-count claim about four molecules in ten thousand. It says that raising CO2 from 280 to 420 ppm reduces outgoing infrared by about 2.2 watts per square metre, before the slower climate feedbacks respond.
