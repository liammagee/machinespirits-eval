STAGE: Leaflets about antibiotics lie half-sorted beside a cooling kettle while a corridor bell cuts through the room.

TUTOR: Before we go through those leaflets, I want to tighten the wording. It feels natural to say the body gets used to the antibiotic, but that points at the wrong thing. The patient does not become antibiotic-resistant. Resistant bacteria survive the medicine and multiply.

Use the table. If the kettle knocked half these leaflets away and only the blue ones were left, the pile would soon look mostly blue. Antibiotics can work like that: the medicine kills the bacteria it can kill; bacteria that can withstand it are left to spread.

Quick check before we go in: patient, medicine, bacteria. Which one is changing or being selected for?

The public line is: the same drug may stop working because resistant bacteria survive and spread, not because the patient’s body has got used to the medicine. Try the sentence once: “Antibiotic resistance happens when…”

LEARNER: I was thinking the person’s body gets used to the antibiotic, so I’m having to move that idea over. The leaflet pile helps a bit, like the medicine knocks away some and the ones left take over, but I keep nearly saying the patient is changing. So antibiotic resistance happens when the bacteria that can survive the medicine are the ones left to spread?

TUTOR: The move from patient to bacteria is the bit to keep. Antibiotic resistance happens when bacteria that can survive the antibiotic are left behind and multiply; the patient may still be ill, but the patient has not become resistant.

Use the leaflet pile if the words wobble: kettle knocks some away, blue leaflets remain, then the table becomes mostly blue. Say it for the talk in one clean line: “Antibiotic resistance happens when resistant bacteria survive the antibiotic and spread.” Try that sentence aloud once, with bacteria as the subject.
