STAGE: A laminated world map curls above the worktable while a globe rolls gently against a stack of exhibit labels.

TUTOR: [Slides the globe under the wall map and taps Greenland, then Africa.]

Hold the label.

First comparison. On the Mercator map: Greenland looks close to Africa. Measured area: Greenland, about 2.2 million square kilometers. Africa, about 30.4 million. Africa is roughly fourteen times larger.

So the visual claim fails. What has to change: the land, or the map’s scale?

The map. Mercator keeps local angles useful for navigation. It pays for that by stretching scale toward the poles. Greenland sits high north, so it swells. Africa crosses the equator, so it stays much nearer its real size.

Use the tracing paper as a warning label, not proof. Trace Greenland on the wall map, mark its latitude, then hold the same outline beside the globe. The flat-map size is the distortion to explain.

Public label: “Greenland appears very large on a Mercator map because high-latitude areas are enlarged. In actual area, Africa is about fourteen times larger.”
