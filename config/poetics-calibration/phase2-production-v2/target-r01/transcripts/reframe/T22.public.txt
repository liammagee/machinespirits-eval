TUTOR: The worry belongs beside these labels. It helps keep a census table from becoming a polite stereotype with arithmetic behind it.

Take this table: a district’s average household income is $52,000. That does not mean the next resident through the archive door earns $52,000, or near it. One household may be far below, another far above. The average describes the district pattern; it does not identify a particular life.

So the line to guard is narrow but important: aggregate evidence can support claims about groups, places, and distributions. It cannot, by itself, support claims about an individual case.

Before the preview, set one draft sentence beside one table. Ask what unit the table actually measured: district, block, household, age group. Then see whether the sentence stays at that same level. If it slides from “this group shows a pattern” to “this person must be like the pattern,” that is the ecological fallacy beginning.

TUTOR: The moral worry is doing useful work here. It is keeping the label honest.

The $52,000 figure belongs to the district row. It can support a claim about the district’s income pattern. It cannot, by itself, make the next household through the archive door stand near $52,000.

The delicate word is “probably.” An average may orient a reader toward the center of a group, but it does not show how crowded that center is. One district may have many households near $52,000; another may be split between very low and very high incomes. The same mean can hide both arrangements.

So keep the grammatical subject at the same level as the evidence. If the table row is district, let the district carry the claim. If the sentence begins to make a household carry it, the label needs household-level evidence, or at least a distribution showing how households are spread.

Before the preview: point to the noun in the sentence, then point to the row in the table. If they are not the same kind of thing, the sentence has begun to slide.
