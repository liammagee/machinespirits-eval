STAGE: A laminated world map curls above the worktable while a globe rolls gently against a stack of exhibit labels.

TUTOR: [Slides the globe under the wall map and taps Greenland, then Africa.]

Hold the label.

First comparison. On the Mercator map: Greenland looks close to Africa. Measured area: Greenland, about 2.2 million square kilometers. Africa, about 30.4 million. Africa is roughly fourteen times larger.

So the visual claim fails. What has to change: the land, or the map’s scale?

The map. Mercator keeps local angles useful for navigation. It pays for that by stretching scale toward the poles. Greenland sits high north, so it swells. Africa crosses the equator, so it stays much nearer its real size.

Use the tracing paper as a warning label, not proof. Trace Greenland on the wall map, mark its latitude, then hold the same outline beside the globe. The flat-map size is the distortion to explain.

Public label: “Greenland appears very large on a Mercator map because high-latitude areas are enlarged. In actual area, Africa is about fourteen times larger.”

LEARNER: I thought Greenland was nearly in Africa’s range because it looks like it on that wall map, and that is exactly the trap. The numbers make the map feel less trustworthy now: 2.2 against 30.4, so the scale has to be doing the damage near the pole. I can say “Mercator preserves angles for navigation” once, but I want the cart label to stay on the practical point: high latitude stretches Greenland, Africa is still about fourteen times larger.

TUTOR: [Sets the tracing paper over Greenland, then taps the globe beside Africa.]

Hold the correction there.

Not “the map is untrustworthy.” Sharper: the map is trustworthy for one job, not this job. Mercator keeps angles useful for navigation. Area comparison is where it fails, especially near the poles.

Label choice: blame “the map,” or blame “the projection’s scale near the poles”?

Use the second.

Cart label:

“Greenland looks very large on a Mercator map because the projection stretches high-latitude regions. In actual area, Greenland is about 2.2 million km²; Africa is about 30.4 million km², roughly fourteen times larger.”

Activity order: wall map first, prediction second, tracing paper third, globe and numbers last. The visitor’s first wrong impression becomes the exhibit test.

Here is the sharper test.

On the wall map, the eye says “near match.” On the measurements, Africa answers: 30.4 against 2.2. Fourteen times larger.

One audit before the label goes on the cart: does it name the mechanism, not insult the map?

Mechanism: Mercator keeps direction and angles useful. It stretches scale near the poles. Greenland sits high north, so the wall-map shape swells.

Then the label can read:

“Mercator is useful for direction and angles, but it enlarges land near the poles. Greenland sits high north, so it looks much larger than its true area. Africa is about fourteen times larger.”

Sequence stays clean: look, predict, trace, compare, correct. The wrong first glance becomes the lesson.
