The worry is not an obstacle to the work; it is part of keeping the label honest. An average is a statement about a group pattern, not a quiet label placed on each person inside the group.

Take one table: if a district has an average household income of $52,000, that does not mean the next resident through the archive door earns $52,000, or even near it. One household may be far below, another far above. The statistic describes the district’s distribution; it does not identify the life of a particular person.

So the careful line is this: aggregate evidence can support claims about places, groups, or patterns. It cannot, by itself, support claims about an individual case. The ecological fallacy begins when the label slides from “this group shows a pattern” to “this person must be like the pattern.”

For the exhibit note, the test is simple. Point to the census table and ask: does this sentence describe the group the table actually measured, or has it smuggled the group’s average onto an individual?
