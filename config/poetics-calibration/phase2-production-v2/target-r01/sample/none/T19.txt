TUTOR: The jar stays useful because it keeps the arithmetic honest: CO2 is tiny by count, about 0.04% of the air. The bench mistake would be to treat bulk percentage as the same thing as climate leverage.

Clean chain:

1. CO2 absorbs infrared at specific wavelengths. Not all heat, not all light, specific bands.

2. Earth’s surface emits infrared in some of those same bands. So CO2 sits in part of the heat’s exit path.

3. Add more CO2, and those bands become more opaque. The exit door is not closed; heat escapes from higher, colder layers, which radiate less efficiently.

4. That creates an energy imbalance: less heat leaves than enters until the surface and lower atmosphere warm enough to restore balance.

5. The size is measurable. Added CO2 since preindustrial air accounts for a little over 2 watts per square meter of forcing; doubled CO2 is about 3.7 watts per square meter before feedbacks.

Now the “other explanations” test.

A stronger Sun should add energy broadly and tend to warm the stratosphere too. Added CO2 predicts a narrower infrared fingerprint and a cooling stratosphere, because more heat is trapped below while the upper atmosphere loses heat differently.

Internal variability is real, but it mostly moves heat around within the system. It does not easily explain a persistent planetary energy surplus with oceans taking up most of the added heat.

Aerosols matter too, but most human-made aerosols reflect sunlight and partly mask warming rather than create it.

Board-meeting checkpoint: if CO2 is the driver, the evidence should show spectral blockage in CO2 bands, more downward infrared at the ground, rising ocean heat, lower-atmosphere warming, and stratospheric cooling. That is the pattern being claimed.

So the argument is not “tiny gas, trust the metaphor.” It is: tiny gas, known wavelength effect; added amount, calculated forcing; measured spectral blockage; warming pattern that fits CO2 better than the main alternatives.
