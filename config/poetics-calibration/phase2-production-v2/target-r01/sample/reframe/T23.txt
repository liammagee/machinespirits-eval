TUTOR: Set the steel coupon on the bench. Strong piece. Now put it in the bridge.

Clip this load arrow over the deck. Trace the force with a pencil before the rig moves: deck, truss member, bolted plate, bearing, table. Stop where the line changes direction. Circle the joint that has to carry the messiest job.

That is the first safety question. Not “is the material strong?” but “where can the load travel?” One weak connection, one bad angle, one support that cannot take the reaction, and the strong member just delivers force to the failure point faster.

Now run the rig slow. Put a finger on this joint. Feel that tick? That is not material strength. That is motion finding a rhythm. Turn the speed a little and watch whether the deflection fades or grows. If the loading frequency lines up with the bridge’s natural frequency, small pushes stack. Resonance can make a strong bridge behave like a loose instrument.

So tomorrow’s claim cannot stop at “we used strong steel.” The claim has to trace the load path, name the critical joints, show the supports, and explain what happens under vibration. Strong compared with which load, through which geometry, across which connection, at what frequency?
