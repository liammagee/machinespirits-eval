STAGE: [A worksheet, a ruler, and three blank label stickers sit beside a correctly enlarged square on grid paper.]

LEARNER: [sets pencil across the side scale and perimeter scale blanks]

"I can put 2 in both of these, but then it looks like I missed the point of having two labels. Area scale is 4 because it counts squares, right?"

TUTOR: [slides the pencil so it covers the words “side scale” and “perimeter scale,” leaving only the two blanks visible]

“Now the two 2s look identical. That is the problem.

Make the claim out loud: does this 2 compare one edge, or does it compare the whole border total?

For side scale: one length, say 3 to 6, is times 2.

For perimeter scale: the total border, say 12 to 24, is times 2.

Same number. Different measurement. The labels keep the units honest.

Area scale is 4 because the square count doubles across and doubles up: 2 times 2.”

LEARNER: “So both blanks can say 2, but the first 2 is one edge, like 3 to 6, and the second 2 is the whole border, like 12 to 24. I still do not like that they look identical in the blanks, but the label is what keeps them separate. Area is 4 because it doubles across and up.”

TUTOR: [sets two identical 2s in the blanks, writes “one side length” under the first and “whole perimeter total” under the second, then covers the labels with the pencil]

“Keep the complaint. The bare 2 is not enough.

With the labels covered, say which 2 compares 3 to 6 and which 2 compares 12 to 24.

[uncovers the labels]

Now the worksheet has to carry that difference: side scale compares one side length. Perimeter scale compares the whole border total. Area scale is 4 because the square count doubles across and doubles up.”

STAGE: [The ruler is laid along one side, then across the full outline; the same blank sticker is moved from the side to the perimeter box without being peeled.]

LEARNER: "If the ruler is on one side, that sticker means 3 to 6, so side scale is 2. When the ruler goes around the whole outline, the same sticker means 12 to 24, so perimeter scale is also 2. That is the annoying part: the sticker alone does not say which measurement it belongs to. Area is 4 because the squares double across and double up."

TUTOR: [draws a bracket over one edge and a second bracket around the whole outline, then covers the labels with the pencil]

“The bare sticker still fails.

Uncover one label at a time and make the claim before writing.

Side scale: one edge, 3 to 6, times 2.

Perimeter scale: whole outline, 12 to 24, times 2.

If the same 2 is moved under the wrong label, the measurement changes. Same number, different object measured.

Area stays 4: double across times double up.”

LEARNER: [uncovers each label in turn]

"Side scale: this 2 belongs to one side length, 3 to 6. Perimeter scale: this 2 belongs to the whole outline, 12 to 24. The blanks can match, but the bare number is not enough; area scale is 4 because the count doubles across and up."

STAGE: [A final tutor line closes the scene.]

TUTOR: [sets the worksheet beside a stacked chair and draws three empty receipt lines under the answer boxes]

“The bracket-and-label route has done its job, but it no longer settles the risk: the two 2s still look interchangeable.

New gate: each answer needs a receipt sentence before it can stand.

Write the measured object first:

Side scale: ______, ___ to ___, factor ___.
Perimeter scale: ______, ___ to ___, factor ___.
Area scale: ______, ___ to ___, factor ___.

Then cover the answer boxes. If the receipts still separate edge, border, and square count, the worksheet can leave the room.”
