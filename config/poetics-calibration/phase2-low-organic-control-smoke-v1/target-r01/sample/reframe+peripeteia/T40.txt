STAGE: [A worksheet, a ruler, and three blank label stickers sit beside a correctly enlarged square on grid paper.]

LEARNER: [sets pencil across the side scale and perimeter scale blanks]

"I can put 2 in both of these, but then it looks like I missed the point of having two labels. Area scale is 4 because it counts squares, right?"

TUTOR: [slides the pencil so it covers the words “side scale” and “perimeter scale,” leaving only the two blanks visible]

“Now the two 2s look identical. That is the problem.

Make the claim out loud: does this 2 compare one edge, or does it compare the whole border total?

For side scale: one length, say 3 to 6, is times 2.

For perimeter scale: the total border, say 12 to 24, is times 2.

Same number. Different measurement. The labels keep the units honest.

Area scale is 4 because the square count doubles across and doubles up: 2 times 2.”

LEARNER: “So both blanks can say 2, but the first 2 is one edge, like 3 to 6, and the second 2 is the whole border, like 12 to 24. I still do not like that they look identical in the blanks, but the label is what keeps them separate. Area is 4 because it doubles across and up.”

TUTOR: [sets two identical 2s in the blanks, writes “one side length” under the first and “whole perimeter total” under the second, then covers the labels with the pencil]

“Keep the complaint. The bare 2 is not enough.

With the labels covered, say which 2 compares 3 to 6 and which 2 compares 12 to 24.

[uncovers the labels]

Now the worksheet has to carry that difference: side scale compares one side length. Perimeter scale compares the whole border total. Area scale is 4 because the square count doubles across and doubles up.”

STAGE: [The ruler is laid along one side, then across the full outline; the same blank sticker is moved from the side to the perimeter box without being peeled.]
An earlier learner line returns to the table: "[sets pencil across the side scale and perimeter scale blanks] I can put 2 in both of these, but then it looks like I missed the point of having two labels." The pause holds on a three-slot reframe card: earlier wording / what that old frame hid / replacement frame. The learner fills all three slots in public speech, without needing to sound certain.]

LEARNER: [slides the ruler from the single side to the full outline, then stops on the two 2s]

“My old line was, ‘I can put 2 in both blanks, so why have two labels.’ That made it too simple, because it hid what the 2 was measuring: one side length, 3 to 6, or the whole perimeter total, 12 to 24. The working rule is: the label has to say what the 2 is measuring, even if both blanks still look annoyingly the same; area scale is 4 because the square count doubles across and doubles up.”

TUTOR: [tears three narrow strips from scrap paper, places one under each blank, then covers the drawing so only the row remains]

“The cover-and-uncover move is done. It proved the bare 2s are ambiguous; it does not prove the worksheet can explain itself without the drawing.

Now make the row pass a stranger test. Write one short tag under each number. The first tag must name the single side length, the second must name the whole perimeter total, and the third must show that area is 4 because it doubles across and up. Then read the row with the drawing still covered.”

LEARNER: [writes under the three blanks: “one side: 3 to 6,” “perimeter total: 12 to 24,” “area: 2 across x 2 up.”]

“My old wording was, ‘why have two labels if both blanks say 2,’ and that hid what the 2 was measuring. With the drawing covered, the tags have to carry it: side length, perimeter total, and area count. I still do not like the two identical 2s, but this row is less sloppy.”

STAGE: [A final tutor line closes the scene.]

TUTOR: [swaps the two identical 2s, then sets the eraser beside the row like an exit stamp]

“The covered row exposed the ambiguity; it does not yet prove the row survives a careless swap. Run the exit gate: read it as a stranger’s worksheet. If the tags still force one side, 3 to 6; perimeter total, 12 to 24; and area scale, 2 across times 2 up equals 4, stamp it done. If not, fix the tag, not the number.”
