STAGE: A giraffe vertebra cast rests between two stools while footsteps and distant exhibit announcements drift in from the gallery.

TUTOR: That stretching story is appealing: a giraffe reaches higher, its neck lengthens, and the offspring inherit the stretch. But the inheritance panel points to a different step.

A neck stretched during one giraffe’s lifetime is not the same as a neck-length difference passed to offspring. Look at these casts as a herd: if two young giraffes are already born with slightly different neck lengths, which difference could show up again in their offspring, the born difference or the stretch from one afternoon of reaching?

Then selection enters. If longer-necked giraffes reach more food in hard times and leave more offspring, inherited neck-length differences can become more common over many generations.

So the change is not “stretch, then pass it on.” It is inherited variation already in the population, with selection shifting which variations become common.
