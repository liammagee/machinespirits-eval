STAGE: [The learner doubles the side of a marked square on the grid, then sets the marker down as if the proof is finished.]

LEARNER: "I doubled the side with the ruler, so I was calling that double the square, but the grid is turning it into four copies. [draws the marker lines through the larger square] Is that just a drawing trick, or is area actually doubling both ways at once?"

TUTOR: [sets the ruler along the original side, then turns it ninety degrees up the larger square]

"Old side: one length. New side: two lengths across. Turn the ruler. Two lengths up.

Now use the marker lines. How many old squares are inside the new one?

[waits]

So the ruler proves one claim: the side doubled. The grid tests the other claim: area. If area means the space covered, can “double the square” still mean double the area here, or does the drawing force a different claim?"

LEARNER: [keeps the ruler on the doubled side, then taps the four marked boxes]

"The side is doubled, yes. But if these are old squares, there are four of them, not two. So “double the square” only works for the side, not the area, which is annoying because that was the evidence I was using."

STAGE: [The tutor slides the ruler across the larger square, drawing one vertical and one horizontal line through the old square's far edges; the large square is now visibly split into four equal smaller squares.]

TUTOR: [marks the four boxes 1, 2, 3, 4.]

“Then the evidence has turned against that claim. It is not a drawing trick.

Say the two claims separately.

Side: doubled.

Area: count it.

One, two, three, four old squares. Two across and two up does not leave two boxes. It makes four.

So this drawing proves double side length. It also proves four times the area. If the target is two old squares of area, which part of the construction cannot stay as it is?”

STAGE: [An earlier learner line returns to the table: "I doubled the side with the ruler, so I was calling that double the square, but the grid is turning it into four copies." The pause holds on a three-slot reframe card: earlier wording / what that old frame hid / replacement frame. The learner fills all three slots in public speech, without needing to sound certain.]

LEARNER: [keeps the ruler on the long side, then moves it away from the four numbered boxes.]

"I doubled the side with the ruler, so I was calling that double the square. That made it too simple, because it hid the second direction: two across and two up is already four old squares, not two. So the replacement is not “double the square”; it is “double the side, then count the area,” and that means the doubled side is the part that cannot stay if the target is two squares."

TUTOR: [slides the marker across boxes 3 and 4, leaving only boxes 1 and 2 visible.]

"Use that replacement.

Side claim: not doubled.

Area claim: count it. This is two old squares.

Shape claim: fails. It is two across and one up, not a square.

So two boxes are enough for area and not enough for the problem. The next construction has to satisfy both at once: square shape, two old squares of area.

What line is worth testing as the side of that square?"

LEARNER: [sets the ruler corner to corner across one old square]

"The two-box strip gets area right, but it is not a square, so my doubled-side line fails. Calling it “double the square” hid the second direction and made four boxes. The replacement test is: keep two old squares of area and still make a square, so the line worth trying is the diagonal of one old square."

STAGE: [A final tutor line closes the scene.]

TUTOR: [draws the matching diagonals through the four old boxes, making a tilted square inside them.]

"Do not accept the diagonal by name.

Count the cut pieces first. Four halves outside the tilted shape. What area is left inside?

Then check the shape: same diagonal on every side, and each corner made by crossing grid directions evenly. If both checks hold, the doubled side is out. The diagonal is the side."
