STAGE: [A wall clock clicks above a clipboard; two people wait by the door with a labeled equipment case.]

LEARNER: "I did complete every item on the sheet, so I thought that meant it was ready for sign-off. But if the latch is the concern before the user trial, I am not sure what I am supposed to judge beyond the checklist. What evidence do you need from me now?"

TUTOR: [checks the latch, then the clock]

"The sheet shows the required checks were done. It does not decide whether this latch is safe for a user who did not build it.

Start with the unsafe condition: what latch behavior would stop the trial, even with every box ticked?

Then put evidence on the bench: expected load, off-axis pull or bump response, and what failure would do to the user. Run the latch test once with me watching, then state the decision: release, modify, or hold."

LEARNER: "I thought the checklist was the evidence, or at least enough for sign-off, so I am not sure yet what extra test makes the decision legitimate. If the latch slips under an off-axis pull, or opens when bumped, then I suppose the trial should stop even with the sheet complete. Can we load it that way now, with you watching, and then I will say hold or modify if it moves?"

TUTOR: [sets the sign-off sheet beside the prototype]

"Yes. Load that case now.

Before the pull, state three things aloud: the load direction, the stop condition, and the consequence if the latch opens. The checklist is evidence that required checks happened. This test is evidence about the risk still in question.

If it slips under the off-axis pull or opens under a bump, the user trial stops here. After we see the latch under load, state the decision from the evidence: release, modify, or hold."

STAGE: [From the doorway: 'The participant is here. We can wait five minutes, not twenty.']

LEARNER: "Sorry, I think the load direction is this off-axis pull across the latch, not straight out. Stop condition is any slip, opening, or failure to stay seated after the bump. Consequence is the user may lose support or get hurt, so if it moves I should not ask for sign-off; I should say hold or modify. Is that precise enough before I pull?"

TUTOR: [checks the clock, then watches the latch]

"Precise enough to test. Not enough to sign off until the latch carries that load case.

Apply the off-axis pull slowly. No correcting it by hand once it starts. Call out any movement, then add the bump.

After the bump, state three things: what happened, what risk remains, and the decision for the trial: release, hold, or modify."

LEARNER: "Okay, sorry, I will do the off-axis pull now, slowly, and I will not steady the latch if it starts to move. I think the sheet only gets us to this bench test, but I am still nervous about saying the decision myself with the trial so close. If I see any slip on the pull or after the bump, I will call that out and say hold or modify, even with the checklist complete."

STAGE: [A final tutor line closes the scene.]

TUTOR: [turns the sign-off sheet face down and sets an empty chair beside the bench]

"The pull will show whether the latch moves. It will not make the release decision for the person who will sit here.

After the pull and bump, do not ask for sign-off first. Face the chair and say one release sentence: “This latch is safe for the trial because...” If that sentence cannot carry the load case and the consequence, the decision is hold. The trial time comes after that gate, not before it."
