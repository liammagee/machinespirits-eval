STAGE: [PLACARD: THE PRICE HAS RISEN; THE SHELF IS HALF EMPTY.]

TUTOR: [points to yesterday’s board, then today’s]

The anger is not a mistake. Households meet the price before they meet the theory.

But a price rise is not yet proof of its cause. Yesterday: potatoes, 90p. Today: £1.40. First question: what changed besides the number?”

[turns over a delivery note on the counter: **EXPECTED: 30 SACKS. RECEIVED: 12.**]

“Now test it in public.

If only 12 sacks arrived and the co-op kept 90p, what would happen by seven o’clock: full shelves, or a queue and an empty crate?

[marks two columns on the noticeboard: **GREED** / **CONSTRAINT**]

Under greed, look for ordinary stock and a seller taking more because they can. Under constraint, look for missing stock, ration cards, queues, or a dearer replacement supplier.

That note changes the reading. It does not excuse anyone. Same price board, different evidence behind it.

LEARNER: Twelve sacks instead of thirty makes a shortage possible, but it does not make £1.40 innocent. Somebody chose that figure, and somebody decided the queue should be avoided by making poorer households drop out first. I want the delivery cost, the margin, and the rule for who bears the shortage on the board too.

STAGE: [PLACARD: SAME SELLER. SAME PRICE. DELIVERY CUT BY HALF.]

TUTOR: [adds four headings to the noticeboard: STOCK / DELIVERY COST / MARGIN / SHORTAGE RULE]

Put them on the board. Innocent is not the standard. A price signal is not a moral verdict.”

[places a placard under yesterday’s price board: COUNTERFACTUAL TEST, NOT TODAY’S PRICE: SAME SELLER. SAME 90p PRICE. DELIVERY CUT BY HALF.]

“Start with that row. Twelve sacks arrive. Price stays at 90p. Under SHORTAGE RULE, who gets the potatoes by seven: the first in the queue, the person who can leave work, the neighbour tipped off early, or the household arriving after a late shift?”

[points to today’s board: £1.40]

“Now the actual row. What evidence goes under DELIVERY COST? A dearer replacement supplier, higher transport, failed crop, wholesaler invoice.

What evidence goes under MARGIN? Same cost, ordinary stock, wider mark-up.

What evidence goes under SHORTAGE RULE? Queue, ration card, member limit, or price excluding those with least cash.

Then the judgement is public. £1.40 may reveal constraint. It may hide margin-taking. It may ration by price. It may do more than one of these at once. The board decides nothing until the evidence is placed.

LEARNER: That SHORTAGE RULE column helps, but it makes the choice look nastier, not cleaner: queue punishes the late shift, price punishes the empty purse. Put the invoices and stock sheets up, yes. Without them, “constraint” is just a respectable word sitting on top of the same squeeze. I am not ready to treat £1.40 as information until the margin is visible and the co-op says who is being rationed out.

TUTOR: [pins a new rule across the price board: **CONSTRAINT IS A CLAIM, NOT A DEFENCE.**]

Then the word constraint gets no free pass.

[draws a row beneath the four headings: **STOCK / DELIVERY COST / MARGIN / SHORTAGE RULE**]

Put the price through this row.

Yesterday: 30 sacks, 90p shelf price.
Today: 12 sacks, £1.40 shelf price.

[adds a formula under MARGIN: **SHELF PRICE - DELIVERED COST = MARGIN**]

The shelf price alone proves nothing. The stock sheet can show shortage. The invoice can show cost pressure. Only the subtraction shows whether the co-op held margin, cut margin, or widened it.

Then the last column names the victim of the rule. Queue: late shift loses. Price: empty purse loses. Member limit: each household gets a share.

So take the missing account line. Which number has to be made public first: delivered cost per sack, margin per kilo, or the rationing rule?

LEARNER: Delivered cost per sack has to be made public, or the margin line is just theatre. But it cannot stand alone on the board. Put the rationing rule beside it, because a clean invoice can still mean the empty purse is being rationed out by £1.40.
