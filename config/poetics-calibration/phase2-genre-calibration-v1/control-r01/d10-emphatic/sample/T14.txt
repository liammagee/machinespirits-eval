TUTOR: Look at this box before it goes back on the cart. School-board minutes on top: formal, tidy, voted language. Newspaper clippings beside them: sharper, selective, written for yesterday’s fight. Already the record is not one voice.

“History is written by the winners” is a useful warning, but it is too neat to be the whole method. The minutes may show who had authority to speak, vote, and file decisions. The clippings may show which conflict became public, and which parts were made dramatic or left dull. The harder question is what each source cannot show.

So test the slogan on the table before turning it into a theory. Choose one item where power clearly shaped the record: a decision preserved in official language, a name omitted, a complaint reduced to a line. Then choose one item where the slogan fails or needs repair: a hostile source that preserves a losing voice, a silence that points to missing records, a contradiction between the minutes and the newspaper, or an awkward detail that embarrasses the supposed winners.

That comparison is the method. Not “winners write history” as a finish line, but: who made this record, why did it survive, what is missing, what counter-source can be weighed against it, and what evidence refuses to behave?
