STAGE: A squared sheet, a ruler, and two empty display pins sit under a narrow desk lamp while the corridor clock clicks too loudly.

TUTOR: Leave the claim there. Mark the doubled square: two grid units across, two up. Count the 1-by-1 squares inside it.

How many?

LEARNER: Four. Two along the bottom, then two directly above them. So the enlarged square is four small squares, not two. The earlier claim is sitting there looking rather wrong now.

TUTOR: Leave the claim visible; it is useful evidence now.

[The ruler rests on the two-unit edge.]

Which doubled here: this edge, or the count of unit squares inside?

STAGE: A corridor bell rings and someone outside asks whether the board is ready; the learner must answer under time pressure with a short, imperfect claim rather than a polished explanation.

LEARNER: The edge doubled. Not the count. The grid has four unit squares: two across, two up. So the old claim is wrong as written.

TUTOR: [The ruler stays on the doubled edge. The four unit squares remain visible.]

Two across, two up: four unit squares.

What should the corrected note say?

LEARNER: The corrected note should say: doubled side, four times the area. The side is two grid units, but the square contains four unit squares. So the earlier note is wrong because it counted the side as if that settled the area.
