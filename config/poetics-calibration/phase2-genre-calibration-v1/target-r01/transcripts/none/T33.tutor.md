FEEDBACK: The draft is pedagogically strong in its main split: supply can explain a price rise without morally acquitting the seller. The placard is sharp, scene-grounded, and directly meets the learner’s concern that “supply slip” could become an alibi. The risk is that it becomes too declarative too quickly. The learner has already made a sophisticated distinction and is asking to be shown the chain; the draft mostly supplies the chain rather than making the learner test it. Add one or two generative questions around the blank tag: what number would merely cover changed costs, and what evidence would show padding? That would turn the learner’s objection into active reasoning rather than a well-framed answer received from the tutor.

Emotionally, the ending “Keep that objection” works because it validates the learner’s moral pressure without soothing or flattening it. But “There. That is the clean split” may be a little too settled for a learner who is deliberately resisting a clean alibi. Better to preserve the split while letting it remain investigatory: clean enough to inspect, not clean enough to conclude.

ZPD fit is mostly appropriate: the learner is ready for margin, cost chain, and market-clearing logic. The list of blanks is useful scaffolding, but it could overload if all variables arrive as a mini-accounting worksheet. Keep the blanks, but make the learner handle only the next decisive contrast: cost-covered rise versus padded margin.

KEEP_OR_CHANGE: revise lightly. Keep the placard, the supply-versus-markup distinction, the delivery slip/new tag staging, and the final reframed question. Change the middle so it asks the learner to infer or test one part of the chain, rather than giving the whole mechanism as lecture.
