It begins from the completed object, does not congratulate the tick-box pass, and forces separation between procedural verification and patient-specific judgment. The allergy-band prompt is useful because it makes the learner name the evidentiary limit of a checklist item rather than merely accept completion as safety.

Emotionally, the clipped pressure fits the scene and stakes. It respects the learner by treating them as clinically accountable, not fragile. Socratically, it asks rather than tells, though it could better scaffold the middle move: from “what the checklist proves” to “what new patient cue overrides form completion.”

KEEP_OR_CHANGE: revise lightly. Keep the object-first opening, the item-specific proof question, the “what it does not prove” contrast, and the thirty-second changed-cue pressure test. Change by adding one small intermediate demand for the learner to name the relevant clinical variable before deciding, so the scene tests judgment rather than only form skepticism.
