STAGE: A hand-lettered placard marked "TOMATOES: $2.40 -> $3.10" is clipped to an empty crate while two chairs face the audience at an angle.

TUTOR: [The tutor taps the empty tomato crate, then turns the workshop placard around.]

“PUBLIC PRICE RULE: Ask what changed before naming the motive.”

Yesterday: twelve crates. Tomorrow: four. Same shoppers, fewer tomatoes.

[Pause. The old shelf label stays in one hand.]

At the old price, what happens first? The early line clears the shelf. The late line gets the apology sign.

[The label printer clicks. A new tag slides out.]

That new number does not settle the moral case. It does not prove innocence. It does not prove greed by itself.

First question: did supply shrink?

Second question: did anyone exploit the shrinkage?

Crop failure, fuel costs, or a smaller delivery can push the market-clearing price up before any speech about character is earned. So read the slip first: what changed between twelve crates and four?

LEARNER: "My first reaction is still: that new tag looks like greed in ink. But the empty crate is bothering that reaction, because if twelve crates turned into four, the old label really does just hand tomatoes to whoever gets here first. I don't want the supply slip used as a clean alibi, though. Show me what changed, then show me why this number and not some padded number."

TUTOR: [The tutor flips a placard onto the folding table.]

“SUPPLY CAN EXPLAIN THE RISE. IT DOES NOT ACQUIT THE MARKUP.”

Clean enough to inspect. Not clean enough to conclude.

[The delivery slip is placed beside the new tag.]

First column: what changed.

Twelve crates became four. Crop loss, fuel, supplier price, smaller delivery. Any of those can make the old label fail. At the old price, the first line empties the shelf. The late line gets the apology sign.

[The tutor taps the new tag once.]

Second column: why this number.

Not “because supply fell.” Too loose.

[The label printer spits out a blank tag.]

Two possible tags go here.

One tag covers changed conditions:
“Wholesale cost rose from ___ to ___.”
“Delivery added ___.”
“Margin stayed at ___.”

The other tag uses the shortage as cover:
“Costs rose some.”
“Price rose more.”
“Margin quietly widened.”

So test it.

What number would cover the changed costs without padding the margin?

And what receipt, invoice, or margin line would prove the new tag is doing more than rationing scarce tomatoes?

[The blank tag stays between the slip and the shelf label.]

The fair question is not “price rise or greed?”

It is:

“What part of the rise pays for the changed conditions, and what part is extra?”

Keep that objection on the table. It is evidence work now.

A prior learner line is played back: "My first reaction is still: that new tag looks like greed in ink. But the empty crate is bothering that reaction, because if twelve crates turned into four, the old label..." The learner must revoice that wording first, name the earlier framing problem, then replace it with a new framing that changes how the earlier line reads before moving on.

LEARNER: "My first reaction is still: that new tag looks like greed in ink, but if twelve crates turned into four, the old label just starts a race." Okay, that docket under the door messes with my frame: I was treating the tag like a confession before reading “Storm loss” and “fuel surcharge.” So maybe this is a split tag, part storm-and-truck math, part maybe-we-can-get-away-with-more. I think I get it, but I still want the margin line before blame leaves the room.

TUTOR: [The facilitator pins one placard above the folding table.]

“NOT A CONFESSION. NOT AN ALIBI. A CLAIM TO AUDIT.”

The new tag no longer gets to stand alone and shout “greed.”

The storm docket does not get to walk in wearing a halo either.

[Three blank shelf labels are laid in a row.]

First label: **cost**.
Old crate cost. New crate cost. Fuel. Spoilage.

Second label: **scarcity**.
Four crates cannot do the work of twelve. The old price turns shopping into a race.

Third label: **margin**.
This is where blame waits with its coat still on.

[The marker is set beside the third label.]

Audit question:

If the co-op paid this much more for tomatoes and transport, what shelf price keeps the old margin?

Then compare that number to the new tag.

If they match, the rise is mostly storm-and-truck math.

If the tag climbs past that line, the extra has a name.

Not proof by glare.
Proof by invoice.

LEARNER: "That new tag looks like greed in ink" — that is still where my hand goes first, but maybe that is me treating the shelf label like a verdict before the invoice hits the table. Under these lights, with four crates where twelve were supposed to be, I can see how the old price makes a race instead of fairness. But I do not want the storm docket wearing a halo; show me the old margin number, then the new tag can answer for whatever climbs past it.

STAGE: closes the scene.
