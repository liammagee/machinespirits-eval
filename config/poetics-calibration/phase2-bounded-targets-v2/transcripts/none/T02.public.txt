STAGE: Chairs scrape into stacks at the far end of the cafe while a brass automaton lifts its hand behind glass and the tutor sets a marked seminar sheet between two untouched cups.

TUTOR: The clockwork case makes the objection look almost irresistible: if a movement is fully determined, it seems less like a choice than a mechanism completing its sequence. Yet one step needs testing before tomorrow’s seminar: “caused” does not yet mean “unfree.” A hand raised under threat, a hand raised by neurological spasm, and a hand raised after judging that a claim is false all have causes. If determinism covers all three, does it erase every difference among them, or is the reason-guided case free in a sense the other two are not?

LEARNER: That automaton in the case still seems to be doing the damage: if the judgment arrives by clockwork too, calling it “my reasons” sounds like giving the mechanism a better story. Threat and spasm are uglier causes, yes, but why should a tidy chain of reasons suddenly count as freedom? Something about that distinction is interesting, but it feels too quick.

TUTOR: The automaton behind the glass is useful precisely because it sharpens that suspicion. A compatibilist should not answer it by saying, “reasons are causes, therefore freedom survives.” That is too quick.

The more careful move is to separate two claims. One says: with the whole past and the laws fixed, a different act was still possible. The other says: the act issued through a capacity to notice reasons, weigh them, and alter course when the reasons alter. A spasm bypasses that capacity. A threat can distort it. A judgment may exercise it, even if its arrival has a causal history.

But that last step is exactly where the dispute sits. Suppose the seminar claim looks false, so the hand rises; then a decisive correction appears, and the hand stays down. The compatibilist calls that difference reason-responsive control. The critic replies that a sufficiently refined automaton could be built to respond in just that way.

So the clockwork has not disappeared. The test is sharper now: if a determined agent can still be guided by reasons in a way a spasm is not, is that enough for freedom, or does freedom require the stronger power to do otherwise with everything prior unchanged?

STAGE: A cafe attendant announces that the museum will close in ten minutes; the learner must answer with a sharp objection or example, not a long reflection.

LEARNER: Oh, then the automaton behind the glass already counts as free if it has a clever enough reasons-system, does it not? It hears the correction, keeps the hand down, and suddenly clockwork becomes control. With ten minutes left, that still sounds like a refined mechanism wearing the name of freedom.

TUTOR: The little figure behind the glass makes the difficulty sharper. If it can register a correction, weigh it against its present course, and change accordingly, then calling it mere clockwork no longer settles the matter. The critic can still say: that is only a more intricate mechanism.

The compatibilist’s reply is narrower than “reasons save freedom.” It is that determinism alone does not erase the difference between an action guided through deliberation and one produced by spasm, coercion, or bypassing manipulation. Whether the automaton has anything like that deliberative control remains the contested step.

For tomorrow, keep the challenge precise: does freedom require a different act with the entire past and laws unchanged, or can it consist in action issuing through reasons-responsive control even in a determined world?

LEARNER: That little figure is precisely the trouble: give the clockwork enough gears to notice corrections, and it starts being called “control.” Oh, I see the compatibilist distinction now, but it still feels as though “reasons-responsive” may be mechanism with a more flattering label.
