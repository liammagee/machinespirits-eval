
## <a id="main-lecture"></a>Giving and Receiving Feedback

---

### The Workshop Is a Practice, Not a Verdict

Workshop is the part of creative writing education where a draft is read by peers, who then discuss it while the author listens. Done well, it is the single most valuable hour of the week.

Done badly — as judgement, as taste-war, as politeness — it teaches nothing and hurts people.

The difference is entirely about how you frame the conversation. Two principles:

1. The workshop discusses what is on the page, not who the author is.
2. The workshop describes reader experience, not verdicts.

```notes
Workshop culture varies from program to program. Some programs are famously brutal; some are famously soft. Neither is a good model.

Brutal workshops produce writers who either harden into defensiveness or quit. Soft workshops produce writers who receive only validation and never learn which sentences aren't landing. Both fail the same craft.

A useful workshop is specific, curious, and kind — and all three at once. "I felt lost on page two" is specific. "I wonder if you've thought about starting later" is curious. "There are some beautiful sentences here I'd like to see earn more weight" is kind.

The worst workshop response is "I loved it." The second-worst is "I didn't really get it." Both give the author nothing to work with.
```

---

### Giving Feedback: Describe Your Reading Experience

The most useful feedback describes your experience as a reader, not your verdict on the writing.

**Less useful**: "The dialogue doesn't work."

**More useful**: "On page three, when Jen answers with 'I've thought about that,' I wasn't sure if she was being honest or deflecting. I wanted to know which."

The more useful version:
- Locates the moment precisely (page three, specific line)
- Describes a reader reaction (uncertainty about Jen's honesty)
- Implies the revision question (does the author want this ambiguity, or is it a craft problem?)

The author can now choose: maybe the ambiguity is intended (great, leave it); maybe it wasn't (now they know what to clarify).

```notes
The shift from "verdicts" to "reader reactions" is the single most important workshop skill. It requires discipline. The impulse is to say "this isn't working" — which is a judgement the author can only agree or disagree with. Neither option teaches anything.

A reader reaction ("I was confused here") is not disputable. The author cannot say "no you weren't." They can only investigate why.

A useful phrase set:
- "I was confused by..."
- "I wanted more of..."
- "I was pulled out of the story when..."
- "I noticed this moment worked because..."
- "I wondered if..."

Notice that all five begin with "I" and describe an experience, not a judgement. That is the move.
```

---

### Giving Feedback: Praise Has to Be Specific Too

Unhelpful: "I loved this." (The author can't rebuild it.)

Helpful: "The paragraph on page four where the grandfather explains the radio — I wanted that voice to come back later. The specificity of 'she could only afford the bottom kind' told me more about the family than anything else in the story."

Specific praise is as valuable as specific critique. It tells the author what IS working, so they can protect it and do more of it.

A rule of thumb: **your praise should be as precise as your critique**. If you can pinpoint exactly what didn't work, pinpoint exactly what did.

```notes
Students often give specific critique and vague praise. "The plot is slow in the middle; also, I really liked it overall." The imbalance teaches the author that their failures have coordinates but their successes are a fog.

Inverted: specific praise, vague critique. "The voice is beautiful; the plot isn't quite there." Also useless. The author now has nothing actionable.

The discipline: for every piece of critique you offer, offer one piece of praise at the same level of specificity. It is a real constraint. Sometimes it is hard to find specific praise in a weak draft; that is fine, you can work at it.

This also helps the author trust the critique. If you noticed what worked, the author believes that you read carefully. Then, when you say "the middle section lost me," they give it weight.
```

### Receiving Feedback: The Silence Rule

In most workshops, while peers discuss your draft, **you don't speak**. Not to defend, not to explain, not to agree, not to thank.

This is hard. The instinct is to jump in: "oh that's because in the NEXT draft, I was going to..." Or: "the reason that character seems distant is that the novel opens with..."

Both are defense. Both short-circuit what the workshop could tell you.

If the reader experienced confusion, your explanation does not fix their confusion — it proves the draft didn't carry the information. The draft has to work without your commentary. Hearing the workshop without speaking forces you to absorb that.

```notes
The silence rule is the hardest part of workshop for beginning writers. They will say "but what if someone misunderstands?" The answer: that misunderstanding is data. A reader experienced the misunderstanding. You cannot fix it by explaining — you can only fix it by revising the draft so the reader doesn't misunderstand next time.

Some programs allow the author to ask questions at the end of the discussion. That is usually enough outlet. Questions, not explanations — "can you say more about where you felt confused" is fine; "but I meant for it to be ambiguous" is not.

Writers who cannot stay silent during workshop tend to get less useful feedback. Peers learn to be polite because the author will argue. The silence rule enforces a conversation the author can actually learn from.
```

---

### Receiving Feedback: Take Notes, Not Verdicts

During workshop, write down what people say. Verbatim if you can. Do not try to evaluate yet — just capture.

Only LATER, alone, do you decide what to do with the feedback. That includes: decide what to keep. You are not obligated to act on every note. But you are obligated to hear all of them first.

A useful pattern, 24-48 hours after workshop:

1. Read your notes with no attachment.
2. Group them: structural / scene-level / sentence-level / global / conflicting.
3. Identify patterns — if three readers said "lost at page 4," that's a structural issue. If one reader disliked a sentence everyone else loved, probably ignorable.
4. Make a list of changes to attempt in the next draft.

Do NOT revise the same day as workshop. You will overcorrect from the most recent critique. Let the notes cool.

```notes
Overcorrection is real. A common pattern: a workshop reader says "the character feels distant." The writer goes home and writes a new scene adding emotional closeness — which undoes what the draft was actually doing (a chosen aesthetic of distance). They've "fixed" the note by destroying what worked.

The 24-hour rule prevents this. Across a day, the emotional charge of the workshop fades. What remains is the substance. Writers who revise same-day are often revising the feeling, not the feedback.

Some notes are bad. Some notes conflict. Some notes come from readers who didn't get the genre or the tone. Part of craft is deciding, over time, which kind of reader you're writing for — which means some notes become "not for this draft" decisions. That's fine. You are not obligated to obey. But you are obligated to hear.
```

---

### Conflicting Feedback

Two readers will often give opposite notes. "The opening is too slow" and "I needed more setup before the story started." How do you decide?

Options:
- **Ignore both.** If the conflict is total, each reader wants something different; the draft may already be reasonable and further changes will just replace one objection with the other.
- **Synthesise.** Sometimes the opening needs to be faster AND contain more orienting detail — both notes are pointing to the same structural weakness from opposite sides.
- **Trust one over the other.** If one reader consistently reads your work well and the other consistently misreads, weight accordingly.
- **Ask other readers.** Conflict is a tiebreak invitation.

What you do NOT do: change the draft to please both readers by toggling between two positions. That produces inconsistent drafts that no reader likes.

```notes
Beginners try to please everyone. The draft gets pulled in opposite directions, and the voice goes muddy.

A fairer framing: the workshop gives you data, not orders. You are the author. You decide. But you decide AFTER hearing, not before.

A useful internal check: after sitting with the notes, ask yourself — if I rewrite to take note X, will the story be closer to what I was trying to do? If yes, take the note. If no, leave it.

This assumes you know what you were trying to do, which is a skill unto itself. Early drafts often don't know. That is fine — workshop can help you find out.
```

### Let's Practice!

**Question 1**: Rewrite each piece of feedback to be more useful.

a. "I didn't like this."
b. "The dialogue felt off."
c. "Great job!"
d. "The ending doesn't work."
e. "I got confused."

**Question 2**: You're the author. During workshop, a reader says: "I don't understand why the narrator leaves at the end. It feels unmotivated."

You're about to say: "She leaves because earlier, on page two, her mother—"

Stop. What should you actually do in this moment? And what should you do after workshop, alone with the note?

**Question 3**: You receive two pieces of feedback on the same story:

- Reader A: "The pacing is too fast in the middle; I needed more time with the relationship."
- Reader B: "The middle drags; you could cut half of the café scene."

How do you interpret this conflict?

```notes
Question 1:
a. "The opening three pages didn't pull me in — I found myself skimming by page two. I think it was that I didn't know whose story it was yet."
b. "The conversation on page five between Ana and her brother — the back-and-forth felt too even, like both characters were equally articulate. I expected more asymmetry, given that one of them is trying to hide something."
c. "The final paragraph where the narrator walks past her own coat — that landed. The silence after a scene of loud emotion felt earned. I want more moments like that in the earlier draft too."
d. "I read the ending twice. The first time I wanted more closure; the second time I saw the restraint. I'm genuinely unsure which version the author wants me to land on."
e. "I got confused at the top of page three — I wasn't sure if the flashback started at 'She remembered' or three sentences earlier at the line break. The transition didn't announce itself."

The pattern: specific location, specific reader experience, open question back to the author. Students who struggle on this tend to give feedback that is still a verdict, just softened ("It wasn't really for me" is still a verdict). Push them until they name what happened in their reading.

Question 2: what you should do IN THE MOMENT — nothing. Silence rule. Write down the note verbatim. Resist explaining.

What you should do AFTER — consider the note honestly. The reader said "unmotivated," which could mean: (a) the motivation is on the page but not legible; (b) the motivation isn't on the page at all; (c) the motivation is there but needs more emphasis.

You thought "well, on page two her mother—" which means YOU think the motivation is on page two. The reader didn't experience it. The question is whether page two carries the weight it needs to. Maybe the scene that motivates the ending is too brief, too buried, or written at a remove that reader's don't feel. That is the revision work.

If, after review, you conclude the setup is enough and one reader just missed it, fine — leave it. But get one or two more readers before concluding. A single missed beat can be the reader; a pattern across readers is the draft.

Question 3: the conflict is not as deep as it looks. Both readers are flagging the middle as a structural problem — they just have opposite hypotheses about the fix. Reader A wants more density (we need to feel the relationship); Reader B wants less bloat (the café scene is wasted minutes).

The most likely synthesis: the middle is long AND not doing the relational work. The fix is not to add pages, and not to cut pages — it's to replace current pages with different pages. Rewrite the middle to earn time with the relationship. Keep the word count similar but change what the scenes are doing.

Students who say "I'll split the difference" (keep the café scene but make it shorter) are doing the worst thing — they'll produce a middle that is neither deep enough for A nor brisk enough for B. The craft answer is almost always "rewrite, don't compromise."
```

## <a id="activity"></a>Activity: Workshop a Short Piece

Here is a short piece of student work. Draft workshop feedback for it — both praise and critique, both specific.

```
PIECE: "The Dry Season" (approx. 150 words)

When the reservoir went down to 40% my father started counting
showers. He would write the date on the mirror with a dry erase
marker and the names of everyone in the family and a tick for
each shower. I was fourteen. I hated the ticks. The marker bled
when the bathroom steamed. I took two showers every time I showered
to protest but my father never caught on, or if he did he never said.
In September the reservoir went below 30% and the ticks started
feeling like more than a tally. My father stopped talking at dinner.
My mother stood at the sink too long. I stopped the double showers.

Five years later my father is dead and we have moved. The new
apartment has a shower I barely notice. Sometimes I think about
the mirror with its ticks. I wonder what any of it was for.
```

Write:
- **Three specific praise notes** (with locations)
- **Two specific critique notes** (with reader experiences)
- **One open question for the author**

```notes
Sample praise notes (good answers resemble these in specificity):

1. "The detail of the dry-erase marker bleeding when the bathroom steamed — that sentence landed hard. The marker's failure under the thing it was tracking carries the whole metaphor without stating it."

2. "The phrase 'my mother stood at the sink too long' — the small quiet detail that does enormous work. I don't need to know what she was thinking. The duration is enough."

3. "The move from 'I hated the ticks' to 'the ticks started feeling like more than a tally' — that's a voice shift I can track. The narrator is becoming more than a 14-year-old across one paragraph, and I watch it happen."

Sample critique notes:

1. "The final line — 'I wonder what any of it was for' — told me something the previous paragraph already showed. I'd rather end on the mirror itself. The wondering is implicit."

2. "The phrase 'to protest' in 'I took two showers every time I showered to protest' — I felt the author explaining something the action already communicated. The double shower IS the protest. 'To protest' told me what was already showing."

Open question: "Did you consider ending the piece earlier — perhaps at 'I stopped the double showers'? The second paragraph ties it up in a way that might be undoing what the first paragraph earned."

If a student writes only praise, push them for critique. If only critique, push for praise. If their notes are general ("I liked the feel" / "something is off in the middle"), push for location and reader experience. The quality of a student's workshop feedback is the clearest signal of how well they're reading — better than any test on craft terms.

One more teacher move: after students draft their feedback, pair them up and have them read their notes aloud to each other. Reading feedback aloud — to the face of the author (even a simulated one) — makes students feel the weight of each sentence. Notes they would happily write on a page they wince at saying. That wince is the calibration tool.
```
