
## <a id="main-lecture"></a>Showing vs Telling

---

### Two Ways to Give the Reader Information

Any moment in a piece of writing can be delivered in one of two modes:

- **Telling** — summarising the information directly. ("She was angry.")
- **Showing** — rendering the information through scene, detail, or action. ("She set the mug down without letting go of the handle, and waited until the rattling stopped before she spoke.")

Neither is better in the abstract. They do different jobs. Telling is fast and portable; showing is slow and memorable.

```notes
New writers are often taught "show, don't tell" as a rule, and they end up writing pages of micro-detail that should have been a single summary sentence. The rule is not "always show." The rule is that emotional or high-stakes moments deserve the reader's attention, which means they deserve to be rendered, not summarised.

A useful test: ask "is this moment something the reader is supposed to feel, or something they just need to know?" If feel, show. If know, tell.

Students who swing too hard toward showing produce bloated, lyric sentences attached to nothing. Students who stay entirely in telling write competent but airless prose — we never get close to anything.
```

---

### The Difference, Concretely

Here is the same moment written both ways.

**Telling**:
> Marcus was nervous about the interview. He had wanted this job for months.

**Showing**:
> Marcus stood outside the office with his resume in one hand and his phone in the other. He checked the time for the fourth time. 8:53. He had rehearsed the sentence in the shower that morning: "I've been following your team's work for months." In the elevator he had worried the sentence sounded memorised and had tried two other versions. Now he couldn't remember any of them.

Notice what changed:
- "Nervous" became a specific behaviour (checking the time, worrying about rehearsal).
- "Wanted this job" became a sentence he rehearsed.
- We are now close to Marcus; in the telling version we were watching him from a distance.

Both versions deliver the same facts. The showing version takes longer, earns more reader investment, and costs more words. Use it when the moment deserves the investment.

```notes
A common failure mode: students write a showing paragraph and then add a telling sentence at the end that explains what the showing already did. "He checked the time for the fourth time. He was nervous." The second sentence undoes the first. The telling sentence says "in case you missed it, here is the label."

If the showing worked, the telling is redundant. If the showing didn't work, the telling won't save it. Cut the telling sentence and trust the reader.

This is sometimes called "the said-bookism problem" but it's broader than dialogue tags. It's any moment where the writer doesn't trust that the scene landed.
```

---

### When Telling Is The Right Move

Telling is not a failure mode. It is the right tool when:

1. **Moving through time quickly.** "The next three weeks were the best of her summer." You don't want three weeks of scene; you want the transition.
2. **Giving the reader necessary background.** "Her father had left when she was nine." If this isn't the subject of the story, summarise and move on.
3. **Low-stakes information.** "The car was red." A red car does not deserve a paragraph.

A story made entirely of showing drags. A story made entirely of telling feels synopsised — like you read the summary instead of the novel. Good prose alternates.

```notes
The ratio depends on the form. A 1,000-word flash story might be 80% showing — there is no room for summary, so every beat has to land in scene. A memoir essay might be 60% telling — personal essays often earn their power from the narrator's synthesis, not from rendered scenes.

Ask beginning writers to mark their drafts: underline telling sentences, circle showing sentences. If the whole page is underlined, push toward scene. If the whole page is circled, push toward compression.
```

---

### The Sentence-Level Test

You can apply the distinction at the sentence level, not just the scene level.

- "She hated the new apartment." — telling
- "She hated the new apartment's smell — some previous tenant's cat, or cleaning product, or both." — partly showing (the cat/cleaning/both specifies)
- "The apartment smelled like a cat had lived somewhere a cleaning product had been used recently." — all showing (no word about hate, but the reader infers distaste)

The third version is harder to write and usually better, because the reader has to do a tiny interpretive act, and that act is what makes the sentence memorable.

```notes
"The reader has to do a small interpretive act" is the whole principle. Telling hands the reader a finished thought. Showing hands the reader the raw material and asks them to assemble the thought.

Readers retain what they assembled. They forget what they were handed.

This is why telling-heavy prose feels forgettable even when the information was accurate. The reader wasn't asked to do anything, so nothing stuck.
```

### Let's Practice!

**Question 1**: Which mode is each sentence?

1. "The meeting ran long."
2. "At 6:15, people started sneaking looks at their phones."
3. "Dad was disappointed."
4. "Dad didn't say anything the whole drive home."
5. "She decided to quit."

**Question 2**: Rewrite this telling paragraph as showing:

> The coffee shop was crowded. Jane was frustrated because she couldn't find a seat. She was tired from a long day.

Limit yourself to three sentences.

**Question 3** (spot the redundancy):

> Omar slammed the door. The plates on the counter rattled. He was very angry.

Which sentence weakens the paragraph? Why?

```notes
Question 1:
1. Telling ("ran long" is a summary).
2. Showing (specific time, specific behaviour).
3. Telling ("disappointed" is a label).
4. Showing (silence across a drive, reader infers the emotion).
5. Telling ("decided" is internal, summarised).

If students get #2 and #4 right but #3 wrong ("Dad was disappointed" — some students argue this is "showing" because it's an observation), push on the test: did the writer hand us a label or material to interpret? "Disappointed" is the label.

Question 2: a good rewrite might be something like:
> The only empty seat at the café was next to the espresso machine, and it was taken by a backpack that no one claimed. Jane stood with her cup cooling and nobody looked up. She counted how many hours she had been awake and lost track.

Notice there is no "frustrated" or "tired" in the rewrite. The reader infers both.

Weaker rewrites keep "frustrated" or "tired" and just add a detail on top. Those are hybrid — telling with decoration — not showing.

Question 3: "He was very angry" weakens the paragraph. The slammed door and rattling plates already did the work. The third sentence adds no information and says "trust me, reader, here is the label." Cut it.

A student who says "maybe the author wants to emphasise the anger" should be pushed: emphasis in writing usually comes from what is shown, not what is told. If you want to emphasise anger, show a second detail (the plate falls to the floor; nobody in the next room speaks). Adding a label does not emphasise — it flattens.
```

## <a id="activity"></a>Activity: A Moment in Two Modes

Write the same small moment twice. Pick one of:

- Someone receiving news they were afraid of
- A first day at a new school or job
- The last conversation between two people who will not speak again
- A decision made at 3 a.m.

**Version A (telling)**: 3-5 sentences. Summarise the moment and name the feelings.

**Version B (showing)**: 5-10 sentences. Render the moment in scene — specific objects, actions, dialogue, silence. Do not name any feelings directly; let the details do the work.

Then read both back. Mark, in Version B, any sentence that labels a feeling (a "told" intrusion). Cut or rewrite it.

```notes
The most common student failure on this exercise: Version B is Version A with adjectives added. "She slowly took the letter, feeling her heart beat fast, her hands sweating nervously." That is not showing. That is telling with modifiers.

A cleaner Version B: "She opened the letter in the hallway, next to the row of coat hooks where her coat still hung. She read it twice. Then she folded it back into the envelope and walked past her coat, out of the apartment."

Notice: no feeling words. The behavior — reading twice, walking past her own coat — tells the reader what they need to know.

If a student's Version B has feeling words, ask them to replace each one with a specific action or object. This is the hardest move in short-form fiction. Worth practising until it's a reflex.

A more advanced point: the showing version doesn't have to be longer. A short showing sentence can be more powerful than a labelled telling sentence. "She did not cry until she was in the car" is seven words and does more work than "She was very sad but held it together."
```
