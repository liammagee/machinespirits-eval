
## <a id="main-lecture"></a>Voice and Point of View

---

### Two Things That Are Easy to Confuse

- **Point of view (POV)** is a structural choice: who is telling the story, and from what vantage?
- **Voice** is a texture: how do the sentences sound? What kind of mind is behind them?

Two stories written in first person (same POV) can have completely different voices. Two stories with the same voice can be written from different POVs. Don't confuse the two.

```notes
Students often use "voice" and "POV" interchangeably, and it muddles the workshop conversation. Be strict about the distinction.

POV is answerable with "who / what vantage" (first person past, third-person limited, second person, omniscient, etc.). Voice is answerable with "what kind of mind / rhythm / vocabulary is this" — and the answer is rarely a single word.

A useful diagnostic: if you changed every "I" in a first-person story to "she," the POV changes. The voice mostly doesn't.
```

---

### The Main POV Choices

**First person** (I / we): the narrator is in the story and tells it as they experienced it.
> "I didn't know what to say, so I said I was sorry, and then I said it again."

**Third person limited**: a narrator outside the story, but we only see inside ONE character's head.
> "She didn't know what to say, so she said she was sorry. Then she said it again. Marcus looked tired; she could not read him."

**Third person omniscient**: a narrator who can see into any character's head and any moment.
> "She didn't know what to say. Marcus, watching her across the table, thought she looked like someone who had rehearsed an apology and forgotten it."

**Second person** (you): rare, but striking when it fits.
> "You open the letter in the hallway. You read it twice. You don't cry until you are in the car."

Each has costs and benefits:
- First person gives maximum intimacy but constrains information to what the narrator knows or would notice.
- Third limited is the modern default — close feeling with more flexibility.
- Omniscient gives scope but can feel distant or old-fashioned if overused.
- Second person pulls the reader into the role but wears out fast if used across a long work.

```notes
Beginning writers often default to first person because it feels natural, then hit a wall when the story needs information their narrator wouldn't have. This is a common cause of POV breaks: the narrator suddenly knows what another character is thinking, and the reader feels the cheat.

A rescue: if your first-person narrator needs to know something outside their experience, have someone tell them. In life, we learn what we don't witness through conversation. The scene where A hears about B's day from C is as old as fiction.

If the story genuinely needs two or more interior vantages, the fix is usually to switch to third limited (single-POV) or third limited with section breaks between perspectives. Don't switch mid-scene unless you're deliberately composing a multi-voice piece.
```

---

### Staying In POV

Once you pick a POV, the reader trusts you to stay in it. Breaking POV creates a tiny unpleasant moment — the reader can't name it, but something feels off.

Watch for these slips in a first-person story:

> I walked into the kitchen. Mom was chopping onions. She was worried about the bank.

The third sentence is a POV break. The narrator (first person) cannot know what Mom is worried about unless Mom told them or shows clear signs. If the narrator knows, the text needs to do the work:

> I walked into the kitchen. Mom was chopping onions too hard. She had been on the phone with the bank that morning, and the call had ended with her saying "I understand" in the tone that meant she didn't.

Now we are still inside the narrator's awareness. They are inferring, not mind-reading.

```notes
POV breaks are the single most common line-level problem in workshop drafts. They're easy to fix once you spot them, but writers don't spot their own.

A debugging habit: highlight every sentence that reports an interior state (thought, feeling, motivation). For each, ask: how does the narrator know this? If the answer is "because they are that character" (first person, own thought), fine. If "because they see it on the other character's face," fine. If "because I, the author, know it" — that's the break.

Omniscient narrators CAN mind-read, so the same sentence is fine there. The rule changes with POV, which is why POV consistency is the meta-rule.
```

### Voice Is Built from Sentence Choices

Voice is what you notice when you can pick up two anonymous pages and tell that Alice Munro didn't write the one by George Saunders.

Voice emerges from:
- **Sentence length** — short and clipped, or long and recursive?
- **Vocabulary** — plain, technical, slangy, formal?
- **Rhythm** — how often does the sentence surprise you?
- **What the narrator notices** — a character who notices light has a different voice from one who notices money
- **What the narrator doesn't explain** — leaving things unsaid is a voice choice

You do not "pick a voice" the way you pick a POV. Voice emerges from drafts. It stabilises over time as you notice what sounds like you and what sounds like somebody you were imitating.

```notes
First-year writers often worry they "don't have a voice yet." The honest answer is: they don't, not yet — and that's fine. Voice is a residue of sustained writing practice. It forms when you write enough that your own habits calcify.

In the meantime, imitating voices you love is a legitimate training exercise. Read a page of Didion, close the book, write a page in Didion's rhythm. Read a page of Saunders, do the same. Over time, your own rhythm peeks through the imitations and becomes the voice.

The worst advice you can give a beginning writer is "just be yourself." They don't yet know what themselves sounds like on the page. Imitation is how they find out.
```

---

### Voice and POV Must Cooperate

A teenage narrator in first person cannot, in the same breath, use the vocabulary of a retired philosophy professor. If they do, the reader doesn't believe the POV.

If you are writing a first-person child narrator:
- Short sentences tend to work better than periodic ones
- Abstract nouns ("complicity," "apotheosis") break the illusion
- What the narrator does not understand is a tool — they see more than they can name

An adult narrator looking back on childhood is a different POV (first-person retrospective). That narrator CAN use adult vocabulary, but now the voice has a doubled quality — the wise voice narrating, the child-self being narrated.

```notes
This is the most subtle POV/voice interaction. A common failure: a writer uses first-person present ("I am eight years old. I walk to school") and then drops in an adult-sized sentence ("The melancholy of the morning pressed against my chest"). Eight-year-olds don't use "melancholy" that way. The moment breaks.

Fix 1: change the sentence to what an eight-year-old would actually notice ("My chest felt heavy. I didn't know why.").
Fix 2: change the POV to first-person retrospective ("I was eight years old. Looking back, I see now that the morning carried a certain melancholy") — now the adult voice has permission to speak.

Both are valid. Don't blur them.
```

### Let's Practice!

**Question 1**: Identify the POV.

a. "You sit at the edge of the bed and don't turn on the lamp."

b. "She opened the envelope. Across the room, Aaron, who had been watching her for several minutes, noticed her hand shake."

c. "I didn't know how much I'd miss her until the house was empty."

d. "The morning of the trial, Caleb ate cereal. His mother, in the kitchen, thought he looked calm. His sister, in the other room, thought he looked afraid."

**Question 2** (POV break):

> Lena knocked on the door. Her roommate, who had been up all night crying about her job, opened it.

Identify the POV break and rewrite to fix it. (Assume Lena is the POV character, third limited.)

**Question 3** (voice):

Read the two sentences below. Which do you think is from a short story? Which from a legal brief? What features tell you?

a. "The plaintiff alleges damages consistent with negligent entrustment; Defendant disputes the scope of duty."

b. "By the time she found her keys, the car had been there for three hours, and it had snowed on it, and she stood in the driveway and laughed."

```notes
Question 1:
a. Second person ("you sit...").
b. Third omniscient — the narrator sees Aaron's interior state and the woman's hand, across the room.
c. First-person retrospective — "didn't know until" signals looking back.
d. Third omniscient — two characters' interiors in consecutive sentences.

Students who call (b) "third limited" are missing that we're getting Aaron's observation ("who had been watching her") while also seeing her hand shake. Third limited would give us one or the other, not both without a section break.

Question 2: the POV break is "who had been up all night crying about her job." Lena, third-limited POV character, cannot know this unless she's been told. Rewrites:

- Move to observation: "Her roommate opened the door. Lena saw the red around her eyes and the bathrobe she only wore on bad days."
- Have it told: "Her roommate opened the door. 'I haven't slept,' she said before Lena could say anything."

Students who rewrite by putting the information in a later dialogue beat are doing it right. Students who just soften to "probably" ("who had probably been up all night") are dodging — a first person / third limited narrator should not guess in that bland a way unless we're inside a particular kind of anxious interior voice.

Question 3:
a. Legal brief. Features: passive-voice tense ("alleges"), abstract nouns ("damages," "entrustment," "duty"), semicolon used to chain parallel clauses without drama, Latinate vocabulary.
b. Short story. Features: concrete objects (keys, car, snow), short rhythmic clauses joined by "and," present emotion (laughter) arriving unexpectedly at the end, specificity of duration ("three hours").

The right way to talk about voice: don't say "(b) sounds more emotional." Say "(b) uses concrete objects and accumulative rhythm, which creates an effect of unplanned observation." The feature-to-effect link is the craft vocabulary worth learning.
```

## <a id="activity"></a>Activity: Same Moment, Two POVs

Take this kernel of a moment:

> A person returns a borrowed book to a friend. The friend senses that the person did not read it.

**Version A**: Write the moment in first person, from the borrower's POV. 6-10 sentences.

**Version B**: Write the same moment in third limited, from the friend's POV. 6-10 sentences.

Do not explain that the book is unread. Let it show.

Then read both. Which version was harder? Which sentence reveals, without stating, that the book is unread?

```notes
The instructive thing about this exercise is that the two versions hit the reveal at different moments. In the borrower's POV, the reader figures out the book is unread at the moment of handoff — maybe the borrower's thoughts about the cover, the weight, the words they can't remember. In the friend's POV, the reader figures it out by watching — the handoff is slightly too fast, the borrower can't name a favourite character.

A clean student version from the borrower's POV might end with:
"I handed it back and said something about the ending being powerful. She asked which ending. I said, 'You know, the ending,' and kept my face still."

A clean version from the friend's POV might end with:
"She had the book already in her hand when I opened the door. She held it out the way you hold a casserole dish back to a neighbor. She said it was beautiful. She didn't say what it was about."

If a student writes the borrower's POV with "I didn't actually read it" stated outright in the narration, push them: the reader should figure it out, not be told. The craft challenge is to communicate the fact without saying it.

A bonus prompt, if the student finishes early: rewrite the friend's version in second person. What changes? Second person would make the reader feel accused — which fits the emotional tone. Sometimes the right POV is the one you try last.
```
