
## <a id="main-lecture"></a>Revision as Re-Vision

---

### Revision Is Not Editing

- **Editing** is fixing what's already there — sentence-level polishing, grammar, rhythm.
- **Revision** (*re-vision*, literally "seeing again") is asking whether what's there should be there at all.

Most beginning writers call editing "revision," which lets them spend hours polishing sentences in a draft whose structure is broken. The draft sounds better afterward but is no closer to working.

```notes
The confusion between editing and revision is the single biggest thing that keeps beginning writers stuck. They finish a draft, they fix typos and polish sentences, and they call it revising. Then they submit it to workshop, and someone says "I didn't understand why any of this happened" — and they're blindsided, because they thought they revised.

The order matters. Revise first (big moves — structure, POV, what the story is even about), then edit (sentences). Editing a section that is going to be cut is a waste.

A quick vocabulary test for a workshop: ask "what's the difference between revising and editing?" Students who conflate them often don't realise they do, until someone makes them say the words.
```

---

### What Revision Looks Like

Revising a draft often means:

- **Cutting whole scenes.** The scene that took you three hours to write may not belong.
- **Changing who the POV character is.** The story might be about the wrong person.
- **Moving the beginning.** First drafts often start a page or two before the real start.
- **Realising the story is about something else.** You thought you were writing about a divorce; the draft reveals you were writing about the daughter.
- **Adding a scene that didn't exist.** You missed a crucial turn.

None of this is editing. You cannot polish your way to these fixes. You have to re-see the draft.

```notes
A famous piece of writerly advice: "Kill your darlings." The phrase is about revision, not editing. Your darlings — the sentences or scenes you love — are often the ones you are protecting rather than earning. They should go first, because you're the worst judge of them.

A practical version: flag every sentence you would be sad to cut. Then ask, about each one, whether the story works without it. If yes, cut it. If no, keep it — but now you know why it's there, and you can strengthen the reasons.

Students who refuse to cut darlings plateau faster than students who will cut anything. The willingness is half of the skill.
```

---

### The Cold Read

A useful revision habit: put the draft away for at least a day (longer is better), then read it as if you didn't write it.

While reading, don't fix anything. Just mark:

- ⚠ wherever you got bored
- ? wherever you were confused
- ♥ wherever something worked
- ✂ wherever you thought "this doesn't belong"

Those marks are your revision map. The next draft addresses them. (Yes, even the ♥ sections — you need to understand WHY they worked, so you can do it again on purpose.)

```notes
Beginning writers resist the cold-read because it hurts. You loved the draft when you finished it. A day later, the flaws are unignorable. The temptation is to skip straight to fixing, without reading honestly — which means you fix the wrong things.

Enforce the distance. Print the draft if you can. Read it with a pen, not a keyboard. Make it feel like reading someone else's work. If possible, read it in a different physical location than the one where you drafted it.

Writers who never gain distance from their drafts tend to produce uneven work — beautiful sentences inside structures that don't hold. The uneven pages are a symptom of too much editing and too little revision.
```

---

### Revision Hurts Because It Is Loss

You wrote 2,000 words. Revision tells you to cut 800 of them. The cut 800 were real work — you wrote them, they took time. Losing them is losing labour.

Two practical moves that help:

1. **Keep a scraps file.** Paste cut passages into a separate document. They still exist. Some of them will come back in another story. Nothing is wasted if it's saved.
2. **Remember the draft is serving the story, not the other way around.** A scene you loved can be cut because the story is better without it. That is what the work asks of you.

The emotional experience of revision is often: "I thought this was better than it was." That is not a character flaw — it is the normal relationship between a writer and a fresh draft. Everyone feels it. Everyone keeps going.

```notes
This is the section where students push back the hardest. They will say "but I liked that part." Or "my teacher said I should keep it." Or "it took me all week to write it."

None of those are reasons. "I liked it" is an assessment of your attachment, not of the scene's function. "My teacher said" is deference. "It took me all week" is sunk cost.

The only real question is: does the story work better with the scene, or without? If the honest answer is "without," cut it. The scraps file is there to make this less painful. Students who trust the scraps file cut more freely.

A darker truth worth occasionally naming: many published pieces of short fiction are 30-50% shorter than their first draft. The discipline of cutting is not a strange requirement — it is the normal ratio of drafting to published work.
```

---

### When to Stop Revising

You can revise a piece forever. At some point you stop. Two signs that a draft is done:

1. **The changes you're now making are reverting.** You rewrite a paragraph, hate it, rewrite it back. The draft has stabilised.
2. **You're fixing sentences the reader won't notice.** Hunting for a perfect word that three readers haven't flinched at means the word is fine.

The opposite failure — stopping too early — looks like this:

1. You feel finished because the draft "sounds done."
2. You haven't cold-read it yet.
3. You're tired.

Tired is not the same as done. A draft that feels finished the day you wrote it is almost never finished.

```notes
Beginning writers stop too early about nine times more often than they stop too late. They confuse emotional closure with structural closure.

The calibration question: "would I be comfortable publishing this under my name, knowing a close reader will read it three times?" If the answer is "not yet, I'd worry about X," the answer tells you what to revise next.

If the answer is "yes," the draft is done, and further revision risks overworking.

Overworking is real but rare. Most student drafts die from under-revision.
```

### Let's Practice!

**Question 1**: Which of these is revision, and which is editing?

a. Changing "said quietly" to "whispered."
b. Cutting the entire opening paragraph because the real story starts in paragraph two.
c. Moving a flashback from chapter 3 to chapter 1.
d. Fixing a comma splice.
e. Changing the POV character from the mother to the daughter.
f. Shortening a dialogue tag.

**Question 2**: A student's draft contains a scene they love — a long, lyric description of a character watching rain from a car. Readers in workshop say "I don't know why this scene is here." The student responds "it's there because it shows her inner state."

What's wrong with the student's response? What would a better response sound like?

**Question 3**: You've done three drafts of a short story. The current draft feels finished to you, but you haven't let it sit overnight. Your deadline is tomorrow morning. What do you do?

```notes
Question 1:
- a. Editing
- b. Revision
- c. Revision
- d. Editing
- e. Revision
- f. Editing

The test: does the change affect structure / meaning / perspective (revision), or polish / grammar / rhythm (editing)? A good diagnostic is "could you describe this change to someone who hasn't read the draft?" Revision changes are describable at the story level; editing changes usually aren't.

Question 2: the student's response is defensive. "It shows her inner state" is the author's intention — not evidence that the scene is earning its place for readers. A better response acknowledges the readers' experience: "They're telling me the scene doesn't connect. Either it's not doing what I think it's doing, or it's disconnected from the rest — let me ask what specifically felt unmoored, and see whether I can either cut it or weld it to a story beat it's actually serving."

Students who defend their darlings in workshop are not yet revising — they're protecting. Ask them to sit with the feedback for 24 hours before deciding to keep the scene. Often, on the second day, they see it.

Question 3: honest answer — publish at your deadline and accept that the draft will not be as strong as it could be. The fix for this is not "push through the tired revision tonight"; it's "next time, finish a draft 48 hours before the deadline so the cold-read is possible."

If you MUST submit tomorrow, at minimum re-read the opening paragraph and last paragraph of each scene. Those are high-leverage spots. Don't try to overhaul structure when you're tired — you'll break more than you fix.

A student who says "I'll just stay up and revise all night" needs to hear: all-night revision tends to introduce errors at roughly the rate it removes them. If you have 3 hours of energy left, you will do better with 30 minutes of fresh-eyed reading tomorrow morning than with 3 hours of tired editing now.
```

## <a id="activity"></a>Activity: Cut 25%

Take a draft of your own — preferably one you finished at least a few days ago. Count the words.

Your job: **cut 25% of the words**, without deleting the story.

Rules:

1. You may not add new sentences (no compensating by expanding).
2. You may edit existing sentences for concision (combine, collapse, remove redundancy).
3. Whole paragraphs are fair game to cut.
4. Before each cut, you must be able to name WHY it's being cut ("this is redundant with paragraph 4" or "this is a telling sentence that the next scene already shows").

After the exercise, read the shorter version. Is the story weaker, the same, or stronger? If weaker, restore only what is strictly necessary — then measure again.

```notes
Almost everyone finds that the 25%-shorter version is stronger. The first-draft version was padded with the writer's own orientation — sentences they needed to write in order to discover the story, which the reader does not need to read.

This is one of the most humbling exercises in the course. Writers feel "finished" at draft 3. The 25% cut reveals that draft 3 is still 33% longer than it needs to be.

If a student says "I can't find 25% to cut," push: look for the softening adverbs ("very," "really," "just"), the throat-clearing transitions ("And then after that..."), the telling sentences that restate something the scene just showed, and the paragraphs that orient the reader to information they already have. Those are usually 10% alone.

A harder variation: do the exercise with another writer's draft. Students often find this easier — they don't have the writer's attachment. Then ask them to apply the same eye to their own. The lesson lands better from outside.

If, after the exercise, the student insists the draft is stronger LONGER, keep the long version. But keep both files. In a week, read both. The shorter one usually wins on second reading. Trust that.
```
