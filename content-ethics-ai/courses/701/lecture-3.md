## Responsibility Without Agents

A self-driving car hits a pedestrian. A résumé screener quietly filters out qualified women. A content recommender radicalizes a teenager over the course of six months of passive watching. In each case, an outcome has occurred that no single human being straightforwardly caused, intended, or even foresaw. The engineers wrote components. The product managers set objectives. The executives signed contracts. The users clicked. No one was at the wheel. And yet someone, somewhere, is harmed, and we have the unshakeable sense that this harm is not a tornado — that a wrong has been done, and that wrongs require wrongdoers.

The philosopher Andreas Matthias described this condition, in a 2004 paper that has gained authority every year since, as the responsibility gap. Traditional frameworks of moral responsibility, he argued, require an agent who knew what she was doing, could have done otherwise, and did it anyway. Autonomous machine systems produce outcomes in which none of those conditions straightforwardly obtains for any individual. The system's behavior emerges from training data the engineers did not write, interactions the deployers did not foresee, and objectives that were specified in coarse terms and then optimized to sharp ones. The gap is not a failure of any particular developer to be careful. It is structural.

### Distributing the Gap

One response is to distribute responsibility across the chain. The designers of the model carry some; the deployers who chose the context carry some; the users whose interactions shaped the system carry some; the regulators who failed to set standards carry some. There is a truth in this picture. AI harms are almost always socio-technical rather than purely technical, and the absence of any single villain reflects the absence of any single point of control. But distribution alone is not a solution. A responsibility split into twenty pieces often reduces to zero pieces in practice, because each party can point to another as the more proximate cause, and the harmed person is left litigating a diagram.

### Product Liability as a Partial Template

The law has faced a version of this problem before. When a Ford Pinto explodes, we do not require the plaintiff to identify the specific welder who installed the fuel tank. Product liability doctrine assigns responsibility to the manufacturer as an institution, in virtue of having placed a defective product into commerce, regardless of whether any named employee was negligent. The framework accepts that complex systems produce harms no individual authored, and responds by holding the organization answerable.

This template is imperfect for AI in specific ways. A car's failure modes are, in principle, enumerable during design. A large language model's are not. A Pinto does not learn new behavior from the drivers who use it. A recommender does. Still, the basic move — treat the deploying organization as a locus of responsibility that does not require individual fault — is one of the few legal technologies we have that scales to socio-technical harm.

### Why "The Algorithm Did It" Is Insufficient

The phrase "the algorithm did it" has become, in boardrooms and press releases, a way of routing accountability into a black box that cannot be interrogated. This is a moral dodge for two reasons. First, the algorithm did not arrive from space. Someone trained it on specific data, chose specific objectives, deployed it in a specific context, and decided which errors were tolerable. Each of those choices is a human act for which a human can be asked to answer. Second, treating the algorithm as an agent that bears responsibility is a category error. Algorithms cannot be punished, shamed, or deterred. They have no interests to be set back by sanction. To say the algorithm is responsible is functionally equivalent to saying no one is.

The honest position is uncomfortable. Responsibility for AI harms is real, it is distributed, and it is often difficult to pin to any single person with precision. The difficulty is not a reason to abandon the concept. It is a reason to build institutions — audits, liability rules, impact assessments, professional norms — that can carry the weight that individual agents, unassisted, cannot. The alternative is not a world without blame. It is a world in which blame attaches only to the victim, because the victim is the only party left visible.
