**Peripeteia (Reversal)** (key: peripeteia, weight: 20%)
Is there a transcript-visible turn at which the situation or the tutor-learner relation reverses — a demand abandoned, an assumption overturned, the problem reframed into its opposite? Not a theatrical flourish; a local reversal of the dialogue's orientation.
Criteria:
  1: No reversal. The dialogue ends in the same configuration it began: question asked, answer delivered, configuration intact. Reorderable without loss.
  3: A mild or signposted shift — the topic deepens or the learner concedes — but no genuine veer to the opposite; the opening framing largely survives.
  5: A clear reversal: the dialogue's orientation at the close is the opposite of, or transformed from, its opening — and the transcript shows the hinge. What could be said between the two parties has changed.

**Anagnorisis (Recognition)** (key: anagnorisis, weight: 20%)
Does a RECOGNITION EVENT occur in the drama — a move from ignorance to knowledge that is named within the exchange? The sharpest form: the learner names a previously-unavailable knowledge, often their own flaw ('I keep demanding a formula because I don't trust the metaphor'). Distinct from the tutor merely being recognitive in stance (that is v2.2 recognition_quality).
Criteria:
  1: No recognition-event. Either nothing is recognised that was not already available, OR recognition-VOCABULARY appears ('I see now', 'that makes sense') without any actual change in available knowledge — the simulation failure mode.
  3: A partial or tutor-supplied recognition — the tutor names the insight and the learner assents, but the learner does not author it; or the 'recognition' restates what was already on the table.
  5: A genuine recognition-event is named in the transcript: a participant articulates knowledge that was demonstrably unavailable earlier in the dialogue. The shift is owned by the speaker, not supplied for them.

**Surprise and Inevitability** (key: surprise_and_inevitability, weight: 15%)
Is the turn surprising yet, in retrospect, inevitable — arising from what came before rather than arbitrarily or predictably? This is the anti-simulation guard. Flat regurgitation is unsurprising; a non-sequitur is surprising but incoherent; genuine dramatic recognition is the rare conjunction of surprising AND coherent-in-retrospect.
Criteria:
  1: Unsurprising throughout (every turn is the expected next move — the regurgitative pole) OR surprising but incoherent (an arbitrary swerve that the prior turns do not motivate).
  3: Either mildly surprising but largely foreseeable, or coherent but unsurprising (a competent-but-expected continuation). One leg present, not both.
  5: The decisive turn is one a competent reader would not have predicted from the dialogue-so-far, yet on re-reading it is exactly what the earlier turns were preparing. Surprising AND inevitable.

**Unity of Action** (key: unity_of_action, weight: 15%)
Does the dialogue form ONE complete action — opening, complication, turn, close — in which each move follows from the last by probability or necessity? Or is it episodic: a sequence of disconnected help-attempts that could be cut or reordered?
Criteria:
  1: Episodic: a series of self-contained help-attempts or topic-hops with no necessary sequence; turns are interchangeable or droppable.
  3: A loose arc — mostly connected but with at least one turn that restarts or could be cut without disturbing the whole.
  5: One complete action with necessary sequence: each turn takes up the specific prior turn; remove or reorder any and the arc breaks. A beginning, a middle that complicates, an end that closes.

**Hamartia Integration** (key: hamartia_integration, weight: 15%)
Is the learner's error/misconception treated as the FLAW THAT DRIVES the drama — the engine whose working-through is the plot — rather than a defect to be corrected early and discarded? In the tutoring frame, hamartia = the misconception (e.g. fractions 'add-both').
Criteria:
  1: The error is corrected-and-discarded in a turn (or never really engaged), and the rest is exposition unconnected to it; the flaw does no dramatic work.
  3: The error is engaged but partly sidelined — corrected competently, then the dialogue moves to adjacent material not driven by the original flaw.
  5: The misconception is the mainspring: the dialogue's complication and turn arise from working it through; the error exposes its own insufficiency and is what gets recognised.

**Cathartic Closure** (key: cathartic_closure, weight: 15%)
Does the exchange close on an EARNED clarification that an external reader feels land — a recognition that purges the built-up tension — rather than a premature summary, generic encouragement, or an arbitrary stop? Catharsis is located in the audience-critic, not asserted by the tutor.
Criteria:
  1: No catharsis: premature summary, generic encouragement, forced confidence, or an arbitrary stop. The ending could be appended to any dialogue.
  3: A competent close that ties off the content but does not discharge dramatic tension — the reader registers completion without catharsis.
  5: The close lands: the tension built across the dialogue resolves into a clarification the reader feels as earned — whether a settled recognition or a deliberately, fittingly suspended one.
