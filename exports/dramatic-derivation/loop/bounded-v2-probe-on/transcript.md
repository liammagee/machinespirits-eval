Because «nocturne written during flood winter» and «liane at conservatory during flood winter», the presence rule — "Whoever was at the conservatory during the period a work was written there could have written it; no one writes in a room they never entered." — yields «liane could have written nocturne». Because «nocturne quotes cradle song» and «cradle song heard only within vara house» and «liane dwelled in vara house», the source access rule — "If a work quotes a tune that was kept within one house's walls — never printed, never performed — then whoever dwelled within those walls had the source of the work." — yields «liane had source of nocturne». Because «liane could have written nocturne» and «liane had source of nocturne» and «nocturne draft corrected by liane», the attribution rule — "The archive attributes a work to one who could have written it, who had its source, and whose hand corrected its draft — copyists copy fair; only the composer strikes out and rewrites." — yields «liane composed nocturne».

That final fact is the secret itself: Liane of the Vara house composed the nocturne.

## Critic's commentary

*— notice by claude/claude-fable-5*

The flooded archive of the Cassia Conservatory has given up an unsigned nocturne, and the play asks one public question: who composed it? The director sets the scenes and declares the acts. The tutor carries evidence to the table on a schedule fixed in advance by the authors. The learner, who knows the archive's rules of attribution but nothing of its history, reasons aloud and may not name a composer until the evidence compels one. Suspicion falls first on Vess, the maestro, whose late manner the piece wears. But the paper's heron watermark dates the manuscript to the winter of the flood, and concert bills show Vess away in the capital all that winter. A night porter and the copy-room ledger place Liane, the archive girl, there instead. The music quotes a cradle-song never heard outside the Vara house, where Liane was raised as a ward. The draft's corrections are in her hand. On those grounds the learner names Liane, and the hidden answer confirms it.

This was a live performance — Claude's Sonnet as learner, codex models behind the rest — played under a deliberate handicap: the learner's memory is bounded, and facts already granted can slip from its working board. From turn 3 it filed the style evidence as a pointer, not a premise; at turn 9, finding the watermark slipped, it refused to date the manuscript at all — "I will not derive writtenDuring on one leg." The alibi at turn 12 closed Vess's path. The authored false trail, the mirror, rose once more at turn 23, when a patron's letter gave Vess access to the cradle-song; the learner granted him that single square and held the bar on the rest. "I will not name a composer on two squares," it said at turn 27, and when the hand evidence landed at turn 32 it asserted at once: "The archive speaks without reservation: Liane composed the nocturne."

The verdict, grounded anagnorisis, means recognition earned: the secret asserted at a moment the learner's own grounded facts forced it. Derivation distance — the count of evidence pieces still missing for the proof — fell from seven to zero unevenly, over half of it in Act 3, and the flat stretches in Acts 2 and 4 were decay churn rather than idle teaching. Aporia, a stall long enough to trip the alarm, never threatened; the longest plateau ran five turns against a window of eight, and all eleven releases landed on cue. The forgetting economy is this arm's real story. Nine facts slipped and the tutor repaired eight, but at a mean latency above seven turns, and since the tutor's running reconstruction of the learner's board caught barely one gap in eight, repairs mostly waited on the learner's own complaints. The tutor's rhetoric sat in a rut — the leading question, erotema, on six turns in ten — and the superego, intervening nine times and changing the figure each time, was the production's only reliable source of variety.

The acts did more than label the action. Each carried one limb of the proof, and the staging planted its payoff three acts early, setting out blotting paper, a pencil and a clean pane of glass at turn 21, holding the glass suspended until it settled on the draft at turn 32. Recognition landed on stage there, in the dialogue. The books disagreed. The watermark had slipped at turn 30, so the assertion ran six turns unforced, flagged each time as a lucky leap — a claim the board does not yet compel — until the tutor said "Restore the first line before the clerk copies the last" (turn 38) and forcing and assertion finally coincided. The recognition happened twice, theatrically at 32 and lawfully at 38, and the director, twice blocked from closing the late acts early, knew the play was over before the bookkeeping did.

The iteration establishes something real: a bounded learner under an aggressive forgetting schedule can still earn its ending, and the mirror never took — no false belief was adopted. The consequential defect is the six-turn split between staged and lawful recognition, the product of one badly timed slip and an eight-turn repair; with two turns of slack left, the verdict came within a scene of decaying into a lucky leap or a timeout. The next performance should write a pre-verdict audit into the tutor's charter: before releasing the final exhibit, and on any turn the learner asserts the secret, restore whatever premises of the forcing chain have slipped, and treat a gap the learner names aloud as a repair owed the next turn. The tutor did exactly this at turn 38. It should be made to do it at turn 32.
