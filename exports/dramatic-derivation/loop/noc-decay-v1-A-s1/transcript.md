## Critic's commentary

*— notice by claude/claude-fable-5*

When the curtain rises the Cassia Conservatory stands flooded, and out of its dripping archive comes a nocturne with no signature. The public question is the plain one: who composed it? The hall's ready answer is Vess, the celebrated master whose late manner the piece wears. Three voices share the stage. The director sets scenes and declares the movements. The tutor carries evidence to the table on a schedule fixed in advance and teaches its use. The learner knows the world's general laws of attribution — paper dates a work, presence permits authorship, a private tune betrays its source, only a composer corrects a draft — but none of its particulars, and must reason aloud. The evidence arrives one piece at a time. A heron watermark and a steward's stock-book fix the nocturne to the flood winter. Concert bills hold Vess in the capital for that whole winter. A porter remembers Liane, the archive girl, tending the copy-room lamps through it. A cradle-song hidden in the middle voice is traced to the Vara house, where Liane was raised as ward, and the draft's corrections prove to be in her hand. The learner concludes that Liane composed the nocturne, and the mechanical checker certifies the recognition as earned.

The learner's discipline is the production's early pleasure. At turn 1 it severs room from rumor — "presiding is not composing" — and at turn 3 it adopts the musicians' style verdict while observing that style appears in none of the six rules. Turn 9 is the first hinge, when the stock-book turns paper into time. Turn 12 is the second, when the alibi lands and the learner declares the Vess hypothesis "no longer merely ungrounded — it is actively blocked." The authored near-miss, the mirror, rises twice: on style at turn 3, and again at turn 23, when a patron's note gives Vess access to the Vara song; the learner grants him the source conjunct and shows in the same breath that source cannot buy presence. The ending comes at turn 32, when the hand evidence is released and the tutor breaks off — "And therefore..." — and the learner completes the sentence: "The rules close without remainder. The nocturne is Liane's."

The instruments complicate that ending.
