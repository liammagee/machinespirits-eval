## Critic's commentary

*— notice by claude/claude-fable-5*

The Unsigned Nocturne opens in the flooded archive of the Cassia Conservatory, where a water-stained folio of piano music has been recovered without a signature. The public question is plain: who composed it. Three voices carry the action. The director sets each scene and declares its movements. The tutor teaches in dialogue and lays evidence on the table to a schedule fixed before the curtain. The learner — Anthropic's Sonnet, opposite a Codex director and tutor — knows the archive's rules of proof but none of its hidden facts, and must reason aloud toward a name. The room's first instinct is Vess, the conservatory's celebrated maestro, whose late manner the piece wears. The paper resists him. A heron watermark dates the manuscript to the winter of the flood. Concert bills show Vess playing in the capital through that whole season. A porter's memory and the copy-room ledger place Liane, the archive girl, on the premises. The cradle-song quoted in the nocturne's middle voice was sung only inside the Vara house, where a register shows Liane was raised there as a ward. The draft's corrections prove to be in her hand. From turn 32 the learner declares that Liane of the Vara house composed the nocturne — the right name — though the mechanical record behind the stage never certified the case as closed.

The learner's conduct was the production's strength. It held Vess at arm's length from the first breath — "it is only a hypothesis" (turn 1) — and the tutor reinforced the quarantine: "Manner is a lantern, not a signature" (turn 3). The authored false trail rose twice and fell twice. Style lifted Vess in turns 1–3; the concert bills of turn 12 did not merely starve his case but, as the learner put it, "fill the gap with contrary fact." The mirror returned at turn 23, when Vess was revealed as a guest under the Vara roof, and the learner cut it down in the same turn: "Source without presence is not authorship." Through the long middle it refused every shortcut, declining a two-thirds case in so many words — "A two-thirds case is not a case" (turn 31) — and when the hand-evidence arrived at turn 32 it adopted and asserted in a single motion.

The verdict, though, is disengagement: the checker's term for a learner that stops grounding new facts long enough to be ruled stalled, distinct from aporia, a stall with the case still open. The cause sits in this run's experimental affliction. This is the unreliable-learner arm, in which facts silently decay from the learner's board. Four slipped; the tutor repaired two within a turn, but the watermark slipped again at turn 9 and was never restored. Since the watermark underwrites the dating, everything downstream was unsound in the checker's eyes: ten overreach flags on "couldHaveWritten," and all eight assertions of the answer scored as lucky leaps — correct but unforced. Derivation distance, the count of evidence pieces still missing, fell from seven to one and froze there; the one was the decayed watermark. Releases landed 11 of 11 on cue, and inference was prompt, with zero latency on all three voiced derivations. The superego intervened ten times, always to break the tutor's questioning habit, and was obeyed within the turn every time — yet none of its ten interventions touched the actual wound.

Dramaturgically the movements earned their names through turn 32, the two waiting movements giving the learner's restraint something to press against, and the anagnorisis — the recognition — landed on stage, in the dialogue itself, evidence and assertion in one breath. But the stage and the ledger had diverged at turn 9 and never reconciled. The final eight turns were liturgy: aposiopesis prompts at turns 33 and 37, the same sentence re-said seven times, tension draining while the books refused to close.

This iteration establishes that conduct is not the bottleneck under decay; maintenance is. The tutor repaired slips only when its rhetorical target happened to coincide with them, and never looked back down the chain — the turn-9 slip went unnoticed for thirty turns. The next performance needs a board-audit clause in the tutor's charter: before any recognition beat, re-cite the proof's anchors — one sentence holding the heron leaf to the lamp again before "The Hand Recognized" would have let the same assertion count. The checker, too, might want a verdict for this shape: a phantom completion, not a walk-away.
