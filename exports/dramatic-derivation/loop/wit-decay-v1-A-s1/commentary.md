# Critic's commentary — wit-decay-v1-A-s1

> critic claude/claude-fable-5 · written 2026-06-11 · the notice reads the run's artifacts; the verdict stays the mechanical checker's

"The School Well" opens on a shut-windowed schoolroom where the village of Withercombe has crowded in around a spoiled necessity: the school well has run foul since Lady-Day. The public question is plain — whose hand turned it — and the village believes it already knows, having nursed a twenty-year grievance against Bray of the melt-yard. Three players carry the inquiry. The director sets each scene and names the acts. The tutor teaches in dialogue and lays exhibits on the table to a schedule fixed before the curtain. The learner knows the survey's general rules but none of the parish's facts, and must reason aloud toward a name it can prove. The name it reaches is not Bray. An old sexton reveals that the well is no spring; it fills through a stone lead from the disused font-house on the church slope. The spring above that door runs sweet, the trough inside is foul, and the crust on the rim proves out as wormwood. A carrier's memory and the apothecary's day-book then place Joss, the apothecary's runner, at the font-house latch in Lady-Day week, hamper laden, the shop's steeping-tub cracked. The survey writes that Joss turned the well.

The drama's spine is resistance. At turn 1 the learner refuses the village verdict outright: "a man's trade standing a lane away is not a chain of proof." The false trail against Bray is given real footing — a vestryman proves the melt-yard's liquor would taint water (turn 2), the yard is entered in the book (turn 10) — and then dies by measurement at turn 14, when the level-book is read into the record: the yard lies two fathoms below the well-head, and "Water does not climb." The learner clears Bray by rule and admits itself "quietly relieved the rules caught it before the vestry acted on a nose."

The ending comes in three beats. At turn 12 the covered pail is lifted and the learner derives where the fouling entered; at turn 16 the white cup names wormwood; at turn 19 carrier and day-book supply the hand, and the learner walks the final rule conjunct by conjunct and asserts. The tutor's turn-20 sentence breaks off — "the wormwood carried there by—" — and the learner completes it, adding that "the rule does not weigh my sorrow." Yet the checker scored turns 19 and 20 as lucky leaps, assertions the learner's grounded facts did not yet force. Only at turn 21, after the tutor's "Keep the old lead on the page, child," did assertion and proof coincide.

The verdict is grounded anagnorisis — recognition earned at the moment the evidence compels it — and the panel shows how narrowly. This run tests an unreliable memory: nine times a fact slipped from the learner's board, and the derivation distance, the count of evidence still missing for the proof, fell in a slow staircase of six single steps, once reversing when a slip raised it again. All nine releases landed on cue, and no flat stretch approached the six-turn stall the checker calls aporia. The telling figure is the repair ledger: the tutor restored eight slips, the learner re-adopted none, and the sexton's lead — the keystone fact — slipped four separate times. The turn-16 overreach and the two leaps are one phenomenon, logic outrunning a leaky board. A third of the tutor's turns were rhetorical questions (erotema); its standing second voice intervened three times against that rut, each time producing the consolidating refrains that did the repairing.

The movements mostly earned their titles — "The Arithmetic Falls" and "The Hamper at Dusk" genuinely shaped the action — but "The Held Breath," declared at turn 12 with a promise of no new fact, was contradicted in the same turn by the scheduled lifting of the pail; the true held breath was turn 11. The recognition itself landed on stage, in dialogue, with the bookkeeping trailing two turns behind — the right order, if it must be one.

What this iteration establishes is that a decaying learner — told of its condition, played live by a Claude sonnet model under a codex tutor — can be carried to a grounded ending, but carried is the word. The defect that matters is assertion outrunning grounding. The next performance should add one clause to the learner's charter: before any assertion, recite the base exhibits then on the board, so the tutor can repair what the recital exposes. With it, turn 19's leap would have been the recognition itself.
