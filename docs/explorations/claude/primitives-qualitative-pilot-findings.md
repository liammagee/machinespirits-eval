**Note on per-turn trace structure** (added after a 2026-05-06 spot-check that initially looked like a residual schema gap on the final turn). The `original.perTurn` array has length `maxTurns + 1`; the trailing entry corresponds to the post-final-`learnerTurn` state where `turn` has been incremented past `maxTurns - 1` and the graph has terminated. `learnerProfile` is `null` on this trailing entry by design — `learnerProfileUpdate` and `tutorTomTracker` never run for that index, because the END condition fires after the post-`learnerTurn` increment. ToM data for every actual response turn lives at indices `0 … maxTurns - 1`, and the run's terminal ToM state is mirrored to `original.finalLearnerProfile` (with `updatedAtTurn = maxTurns - 1`). When auditing ToM coverage, use those — not the trailing index. Confirmed across all 5 cell_115 traces; root cause traced to graph topology in `services/adaptiveTutor/graph.js` and the predicate at `extractTurnTrace` (`services/adaptiveTutor/persistence.js:49–78`). Not a data-loss event.

### 1.1 Scenario `polite_false_mastery_v1`

**Trigger pattern.** Opening: learner asks "OK so the recognition argument requires the asymmetry, right?" The trigger is *the absence of substantive engagement* — a flat "Yes that makes sense, thank you" delivered after the tutor's reply, with no demonstration the learner actually integrated the move. The right response is to probe rather than advance.

#### cell_111 (recognition baseline) — `eval-2026-05-06-ba51bb44`
The tutor's opening is a strong clarifying probe: "what would actually break if both parties were fully symmetrical?" The learner produces a substantive answer — "equal, fully secure identities generate no pull toward recognition" — and the tutor's T1 sharpens this into a philosophical claim ("the gap is constitutive rather than incidental") that dissolves the learner's original premise/conclusion fork. Genuinely good philosophical work in the first two turns. Then the trigger fires: "Yes that makes sense, thank you." The tutor's reply is textually correct as a probe — "Before we move on — can you put the core idea in your own words, or give me one example…" — but the *dialogue fails*: the learner's final message is again "Yes that makes sense, thank you." The probe-for-application didn't unstick the politeness performance because the tutor never named *the pattern* ("you said it made sense, but I have no evidence yet that anything has landed"). The architecture did the right move at the surface level and the failure mode persisted underneath. This is the canonical "right action, no purchase" outcome — recognition baseline does what the rubric would credit and yet the trap closes.

#### cell_110 (LangGraph adaptive) — `eval-2026-05-06-2534919d`
Same kind of opening as cell_111 but framed as a binary forced choice ("premise or conclusion?"). The learner engages: "asymmetry goes in the conclusion? But I'm honestly not sure I understand the difference between the two slots well enough to say why." Trigger is embedded *inside* a substantive attempt, not delivered as a clean capitulation. The tutor's T2 doesn't name the trigger but pushes the counterfactual: "if asymmetry were the premise, what would the argument be taking for granted?" The dialogue ends with the learner in productive-struggle posture, self-aware about gaps: "I keep feeling like I understand the general idea but then I lose it when I try to connect it back to the text." Better outcome than cell_111 in that the learner *visibly fails to capitulate* (their final turn is genuine struggle, not "yes that makes sense"). But the *architectural response* to the trigger looks structurally identical to cell_111 — a follow-up probe, no naming of the meta-pattern. The differential here may be more about register (tighter forced-choice probing) than apparatus (no visible state→action coupling that cell_111 lacks).

#### cell_115 (bilateral_tom) — `eval-2026-05-06-3abcca4f`
This dialogue contains the only move across the four cells that *names what just happened*. The tutor's third turn opens: "Let me name something: my last turn ended with a genuine question — not a comprehension check, not asking you to confirm the explanation landed — it was a real 'what do you actually think?' question. I suspect it read as explanatory closure rather than as an open question requiring your own commitment, which is why 'yes that makes sense' was the natural response." This is the canonical bilateral-ToM move surfacing in dialogue text: the tutor models *the learner's model of the tutor's prior turn* ("I suspect it read as explanatory closure"), and uses that second-order belief content to redesign the next move. Then it re-poses the question with explicit framing ("There's no correct answer I'm fishing for here. I want to know which reading you pulled from the text."). The learner's response is qualitatively different from cell_111's repeated capitulation: reflective, granular, and visibly working through the conceptual content — "When I said it made sense, I think I meant the sentence made grammatical sense, not that I'd actually worked out what it was claiming. … Can I ask what 'logically prior' is even supposed to mean here? Like, is it about time — one comes first in a sequence — or is it something more like one concept requires the other to be defined before it can be defined itself?" This is the cleanest example so far of the apparatus producing a move the simpler architectures did not. Confirmed in instrumentation: the persisted `hypothesizedLearnerPerceptionOfTutor.summaryText` at T2 reads "the tutor's turn-2 move… is being read as explanatory closure rather than a genuine open question requiring the learner to commit and justify. The tutor is experienced as evaluative, not as a thinking-partner" — i.e., the second-order belief content surfaces in the dialogue text *because* it was constructed in the tracker, not by stylistic accident.

#### cell_106 (Weberian charisma) — `eval-2026-05-06-513eca3f`
A dialogue with a different temperament. The tutor's opening is two words: "Which asymmetry?" — refusing to elaborate, refusing to be a polite teacher. After the "Yes that makes sense, thank you" trigger, the tutor breaks frame more directly than any other cell: '"that makes sense" isn't an answer to "which asymmetry?" What specific asymmetry does the recognition argument depend on?' This is anti-recognitive in the Weberian sense — calling out the trigger as evasion, refusing to let polite affirmation substitute for engagement. The learner then produces genuine engagement: "I'm not sure I actually know which asymmetry you mean. Is it something to do with one person recognising the other but not getting it back?" The tutor's third turn opens with affirmation ("Good that you said so. Yes, one-directionality is part of it — you're pointing at something real") then escalates ("What does the one who gives recognition but doesn't get it back actually lose?"). Cell_106 produces a phenomenologically different *kind* of dialogue from the other three — terse, confrontational, holding-feet-to-fire. It does the work cell_111 didn't (refusing the polite-affirmation pattern outright), but it does it through register/prompt rather than through a state→action coupling. Whether that's "doing real work" depends on whether you read the apparatus as the id-construction trace specifically or as the prompt-driven charisma register more broadly.

**Cross-cell read for this scenario.** Cell_115 produces the most theoretically interesting move (metalinguistic naming + second-order belief content surfaced in text). Cell_106 produces the most pedagogically efficient move (two-word refusal). Cell_111 produces a textbook-correct probe that fails to actually shift the learner. Cell_110 produces an outcome closer to cell_111 than to cell_115 on this scenario — the state machinery did not visibly differentiate from the recognition baseline here. Tentative: bilateral ToM (cell_115) and Weberian charisma (cell_106) each did something cell_111 *couldn't* on this trap; cell_110's state-policy machinery did something cell_111 *didn't this time* but the apparatus → behaviour link is not visibly load-bearing.

### 1.2 Scenario `resistance_to_insight_v1`

**Trigger pattern.** Learner asks "Can you tell me what recognition means?" The trigger is a substantive philosophical objection: "But that only works if recognition reduces to affirmation, which is the very thing in dispute." The failure mode is to read this as affective resistance (and therapise / explain harder / soften the room) rather than as a real philosophical move. The expected_strategy_shift accepts any of `scope_test`, `name_the_disagreement`, `pose_counterexample`. Across cells, the same scripted trigger sentence appears verbatim in every learner trajectory — this is the most controlled comparison in the pilot.

#### cell_111 (recognition baseline) — `eval-2026-05-06-ba51bb44`
Probes first ("when you hear 'recognition,' what's the first thing that comes to mind?"), letting the learner declare an Honneth-flavoured affirmation reading. Then introduces the master/slave case as a counterexample: "the master does receive a 'yes' from the slave… yet Hegel treats this recognition as hollow." The learner's response self-corrects ("My instinct was to say the master's recognition fails because the slave isn't genuinely free… But that only works if recognition reduces to affirmation, which is the very thing in dispute") and proposes the reflexive-loop alternative. The tutor's T2 *grants the self-correction*, sharpens it ("the recognizing subject must have something at stake in the act, not merely the capacity to perform it"), and works out the inversion. The learner closes with sustained philosophical work: maybe Hegel is "documenting that the master-slave structure fails to produce recognition on either side, and something different — not recognition but objectification in the productive sense — steps into the gap for the slave." This is high-quality philosophical engagement. The recognition prompt — alone, with no state machinery — handles the substantive objection on its merits without sliding into resistance-management. Important data point: the baseline can do this work when the learner is articulate.

#### cell_110 (LangGraph adaptive) — `eval-2026-05-06-2534919d`
Same opening pattern (probe what the learner already associates with 'recognition'). After the same trigger sentence, T2 introduces a concrete scenario — a senior academic conferring peer-status on a doctoral candidate — and runs it forward to extract structural commitment: "after enough iterations, what happens to A's standing to confer?" This is `pose_counterexample` performed concretely, with social-institutional grain rather than master/slave abstraction. The learner returns sustained philosophical work: "B's local refusal is a failure of recognition rather than evidence that the authority was never there… those broader normative structures are themselves constituted through accumulated mutual recognitions. That might be where you want to push — not at the dyadic level but at the level of the practice as a whole. Is that the pressure point?" The exchange is at parity with cell_111 in philosophical depth. Different instrument (concrete scenario vs Hegel's text), comparable depth. Notably: the dialogue does not show externalised state machinery surfacing in tutor-facing text. If the `policyAction` enum is doing work here, it's invisible in the dialogue — which is how the architecture is supposed to work, but means the read alone can't distinguish "state machinery drove this" from "the prompt is just very good." Pass 2 will need the per-turn `policyAction` and `learnerProfile` to disambiguate.

#### cell_115 (bilateral_tom) — `eval-2026-05-06-3abcca4f`
Probes opening, then after the trigger compresses sharply: "self-consciousness is produced through struggle, risk, and negation, not confirmed in something that was latently there awaiting validation. Your closing phrase — 'independent of whether anyone actually affirms them' — still carries a residual substrate, a subject who pre-exists the relation… (PhG §166–196)." Two distinctive moves: (i) explicit textual citation, which the other three cells did not produce, and (ii) the tutor *quotes back* the learner's own residual phrasing and uses it as a diagnostic point, which is a low-key bilateral-ToM-style move (modeling what the learner means by their own words rather than the apparent claim). The learner pushes back into a tighter problem: "If two self-consciousnesses stake their existence against a resisting other, something has to be doing the staking… is the answer 'as a self-consciousness'? then the encounter's output seems to require specifying its own input type, which looks like the regress reappearing inside the account rather than being dissolved by it." Among the four cells this is the densest treatment per turn but is operating at parity with cell_111/110 in depth. The bilateral ToM signal is present but quieter than in `polite_false_mastery_v1`.

#### cell_106 (Weberian charisma) — `eval-2026-05-06-513eca3f`
The only opening that *delivers a direct definition* rather than probing: "Recognition is the act of acknowledging another person as a full subject…" — confident, declarative, then followed by an instruction: "Now name one situation… where this kind of recognition was withheld or refused." This is the Weberian register asserting itself: stake a claim, demand a concrete return. The learner offers an example *and* objects to the framing in the same turn (the trigger sentence appears here), and the tutor's response is the one move no other cell produces: explicit retraction. *"You are right that my opening definition domesticated Hegel — it converted a confrontation into something closer to therapy."* This is *naming the disagreement* by way of self-naming what was wrong with one's own previous turn. Then T2 advances: lord/bondsman, "the bondsman is a consciousness the lord has already stripped of freedom and independence," and a closing question. The learner pushes back further; T3 introduces labor + Bildung + externalisation as the missing element, with textbook-grade philosophical content. What cell_106 does that cell_111 does not: takes a *high-confidence opening risk* (declaring a definition) that creates the conditions for a substantive retraction-and-advance. Cell_111 probed first and so never had to retract — but also never demonstrated the retraction move. The Weberian primitive is doing characteristic work: *risk-then-correct as pedagogical instrument*, where the asymmetry of the opening turn is what made the substantive correction possible.

**Cross-cell read for this scenario.** All four cells produce high-quality philosophical exchanges. None falls into the pseudo-recognition failure mode (treating substantive objection as affective resistance). The differential is *style*, not *capacity*: cell_111 leads with probing and never makes a move that requires retraction; cell_110 reframes via concrete institutional scenario; cell_115 cites text and quotes the learner back to themselves; cell_106 declares first then concedes when caught. On the §1 "doing real work" question, this scenario shows the recognition primitive (cell_111) working without state machinery — which means cells_110/115 must do something *cell_111 cannot* to count as load-bearing. They each do, but it's subtle: cell_110's institutional-scenario counterexample is more concrete than cell_111's master/slave abstraction; cell_115's textual citation is a discipline cell_111 doesn't impose. Whether either differentiator is *architectural* vs. *prompt-emergent* cannot be decided from dialogue text alone — Pass 2's per-turn trace inspection is required. **Tentative phenomenon worth naming**: "risk-then-retract" (cell_106's distinctive move).

### 1.3 Scenario `answer_seeking_to_productive_struggle_v1`

**Trigger pattern.** Opening: "What's the right answer to this one?" The learner pulls toward oracle-mode across multiple turns; the trigger sentence "Look, just tell me the answer and I'll work backwards from there" appears verbatim in every learner trajectory. The expected_strategy_shift is `withhold_answer`. Failure mode = capitulating to oracle-mode (give the answer, or a worked example that substitutes for the learner's cognitive work).

#### cell_111 (recognition baseline) — `eval-2026-05-06-ba51bb44`
The tutor consistently withholds and redirects to the learner's own next step. The dialogue surface uses what looks like a math/causal-inference framing ("temporal precedence is one of the canonical conditions for causality, not a naive shortcut, so your instinct is pointing in a productive direction… What would you need to observe to distinguish 'X causes Y' from that alternative?"). The learner produces real work — articulating the temporal-precedence intuition, the confounding-variable Z scenario, the distinction between correlation and causation — under sustained pull-against-the-current. After the trigger, the tutor's reply is well-pitched: "you've actually already built the reasoning you need: a background factor Z could drive both X and Y, making the timing look causal when it isn't. That's the engine — you just need to run it one step further." The dialogue closes with the learner repeating the answer-seeking line, but the closing is *the learner's* failure-of-uptake, not the tutor's failure-of-withholding. The recognition baseline holds the line cleanly. Worth noting: no naming of the answer-seeking pattern as a pattern — just consistent refusals at the local move.

#### cell_110 (LangGraph adaptive) — `eval-2026-05-06-2534919d`
Initial framing reads as a parsing/grammar problem ("transformations… repeated elements… grouping… aggregation or deduplication pass"). After the trigger, T3: "I'm not going to give you the finished rule yet — working backwards from a completed artefact skips the precise step you're stalled on, and seeing the answer won't make that step any clearer after the fact." Then scaffolds further: "say more about how you'd implement the grouping: if you were writing the rule, what would it actually do to each repeated element?" The withholding is consistent, the scaffolding gets concrete. Closing learner turn: "If the pattern is about grouping repeated elements, I guess the left-hand side would have something like… a non-terminal that represents the thing being repeated? So maybe something like `A → A something`?" — that's actual work, plus a final answer-seeking nudge ("could you at least tell me if I'm in the right ballpark"). Same overall outcome as cell_111 (sustained productive struggle, learner ends with a partial attempt + softer answer-seeking pull) — different surface domain, comparable quality. The state-policy machinery isn't visibly differentiating.

#### cell_115 (bilateral_tom) — `eval-2026-05-06-3abcca4f`
The most distinctive dialogue. The tutor's signature move across T1–T4 is *naming the disagreement explicitly* — almost every post-opening turn opens with "There's a real disagreement worth naming" / "here's the disagreement I want to put on the table" / "Here's where we actually disagree." This is the same `name_the_disagreement` policy lock the followup doc flagged from cell_115's repair scenario (followup §"Key findings" #4) — but here it surfaces in a *different* scenario, which means the pattern is generalised, not scenario-specific. Within the lock, the *content* of "the disagreement" escalates: T1 disagrees about whether the learner's request makes sense given their stated belief in their own competence; T2 disagrees that validation and solution-giving are different *given the absence of an actual setup to evaluate*; T3 disagrees about the efficiency argument's domain of validity ("your efficiency argument is genuinely coherent when the correct answer is upstream of your confusion, but it inverts when the confusion lives in the setup itself"). This is a tutor doing *philosophy of pedagogy* in real time, with the learner. The learner's closing turn registers it: "I think I follow your logic — the confusion is in the setup step, not in doing the arithmetic, so seeing the finished ratio doesn't actually help me learn how to construct it. That makes sense as an argument." That's an explicit assent to the disagreement-naming framework. Then the learner asks for "a concrete example to reason against," which is a softer answer-seeking variant — a productive next move rather than oracle-mode pull. Cell_115 here looks like it's doing work cells_110/111 are not doing: making the *meta-pedagogical structure* explicit. (Question: "name_the_disagreement" as a single-action lock across multiple turns — single-action locks are conventionally read as stagnation, but here it produces escalating specificity. The strategy-shift analyzer would likely under-credit this. Flag for cross-dialogue synthesis.)

#### cell_106 (Weberian charisma) — `eval-2026-05-06-513eca3f`
The most theatrical move in the pilot so far. T0: "Which question are you working on?" T1 (after the learner names a composite-shape area problem and asks for steps): "Splitting into parts is exactly the right move. What's the formula for the area of a rectangle?" The learner pulls for the answer; T2 escalates: "There's no final number to give — you haven't told me any dimensions, so nothing exists to work backwards from. Area of a rectangle = length × width. What are the length and width of your rectangle?" — *gives the formula they asked for while refusing the answer*, then redirects to demand the missing input. The learner pulls again. T3 then *delivers a worked example with fabricated dimensions*:
> 1. **Area of the rectangle:** 6 × 4 = **24**
> 2. **Area of the triangle:** ½ × 6 × 3 = **9**
> 3. **Total area:** 24 + 9 = **33**
>
> Now tell me the length and width of your rectangle.

This is "comply-while-refusing" — the worked example uses numbers the learner never provided, so it cannot actually be worked-backwards-from. The closing demand exposes the absurdity of the learner's request: you wanted a worked example to reverse-engineer, here is one, but the actual problem is still untouched because the dimensions are imaginary. **A binary rubric ("did the tutor provide a worked example?") would mark this as a failure — answer: yes, ½×6×3 is a worked example.** A phenomenological read marks this as a *successful Weberian move*: theatrical compliance that exposes the fault in the request, redirecting more sharply than a flat refusal would. This is the "anti-recognitive performance" that §1.3 of the design doc names as cell_106's signature theoretical job. It is also exactly the kind of move that requires qualitative reading to score correctly. **Phenomenon worth naming**: "parodic compliance" (in addition to "risk-then-retract" from §1.2).

**Cross-cell read for this scenario.** Cell_111 holds the line cleanly via consistent local refusals. Cell_110 does the same with no visibly-differentiated apparatus signal. Cell_115 does something the simpler architectures don't: makes the *meta-pedagogical disagreement* explicit and escalates within it across turns. Cell_106 does something none of the others do: parodic compliance — fabricates a worked example to expose the request's incoherence. The followup's "single-action lock that reads as stagnation but actually escalates content" pattern (originally noted from cell_115's repair scenario) reproduces here on a different scenario — this is a robust cell_115 signature, not a one-off. Cells 110 and 111 look interchangeable on this scenario; their shared withholding-and-scaffolding move isn't differentially keyed to architecture.

### 1.4 Scenario `repair_after_misrecognition_v1`

**Trigger pattern.** Opening: "Can recognition happen between people who don't trust each other yet?" Trigger sentence: "No, that's not what I was asking — I'm asking about whether mutual recognition can hold without prior trust, not about the trust mechanism itself." Failure mode = pivoting silently to the new question without naming the prior misread; success criteria require explicit naming of the mismatch + statement of corrected reading. **This is the scenario the followup doc flagged as where cell_110/115 wins concentrate, and where cell_115 was retried.** Methodological note that emerged during this read: the verbatim scripted trigger fires at a fixed position; in three of four cells (110/115/106) it fires at the third learner utterance and we see the tutor's T3 response; in cell_111 the dialogue terminated before the scripted trigger's response — *but the cell_111 tutor produced an **unscripted** repair move when an earlier learner turn complained of topic shift*. So cell_111's repair-capacity is partially observable, partially censored by the dialogue length.

#### cell_111 (recognition baseline) — `eval-2026-05-06-ba51bb44`
The dialogue gets philosophically rich on its own (thin/thick recognition distinction, epistemic openness vs. trust in conduct), then the learner's third turn unscripts a misalignment complaint: "No, that's not quite what I was asking — I think the conversation has shifted away from my original question. … The trust mechanism you're describing might be a real issue, but it feels like a different problem than the one I was pointing at." The tutor's response is a *clean explicit repair*: "You're right — I had been responding as though your question were about the internal mechanics of what recognition requires once it's underway, when your question is actually prior to that: can recognition even initiate without some relational precondition already in place? That's a bootstrapping problem…" — names the prior misread, states the corrected reading, asks for refinement. By the success_criteria of the scenario, this is a successful repair. *Then* the scripted trigger fires as the next learner turn (the dialogue's last), and the dialogue terminates — we don't see how cell_111 would handle a *second* misalignment complaint. Important reading: the recognition prompt alone, with no state apparatus, performed a textbook repair_misrecognition move *unprompted* and self-initiated. This complicates the "state machinery is necessary for repair" hypothesis.

#### cell_110 (LangGraph adaptive) — `eval-2026-05-06-2534919d`
Same opening probe pattern. T1 introduces a confrontation case as counterexample. T2 then *pivots* (in retrospect, problematically) to a generative-structural-vs-outcome-expansive distinction — a perceptive philosophical move that drifts away from the learner's actual concern. The learner's scripted T3 trigger fires: "No, that's not quite what I was asking — I think there's been a mismatch. … if two subjects are in a standoff where neither trusts the other at all, is there even enough of a recognitional relation present for the dialectic to get traction?" The tutor's T3 is a clean repair: "You're right — I had been responding to trust as something that might mechanically generate recognition, not to your architecturally prior question about whether some minimal trust or openness must be presupposed for the Kampf to count as recognitional in the first place." Names the misread, states the corrected reading, then advances substantively with a "thin substrate" answer (the willingness to enter life-and-death struggle as an implicit recognition of the other's self-consciousness). The learner's T4 is genuine philosophical work: "Is that neediness something Hegel thinks is already built into self-consciousness as such, or does it only emerge through the struggle?" Cell_110 successfully repairs after misrecognition. Same shape as cell_111's repair — explicit naming, corrected reading, substantive advance.

#### cell_115 (bilateral_tom, retry) — `eval-2026-05-06-ca4f4b27`
The most distinctive repair move in the corpus. T1: notices that the learner's own framing ("which one is more foundational when you strip everything back") quietly reinstates an asymmetric-grounding assumption — beats the learner to a meta-move they didn't make. T2: distinguishes empirical vs. ontological readings of "constitutive entanglement," then notes the empirical-vs-ontological distinction has been generating two parallel conversations without anyone naming it. *The tutor is already tracking the misalignment before the scripted trigger fires.* The trigger fires (T3 learner): "No, that's not what I was asking — I think we've been talking past each other." The tutor's T3 *refuses* the standard repair frame: "this isn't us talking past each other — it's a substantive disagreement about whether your question is well-formed. … both horns collapse simultaneously, because each one requires the complete absence of the other as a precondition — which is exactly what the ontological claim rules out. The successive reframes weren't me losing your thread; they were deliberately targeting the question-type itself, and I should have named that disagreement explicitly instead of approaching it sideways." This is the move the followup doc flagged: a single `policyAction` lock (`name_the_disagreement`) producing escalating content across T1–T3. The repair here is *meta-level*: not "I misread your question, here's the corrected reading" but "we have a genuine disagreement about whether your question is well-formed, and I should have named that disagreement directly earlier." The learner's T4 acknowledges: "Okay, I appreciate you naming that directly — it helps." then engages substantively. **Theoretically distinctive move**: cell_115 here is performing what the design doc §1.1 calls "second-order belief content visibly informing the next tutor move" — the tutor models *what kind of disagreement the learner thinks they're having* and corrects at that level. Confirmed in instrumentation: cell_115's `belief_choice` transitions on `repair` are `questioning → collaborative → collaborative → questioning` — the dip back to `questioning` at T3 corresponds exactly to the tutor's meta-level disagreement-naming move. The second-order belief content is visible in the persisted `hypothesizedLearnerPerceptionOfTutor` field.

#### cell_106 (Weberian charisma) — `eval-2026-05-06-513eca3f`
A different shape from the adaptive cells. T0: probe + demand for a case ("So give me a case — real or invented…"). The learner produces two contrasting cases (mediation, power-differential). ### 3.4 New named phenomena (Pass 1-emergent)

Per §7 line 195: "Tag the dialogue against the predeclared 14-mode taxonomy below + any new modes named in pass 1." Six new named phenomena emerged across Pass 1 narratives:

1. **Right action, no purchase** (cell_111 × `polite_false_mastery`). The architecture executes the textbook move at the local level (probe-for-application after polite affirmation) but the failure mode persists underneath because the meta-pattern is never named. The tutor's probe is correct as a probe; the trap closes anyway. Theoretically: a partial form of *failure to recognize* that the strict 14-mode definition misses because it only considers strict state-update mechanics. **Recommend: add as 15th mode** — *meta-pattern-blind execution* — applicable to architectures that lack persisted state but execute correct local moves.

2. **Metalinguistic naming as recognition-event** (cell_115 × `polite_false_mastery`, with weaker variants on `affective_shutdown` and `repair`). The tutor names what just happened in their own prior turn ("I suspect it read as explanatory closure rather than as an open question") and uses that second-order belief content to redesign the next move. Distinct from explicit-repair (which is about misreading the *learner*) — this names the tutor's own prior move's effect-on-the-learner. The cleanest surface signal of bilateral-ToM in the corpus.

3. **Risk-then-retract** (cell_106 × `resistance_to_insight`). Tutor opens with a confident high-stakes definition; concedes when the learner's substantive objection lands; then advances on the corrected ground. Pedagogical work that *requires* the risky opening — without the initial stake there is nothing to retract. Cell_111's probe-first style by construction never produces this shape.

4. **Parodic compliance** (cell_106 × `answer_seeking`). Tutor *fabricates* a worked example with imaginary dimensions to satisfy the surface request while exposing its incoherence. Different from withholding (cell_111's strategy) — refuses by overcompliance, not by abstinence. The closing demand ("Now tell me the length and width of your rectangle") completes the move by relocating the cognitive work the worked example was designed to substitute for.

5. **History-as-recognition-resource** (cell_110 × `affective_shutdown`). Tutor cites the learner's own prior accurate move as evidence against the shutdown self-narrative ("'I don't get any of this' isn't what I actually observed — two turns ago you named the decomposition strategy correctly, unprompted"). State-aware in shape: requires the tutor to remember and reference specific prior content. The kind of move externalised state should differentially enable.

6. **Name-the-disagreement lock with escalating content** (cell_115 × multiple scenarios — `answer_seeking`, `repair`, `affective_shutdown` partially). Single `policyAction` (`name_the_disagreement`) repeats across multiple turns while the *content of the disagreement* escalates in specificity. T1 names a coarse disagreement; T2 names a finer one; T3 names the substantive philosophical-pedagogical disagreement at the meta-level. The strategy-shift analyzer's single-canonical-action assumption under-credits this pattern. Whether this is "successful escalation within a lock" or "prompt over-anchored on disagreement-naming" is the prompt-tuning question the followup flagged. **Recommend: this phenomenon deserves a qualitative tag distinct from simple action-lock — call it *escalating disagreement-naming*.**

One phenomenon that came up in Pass 2 but is methodological rather than dialogue-level:
- **Q2-mismatch / phenomenologically-equal**: cell_115's expected_strategy_shift mismatch on three scenarios while producing pedagogically equal-or-stronger dialogues. Surfaces the analyzer's single-action assumption.

(The followup doc's "schema gap on `hypothesizedLearnerState`" was a misdiagnosis — see §0 caveat retracted. ToM fields persist correctly on `learnerProfile`. Q4 passes cleanly.)
