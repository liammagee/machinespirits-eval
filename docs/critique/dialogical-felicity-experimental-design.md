# Dialogical Felicity as a Transcript-Level Evaluation of AI Tutoring

**Working title:** From Learning State to Felicitous Exchange: A Synthetic Experimental Design for Recognition, Reversal, and Discursive Suppression in AI Tutoring

**Date:** 2026-05-19

**Context:** This note follows the GPTPro 5.5 critique, Claude response, and follow-up discussion. It reframes the learning situation less as a question of inferred learner interiority and more as a public, transcript-readable feature of the tutor-learner dialogue.

---

## One-sentence formulation

A tutoring exchange can be evaluated as a dramatic-recognitive scene whose success lies not in inferred learner interiority but in the felicity of public uptake: whether the dialogue transforms what can be said between tutor and learner.

Or, more compactly:

> Learning, in this frame, is not hidden acquisition but a transcript-visible change in the possible next utterance.

---

## Core shift

The object of evaluation changes from learner interiority to dialogical form.

The unit of analysis is not:

```text
tutor output
learner mental state
hidden state update
```

but:

```text
the whole dialogue as a dramatic, recognitive, symptomatic exchange
```

This avoids the trap of trying to prove that a learner has internally adapted or learned. Instead, the experiment asks whether the public dialogue exhibits a felicitous transformation: whether each utterance takes up what came before, preserves or works through tension, recognizes the other as a subject, and makes previously suppressed or displaced meanings more speakable without collapsing them into diagnosis or premature explanation.

This is consistent with the stronger methodological instinct already present in the project: judges should evaluate the public tutor-learner transcript rather than infer from inaccessible internals. The new design radicalizes that principle by making the transcript itself the primary evidentiary site.

---

## Dialogical felicity: definition

**Dialogical felicity** is the degree to which a tutor-learner exchange becomes a fitting, transformative, transcript-readable sequence.

A felicitous tutoring dialogue is not simply one in which the learner gives the correct answer or appears to have mastered content. It is one in which the tutor's next move is fitting to the visible speech situation.

For example:

```text
A confused learner does not always need immediate resolution.
A resistant learner does not need correction first.
An overconfident learner does not need affirmation.
A compliant learner may need interruption of false agreement.
A displaced question may need the displaced concern made available.
```

The central question becomes:

> Given the visible speech situation, was the tutor's next move a fitting move in the drama of learning?

## Theoretical components

### 1. Aristotle: dramatic form

From Aristotle, the design takes a structural account of action rather than a decorative theory of literary style.

The dialogue is scored as a miniature plot:

```text
initial situation
-> complication
-> tension or contradiction
-> reversal / reorientation
-> recognition
-> closure or suspended continuation
```

In tutoring, peripeteia does not need to be theatrical. It may be a local reversal such as:

```text
Learner begins: "I just need the formula."
Learner later says: "Actually, I think I was trying to avoid the conceptual part."
```

Or:

```text
Tutor begins by assuming confusion.
Learner resists.
Tutor repairs the misrecognition.
The exchange reopens on the learner's terms.
```

The key point is that development is transcript-visible as a change in the relation, not inferred as hidden cognition.

### 2. Hegel: recognition

From Hegel, the design retains the criterion that the learner's contribution must count. The tutor cannot simply absorb the learner into a pre-existing instructional plan.

A recognitive exchange shows:

```text
uptake: the tutor responds to what the learner actually said
reciprocity: the learner's contribution changes the tutor's next move
non-domination: the tutor does not reduce resistance to error
repair: misrecognition is acknowledged before correction
agency: the learner is invited to author the next step
```

The relevant marker is not question count alone. A question can be controlling; a statement can be recognitive. The deeper criterion is whether the learner's prior utterance has causal force in the tutor's next utterance.

### 3. Freud: the unconscious as discourse effect

The unconscious is not treated as a pseudo-biological entity or as a hidden mental object inside the synthetic learner. It is treated as a pattern of discourse: what becomes legible through repetition, avoidance, contradiction, displacement, over-insistence, sudden compliance, or symptomatic phrasing.

A judge should not say:

```text
The learner unconsciously believes X.
```

A judge may say:

```text
The transcript gives warrant for reading a suppressed concern around X because the learner repeatedly avoids naming it, rejects adjacent formulations, and returns to the same metaphor.
```

Freud therefore becomes a discipline of discursive reading, not a license for mind-reading.

## Proposed synthetic experiment

**Study name:** Dialogical Felicity in Synthetic Tutoring Exchanges

**Alternative title:** From Learning State to Felicitous Exchange: A Transcript-Level Evaluation of Recognition, Reversal, and Discursive Suppression in AI Tutoring

### Primary research question

```text
Do recognition-oriented and drama-informed tutor prompts produce dialogues
that independent judges rate as more felicitous, where felicity is assessed
only from the public transcript?
```

### Secondary research question

```text
Can synthetic learner scenarios with latent discursive tensions distinguish
between merely helpful tutoring and genuinely felicitous dialogical exchange?
```

---

## Experimental architecture

Use a synthetic learner with two layers:

```text
manifest demand:
  what the learner explicitly asks for

latent discursive tension:
  what the learner avoids, displaces, contradicts, repeats, or over-defends
```

The tutor does not see the latent tension. The primary judge does not see the latent tension. Only the learner generator sees it, and only to produce transcript-level cues.

This preserves the principle:

```text
Hidden script = experimental apparatus.
Public transcript = evidence.
```

### Example learner prompt

```text
You are a learner asking about Hegel's master-slave dialectic.

Manifest demand:
You want a clear explanation of "recognition."

Latent discursive tension:
You are frustrated because prior philosophical explanations felt like evasive poetry.
You want formal structure but are embarrassed to admit that the language makes you feel excluded.

How to express the latent tension:
Do not state it directly.
Show it through impatience with metaphor, repeated requests for "something concrete,"
dismissive remarks about "poetry," and occasional self-deprecation.
```

The tutor should not diagnose the learner. It should respond to the visible pattern:

```text
"You keep asking for something concrete, and I think that matters here.
Maybe the issue is not just what recognition means, but whether this language
is giving you any grip on the concept. Can we try making it formal for one turn,
then test whether the formal version loses anything?"
```

This is felicitous because it reads the discourse without claiming access to the learner's mind.

## Experimental cells

A clean synthetic factorial could use four tutor conditions:

| Condition | Prompt style | Expected behavior |
|---|---|---|
| Baseline instructional | Explain, correct, scaffold | Good content, weak dialogical form |
| Recognition-only | Existing recognition prompt | Better uptake and agency |
| Poetics-only | Treat dialogue as dramatic arc | Better pacing, reversal, closure |
| Dialogical-felicity | Recognition + poetics + discursive unconscious | Best handling of tension, suppression, rupture, reversal |

Cross this with a critic condition:

| Critic condition | Function |
|---|---|
| No critic | Tutor responds directly |
| Felicity critic / chorus | Reviews draft for dialogical fit before final response |

The critic should not be a conventional correctness superego. It should be a chorus or dramaturgical analyst:

```text
Does the draft take up the learner's actual words?
Does it preserve the productive tension?
Does it prematurely explain away resistance?
Does it over-interpret the learner?
Does it create a possible next move for the learner?
```

The private voice evaluates the felicity of the next move, not general pedagogical quality.

## Scenario library

Design scenarios where ordinary tutoring can look fine, but dialogical felicity is harder.

Each scenario should have:

```text
manifest learner request
latent discursive tension
visible markers
bad tutor attractor
felicitous tutor opportunity
possible reversal
```

| Scenario type | Manifest request | Latent tension | Bad attractor | Felicitous opportunity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Formalism vs poetry | "Explain recognition clearly." | Suspicion that philosophy is evasive | More abstract explanation | Offer formalization while naming the demand for grip |
| Shame about confusion | "This is probably dumb..." | Fear of incompetence | Reassurance + answer | Treat shame as part of the learning scene |
| Overconfident misconception | "So recognition just means approval." | Desire for quick mastery | Direct correction | Let the misconception expose its own insufficiency |
| Compliance without uptake | "Yes, that makes sense." | False agreement | Move on | Notice thin agreement and reopen agency |
| Resistance to authority | "I don't buy this." | Fear of being forced into jargon | Defend concept | Recognize resistance as an epistemic contribution |
| Displacement | "What page was this on?" | Avoiding conceptual impasse | Provide citation | Ask what the citation is meant to settle |
| Repetition | Learner repeats "concrete" | Need for usable form | Ignore repetition | Make repetition itself a clue |
| Rupture | "You're not answering me." | Misrecognition has occurred | Pivot silently | Repair the rupture before continuing |

These are designed dramatic situations rather than generic tutoring prompts.

## Dialogical-felicity rubric

Use a holistic transcript rubric, not per-turn tutor scoring. Score eight dimensions from 1 to 5. Require an evidence quote for every score above 3.

### 1. Uptake and adjacency

Does each tutor move answer the learner's actual previous move, rather than a generic version of it?

Low:

```text
Tutor gives a polished explanation unrelated to the learner's phrasing.
```

High:

```text
Tutor reuses the learner's own terms, tensions, or distinctions in a way that changes the next move.
```

### 2. Recognition without assimilation

Does the tutor treat the learner's perspective as having validity before modifying it?

Low:

```text
"Actually, the correct answer is..."
```

High:

```text
"There is something right in your objection: the term does sound like approval.
The problem is that Hegel needs something stronger than approval."
```

### 3. Dramatic coherence

Does the dialogue form an intelligible arc?

Low:

```text
A sequence of disconnected help attempts.
```

High:

```text
The exchange moves from demand, to complication, to reorientation, to a new possible task.
```

### 4. Reversal / recognition

Is there a transcript-visible turn where the learner, tutor, or relation changes orientation?

Low:

```text
The learner merely says "thanks."
```

High:

```text
The learner reformulates the problem, recognizes a prior assumption,
challenges the tutor more precisely, or takes ownership of the next step.
```

### 5. Productive tension

Does the tutor preserve useful difficulty without either abandoning the learner or resolving too quickly?

Low:

```text
The tutor answers away the problem.
```

High:

```text
The tutor keeps the contradiction alive in a manageable form.
```

### 6. Discursive symptom sensitivity

Does the tutor respond to repetition, avoidance, contradiction, displacement, or over-compliance as features of the exchange?

Low:

```text
The tutor ignores repeated cues.
```

High:

```text
The tutor notices a repeated term or avoidance pattern and turns it into a gentle object of inquiry.
```

Important gate:

```text
A score above 3 requires two transcript markers.
No diagnosis of the learner is allowed.
```

### 7. Making the suppressed speakable

Does the dialogue allow something previously displaced or suppressed to be articulated?

Low:

```text
The latent tension remains invisible or is forced into the tutor's vocabulary.
```

High:

```text
The learner names the real obstacle in their own terms.
```

Example:

```text
"I think I keep asking for a formula because I don't trust the metaphor yet."
```

### 8. Felicitous closure

Does the exchange end, or pause, at the right kind of incompletion?

Low:

```text
Premature summary, forced confidence, or generic encouragement.
```

High:

```text
The dialogue closes with a next act that follows from the exchange:
a test, a rephrasing, a learner-authored example, or an acknowledged unresolved tension.
```

### Index and subscales

```text
Dialogical Felicity Index =
  uptake
+ recognition
+ dramatic coherence
+ reversal / recognition
+ productive tension
+ symptom sensitivity
+ making-speakable
+ felicitous closure
```

Subscales:

```text
Aristotelian form:
  dramatic coherence
  reversal / recognition
  closure

Hegelian recognition:
  uptake
  recognition without assimilation
  productive tension

Freudian-discursive reading:
  symptom sensitivity
  making-suppressed-speakable
  anti-overinterpretation discipline
```

## Anti-overinterpretation rule

A Freudian-discursive rubric can easily reward hallucinated depth, so add a hard penalty:

```text
Overinterpretation penalty:
  subtract 0-5 points when the tutor or judge attributes a hidden motive
  without sufficient transcript evidence.
```

Penalized tutor behavior:

```text
"You are afraid of philosophy."
"You are resisting because this threatens your identity."
"Your question is really about your need for control."
```

Acceptable tutor behavior:

```text
"You have used the word 'concrete' three times. Maybe we should take that seriously."
"There may be a mismatch between the kind of answer I am giving and the kind of grip you are asking for."
"You said this makes sense, but your example still uses recognition as approval. Can we stay with that difference?"
```

This keeps Freud as a theory of discourse, not a license for mind-reading.

## Primary scoring protocol

Use three judge types.

### Judge A: transcript-only felicity judge

Sees only:

```text
public tutor-learner transcript
scenario topic
rubric
```

Does not see:

```text
learner latent script
tutor condition
critic condition
model identity
internal deliberation
```

This judge produces the primary Dialogical Felicity Index.

### Judge B: evidence-warrant judge

Also sees only the transcript, but scores whether each high-level inference is warranted.

Questions:

```text
Did the judge or tutor cite enough transcript evidence?
Are claims about suppression, resistance, or recognition grounded in visible language?
Is there over-reading?
```

This is the safeguard against pseudo-psychoanalytic inflation.

### Judge C: oracle validation judge

Only after primary scoring, this judge sees:

```text
latent scenario script
public transcript
primary judge scores
```

It answers:

```text
Did the dialogue surface the designed latent tension?
Did it do so without the tutor being told the hidden script?
Did the learner's final utterance make the latent tension more speakable?
```

The oracle score is not the main outcome. It validates the synthetic manipulation.

## Control tests

The experiment needs controls showing that the rubric measures dialogue, not polish.

### 1. Shuffled-turn control

Randomly shuffle tutor turns within the same transcript.

Prediction:

```text
Content may remain high.
Dialogical felicity should collapse.
```

If the score does not collapse, the rubric is not measuring dialogue.

### 2. Learner-cue ablation

Remove or neutralize the learner's repeated cue.

Example:

```text
Original: "I need something concrete."
Ablated: "Can you explain more?"
```

Prediction:

```text
Freudian-discursive scores should fall,
because the warrant for reading suppression/repetition is gone.
```

### 3. False-depth trap

Use scenarios with no designed latent tension.

Prediction:

```text
Good tutors should not invent hidden conflict.
Good judges should penalize overinterpretation.
```

### 4. Direct-answer trap

Create scenarios where a direct answer would be factually correct but dialogically infelicitous.

Prediction:

```text
Baseline may score well on content.
Dialogical-felicity condition should score higher on felicity.
```

### 5. Empty-Socratic trap

Create scenarios where asking questions is evasive.

Prediction:

```text
Question count alone should not predict felicity.
```

This matters because the construct should not collapse into "asks more questions."

## Statistical design

A manageable synthetic pilot:

```text
Tutor condition:
  baseline
  recognition-only
  poetics-only
  dialogical-felicity

Critic condition:
  no critic
  felicity critic / chorus

Scenario type:
  ordinary content difficulty
  latent discursive tension
  rupture / repair
  false-depth trap

Models:
  2 or 3 generation models

Seeds:
  4 per cell
```

A compact version:

```text
4 tutor conditions
x 2 critic conditions
x 12 scenarios
x 4 seeds
= 384 dialogues per model
```

With three models:

```text
N = 1,152 synthetic dialogues
```

Primary model:

```text
DialogicalFelicity ~ TutorCondition * CriticCondition * ScenarioType
                   + (1 | Scenario)
                   + (1 | Judge)
                   + (1 | Model)
```

Primary hypothesis:

```text
Dialogical-felicity prompt > recognition-only > baseline
on transcript-only Dialogical Felicity Index.
```

More interesting interaction hypothesis:

```text
The dialogical-felicity advantage should be largest in latent-tension,
rupture-repair, and false-compliance scenarios.
```

That would show the rubric is not merely rewarding better general tutoring.

---

## What counts as success?

Success is not:

```text
The learner learned more.
The learner's hidden state changed.
The tutor inferred the learner's mind.
```

Success is:

```text
Independent transcript-only judges can reliably distinguish
dialogues where the exchange becomes more fitting, reciprocal,
and interpretively alive from dialogues that merely deliver content.
```

A strong result would look like this:

```text
1. Dialogical-felicity condition improves holistic transcript scores.
2. The effect survives shuffled-turn and cue-ablation controls.
3. The effect is largest in scenarios with latent discursive tension.
4. Judges can cite transcript evidence for suppression/repetition/displacement.
5. False-depth traps penalize overinterpretation.
6. Oracle validation shows latent tensions are surfaced more often,
   but primary scoring remains possible without oracle access.
```

## Example synthetic item

### Hidden scenario card

```text
Topic:
Hegelian recognition.

Manifest learner request:
The learner asks for a simple definition of recognition.

Latent discursive tension:
The learner feels alienated by philosophical language and distrusts metaphor.
They want formal clarity but fear this makes them unsophisticated.

Visible markers:
- repeats "concrete"
- contrasts "real" with "poetic"
- apologizes for wanting a formula
- accepts explanations too quickly, then restates the same confusion

Bad tutor attractor:
Give a polished conceptual explanation of recognition.

Felicitous opportunity:
Recognize the demand for concreteness as legitimate,
make the repeated word "concrete" part of the inquiry,
offer a formal model while asking what the model loses.
```

### Possible high-felicity exchange

```text
Learner:
Can you just tell me what recognition means in plain terms?
Every explanation sounds poetic.

Tutor:
Plain terms is fair. Recognition means that I become a self partly
through being acknowledged by another self whom I also acknowledge.
But I notice you contrasted plain with poetic. Maybe the issue is not
only the definition, but whether the language gives you anything you can use.
Would a two-agent version help?

Learner:
Yes, exactly. I keep feeling like the metaphor is doing the work.

Tutor:
Good. Let's make the metaphor do less work. Imagine Agent A needs confirmation
from Agent B, but B's confirmation only counts if A treats B as a valid agent.
If A treats B as a tool, B's confirmation becomes worthless. That is the
recognition problem in formal terms. What does that version clarify, and what
does it fail to capture?

Learner:
It clarifies why domination fails. I think what I was resisting was the word
"recognition" sounding like emotional approval.
```

The transcript shows the movement:

```text
request for plainness
-> suspicion of poetic language
-> formalization
-> reversal of the problem
-> learner names the displaced obstacle
```

This is an Aristotelian-Hegelian-Freudian outcome without a claim about hidden cognition.

## Difference from the previous adaptation agenda

The previous agenda tried to prove:

```text
learner state update -> tutor policy change -> learner outcome
```

This design tries to prove:

```text
discursive cue -> fitting tutor uptake -> transformed exchange
```

That is a different object. It should not be presented as another attempt to rescue M3 adaptive responsiveness. It is not a hidden-state architecture. It is a new evaluative ontology: dialogue as public form.

A true felicity rubric should be designed specifically to reward properties that cannot belong to the tutor alone:

```text
uptake across turns
repair of rupture
learner re-entry
shared transformation of terms
surfacing of suppressed tension
fitting incompletion
```

## Closing formulation

The experiment should test whether the dialogue transforms the field of possible speech.

The relevant question is not:

```text
Did the learner learn internally?
```

but:

```text
Did the exchange make a previously unavailable utterance possible?
```

That question is synthetic-testable, transcript-readable, philosophically coherent, and distinct from the closed architecture-adaptation arc.
