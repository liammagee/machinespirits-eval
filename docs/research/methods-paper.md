abstract: |
  LLM-tutor evaluation pipelines routinely report *mediator* findings: response-level features (question-asking, acknowledgement, scaffolding) are claimed to explain a portion of the score gap between conditions. Two methods-level assumptions underlie these claims and frequently fail in practice. **First**, that pooled-r between feature and score across conditions identifies within-condition mechanism. **Second**, that rubric dimensions are discriminantly valid such that per-dimension mediation claims are warranted. We articulate a **three-criterion within-cell mediator diagnostic** (large family contrast, small within-family contrast, positive within-cell Pearson r) and pair it with a **rubric over-specification check** based on principal-components analysis of dimension scores. We illustrate both corrections on a five-pass mechanism analysis from a published recognition-theoretic apparatus [@magee2026geist]: of seven candidate response-level features, only one (ending the tutor turn with a question) survives all three criteria; one (embedding-similarity to an intersubjective canonical) exhibits a textbook Simpson's paradox; the rubric's eight dimensions reduce empirically to one factor (PC1 = 80.7\%). Both findings are robust across three independent LLM judges (Sonnet 4.6, Opus 4.7, GPT-5.2), survive multivariate OLS control, and replicate across multiple runs and applications within the source apparatus, with a single-turn-vs-multi-turn scope condition that itself follows from the diagnostic's logic. We argue that any LLM-tutor evaluation reporting mediator-r should report within-cell as well as pooled correlations, and that any evaluation reporting per-dimension claims should report PCA dimensionality alongside rubric scores.

# 1. Introduction

LLM-as-judge evaluation of AI tutors has matured rapidly, and most published pipelines now report not only headline score gaps between conditions but also some account of the response-level *mechanisms* by which prompts and architectures alter score. The standard analytic move is mediator analysis: identify a candidate response-level feature ($f$, e.g., question-asking rate, acknowledgement markers, scaffolding moves), regress score on condition with and without $f$ as a covariate, and report the indirect-effect proportion. The reasoning is well-established in the broader social-science methodology literature [@baron1986moderator; @mackinnon2008introduction], and applies in principle to LLM-tutor data with no special accommodation.

Two assumptions, however, are routinely made in practice and routinely fail on LLM-tutor data:

1. **Pooled correlations identify within-condition mechanisms.** When a feature $f$ correlates with score across pooled data from multiple conditions, the pooled-$r$ is treated as evidence that $f$ predicts score *within* each condition. This assumption fails when conditions differ substantially in their distribution of $f$ and in their score levels, producing **Simpson's paradox** [@pearl2009causality] at the response level: the pooled correlation can be entirely driven by between-condition variance while the within-condition correlation is zero, or even reversed.

2. **Rubric dimensions are discriminantly valid.** When an LLM-as-judge rubric scores responses on $k$ dimensions, mediator analyses on individual dimensions assume the dimensions measure $k$ distinguishable constructs. This assumption fails when the dimensions are highly correlated and load on one or two empirical factors: per-dimension mediation claims then describe shifts in the underlying factor along whichever surface dimension the prompt happens to foreground, not movement of $k$ independent skills.

We articulate two methods-level corrections that address these assumptions directly. Section 2 specifies a **three-criterion within-cell mediator diagnostic** that requires positive within-condition Pearson r as a separate criterion from family-level contrast, ruling out Simpson's-paradox false positives. Section 3 illustrates the diagnostic on a five-pass mechanism analysis published in a recognition-theoretic apparatus [@magee2026geist]. Section 4 adds the **rubric over-specification check** based on principal-components analysis of dimension scores. Section 5 reports the diagnostic's behaviour across multiple runs, judges, generation models, and applications within the source apparatus, and surfaces a single-turn-vs-multi-turn scope condition that the diagnostic itself flags. Section 6 situates the contribution against related work in causal inference, mediation analysis, and rubric construct validity. Section 7 concludes with the practical recommendation: any LLM-tutor evaluation reporting mediator-r should report within-cell as well as pooled, and any evaluation reporting per-dimension claims should report PCA dimensionality alongside rubric scores.

# 2. The three-criterion within-cell mediator diagnostic

Consider a factorial design with two prompt families $A$ (recognition / intersubjective) and $B$ (transmission / behaviourist), and within each family two cells: $a_1$ and $a_2$ for family $A$, $b_1$ and $b_2$ for family $B$. The headline finding is a substantial score gap between $A$ and $B$ on aggregate. The methodological question is which response-level features mediate that gap.

A response-level feature $f$ qualifies as a within-cell mediator candidate when all three criteria are met:

1. **Large family contrast.** $|d_{AB}(f)| \geq 0.5$ between $A$ and $B$ on the feature itself. The feature distinguishes the families. If it does not, it cannot mediate the family-level effect.

2. **Small within-family contrast.** $|d_{a_1 a_2}(f)| < 0.5$ between two cells within the same family. Both family-member prompts elicit the feature; if only one does, the feature is a *prompt marker* (specific to one prompt) rather than a *family marker* (shared across the family), and any score correlation reflects prompt-specific properties not family-level mechanism.

3. **Positive within-cell Pearson r with score** in each family-member cell: $r_{a_1}(f, \text{score}) > 0$ and $r_{a_2}(f, \text{score}) > 0$. When the prompt is held constant, the feature still predicts higher scores. This isolates the response-level mediating role from cross-cell variance that the family contrast (criterion 1) already captures.

A feature passes the mediator bar only by satisfying all three criteria. Failure on any one criterion disqualifies the feature as a within-cell mediator while preserving its possible role as:

- A **family marker**: passes criteria 1 and 2 but fails criterion 3. The feature characterises the family but does not predict scores within it. The feature is downstream of the family-level treatment but not the channel through which the treatment operates.
- A **prompt marker**: passes criterion 1 but fails criterion 2. The feature distinguishes one specific prompt rather than the family; it is part of the prompt's surface composition, not the family's pragmatic stance.
- A **negative correlate**: criteria 1 and 2 may pass, but criterion 3 fails *with negative sign*. The feature appears more often in lower-scoring responses within cells. This pattern is informative when the feature is one a researcher might naively try to optimise for: explicit acknowledgement markers, for instance, are surrogate moves that responses fall back on when substantive engagement is thin.
- **Simpson's paradox disqualified**: criterion 1 passes (large family contrast); criterion 3 fails (negative within-cell r in both family $A$ cells); pooled $r$ across all four cells is positive. The pooled positive correlation is driven entirely by between-family variance — typically by an outlier cell whose feature mean and score mean both lie at one extreme. The diagnostic flags this case explicitly because pooled-r alone would have presented the feature as a strong mediator candidate.

The third disqualification mode is what makes the diagnostic methodologically substantive. Without criterion 3, a feature exhibiting large family contrast and significant pooled-$r$ — the most common combination in published mediator analyses — would pass through as a confirmed mediator. Pearl's [-@pearl2009causality] formalisation of Simpson's paradox shows that pooled correlations *cannot* identify within-stratum effects when the strata differ on the predictor; the third criterion enforces the stratum-level check that the formalisation requires.

# 3. Worked example: a five-pass mechanism analysis

We illustrate the diagnostic on the five-pass response-level mechanism analysis reported in Magee [-@magee2026geist §7.10, §7.10.1, §8.6.1]. The source corpus is a four-cell matched-specificity comparison: cell 1 (generic baseline), cell 5 (recognition-theoretic prompt with explicit Hegelian vocabulary), cell 95 (matched-pedagogical prompt grounded in Vygotsky/Piaget/Kapur/Chi/VanLehn/Graesser, no Hegelian vocabulary), and cell 96 (matched-behaviorist prompt grounded in Skinner/Gagné/Keller/Thorndike/Rosenshine, no recognition or constructivist vocabulary). Cells 5 and 95 sit in the **intersubjective family**; cells 1 and 96 in the **transmission family**. The headline factorial finding [@magee2026geist §7.9] is a between-family pooled Cohen's $d \approx 1.38$, with within-Hegelian-family pooled $d = 0.15$ — recognition and matched-pedagogical are statistically indistinguishable, while both substantially outperform the transmission cells.

The five-pass sequence asked: which response-level feature, if any, mediates the between-family effect? Each pass added a new feature class:

- **Pass 1**: theory-driven Hegelian recognition lexicon (recognition / mutuality / autonomy / dialectic / transformation / intersubjectivity / struggle / repair / hegel / genuine).
- **Pass 2**: Vygotskian/constructivist intersubjective lexicon (scaffolding / construct / sense-making / productive struggle / dialogic / inquiry).
- **Pass 3**: regex-based pragmatic features (question-mark rate, second-person density, ends-with-question, acknowledgement markers, epistemic hedges).
- **Pass 4**: refined regex (indirect questions, scaffolding-move imperatives, inclusive framing, modal invitations, broad acknowledgement, broad hedge).
- **Pass 5**: embedding-based similarity to hand-authored intersubjective and transmission canonicals (OpenAI text-embedding-3-small).

We apply the three-criterion diagnostic to seven representative features from this sequence. The Sonnet 4.6 judge produces the within-cell Pearson $r$ values reported in Table 1; cells 5 and 95 each carry $n \approx 50$ rows.

| Feature | Crit 1 (family d) | Crit 2 (within-family d) | Crit 3 (within-cell r in cell_5 / cell_95) | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hegelian lexicon density | small | very large (cell_5 $\gg$ cell_95) | small | Prompt marker only |
| Intersubjective lexicon density | moderate | very large (cell_95 $\gg$ cell_5) | mixed signs across cells | Prompt marker only |
| Question-mark rate | 0.61 | 0.47 (cell_5 $3\times$ cell_95) | small | Prompt marker, modest predictor |
| Scaffolding-move imperatives | 0.59 | 0.12 | $+0.19$ / $+0.05$ | Family marker, weak mediator |
| Broad acknowledgement | 0.14 | small | $-0.18$ / $-0.31$ | Negative correlate |
| **Ends-with-question** | **categorical** (4.5\% / 2.1\% vs 0\%) | **0.13** | **$+0.325$ / $+0.392$** | **Mediator** |
| Intersub. canonical similarity | **0.81** | 0.27 | **$-0.28$ / $-0.24$** (negative within-cell, positive pooled) | Family marker; **Simpson's paradox** disqualifies as mediator |

**Reading the table.** The two lexicon passes reveal the importance of criterion 2: cell 5 uses Hegelian vocabulary moderately while cell 95 actively suppresses it (a within-family $d \approx 1.0$); conversely cell 95 is hyper-dense in Vygotskian intersubjective vocabulary while cell 5 has only trace amounts (within-family $d \approx 3$). Both lexicons are *prompt markers*, not family markers — they characterise specific prompts within the family rather than what the family shares. Criterion 2 catches this; pooled-r alone would have presented either lexicon as a mediator if criterion 1 were satisfied.

**Scaffolding-move imperatives** ("Try ...", "Notice ...", "Consider ...") are the cleanest family marker in the sequence: family $d = 0.59$, within-family $d = 0.12$ (both intersubjective cells produce them at similar rates; transmission cells do not). Within-cell r is modestly positive in cell 5 (0.19) and near zero in cell 95 (0.05); the feature is *characteristic* of the intersubjective family but only weakly mediating.

**Broad acknowledgement** (quoted text, "your X" possessives, paraphrase markers) is a *negative correlate*: within-cell r is $-0.18$ in cell 5, $-0.31$ in cell 95, $-0.38$ in cell 1. Verbose surface-level acknowledgement appears more often in lower-scoring responses — surrogate moves that responses fall back on when substantive engagement is thin. The rubric is not fooled by surface empathy. This finding is methodologically important for the diagnostic because it shows that the third criterion's *direction* matters: a negative within-cell r is informative, not just disqualifying.

**Ends-with-question** is the only feature in the sequence that satisfies all three criteria. Family contrast is categorical: transmission cells produce ends-with-question in 0\% of responses; intersubjective cells in 4.5\% (cell 5) and 2.1\% (cell 95). Within-family $d = 0.13$ is small. Within-cell Pearson $r = +0.325$ (cell 5) and $+0.392$ (cell 95). When the prompt is held constant and a response happens to end with a question, that response scores higher. Pragmatically, this is the act of *ceding initiative back to the learner*; the within-cell correlation captures situations where doing so is contextually appropriate.

**Intersubjective canonical similarity** is the diagnostic's most important illustration. The embedding-derived feature exhibits a textbook Simpson's paradox: pooled $r = +0.26$ across the four cells, but within each intersubjective cell the correlation reverses sign ($r = -0.28$ in cell 5, $r = -0.24$ in cell 95). The pooled positive correlation is driven entirely by the cell 96 outlier (transmission cell with both very low embedding-similarity *and* very low rubric scores) anchoring the lower-left of the pooled scatter. Without criterion 3, the feature would have passed as a strong mediator; with criterion 3 it is correctly identified as a family marker — embedding-similarity to a generic intersubjective canonical captures family-level pragmatic *form* (turn-taking, scaffolding, inclusive framing) but not response-level *substance*. Responses that match the canonical too closely sound formulaic; the rubric rewards substantive engagement, which the canonical similarity does not measure.

# 4. Rubric over-specification: a complementary caution

The diagnostic addresses pooled-vs-within-cell mediator inference but presupposes that the rubric's per-dimension scores are themselves meaningful as separate constructs. This presupposition is independently testable and frequently fails. Magee [-@magee2026geist §8.6] reports principal-components analysis on 1,584 per-turn observations under an eight-dimension v2.2 LLM-tutor rubric: PC1 explains **80.7\%** of variance; the Kaiser criterion yields exactly one factor with eigenvalue $> 1$; sampling adequacy is excellent (KMO = 0.938); the mean inter-dimension correlation is $r = 0.776$ (range 0.589–0.921). The pattern replicates across conditions (PC1 $= 80.2\%$ base, $75.6\%$ recognition) and across generation models (PC1 $= 77.3\%$ DeepSeek, $68.0\%$ Haiku). A forced two-factor varimax rotation separates `content_accuracy` (loading 0.923 on Factor 2) from the seven pedagogical dimensions (loadings 0.68–0.85 on Factor 1).

The eight dimensions, in other words, measure essentially **one underlying construct** plus a separable content-accuracy modifier. Per-dimension mediation claims under such a rubric describe shifts in the single underlying factor along whichever surface dimension the prompt's wording happens to foreground; they do not describe selective movement of $k$ independent skills.

This finding pairs directly with the within-cell mediator diagnostic. Consider a published mediator analysis that reports: "the recognition prompt improves `adaptive_responsiveness` ($d = 0.6$) without affecting `clarity_structure` ($d = 0.1$); therefore recognition selectively engages adaptive responsiveness." Under PC1 = 80.7\%, this claim is weakly supported at best: the apparent dimension-specific differential could reflect the prompt's wording foregrounding one facet of an underlying construct that *did* shift overall, with the other dimension's null result reflecting weighting of the prompt against that facet rather than insensitivity. The honest reading is "recognition shifts overall pedagogical quality with prompt-specific dimensional emphasis," not "recognition selectively engages adaptive responsiveness."

We therefore propose a **rubric over-specification check** as a methods-level companion to the diagnostic: any LLM-tutor evaluation reporting per-dimension findings should also report (a) the dimensionality of its rubric under PCA on a representative sample of scores, (b) the inter-dimension correlation matrix, and (c) the proportion of variance explained by PC1. When PC1 explains $\geq 70\%$ of variance, per-dimension claims should be hedged accordingly; per-dimension *mediation* claims should be retracted unless the dimensions are demonstrated to be discriminantly valid on the specific dataset under analysis.

The pairing has two effects. First, the within-cell diagnostic protects against Simpson's-paradox false positives; the rubric check protects against per-dimension construct-validity false positives. Second, both checks are computationally inexpensive: the within-cell diagnostic requires only the per-cell correlation matrix; the rubric check requires only PCA on dimension scores. Neither requires re-running the underlying generation or judging.

# 5. Application across runs and conditions

The diagnostic was developed against a single corpus (the four-cell A10b matched-specificity comparison) and one judge (Sonnet 4.6). To assess its stability and to surface scope conditions, we apply it to additional runs, judges, and applications within the same recognition-theoretic apparatus [@magee2026geist §7.10.1].

**Cross-judge replication.** The corpus was scored by three independent judges (Sonnet 4.6, Opus 4.7, GPT-5.2). The ends-with-question within-cell $r$ remains positive in 5 of 6 cell-judge combinations (Sonnet cell_5 $r = +0.325$, cell_95 $r = +0.392$; Opus cell_5 $r = +0.189$, cell_95 $r = +0.321$; GPT cell_5 $r = +0.116$, cell_95 $r = -0.011$). The single exception is essentially zero rather than a sign-flip; magnitudes vary but no judge produces a meaningfully negative within-cell correlation in either intersubjective cell. The Simpson's-paradox disqualification of intersub_advantage replicates in all three judges: pooled $r$ is positive (Sonnet $+0.26$, Opus $+0.36$, GPT $+0.14$) while within-cell $r$ is negative in cell 5 (Sonnet $-0.28$, Opus $-0.08$, GPT $-0.22$) and in cell 95 (Sonnet $-0.24$, Opus $-0.15$, GPT $-0.40$). Neither finding is judge-specific.

**Multivariate OLS control.** A six-predictor regression (ends-with-question, second-person density, scaffolding moves, broad acknowledgement, question-mark rate, intersub_advantage) was fitted per cell. The ends-with-question partial coefficient remains positive and significant in both intersubjective cells: cell 5 $\beta = 26.34$ (SE 10.85, $p = 0.02$); cell 95 $\beta = 38.52$ (SE 16.49, $p = 0.02$). The single-feature mediator interpretation is not collinearity-driven. The intersub_advantage Simpson's-paradox direction also survives multivariate control.

**Cross-run, cross-application replication.** The diagnostic was applied to five additional runs spanning independent philosophy generations (DeepSeek), a different domain (programming, Haiku ego), a different application (peer-support listener coaching, Haiku ego), and multi-turn messages-mode dialogues (DeepSeek). Within-cell ends-with-question $r$ in single-turn intersubjective cells:

- A10 v2 cell 5: $r = +0.346$
- A6 programming cell 5: $r = +0.202$
- D2 peer-support coaching cell 5: $r = +0.739$ (the strongest within-cell $r$ in the sequence)

In multi-turn cells the relationship reverses sign:

- Paper 2.0 cell 84 (messages-mode, single-agent recog): $r = -0.301$
- Paper 2.0 cell 86 (messages-mode, multi-agent recog): $r = -0.203$
- A2 cell 61 (dialectical multi-agent recog with dynamic learner): $r = -0.087$
- A2 cell 63 (dialectical multi-agent recog, bidirectional profiling, dynamic learner): $r = -0.199$

The single-turn-vs-multi-turn split has a clean pragmatic interpretation. In single-turn settings, ending the response with a question is the only available channel to cede initiative back to the learner; doing so is rewarded. In multi-turn settings, the rubric scores the full dialogue arc; ending the *final* turn with a question leaves the conversation unresolved, and the judges penalise it. The same surface feature signals different pragmatic acts in different discourse contexts. The diagnostic's logic itself produces this scope condition: criterion 3 is checked within-cell, and the same feature can meet criterion 3 in one set of cells (single-turn) and fail it in another (multi-turn). The mediator interpretation is correctly hedged to its applicable scope.

**Generalisation beyond the source apparatus.** The application examples reported above all share the recognition-theoretic source corpus. The methods paper's stronger generality claim --- that the diagnostic transfers to other LLM-tutor evaluation pipelines using different rubrics, prompts, and judges --- requires application to independently published rubrics. Candidate datasets include the ICAP-aligned LLM-as-judge studies [@chi2014icap] and other published LLM-tutor evaluation pipelines that release per-row scores with condition labels. The protocol is straightforward: extract per-row condition labels, dimension scores, and a candidate response-level feature; compute the three criteria per cell + pooled; report the verdict. Application of the diagnostic to such independent corpora is the natural next step; we leave it as future work, noting that the source-apparatus replications above already demonstrate the diagnostic's stability across judges (3), generation models (3), domains (3), applications (2), turn modes (2), and architectures (3).

# 6. Related work

**Causal inference and mediation.** The within-cell mediator diagnostic operationalises Pearl's [-@pearl2009causality] formalisation of Simpson's paradox in the specific context of LLM-tutor evaluation: pooled correlations cannot identify within-stratum mechanism when strata differ on the predictor, and the third criterion enforces the stratum-level check the formalisation requires. The diagnostic is also adjacent to the broader mediation-analysis literature [@baron1986moderator; @mackinnon2008introduction; @imai2010general], which formalises the necessary conditions for a feature to mediate a treatment effect under causal-graph assumptions. Our contribution is restricted in scope: we do not claim causal mediation in the strict counterfactual sense, since the LLM-tutor data is observational rather than experimental at the within-cell level. We claim only that pooled-r alone is insufficient evidence of within-cell mechanism and that within-cell r is a necessary supplementary check.

**LLM-as-judge methodology.** Recent work has documented systematic biases in LLM-as-judge evaluation [@zheng2023judging; @gu2025surveyjudge; @liu2025discerning; @li2024llmsjudges] and proposed structured rubric-based methodologies [@hashemi2024llmrubric] for the LLM-tutor sub-domain specifically. Attention has focused primarily on judge-side artifacts (positional bias, length preferences, stylometric cues). The two corrections we propose are orthogonal to this literature: they address how mediator analyses are *interpreted* over judge scores, not how the judge scores themselves are produced. The corrections compose with judge-bias hedging rather than replacing it.

**Rubric construct validity in education.** PCA on rubric scores has a long tradition in educational measurement [@brookhart2013rubrics], where rubric over-specification is a known concern even for human raters. Our contribution is to import this routine check into the LLM-tutor evaluation pipeline, where it has been notably absent: of the LLM-tutor evaluation studies surveyed for this paper, none reported PCA on dimension scores prior to making per-dimension mediation claims. Treating per-dimension mediation as license to claim selective skill-targeting is widespread, despite the empirical regularity that LLM-as-judge rubric dimensions tend to load on one or two factors when measured.

**Behavioural-feature mediation in LLM evaluation.** A small body of work has applied behavioural-feature mediation specifically to LLM tutoring [@magee2026geist §6.1.7]: question-asking rate as a mediator of the recognition effect, with a Baron-Kenny test reporting 42\% direct mediation and 91\% on the holistic outcome. Our diagnostic does not conflict with this finding: the question-asking finding survives the three-criterion check on single-turn cells (criterion 1 is large, criterion 2 is small, criterion 3 is positive). The diagnostic's contribution is to flag when a similar mediation finding *does not* survive — most importantly, the embedding-similarity case in §3 — and to make the failure modes diagnosable rather than implicit.

# 7. Conclusion

LLM-tutor evaluation is rapidly accumulating mediator findings of the form "feature $f$ explains $\alpha$\% of the score gap between conditions $A$ and $B$." The empirical regularity that licenses such findings is rarely tested explicitly: that pooled correlations across conditions identify within-condition mechanism, and that rubric dimensions are independently scorable. We have argued that both regularities frequently fail on LLM-tutor data, and that two methods-level corrections — a three-criterion within-cell mediator diagnostic and a rubric over-specification check — should accompany any future mediator analysis at this scale.

The corrections are computationally inexpensive and impose no new data-collection burden. They reframe existing analyses rather than requiring re-runs: the within-cell diagnostic uses the same per-row data as pooled mediation; the rubric check uses the same dimension scores. They are most informative where they would change a published conclusion: a feature with significant pooled-r and large family contrast that *fails* the within-cell criterion is a candidate retraction, and a per-dimension claim under PC1 $\geq 70\%$ is a candidate hedge. Application of the diagnostic to one already-published mechanism analysis [@magee2026geist] revealed exactly this pattern: of seven candidate response-level features, only one survived all three criteria, and one (embedding-similarity to an intersubjective canonical) exhibited a textbook Simpson's paradox that pooled-r alone would have presented as a strong mediator.

We are not arguing that pooled-r mediator analyses should be abandoned; they are useful exploratory tools, and many published findings in the LLM-tutor evaluation literature will survive the within-cell check intact. We argue only that pooled-r should be *reported alongside* within-cell r, and that rubric-dimension claims should be *reported alongside* PCA dimensionality, so readers can evaluate the methodological strength of the inference. When a published claim does not survive these checks, the appropriate response is to retract or hedge, not to dispute the diagnostic. Both corrections operate in the spirit of methodological transparency rather than methodological scepticism.

The diagnostic and the rubric check together suggest a small revision to the standard LLM-tutor evaluation report: add a within-cell-r column to mediator tables, and add a "PCA dimensionality" line to the rubric description. With these two additions the field can begin to distinguish family markers from within-cell mediators, and per-dimension claims from underlying-construct claims, more reliably than is currently practice.
