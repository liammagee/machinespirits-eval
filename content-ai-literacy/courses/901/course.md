## Description

The four lectures in this course move from theory to method to practice. The first builds a historical lineage from Weber's analysis of charismatic authority through Benjamin's account of mechanical reproduction to the conditions of present-day algorithmic media — establishing why "artificial charisma" is the right name for what generative video models now do. The second teaches a vocabulary for decomposing a charismatic short-form video into its craft components: auditory dynamics, visual semiotics, character framing, content discourse. The third introduces a critical method drawn from recent empirical work — prompt-and-compare, in which a real artifact is reverse-engineered into a structured prompt, run through a generator, and audited against the original. The fourth surveys what current models reproduce well, what they fail at, and what literacy this distinctive failure profile demands of viewers, students, and citizens.

The course assumes no technical background in machine learning. It does assume willingness to look closely at media one might ordinarily scroll past, and to take seriously the proposition that the cultivation of attention has become an industrial process whose outputs are not innocent.
