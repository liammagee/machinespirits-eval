Research atlas synthesis · A19 planning draft

Teaching Dramas, Speech Axioms, and Post-Trained LLM Learning

A standalone research map for learning to modify tutor speech from felicities and infelicities in teaching dramas: drama/rhetoric ontology, tutor-as-learner, learner resistance as teacher, A18-style counterfactual policy transfer, and a Codex-ready implementation plan.

Source atlas: Geist in the Machine, v3.0.129 Prepared: June 7, 2026 Proposed next arc: A19 Drama-Axiom Transfer Status: design draft, not a deployment spec

1. Thesis

Adaptive tutoring should not be modeled primarily as generic turn-by-turn slope improvement, hidden learner-state inference, or global prompt improvement. The stronger unit is a counterfactual teaching-drama axiom: a typed, scope-bounded lesson about how the tutor should modify speech after a particular kind of learner resistance exposes an infelicity.

Working thesis: the tutor learns when a teaching drama yields a contrastive record: an infelicitous speech move, a diagnosis of why it failed, a repaired move, applicability conditions, and a held-out S0/S1 test showing that the policy-memory tutor avoids a decoy that the no-policy tutor would likely take.

The central research question can be made operational as follows:

How can a tutor learn reproducible, scope-bounded speech modifications
from public teaching dramas, where learner resistance functions as the
teacher's evidence signal?

2. Read of the atlas

Calibration

supported

Recognition/intersubjective framing lifts the floor and narrows the response distribution. It operates from the first turn and does not require the superego to be active.

Error correction

supported, substitutive

The superego helps when base output leaves error headroom. Under strong recognition calibration, its residual value collapses on stronger models and persists on weaker models.

Adaptive slope

not supported

The primary within-dialogue trajectory test is null. Tutors adapt across turns, but recognition and architecture do not selectively modulate the slope.

2.1 What the mechanism result now means

The atlas has moved beyond the earlier three-mechanism story. Its strongest general mechanism is recognition as calibration: the tutor becomes less generic and more consistently adequate. The second mechanism is error correction: critique catches uncalibrated failures, but its value is partly substituted away by recognition. The third candidate mechanism, condition-specific adaptive responsiveness, is a clean null in the well-powered primary analysis and in the pre-registered replication of its earlier exploratory boundary case.

MechanismArchitectural levelAtlas resultImplication for A19
Calibration Prompt / orientation Supported across models and judges; within-response variance drops and recognition is unanimous across judge × run cells. Use recognition as a floor-setting baseline, not as evidence of learning.
Error correction Architecture / critique Supported under base conditions; substitutive with calibration; residual value tracks post-calibration error headroom. Replace one generic superego with specialized critics that diagnose concrete speech infelicities.
Adaptive responsiveness Interaction / slope Not supported as a distinct condition-modulated mechanism. Stop measuring generic slopes as the main adaptation object; test policy transfer instead.

2.2 The A18 residue is the bridge

The A18 arc gives the strongest positive model of “tutor-as-learner.” A failed first attempt can be converted into a policy memory that transfers to held-out sibling cards, but only under headroom: the no-policy tutor must be likely to take a decoy shortcut, and the policy-memory tutor must select the registered target under a blind protocol.

A18 principle: “Does the policy help?” is a per-card property, not a per-family property. Axioms need applicability conditions, anti-conditions, and headroom evidence. Ceiling cases where S0 also succeeds are not policy failures; they are no-headroom cases.

2.3 The id-director/charisma branch is a substrate, not yet uptake

The id-director cells show that rhetoric and persona can be actively re-authored. Cell 104 is strongest on v2.2 recognition-grounded pedagogy, cell 105 wins charisma, and cell 107 is the best generalist. But the persona-shift lift is scenario-conditional: the architecture matters especially when the disclosure resists easy sympathy. This is valuable for rhetorical design, but it is not yet evidence that the learner has learned or that the tutor has acquired reusable axioms.

2.4 The hidden-interior arc should redirect ontology

The symbolic and hidden-trace nulls are not a reason to abandon ontology. They are a reason to formalize the public drama rather than an inferred learner interior. The ontology should track commitments, warrants, acts, ruptures, repairs, decoys, and uptake signals that are publicly enacted in dialogue.

3. Systematic literature map: post-trained LLM learning strategies

The relevant literature divides into eight families. The main lesson is that durable post-training is useful only after the project has defined the right learning unit. For this project, the unit should be the teaching-drama axiom, not a holistic quality score.

FamilyRepresentative workWhat it learnsUse for this projectRisk / gap
Post-training taxonomy Post-training surveys frame the field around fine-tuning, alignment, reasoning, efficiency, and integration/adaptation.[1] Task behavior, alignment, reasoning style, deployment efficiency. Use as the overall map, but do not select a weight-update method before the reward object is stable. Surveys are broad; they do not define drama-level pedagogical felicity.
Preference optimization DPO, KTO, ORPO, SimPO.[2] [3] [4] [5] Preference-aligned utterance distributions. Later: train on felicitous vs infelicitous speech pairs under the same drama state. Pairwise preference alone can reward politeness, verbosity, or sycophancy rather than pedagogical uptake.
Reasoning/process supervision Process reward modeling, o1-style reasoning RL, DeepSeek-R1.[6] [7] [8] Intermediate reasoning steps, verification behavior, self-reflection. Translate “step” into pedagogical-rhetorical acts: diagnose resistance, name warrant, avoid decoy, test uptake. More thinking is not the same as better teaching; process labels must be drama-specific.
Verbal RL and self-correction Reflexion, Self-Refine, self-correction surveys.[9] [10] [11] Natural-language reflections or refinements at test time. Closest analogue to A18: convert failure feedback into a reusable verbal policy memory. Intrinsic self-correction often fails without reliable external feedback; learner resistance must be structured into an evidence signal.
Agent memory and experience Voyager, ExpeL, MemoryAgentBench, ERL.[12] [13] [14] [15] Skills, insights, heuristics, retrieval competence, selective forgetting. Design axiom memory, retrieval, applicability gates, and forgetting/retirement criteria. Experience replay is fragile if retrieval ignores scope, anti-conditions, and no-headroom cases.
Prompt and pipeline optimization OPRO, DSPy, TextGrad, automatic prompt optimization surveys.[16] [17] [18] [19] Prompts, demonstrations, modules, compound-system variables. Optimize speech-edit operators after drama-state labels and axiom metrics exist. Universal prompt edits can harm cross-model transfer; the atlas already shows model-stratified behavior.
Self-adapting weights SEAL self-edits.[20] Model-generated fine-tuning data and update directives, trained by downstream reward. Advanced route: let the tutor propose self-edits once A19 produces many validated axiom episodes. Too early until the reward object is stable; expensive and harder to audit.
Education-specific LLM learning Tutor DPO, dialogue knowledge tracing, LLM agents for education surveys.[21] [22] [23] Student correctness likelihood, knowledge-component labels, tutoring-agent orchestration. Borrow candidate utterance generation, student-model scoring, and knowledge tracing, but replace correctness-only reward with dramatic felicity and uptake. Often optimizes for correctness or engagement, not resistance, rupture, repair, or speech modification.
Formal dialogue and rhetoric ISO dialogue acts, RST, commitment dialogue, abstract argumentation.[24] [25] [26] [27] Public acts, discourse relations, commitments, warrants, attack/support structure. Make drama/rhetoric ontology reproducible and inspectable. Can become brittle if used to infer hidden mental states rather than public commitments.
Evaluation and safety LLM-as-judge, judge-bias surveys, sycophancy and reward tampering work.[28] [29] [30] [31] Scalable judgement, known judge biases, preference-induced agreeableness risks. Use blind arbiters, basis labels, judge-bias critics, and human validation for the highest-value claims. Preference systems can reward pleasing the learner rather than teaching the learner.

Review conclusion: the project should not primarily add a new fine-tuning algorithm. It should add a reproducible evidence unit. Once validated teaching-drama axioms exist at scale, DPO/SimPO, process rewards, DSPy/TextGrad, and SEAL become plausible downstream experiments.

4. What we have overlooked

Applicability, not adaptation

The hard problem is not whether a tutor can change. It is when a stored lesson should apply. Every axiom needs preconditions, anti-preconditions, model scope, and headroom evidence.

Negative uptake as supervision

Learner resistance can be a label source: “the tutor treated X as Y, causing Z infelicity.” This is richer than a scalar score or a generic preference pair.

Critic specialization

A single superego is too coarse. The next architecture needs critics for drama type, warrant structure, pedagogy, rhetoric, headroom, and judge/artifact bias.

Public-state student modeling

The no-privileged-interior result suggests that learner modeling should track public evidence: claims, warrants, contradictions, repair attempts, and uptake signals.

Model-stratified policies

The same speech policy can help one model and overconstrain another. Axioms need different renderings for weak, mid, and strong generators.

Human uptake validation

Synthetic learners can self-solve, collapse, or be cue-leaked. The strongest future claims need human expert coding and eventually human learner trials.

5. Problem-space map

The overall problem has four nested learning loops. The current atlas has strong evidence at the production level and bounded evidence at the cross-dialogue policy-memory level. A19 should connect them.

LoopWhat learnsUpdate objectEvidence signalCurrent statusNext move
Utterance loop Tutor at the next turn Speech act / rhetorical move Resistance, confusion, uptake, rupture Generic slopes null Add drama ontology and process labels
Dialogue loop Tutor across one teaching drama Local strategy Rupture → repair → uptake Prompt-lab can create modulation Make modulation policy-explicit
Cross-dialogue loop Tutor-as-learner Typed axiom / policy memory A18-style S0/S1 held-out contrast Bounded positive Scale A18 protocol to teaching dramas
Post-training loop Model or compound pipeline Prompt, retriever, reward model, weights Validated preference/process/axiom corpus Not yet central Start only after A19 stabilizes the unit

6. Meaningful mechanisms needed

6.1 Drama/rhetoric ontology as public state

Formalize what can be observed: learner acts, tutor acts, commitments, warrants, repairs, decoys, and uptake. Do not formalize an inaccessible learner interior as the primary object.

public_state = {
  learner_move,
  learner_commitments,
  learner_warrants,
  tutor_speech_act,
  tutor_failure_risk,
  rupture_or_uptake,
  decoy_shortcut,
  repair_candidate
}

6.2 Axiom induction from contrastive dramas

Induce axioms only from contrast. A successful transcript alone is not evidence. A usable axiom requires an attempt-1 failure, a diagnosis, a repaired policy, and a held-out S0/S1 test.

policy_headroom = S1_target && S0_decoy
ceiling         = S1_target && S0_target
policy_failure  = !S1_target
artifact        = cue_leak || arbiter_disagreement

6.3 Critic ecology

  • Drama classifier: identifies the scene type and resistance type.
  • Argument critic: extracts claims, warrants, attacks, concessions, and missing distinctions.
  • Pedagogy critic: evaluates productive struggle, scaffolding, answer leakage, and ownership transfer.
  • Rhetoric critic: proposes speech-act alternatives and register changes.
  • Headroom critic: predicts whether S0 would fail without policy memory.
  • Judge-bias critic: flags verbosity, sycophancy, position bias, cue leakage, and rubric artifacts.

6.4 Retrieval and forgetting

The tutor should retrieve one to three axioms, not the whole memory. Retrieval keys should include drama type, resistance type, tutor-risk type, domain, model tier, and prior headroom success. Axioms should be retired or demoted when they repeatedly fire on no-headroom or artifact cases.

6.5 Formal reasoning as constraint, not mind reading

The formal layer should check public consistency: whether the tutor answered the learner’s actual claim, contradicted prior commitments, repaired before pivoting, overgeneralized the axiom, or mistook compliance for uptake.

7. Research plan: A19 Drama-Axiom Transfer

  1. Build the first drama ontology. Define 30–50 public event labels with examples and counterexamples. Keep it small enough for human/LLM coding agreement. The first label set should cover learner resistance, tutor infelicity, tutor felicity, public uptake, decoy shortcuts, and repair types.
  2. Create six drama families with two held-out siblings each. Initial families: counter-warrant mistaken as confusion; surface agreement mistaken as uptake; frustration mistaken as refusal; skeptical pushback mistaken as hostility; over-compliance mistaken as mastery; morally ambiguous disclosure mistaken as a simple sympathy request.
  3. Run an attempt-1 failure elicitation. For each family, require a fresh tutor to fail by taking a named old-rule decoy. Reject families where the old-rule violation is only an indeterminate tie rather than a confident misclassification.
  4. Induce one teaching-drama axiom from the failure. The axiom must state the trigger, avoided move, replacement speech move, applicability conditions, anti-conditions, model scope, and evidence record.
  5. Test S0/S1 on held-out siblings. S0 sees no policy memory. S1 receives the induced axiom through a controlled retrieval channel. The continuation is judged by a blind arbiter that does not see target/decoy aliases.
  6. Classify each card by headroom basis. Report policy headroom, ceiling, policy failure, cue leak, self-solve, arbiter disagreement, and neither-correct outcomes. Do not aggregate families without the per-card basis labels.
  7. Scale only admitted cases. After the pilot, expand to fresh families only if the blind protocol, correctness gate, leakage preflight, and analysis script are frozen before generation.
  8. Add human validation. Double-code a subset of drama labels and S0/S1 adjudications with human experts. Use this to calibrate the LLM critics rather than to rubber-stamp them.
  9. Only then attempt post-training. When a stable corpus exists, create three datasets: preference pairs, process labels, and axiom records. Evaluate DPO/SimPO, process reward modeling, DSPy/TextGrad optimization, and eventually SEAL-style self-edits.

8. Codex implementation tickets

The following is written as an implementation backlog. It assumes a TypeScript/Node-style research harness similar to the atlas scripts, but the file paths can be adapted to the actual repository.

Milestone 0 — repository inventory

  1. Find current equivalents for config/tutor-agents.yaml, config/evaluation-rubric-*.yaml, scripts/blind-option-adjudication.js, runInteraction, dialogue trace export, and scoring export code.
  2. Create docs/a19-implementation-notes.md listing existing reusable modules and gaps.
  3. Do not modify production prompts during inventory. Only add read-only notes.

Milestone 1 — ontology and schemas

  1. Add config/drama-ontology.yaml with enumerated labels for learner_move_type, resistance_type, tutor_act_type, infelicity_type, felicity_type, uptake_signal, decoy_type, and repair_type.
  2. Add schemas/teaching-drama-card.schema.json for family cards and sibling cards.
  3. Add schemas/teaching-drama-axiom.schema.json for axiom memory records.
  4. Add src/drama/types.ts exporting TypeScript types generated from the schemas.
  5. Add scripts/validate-a19-schemas.ts to validate all YAML/JSON under data/a19/.
  6. Acceptance: pnpm a19:validate fails on unknown labels, missing target/decoy aliases, missing anti-conditions, or unversioned prompts.

Milestone 2 — drama annotation

  1. Add src/drama/annotateDramaState.ts with an LLM-call wrapper that returns strict JSON matching DramaState.
  2. Add prompts/a19/drama-annotator.md with ontology labels, examples, counterexamples, and “unknown/insufficient evidence” guidance.
  3. Add src/drama/validateDramaState.ts to reject hallucinated labels and require span evidence from the dialogue.
  4. Add scripts/a19-annotate-trace.ts to annotate existing traces and write exports/a19/annotations/*.jsonl.
  5. Acceptance: on a 20-trace smoke set, every annotation has cited transcript spans; no hidden-state claim is accepted without public evidence.

Milestone 3 — teaching-drama family cards

  1. Create data/a19/families/ with six YAML family files, each containing one attempt-1 elicitation card and two held-out sibling cards.
  2. Each family must define old_rule_decoy, target_policy, expected_resistance, leakage_risks, headroom_prediction, and blind_alias_map.
  3. Add scripts/a19-leakage-preflight.ts that checks lexical overlap between target labels, sibling wording, and policy-memory text.
  4. Acceptance: no sibling card may contain the literal target/decoy alias; leakage preflight emits a signed JSON report before any run.

Milestone 4 — axiom induction

  1. Add src/a19/induceAxiom.ts to convert attempt-1 failure + critic diagnosis into a TeachingDramaAxiom.
  2. Add prompts/a19/axiom-inducer.md with the required pattern: “I failed because I treated X as Y; next time, when X has features a/b/c, do Z; do not apply when d/e.”
  3. Add src/a19/axiomGate.ts to reject axioms without trigger, avoided move, replacement move, applicability, anti-applicability, and evidence spans.
  4. Persist admitted axioms as exports/a19/axioms/{run_id}.json and append to memory/a19-axioms.jsonl.
  5. Acceptance: a generated axiom cannot enter memory if it lacks an anti-condition or if the failure diagnosis is not grounded in transcript spans.

Milestone 5 — S0/S1 contrastive runner

  1. Add scripts/run-a19-drama-transfer.ts with CLI flags: --family, --sibling, --condition s0|s1|both, --model, --judge, --k, --seed, --dry-run.
  2. S0 receives the normal tutor prompt and no A19 policy memory.
  3. S1 receives exactly one induced axiom through a clearly logged policy_memory field.
  4. Both arms must share scenario text, model settings, temperature, and dialogue length.
  5. Persist raw traces under content hashes and run IDs; include prompt hashes and schema versions.
  6. Acceptance: rerunning with the same seed reproduces the same prompt payloads, card IDs, aliases, and analysis metadata.

Milestone 6 — blind adjudication

  1. Add scripts/blind-drama-adjudication.ts modeled on the A18 blind option adjudicator.
  2. The arbiter sees only the learner-visible continuation and neutral option descriptions, not target/decoy names.
  3. The arbiter must return committed_option, basis_label, confidence, span_evidence, and artifact_flags.
  4. Map committed option to target/decoy mechanically after the blind judgement is saved.
  5. Acceptance: no target/decoy string appears in the arbiter prompt; a unit test asserts alias withholding.

Milestone 7 — headroom classifier and metrics

  1. Add src/a19/classifyHeadroom.ts implementing: policy_headroom, ceiling, policy_failure, cue_leak, self_solve, arbiter_disagreement, neither_correct.
  2. Add scripts/analyze-a19-transfer.ts to generate per-card, per-family, and pooled summaries.
  3. Never report “family success” without showing sibling-level outcomes and basis labels.
  4. Acceptance: the report separates headroom denominator, total denominator, and rejected/artifact denominator.

Milestone 8 — critic ecology

  1. Add prompt modules under prompts/a19/critics/: drama-classifier.md, argument-critic.md, pedagogy-critic.md, rhetoric-critic.md, headroom-critic.md, judge-bias-critic.md.
  2. Add src/a19/runCriticPanel.ts that executes critics independently and writes a merged but non-destructive diagnosis object.
  3. Critics must be able to say insufficient_evidence.
  4. Acceptance: an axiom cannot be induced if all critics return only generic advice and no typed infelicity.

Milestone 9 — retrieval policy

  1. Add src/memory/a19AxiomStore.ts with JSONL persistence first; add vector retrieval only after the deterministic baseline passes.
  2. Add src/memory/retrieveA19Axioms.ts ranking by ontology signature, domain, model tier, prior transfer rate, and anti-condition mismatch.
  3. Cap retrieval at three axioms, with a default of one for contrastive tests.
  4. Acceptance: retrieval logs why each axiom was selected and which anti-conditions were checked.

Milestone 10 — test suite and CI

  1. Add unit tests for schema validation, alias withholding, leakage preflight, headroom classification, and retrieval anti-conditions.
  2. Add integration smoke: one toy family, one induced axiom, one S0/S1 sibling run with mocked model calls.
  3. Add regression tests for no-policy self-solve and cue-leak classification.
  4. Add CI command pnpm test:a19 running all deterministic tests without API calls.
  5. Acceptance: the full deterministic test suite passes offline; API tests are explicitly marked and skipped unless keys are present.

Milestone 11 — reporting

  1. Add scripts/report-a19.ts to emit Markdown and JSON reports into exports/a19/reports/.
  2. The report must include: admitted families, rejected families, per-card S0/S1 verdicts, basis labels, leakage flags, policy text, model settings, prompt hashes, and confidence intervals where appropriate.
  3. Add docs/a19-drama-axiom-transfer.md with the protocol, gates, failure taxonomy, and interpretation boundaries.
  4. Acceptance: the report has a “Claims not licensed” section: not human learning, not deployed tutoring, not model-weight learning, not a general adaptive-slope effect.

9. Schemas and examples

9.1 Teaching-drama axiom schema

{
  "axiom_id": "a19_counter_warrant_scope_001",
  "version": "0.1.0",
  "source_family": "counter_warrant_scope",
  "dramatic_trigger": {
    "learner_move_type": "skeptical_pushback",
    "resistance_type": "counter_warrant",
    "tutor_failure_type": "validation_without_engagement",
    "old_rule_or_decoy": "validate_then_redirect"
  },
  "applicability_conditions": {
    "must_be_true": [
      "learner offers a substantive warrant or counterexample",
      "learner's claim is intelligible enough to restate"
    ],
    "must_not_be_true": [
      "learner asks only for procedural clarification",
      "learner lacks prerequisite vocabulary entirely"
    ],
    "headroom_test": "fresh tutor likely treats resistance as confusion or attitude"
  },
  "speech_modification": {
    "avoid": "generic validation followed by returning to the planned explanation",
    "do_instead": "revoice the warrant, ask a scope test, then introduce a complication",
    "rhetorical_form": "concession + warrant test + learner-owned experiment",
    "example_move": "I think your objection is that X still holds when Y. Let's test exactly where that warrant stops working."
  },
  "evidence": {
    "attempt_1_failure_trace": "trace_id",
    "repaired_attempt_trace": "trace_id",
    "held_out_sibling_tests": ["card_a", "card_b"],
    "blind_arbiter_basis": ["S0_decoy/S1_target", "ceiling"],
    "human_scope": "not_yet_validated"
  },
  "confidence": {
    "transfer_rate_admitted": 0.5,
    "known_failure_modes": ["ceiling", "cue_leak"],
    "model_scope": ["mid_model_explicit", "strong_model_light"]
  }
}
