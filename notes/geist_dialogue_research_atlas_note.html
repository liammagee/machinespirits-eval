2. Critique and appraisal

I scanned the PDF as a full draft rather than just an abstract-level summary. My appraisal: you are now past the “interesting pilot” stage and into a credible mechanism paper, but the draft is carrying too many papers at once. The core empirical story is strong, internally self-correcting, and unusually transparent; the current document is not yet publication-shaped because it over-explains, over-appends, and lets side experiments compete with the central claim. fileciteturn0file0

Where the paper is now

The core result has stabilized into a clean three-part mechanism story: calibration works, error correction works conditionally, adaptive responsiveness does not work as a general mechanism. The paper tests three proposed mechanisms across DeepSeek V3.2, Haiku 4.5, and Gemini Flash 3.0, with three independent judges and 1,296 scored rows; the headline claim is that two mechanisms replicate across the tested model range while the third is null on the primary analysis. fileciteturn4file16

That is a good place to be. The strongest contribution is no longer “Hegelian recognition improves AI tutoring.” The stronger, more defensible claim is:

An intersubjective-pedagogy prompt orientation reliably improves LLM tutor output quality by narrowing the output distribution and lifting weak pedagogical dimensions; a superego-style critic adds error correction mostly when the base model is weak or uncalibrated; neither intervention reliably changes within-dialogue adaptation slopes over 3-5 turns.

That claim is much sharper than the original theoretical frame. It is empirical, falsifiable, and actually supported by the results.

The most important evidence is the cross-model/cross-judge replication. The paper reports that recognition effects are unanimous across all 9 judge-by-run cells, with large effect sizes, and that the three-model setup was explicitly designed to separate mechanism effects from model artifacts. fileciteturn4file5 Calibration is especially strong because it appears prompt-level: recognition narrows within-response dimension variance, lifts the floor, and does not require the superego. fileciteturn4file6

The second major finding, error correction, is also useful but more nuanced. The superego helps under baseline conditions, but once recognition has already calibrated the ego, the superego becomes redundant on stronger models and remains useful mainly on the weaker Gemini Flash condition. The paper’s “universal substitution with model-dependent residual” formulation is one of its best ideas: the mechanisms overlap in function, but the amount of residual value left for the critic depends on model capability. fileciteturn4file10

The third mechanism is appropriately killed. Adaptive responsiveness was theoretically attractive, but the paper reports high cross-turn adaptation in all conditions and no reliable condition-dependent slopes. That null is not a weakness; it is one of the paper’s credibility markers. fileciteturn3file6

The strongest parts

The best part of the paper is that it no longer tries to make every result positive. It says: two mechanisms survive, one does not. That gives the empirical arc discipline.

The second strongest part is the apparatus. The provable discourse framework, trace logging, run IDs, claim verification, bug registry, and correction history are more than engineering detail. They are arguably the paper’s most transferable methodological contribution. The paper describes a framework where quantitative claims are machine-checked against data, and the revision history shows the apparatus catching real errors rather than merely decorating the paper. fileciteturn4file5

The third strong point is the A10/A10b reframing. This is conceptually important. The paper now admits that the active ingredient is not uniquely Hegelian vocabulary. Matched pedagogical prompts grounded in constructivist/intersubjective traditions perform equivalently to the recognition prompt, while behaviorist/transmission prompts underperform. The effect is therefore located at the orientation-family level rather than in Hegel-specific language. fileciteturn2file1 This makes the paper much more defensible, because it avoids the weak claim that the word “recognition” or Hegelian framing itself is magic.

The fourth strong point is the self-correction around limitations. The paper openly acknowledges synthetic learners, LLM judging, rubric evolution, process-tracing ambiguity, single-system scope, cost, and pilot-data correction issues. The limitations section is not perfunctory; it materially narrows the claims. fileciteturn4file17

The central problem

The paper’s biggest problem is not evidence. It is shape.

At 280 pages, the document reads like a lab notebook, a methods manifesto, a revision history, a theory paper, a mechanism paper, and three follow-on papers bundled into one object. That is valuable as provenance, but punishing as an article.

The core paper should be much shorter and more ruthless. Right now, the strongest finding is partly buried under architectural extensions, adaptive-runner trap scenarios, id-director/charisma work, A14 state-policy material, concealed-interior negative results, revision-history entries, and meta-methodological reflections. Some of that is genuinely interesting, but it dilutes the main contribution.

The paper currently has three separable papers inside it:

  1. Main empirical mechanism paper: recognition/intersubjective prompts, calibration, superego error correction, adaptive-responsiveness null.
  2. Methods paper: provable discourse, process tracing for agent architectures, rubric/PCA caveats, within-cell mediator diagnostic.
  3. Architecture-extension paper: id-director, charisma rubric, adaptive runner, trap scenarios, state-policy experiments.

For submission, the first paper should be the main article. The second should be a methods companion or major supplement. The third should be moved almost entirely out, except for a short “extensions and future work” paragraph.

The theory is better now, but still needs tightening

The Hegelian frame is rhetorically powerful, but the evidence now points to a broader and less Hegel-specific mechanism. The paper handles this reasonably by saying recognition theory is the clearest philosophical articulation of the intersubjective-pedagogy family rather than the uniquely necessary operational ingredient. fileciteturn2file9

That is the right move. But the draft still sometimes sounds as if the empirical effect vindicates recognition theory more strongly than it really does. The data supports recognition as a productive design grammar, not recognition as the uniquely causal theory. The safest formulation is:

Recognition theory generated the hypotheses and made the mechanism legible; the empirical active ingredient appears to be a broader intersubjective-pedagogy orientation.

That sentence should govern the whole paper.

I would also reduce the philosophical genealogy in the introduction. The Dewey/Vygotsky/Piaget/Kapur/Chi/VanLehn/Graesser lineage is useful, but it currently absorbs too much front-end oxygen. Readers need the design-relevant distinction: intersubjective/constructivist orientation versus transmission/behaviorist orientation. The full genealogy can go to an appendix or theory note.

The methods are impressive, but the statistical presentation needs another pass

The statistical approach section is too thin relative to the complexity of the data. It states effect-size conventions, ANOVA design, within-judge comparisons, and epoch filtering, but the actual design has repeated scenarios, multiple judges, multiple models, cells, runs, and correlated rows. A conventional ANOVA/effect-size presentation is readable, but reviewers will ask whether independence assumptions are being stretched. The paper should include a hierarchical robustness analysis.

A good robustness model would be something like:

score ~ recognition * architecture * model + (1 | scenario) + (1 | judge) + (1 | run)

or a Bayesian equivalent. For trajectory claims, use mixed slopes by dialogue/scenario where possible. The headline effects are probably large enough to survive, but the paper needs to show that.

The rubric issue is also serious. The PCA finding that PC1 explains 80.7% of variance means the eight tutor dimensions mostly behave like one general tutoring-quality factor, with content accuracy as a possible second facet. The paper acknowledges this well, but the implications should be pushed upward into the results, not buried in limitations. fileciteturn3file12 Any dimension-level claims should be phrased as facet-level descriptive patterns, not independent construct claims.

This matters because “calibration” partly means “the general quality profile becomes less uneven.” That is still real, but it is not the same as independently improving eight separable pedagogical skills.

The LLM-judge issue is managed, not solved

The cross-judge validation is a major strength: three judges, blind scoring, unanimous recognition-effect directions, and reported reliability ranges. fileciteturn4file17 That is enough for internal LLM-output evaluation.

But process-level claims remain more vulnerable. The paper itself admits that superego taxonomy labels and revision-quality judgments are LLM-assessed claims about LLM-internal or LLM-generated processes. Cross-judge scoring helps with output-level claims, but it does not fully validate the critique taxonomy or “substantive vs cosmetic” revision categories. fileciteturn4file17

The next critical validation is not another model run. It is the human coding pilot the paper already describes: sample ego-superego exchanges, have independent human coders classify critique categories and revision depth, compute reliability, and compare human labels to the LLM classifier. The paper says this is operationalized but still outstanding. That should be treated as a gate for strong mechanism claims.

Without that human coding, calibration is strong; error correction is plausible but still partly internally judged.

The human-learning question remains completely open

The paper is very clear that the findings concern tutor output quality assessed by LLM judges interacting with synthetic learners, not demonstrated human learning. That limitation is decisive. Synthetic learner behavior may not map onto human confusion, resistance, engagement, or conceptual transfer. The paper says the pedagogical significance depends on whether improved tutor behavior produces better learning. fileciteturn4file12

This should be foregrounded in the abstract and conclusion even more than it already is. The cleanest phrasing is:

This paper establishes mechanisms for improving judged tutor output quality, not learning outcomes.

That is not a demotion. It is the honest scope. A later human-learner study can test outcome validity.

The adaptive-responsiveness null is valuable and should be made more central

The draft sometimes treats adaptive responsiveness as a failed third mechanism, then keeps reopening adjacent versions through longer dialogues, dramatic recognition, peripeteia, longitudinal arcs, and writing-pad dynamics. Some of that may be real, but it muddies the central result.

The paper’s clean contribution is: within-dialogue adaptation is already high across conditions; recognition changes level, not slope. That is important. It tells us that the design works by setting the tutor’s initial stance and constraining its response space, not by making it progressively more adaptive over short interactions. fileciteturn4file13

The longitudinal/pre-alignment material near the end may be promising, but in this paper it should be framed as future work, not another partially integrated mechanism. Otherwise reviewers may feel the paper kills M3 and then tries to resurrect it under another name.

The id-director material should be cut or quarantined

The id-director/charisma section is interesting, but it is not part of the main mechanism story. The paper itself says the extension differs from the core along major axes: single-judge primary scoring, one id/ego model family, heterogeneous and sometimes low per-cell N, and a different Weberian charisma rubric. fileciteturn3file10

That is exactly why it should not sit in the main results. It distracts from the strong core and invites reviewer objections that are irrelevant to the main claim. Move it to an appendix, supplement, or separate paper. In the main paper, one paragraph can say: “Architectural extensions are under exploration but outside the evidentiary scope of the present mechanism claims.”

The revision history is a strength, but not as main-paper reading

The correction history is admirable. Bugs are named, invalidated runs are retracted, judge panels are distinguished, and prior claims are corrected. That builds trust. The Appendix E record of retractions and corrections is unusually transparent. fileciteturn4file4

But the current draft forces the reader to carry too much revision context. For a reviewer, the history can start to feel like instability rather than transparency. Keep the audit trail, but package it as supplementary provenance. In the main paper, include a short “Data and correction provenance” subsection with three points: which runs are headline, which are pilot, and which claims are excluded or retracted.

What I would do next

First, freeze the core paper around one claim:

In LLM tutoring, intersubjective-pedagogy prompting improves judged tutor output primarily through prompt-level calibration; superego critique provides partially substitutable error correction, useful mainly when base generation quality leaves uncorrected failures; short-window adaptive responsiveness does not mediate the effect.

Second, cut the main manuscript to something like 35-50 pages. Move the following to supplement or separate papers: id-director, adaptive runner/trap scenarios, A14, concealed interior, most of the revision history, most prompt listings, and the long apparatus reflections. Keep only what is needed to support the mechanism story.

Third, run the human expert coding pilot for the superego taxonomy before making strong process-tracing claims. That is the cheapest high-value validation left.

Fourth, add a mixed-effects or clustered-robust statistical appendix. Do not rely only on simple ANOVA/effect-size contrasts for data with scenarios, judges, runs, and model clusters.

Fifth, reframe the theory throughout: recognition theory as the explicit design grammar for an empirically broader intersubjective-pedagogy effect. That preserves the philosophical contribution without overclaiming.

Sixth, keep the human learner study as the next empirical frontier, not a requirement for this paper. The current paper can stand as an output-quality mechanism paper, provided it does not imply learning outcomes.

Bottom line

You are in a strong but dangerous position. The core paper is now credible: the effects are replicated, the null is clean, the mechanism interaction is interesting, and the apparatus is unusually rigorous. The danger is that the draft’s ambition will obscure the contribution. It currently reads like a whole research program compressed into one manuscript.

The next move is not more breadth. It is consolidation. Cut the side quests, validate the process taxonomy with humans, add hierarchical robustness, and let the paper say one thing cleanly:

Recognition-like intersubjective prompting works mainly by calibrating tutor production; multi-agent critique is a conditional backup, not the main engine; short-term adaptive responsiveness is not the mechanism.

4. Research atlas / dossier proposal

Yes — that changes the appraisal. The right question is not “how do we compress this into a journal article?” but what genre can preserve the work’s generative, branching, self-auditing character without becoming a book-shaped swamp?

My answer: represent it as a versioned research atlas / dossier, with a short synthetic spine and a set of semi-autonomous “papers” or “modules” arranged as an interconnected research programme.

Not a single paper. Not a book. Not merely a supplement. Something closer to:

A citable research dossier: a modular, versioned, empirically audited atlas of dialectical agent architectures.

That genre fits the thing you are actually building.

Why a single paper is the wrong master-form

A single paper forces the work to pretend that it has one central empirical question. It does not. It currently has several partially overlapping objects:

The mechanism paper: recognition, calibration, error correction, adaptive-responsiveness null.

The apparatus paper: provable discourse, process tracing for agent architectures, rubric iteration, machine-checked claims.

The architecture paper: ego/superego, id-director, drama machine, state-policy runners, learner modeling.

The theory paper: recognition, Bildung, psychoanalysis, charisma, intersubjective pedagogy, sycophancy, ontology of agent interiors.

The negative-results archive: hidden-interior nulls, adaptive-elaboration nulls, state-richness reversals, failed mechanism stacks.

The design laboratory: not merely what was found, but how the system learns what it can and cannot make observable.

Trying to force all of that into one conventional article will either make the article unreadable or, worse, falsify the work’s nature. The sprawl is not incidental. The draft explicitly treats the evaluation apparatus itself — provable discourse, rubric iterations, corrections, and tests — as a transferable method, not just as background infrastructure. fileciteturn7file2 The paper also says the apparatus mirrors the mechanisms it studies: calibration narrows acceptable claims, error correction catches stale assertions, and rubric iteration becomes a research analogue of adaptive responsiveness. fileciteturn7file3

So the object is not just “findings about tutoring.” It is a self-correcting research machine about self-correcting pedagogical machines.

That needs a form that lets the machine remain visible.

The right form: a research atlas

I would call the overall object a research atlas rather than a paper, book, or edited volume.

An atlas has several useful properties:

It has a map, not a single linear argument.

It contains regions, routes, dead ends, overlaps, and scales.

It allows local detail without pretending every detail belongs in the same argumentative paragraph.

It can mark which territories are settled, which are provisional, which are failed expeditions, and which remain unexplored.

That is exactly what your work needs.

The central unit should be something like:

Geist in the Machine: A Research Atlas of Recognition, Charisma, and Dialectical Agent Architecture

or, if you want less grandiosity:

Dialectical Agent Pedagogy: A Versioned Research Dossier

or:

The Geist Dossier: Mechanisms, Architectures, and Evaluation Methods for Dialectical AI Tutoring

“Dossier” may be the most accurate word. “Atlas” is more elegant. “Compendium” is more neutral. “Edited volume” is close, but it implies separate contributors and finalized chapters. Your thing is more like a single-author edited volume plus lab archive plus methods platform.

The spine should be short

The mistake would be to make the atlas itself one enormous undifferentiated PDF. You need a spine document that orients the reader and then points outward.

The spine should be perhaps 25-40 pages. Its job is not to prove everything. Its job is to say:

This is the research programme.

These are the major arcs.

These are the settled findings.

These are the live hypotheses.

These are the dead mechanisms.

These are the evidentiary standards.

These are the modules that carry the detailed argument.

The current abstract and introduction already contain the ingredients: recognition as intersubjective-pedagogy orientation, three mechanisms, process tracing, provable discourse, apparatus-as-method, id-director/charisma as architectural extension, and limitations around synthetic learners and LLM judges. fileciteturn6file0 But instead of asking that introduction to carry a 280-page paper, make it the editorial introduction to the dossier.

Think of the spine as the “volume introduction” in an edited collection, except the “chapters” are citable research modules.

The modules

I would represent the work as a constellation of papers, each with its own claim-status, data status, and relation to the others.

Here is the architecture I would use.

Module Working title Status Function

1 Recognition as Calibration Core empirical The clean mechanism result: intersubjective prompts improve tutor output by floor-lifting and variance narrowing.
2 Superego as Conditional Error Corrector Core empirical Shows error correction, substitution with calibration, and model-dependent residual value.

This structure preserves the edited-volume feel while avoiding the burdens of an actual book.

The key is that each module should be independently readable, but the atlas spine should define the cross-module vocabulary: recognition, calibration, superego, id, charisma, ontology, state, trace, claim, kill gate, rubric, learner interior.

The strongest organizing principle

I would not organize the dossier chronologically. I would organize it by mechanism family.

The whole programme seems to be asking:

What kinds of agent-internal structure produce better pedagogical relation, and which apparent structures are illusory, redundant, or too costly to pay?

That gives you three large families:

1. Recognition mechanisms

These are the mechanisms that actually work most cleanly: prompt-level calibration and conditional error correction. The current paper reports recognition as the dominant effect across judges and models, and locates the active ingredient at the broader intersubjective-pedagogy orientation rather than Hegel-specific vocabulary. fileciteturn7file8

This is the most “normal science” part of the work. It can be written as conventional empirical papers.

2. Dramatic / charismatic mechanisms

These are the mechanisms concerned with voice, persona, conflict, authority, transformation, and the staging of the encounter.

The Drama Machine line matters because it gives you a vocabulary for internal tension: agents do not merely execute goals; they become more lifelike or pedagogically interesting when sub-agents represent competing motivations. The paper already frames this as a source of deliberative refinement, productive tension, and role differentiation. fileciteturn7file6

The charisma work then becomes the second great pole: not recognition as mutual subjecthood, but charisma as extraordinary authority, affective pull, transformative invitation, anti-routinization, and witness/proof. The id-director section is not a side quest if the atlas form is adopted. It becomes a parallel architecture family: instead of a durable ego corrected by a superego, you test a transient ego repeatedly authored by a durable id. fileciteturn7file12

That is a substantial research line. It deserves its own module.

3. Ontology / state / interior mechanisms

This is where the work may become most philosophically interesting, even when the empirical results are negative.

The hidden-interior and state-policy results seem to be saying something like:

There may be no privileged structured interior beneath the text that the system can exploit, even when the apparatus generates and logs that interior itself.

That is a major result, not merely a failed implementation. One section reports that the logged hidden deliberation did not encode recoverable, family-transferable information about the concealment it was supposed to explain, concluding that the hidden trace is another stream of in-context LLM text rather than a privileged latent variable. fileciteturn7file15

Likewise, the state-policy / ontology-based work seems to be converging on a hard lesson: richer externalized learner state, hand-authored policy actions, counterfactual replay, and fitted policies often do not outperform the strong implicit base. One synthesis says that implicit, hand-authored, and fitted-parametric state-action policy realizations are all null relative to the implicit base, with the apparatus itself becoming the durable contribution. fileciteturn8file8

That belongs in the atlas because it is philosophically continuous with Hegel/psychoanalysis/recognition. It asks whether there is a modelable interior at all, or whether “interiority” in these systems is mostly an artifact of trace design.

What the atlas lets you say that a paper cannot

A conventional paper wants a clean claim like:

Recognition improves AI tutoring through calibration and error correction.

That is true, but incomplete.

The atlas can say something much richer:

We built dialectical agent architectures to test whether pedagogical relation can be engineered through recognition, critique, dramatic conflict, charismatic voice, and explicit learner-state ontology. Some mechanisms work: recognition calibrates output, and superego critique conditionally corrects it. Some mechanisms fail cleanly: short-window adaptive responsiveness, rich hidden learner interiors, and several state-policy elaborations do not pay. Some mechanisms open a second axis: id-directed charisma can produce extraordinary pedagogical voice but trades off against recognition-oriented elicitation. The apparatus that makes these distinctions visible is itself a contribution.

That is the actual shape of the work.

The dossier needs explicit claim-status labels

Because the work is sprawling and still alive, readers need a status grammar.

Every module should mark claims as one of:

Settled within current evidence — replicated across main cells, judges, models, or pre-registered checks.

Supported but scope-bound — real in this architecture, model range, rubric, or scenario family.

Exploratory — promising but underpowered or judge-/scenario-conditional.

Killed / closed — tested and rejected under a specified gate.

Speculative / theoretical — conceptually important but not yet empirically resolved.

This would transform the sprawl from “too much stuff” into “a research ecology with epistemic statuses.”

The current work already has this ethos. The adaptive-responsiveness result, for example, is not just a failure; it is a well-powered null in the primary analysis with a failed replication of the exploratory boundary condition. fileciteturn7file2 The id-director work similarly distinguishes architecture specializations, rubric divergence, judge-conditional magnitudes, and small-N traps rather than forcing one champion cell. fileciteturn8file4

Make that explicit as a representational principle.

The most important editorial distinction

You need to distinguish archive from argument.

The archive includes all runs, cells, corrections, prompts, rubrics, logs, failed branches, and provenance.

The argument is the current interpretation of what those materials show.

Right now, the draft sometimes merges them. The atlas form lets you separate them without suppressing either.

A good structure would be:

Layer 1: Spine A readable synthetic introduction to the programme.

Layer 2: Citable modules Each module is paper-like: abstract, claim, method, evidence, limitations, status.

Layer 3: Technical annexes Rubrics, prompts, cell definitions, run IDs, judge configurations, PCA, robustness checks.

Layer 4: Machine-checkable archive Provable discourse claims, YAML claim registry, tests, data tables, trace logs.

Layer 5: Change log Corrections, retractions, version history, closed arcs.

That way the work can remain sprawling without making every reader walk through every corridor.

How to publish or circulate it

There are a few viable outward-facing versions.

Option A: The “special issue” model

You release 5-7 linked working papers, each with its own abstract and citation, under one umbrella title.

This is closest to the edited-volume feel.

Example:

Geist in the Machine I: Recognition as Calibration
Geist in the Machine II: Superego Critique and Conditional Error Correction
Geist in the Machine III: Process Tracing for Agent Architectures
Geist in the Machine IV: The Drama Machine and Pedagogical Conflict
Geist in the Machine V: Charismatic Pedagogy and the Id-Director
Geist in the Machine VI: Ontology, State, and the Non-Privileged Interior
Geist in the Machine VII: Provable Discourse as Research Infrastructure

The advantage is legibility. The disadvantage is maintenance overhead.

Option B: The “living dossier” model

You maintain one canonical website/repository/PDF bundle with versioned modules and a stable spine.

The advantage is that the work can continue growing. The disadvantage is that academia may struggle to cite it unless you snapshot versions with DOIs or arXiv-style releases.

Option C: The “program paper plus dossier” model

You write one 15-25 page programmatic paper:

Dialectical Agent Pedagogy: A Research Programme

Then the full atlas sits behind it as the evidence base.

This may be the most practical. The program paper explains why the project exists and what the main arcs are. The dossier contains the sprawling methodological and empirical record.

Option D: The “methods platform plus case studies” model

You foreground provable discourse and process tracing as the main contribution, and treat recognition, charisma, and ontology as case studies.

This would make the work more acceptable to AI evaluation / HCI / learning analytics audiences, but it might understate the philosophical originality.

My recommendation is Option C plus Option B: a short programmatic article that points to a versioned research atlas.

How the ongoing work fits

Drama Machine arc

Research question:

Can productive internal conflict be engineered as a stable source of pedagogical quality, rather than merely simulated as roleplay?

Core constructs: tension, deliberative refinement, role differentiation, conflict without incoherence, anti-sycophantic friction.

This extends the ego/superego result but is not reducible to it.

Charisma arc

Research question:

Can an AI tutor produce transformative authority without collapsing into domination, sycophancy, or theatrical self-display?

Core constructs: Weberian charisma, witness, call-and-response, anti-routinization, persona shift, recognition-charisma tradeoff.

The current id-director findings already point to a real tradeoff: c104, c105, and c107 appear as distinct design points rather than one super-cell, with recognition and charisma rewarding different uses of voice. fileciteturn8file0

That is rich enough for a standalone paper.

Ontology arc

Research question:

What kind of “learner model” is real for an LLM agent: a structured state, a trace, a policy, a conversational surface, or a fiction maintained by the architecture?

Core constructs: explicit learner state, hidden deliberation, trap families, state-action policies, profile updates, non-privileged interiors.

This is likely the deepest theoretical arc. Negative results here may matter more than positive ones, because they constrain what “interiority” can mean in agent systems.

My strongest recommendation

Do not choose between “single paper” and “set of papers.”

Create a canonical atlas, then derive papers from it.

The atlas is the real work.

The papers are export formats.

That lets you preserve the intellectual ecology without asking every venue to accept the whole ecology at once.

The canonical object might be:

The Geist Atlas v1.0: Dialectical Agent Pedagogy, Recognition, Charisma, and the Limits of Learner-State Ontology

Then individual articles become:

“Recognition as Calibration in LLM Tutoring”
“Process Tracing for Multiagent AI Systems”
“The Id-Director: Charismatic Pedagogy in Agent Architecture”
“There Is No Privileged Interior: Negative Results in Learner-State Modeling”
“Provable Discourse: Machine-Checked Claims for Empirical AI Research”

This also solves a rhetorical problem. Reviewers of a single paper will complain that the id-director or ontology sections distract from recognition. Readers of the atlas will understand that these are neighboring territories in the same research programme.

The phrase I would use

I would describe the work like this:

This project is a versioned research atlas for dialectical agent pedagogy: a modular, machine-audited dossier of theory, architecture, evaluation apparatus, empirical findings, negative results, and open mechanism arcs. Its unit is not the article or the book chapter but the claim-bearing module: each module states a mechanism, records its evidentiary status, links to the traces and rubrics that adjudicate it, and situates it within the larger programme.

That is the adequate representation.

It preserves the sprawl, but disciplines it.

It lets the work remain unfinished, but not formless.

It makes the archive visible without confusing the archive with the argument.

And it gives you room to keep pushing the drama machine, charisma, and ontology-based model without making the recognition paper carry everything.
