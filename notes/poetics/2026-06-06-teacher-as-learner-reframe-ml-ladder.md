# Teacher-as-learner reframe: what it unlocks for ML / fine-tuning, and the binding constraint

- **Date:** 2026-06-06
- **Status:** design analysis. No new empirical claim. Future-work framing for the A18 arc. **Updated 2026-06-06 — the retrieval rung this note endorsed is now run-null offline (§6.9.8); see the Update block below.**
- **Claim boundary:** simulated teacher-as-learner only. This note does **not** demonstrate human learning, deployed tutoring, hidden-interior measurement, or model-weight learning. It reasons about what *would become available* under the reframe, and what gates that availability.

So the ladder's lowest rung — in-context/retrieval, the one this note called the justified next move — is itself closed-null on the single pivotal decision (a necessary condition for the multi-turn slope A15 targeted). The full cross-dialogue slope claim (turn-5 ≠ turn-1) would still need the live cell plus a paid run, which is the foreclosed "add embeddings and rerun" treadmill (§6.10 / §7); the offline gate settles the necessary condition without paying it.

## The question

If we reframe the problem from "does this tutoring transcript look adaptive?" to "can the tutor *learn* — revise its own strategy policy by overcoming simulated learner resistance?" (the A18 frame), does that free up new approaches to machine learning or to fine-tuning an already-trained LLM?

Short answer: **yes, the reframe opens the door the old frame structurally could not — but the binding constraint is not "can we fine-tune," it is "is the transfer signal real," and that is exactly the question A18.37 is testing. Adjudicated by a blind three-critic panel (§7.9), the tutor's saved strategy demonstrably helped the learner on 5 of 8 held-out test scenarios across four fresh families. The right way to count is by the individual held-out scenario, not by the family, because in three of the four families the two scenarios disagreed (the strategy helped one but not its twin). A real but thin and conditional signal, not an unconditional "fresh families transfer."**

(Terms used throughout: a **family** is one type of reasoning trap — e.g. "the answer is the token sitting *between* two markers," or "the answer only appears once two panels are slid together so they line up." Each family was then tested on **two held-out scenarios**: the same trap, different surface details. Four families × two scenarios = eight held-out scenarios. The paper §7.9 calls an individual held-out scenario a "sibling" or "card"; this note avoids that shorthand.)

## What the reframe structurally contributes: a labeled train/test split

The surface-responsiveness frame had **no label**. Scoring "does this look responsive" is why the poetics, timing-pair (`project_timing_pair_coherence_confound`), and §6.10 hidden-interior arcs kept dissolving into critic-gullibility, coherence, and author confounds — there was nothing to be right or wrong *about*.

The teacher-as-learner frame manufactures one: a `(training seed → held-out scenario)` pair that shares an *obstruction grammar* but differs in surface content, plus a transfer success criterion. That tuple — `(learner_state_pattern → policy_action → did-it-transfer-to-held-out)` — is a reward signal with a built-in train/test split. A held-out scenario cannot be gamed by a fluent transcript. **That split is the precondition for any form of learning**, in-context or in-weights. This is the real contribution of the reframe; everything below follows from it.

## The ladder the split unlocks (increasing weight; first rung already built)

1. **In-context / retrieval, no weight updates.** A18's policy ledger + counterfactual replay; A15's cross-dialogue retrieval cache. The reframe makes the retrieval key and the outcome label well-defined. *Built (A18); A15 retrieval form run-null offline 2026-06-06 — see the Update block above and §6.9.8.*
2. **Rejection-sampling SFT (STaR-style).** Generate K candidate policy revisions, keep the ones that transfer to held-out scenarios, fine-tune on the winners. This is D3's Bridge-3 best-of-N selector (which hit d=0.41, "suggestive") promoted from an *inference-time* selector into a *training* loop. The selection signal already exists.
3. **Preference optimization (DPO/KTO).** A18's contrastive panel (`run-recursive-tutor-policy-contrast-panel.js`) already emits *pairs* — S1 (policy-memory) vs S0 (no-policy) on the same held-out scenario, with a blind-critic preference. That is a preference dataset. The panel flips from an evaluation instrument into a label generator.
4. **RL with the learner as environment.** Transfer success = reward, policy revision = action, the local-gate / panel = reward model. A15 explicitly scopes this out as "A1 pilot territory."

The apparatus already built as *evaluators* doubles as a training pipeline's supervisors: the held-out scenario (→ train/test split), the A18.13 policy-correctness gate (→ reward shaping that rejects "adaptive but wrong policy"), and the contrastive panel (→ preference pairs). That is roughly two-thirds of a rejection-sampling or DPO loop.

## The binding constraint — three reasons not to reach for weights yet

1. **Signal trustworthiness is unsolved, and it is the actual bottleneck.** A18's entire A18.5→A18.37 history is the record of how fragile this transfer signal is: repeated `no_local_headroom` (S0 does as well as S1), family-after-family preflight failures on `public_self_solving` and `inverse_rule_instability`, and a strict-rule panel *failure* at A18.21. ML is only as good as its reward, and **fine-tuning amplifies whatever Goodhart hole the reward has** — you already saw the shape of this in D3 Bridge-2 (coupling-targeted superego moved the surface marker, not the semantic channel), in `feedback_closed_loop_eval_tells`, and in poetics critic-gullibility. Train on a leaky transfer signal and you get a tutor better at passing the gate, not at teaching.
2. **The model already knows most of it.** A3 (capability-threshold: prosthesis *hurts* capable models), A10 (density substitutes for recognition), A14/A16 (apparatus-positive, outcome-null) all say the same thing — capable LLMs already infer most of what these mechanisms inject; cf. `project_adaptivity_what_works` ("gains come from new signal, not re-encoding what the model already infers"). Resistance that generic pedagogy *can't* already handle is rare to construct — that is the whole reason A18 burned A18.17–A18.34 on family authoring.
3. **The substrate posture.** This is a provider-agnostic, editable research substrate. A15 and A16 both scope weight updates out *explicitly* — "the maintenance burden of a fine-tuned artifact dominates the research-substrate's editability needs." A frozen, provider-locked model breaks the swap-any-model factorial the whole repo runs on. If the line is ever crossed, a **LoRA adapter on the policy head only — kept swappable, not merged** — is the form that least violates the posture (A15 ruled LoRA out on maintenance grounds, not feasibility).

## The empirical hinge: A18.37 is the gate

A18.37 (replication fanout across the 12-family A18.35 pool) is the task that decides whether there is a signal worth training on at all. The canonical adjudication is already in the paper (§7.9): a blind three-critic arbiter (`scripts/blind-option-adjudication.js`) reads only the held-out continuation, names the option the learner commits to and on what basis, with the target/decoy aliases held out of the critic and matched mechanically downstream. On that one trusted channel:

- **Policy memory produces a blind-detectable held-out advantage on 5 of 8 held-out scenarios across four fresh families.** Every elicited family converges on at least one of its two scenarios, and three families *split* across their two scenarios — which is why the counting unit is the **individual held-out scenario, not the family**: "does the saved policy help" is a property of the individual scenario, not of the family it belongs to.
- The no-headroom scenarios fail for two mechanistic reasons the blind basis-label separates: the fresh no-policy tutor either rediscovers the relation unaided, or its surface shortcut happens to coincide with the target on a cue-leaky scenario. A *k=3* rerun pins these to deterministic extremes (self-solve 3/3 vs headroom 0/3) — structural, not a teacher coin-flip.
- An independent committed-answer cross-check (`scripts/readjudicate-a18-transfer.js`) reads the same turns by hand and **agrees with the blind arbiter scenario-by-scenario on every shared scenario**. It also independently demonstrates *why* a lexical correctness rule cannot stand in for the read: three lexical matchers (contiguous / ordered / order-free) disagree on 10 of 20 arms — one rule false-negatives "slot six has a neri", another false-positives a wrong option the learner names only to reject. Only the committed (semantic) read is stable. This is the same surface-vs-function failure the project studies elsewhere, turned on the measurement instrument; §7.9 is right to use the blind read and retire the lexical overlay channel.

The implication for the ladder is direct. There is a real multi-family signal, not a singleton — but it is thin (5 of 8 *held-out scenarios* on a structurally-identified subset, four families of a 12-family pool with the rest unrun or unresolved), it is *conditional* (it transfers on the scenarios where a fresh tutor would otherwise take a decoy shortcut, not unconditionally), and reading it at all requires the blind committed-option channel because every surface correctness gate is itself a costume. Fine-tuning on a signal that can only be read correctly by a blind semantic arbiter — and whose surface proxies provably disagree — is the worst case for Goodhart: train on a lexical gate and you get a tutor better at the gate, not at teaching. That is the same failure already seen in D3 Bridge-2, `feedback_closed_loop_eval_tells`, and poetics critic-gullibility.

## Decision rule

The signal is "real but thin, conditional, and readable only through a blind semantic channel." That sets the *reason* to stay weight-free, not just the conclusion:

- **Stay weight-free for now.** 5 of 8 held-out scenarios is a genuine multi-family signal, but it is thin, conditional (transfer only on the scenarios a fresh tutor would otherwise shortcut), and — most decisively — readable only by an arbiter blind to the answer key, because every surface correctness proxy provably disagrees. Distilling that into weights would amplify the proxy fragility. ~~The justified move remains the in-context / retrieval rung (A15), which attacks the multi-turn slope gap without claiming internalization.~~ **Superseded 2026-06-06 (see the Update block above):** the in-context/retrieval rung (A15) is itself now run-null on the transfer-valid channel — the same leave-one-family-out boundary the parametric lever failed. The justified move is therefore *not* "retrieval next" but **widen the trusted channel first** (the blind arbiter on the two uncovered §7.9 families); no rung, weighted or weight-free, is justified until a stable transfer rate survives that widening.
- **What would change the decision:** first, widen the trusted channel rather than the proxy — run the blind arbiter on the two resolved families it has not yet covered (`overlay_registration`, `pointer_chain_two_hop`), taking the count of adjudicated held-out scenarios from 8 to 12 under one instrument; settle whether headroom needs the function-level reasoning-basis guard or merely option-advantage (it decides `pointer_chain`); then resolve the remaining unrun families. **If** a stable transfer rate (counted by individual held-out scenario) across fresh families survives that, **then** rejection-sampling SFT on transfer-winning policy revisions — as a swappable LoRA adapter — is the defensible first weight-update rung: cheapest, preserves the most general capability, bounded and testable claim. Internalization-in-weights is a strictly stronger claim and still needs the trustworthy signal *first*.
