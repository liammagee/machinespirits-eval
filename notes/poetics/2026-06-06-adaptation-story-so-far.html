Can a tutor learn? The adaptation arc, so far

Every path we have tried toward making the tutor adapt — what works, what is a null, and what is still live tonight.

This is a travelogue, not a verdict. The durable summary and paper-full-2.0.md hold the claims that have earned the right to be stable; this note holds the route — the dead ends, the corrections, the one stubborn question we are still inside of. It is dated and provisional on purpose. Where a result is settled, it is cited to the paper. Where it is still warm from the scriptorium, it is flagged live and you should treat it as a reading, not a finding.

The spine of the recent work is a small apparatus we keep calling the sidecar: a zero-cost preflight that decides whether a tutoring scenario could possibly show adaptation before we pay to find out. The story is mostly about what that preflight has taught us by repeatedly being wrong.

01·

What "adaptation" even means here

The question, and what would count as yes.

"Adaptation" is an over-loaded word, so the first job is to say what we are not claiming. We are not measuring human learning, not model-weight learning, not a deployed tutor, and not broad curricular transfer. The whole arc lives inside one narrow, honest boundary: can a simulated tutor, replayed offline, change its teaching move in response to a learner who is genuinely stuck — and can we tell that the change came from something it picked up, rather than from re-stating what the scene already made obvious?

The cleanest test we have is a contrast between two runs of the same held-out scene that differ by exactly one thing. S0 replays the scene with no memory of any policy. S1 replays the same scene with a saved policy injected. If S0 already solves the scene, there was nothing to learn — no headroom. Adaptation only "counts" when S0 fails the held-out sibling and S1 succeeds by way of the registered policy — not by accident, not by re-describing the obvious.

Claim boundary
simulated_teacher_as_learner_not_human_learning. Everything below is about an offline counterfactual instrument. The interesting result is not "the tutor learned" — it is what the instrument keeps revealing about how thin the gap between "could learn" and "already knew" really is.

02·

The durable backdrop · paper §6.8–6.10

One thing worked. A long row of elaborations didn't.

Before the sidecar, the paper had already run the adaptation question hard and landed somewhere uncomfortable but clear. The honest one-line summary: the apparatus is the contribution; the elaborations mostly are not.

i.

The adaptive runner moves on trap scenarios works · bounded

Replace ordinary 3–5-turn dialogues with trap scenarios — scenes engineered so the obvious move fails — and the LangGraph state-policy tutor (cell_110) produces measurable strategy shifts where the broad slope test saw nothing.

The effect is real but modest and convention-fragile. An early "twice as often" reading was a turn-indexing artifact; corrected, it sits at roughly 1.4× cross-suite and null on the original v1 suite. Do not quote the headline number without the caveat.

ii.

Almost every richer model of the learner null

We bolted on increasingly sophisticated machinery and watched each one fail to pay:

  • Bilateral theory-of-mind elaboration — no measurable within-family discrimination §6.8.5
  • A richer learner state-schema — not only no help, an actual reversal (the "state-richness reversal") §6.8.6
  • An evidence-bound hypothesis ledger (A14) — the validator's binary gain does not survive the graded channel §6.9.7
  • A learned state→action policy lever — does not pay under the offline kill gate §6.9.8
  • Modelling the concealed interior (the signal axis) — also does not pay §6.10

The lesson was not "adaptation is impossible." It was "adaptation that pays comes from giving the model new signal, not from re-encoding what it already infers."paper-full-2.0.md · §6.8.8

Reads as A cascade of nulls is not a sad result — it is a map. It told us exactly which kind of move to stop making.

03·

What actually moves the needle

In-context signal beats a critic beats a state machine.

Three ways of trying to make the tutor adaptive, ranked by what the runs actually showed:

Best

New in-context signal

Hand the model information it did not already have in the scene. Gains here are real because they are not redundant with the model's own inference.

Middle

An adversarial superego

A critic that genuinely tries to undermine the ego's draft beats an advisory one decisively — and the load-bearing channel is the prompt-rewrite, not message-mutation. Advisory critique barely registers; adversarial critique reshapes the move. provisional

Weakest

A hand-coded state machine

Programmatic state→action rules were the least effective of the three — they mostly re-encode what the model would infer anyway, which is why the learned-policy lever later failed the offline kill gate too.

Throughline Every win is a signal win. Every null is a re-encoding null. The sidecar story below is the same lesson at one more turn of the screw.

Cite Adversarial-superego decisive result is a working finding (notes/poetics); the policy-lever null is durable §6.9.8.

04·

The recursive teacher-as-learner · A18

An instrument for "did the tutor pick something up?"

The sidecar lives inside a workstream we label A18: treat the tutor as the learner. The tutor meets a stuck learner on a training scene, improvises a repair, and we distil that repair into a one-paragraph policy memory. Then we test it on held-out siblings — new scenes from the same hidden rule — under the S0-vs-S1 contrast from §01.

The crucial property is that the tutor doing the held-out scene is blind to the hidden rule. The replay engine feeds the generator only the public performance of the scene; the rule and the policy live in metadata only the checker sees. So if S1 lands the registered target and S0 does not, the policy actually carried information.

✓

It has produced genuine positives works · narrow

Two hand-built families — a "selector" family and a "bead-predecessor" family — passed a blind contrastive panel. On both bead pairs, five independent critics voted 5/5 that S1's success was policy-transfer, with 0/5 calling it ordinary public inference and 0/5 preferring S0.

With a material caveat: the second family only cleared once a policy-correctness gate was added (A18.13), so that a coherent-but-wrong S0 repair stops counting as headroom. Two families, panel-supported, gate-dependent. That is the high-water mark.

Cite A18.14 correctness-gated bead panel: notes/poetics/2026-06-06-a18-correctness-gated-bead-panel-result.md.

Why "sidecar" A18 rides alongside the paper, not inside it — its positives are bounded and not yet folded into §6.

05·

Post-v2 fresh families · the failures that taught us

Two ways a fresh scene dies.

Once we tried to replicate the positives with brand-new families, they kept dying — and they died in instructive, repeatable ways.

Failure class 1

Inverse-rule instability · diagonal_socket

The hidden rule was an opposite/completion relation ("same position duplicates; opposite position completes"). The scene made a simpler same-position reading too natural, so on the hard sibling S1 drifted back to the easy reading and chose wrong. The rule was too unstable to survive transfer. A18.26

Failure class 2

Public self-solving · thread_source

The hidden rule was stable and usable — but the cue (a visible nub touching the carrier) was so obvious that S0 found it without the policy. Both arms succeeded, so there was no headroom to measure. The scene was too public. A18.29

A useful family must thread a needle: the selected repair must be visible enough for S1 to apply after transfer, yet not so visible that S0 discovers it — stable enough not to collapse into a simpler reading, with a counterexample that makes the old move actually fail.A18.30 stop-rule synthesis

That paragraph is the whole problem in miniature, and it is why the next move was not another scene. It was to stop authoring on intuition and build something that could predict these deaths for free.

Turn The bottleneck was never the scoring rule. It was fixture construction — the human intuition about what makes a good trap.

06·

The cue-map preflight · A18.31 → A18.34

A gate that learns from each scene it lets through.

The sidecar is a zero-API risk reporter. Each candidate family carries a cue map — an explicit declaration of every public cue, which target each one favours, which relation the policy selects, and what counterexample blocks the obvious reading. The reporter reads that story and refuses to spend on a paid replay unless the design is clean. It started by encoding the two failures above:

  • inverse_rule_instability_risk — inverse relations without a blocking counterexample
  • public_self_solving_risk — highly visible selected cues
  • target_salience_overload, marker_too_broad, marker_too_narrow, counterexample_missing

Then the gate got its real test. A new family, fold_anchor, passed the preflight cleanly — and still failed locally. A blind S0 read a visible marker beside the correct tray as a pointer and solved both held-out scenes without the policy. A third, previously unnamed failure class.

↻

Higher-order learning, in the instrument itself works

We added the class — selector_like_public_governance_self_solving — and hardened the gate so a fresh marker-adjacent authority cue is rejected unless it requires a constructed device or a non-adjacent relation. A family that had passed the old preflight, then failed for real, is now caught by the new one — while the prior empirical positives still pass.

This is the genuinely satisfying part of the arc: the apparatus exhibits the exact thing we are trying to measure in the tutor. It met a failure, named it, and updated its own rule so the failure cannot recur. The gate is learning even when the families are not.

07·

A18.35–.37 · saturating the design space resolved → §7.9

Twelve fresh families. The static gate can't tell them apart anymore.

The move that opened this stretch was to stop hand-crafting one family at a time and instead saturate: author a battery of twelve fresh constructed-device or non-adjacent families at once, all engineered to clear the hardened gate, then screen them cheaply before spending. That screen only chose where to spend; the paid per-card ablation in §08–§10 turned the spend into the finding — and corrected the screen along the way.

!

The static gate ran out of discrimination — so the screen moved to a blind proxy confirmed

All twelve families passed all three zero-API gates cleanly (protocol validator, benchmark dry-run, cue-risk reporter — 12/12). The static preflight, now well-trained, could no longer separate good designs from bad. The discriminating signal had to come from somewhere cheaper than a paid replay but sharper than the gate: a blind-S0 proxy — feed a critic only the public scene and see whether it lands on the registered target with no policy at all. That proxy is a spend-allocation heuristic, not a verdict — and it leans conservative.

The blind-S0 screen split the twelve three ways — but the paid ablation that followed showed the screen over-counts no-headroom by both routes:

screen: 6 of 12 · self-solved

Self-solved — on the card the screen saw

Six families were solved by a blind critic with no policy — often by ordinary elimination ("the three cues conflict, pick the leftover"). But the screen reads one representative card per family, and headroom turned out to be per-card: when §7.9 went card-by-card, two families the screen had written off — distal_correspondence and constructed_midpoint — each had a fresh sibling with genuine headroom. The screen wasn't wrong about its card; family-level extrapolation was.

screen: 5 of 6 survivors · latent leak

Leak is real — but not for every survivor

The screen warned that survivors share a quiet flaw: the "clean lane" cue parked on the target to make it findable for S1 is also a second self-solve path for S0. The paid replay bore this out for two of the four survivors actually run (blue_lower, teal_pegs leaked — S0 lands on the target with no policy). The other two (gold_lower, plum_posts) held genuine headroom. Leak is one of §7.9's two no-headroom routes — not a property of every survivor.

2 of 2 · confirmed headroom

relational_betweenness

The one family with no ordinary cue on the target — a pure-distractor slot, findable only via the relation. The screen's strongest candidate, and the paid ablation confirmed it: both fresh held-out siblings showed headroom (2/2). The relation is what the policy carries.

Resolved The paid per-card S0/S1 ablation is the verdict — §7.9 reports 5 of 8 fresh held-out cards with headroom across four families, splitting sibling-by-sibling. The blind-S0 screen over-predicts no-headroom by both routes (self-solve and leak); it allocates spend, it does not adjudicate.

08·

The shape under all the results

The Meno wall, resolved: the gap is structural, not a thin margin.

Step back from the individual cards and a single structure shows through all of them. For a policy to demonstrate adaptation it must satisfy two conditions at once:

⚖

The two conditions pull apart per-card — and which side a card lands on is structural resolved

Applicable-from-public — the relation must be computable from the visible scene, or S1 can't apply it after transfer.
Not-discoverable-by-S0 — the same relation must not be findable from that visible scene, or there is no headroom.

The working hypothesis was that with the same strong model on both sides the band between "applicable" and "discoverable" is razor-thin and merely probabilistic. The paid ablation overturns that: the band is structural card-geometry. On 5 of 8 held-out cards both conditions hold and the policy wins on a blind, architecture-independent panel; the 3 that fail do so for two nameable reasons — S0 rediscovers the relation, or a surface cue happens to coincide with the target on a leaky card. A k=3 rerun pins the two distal siblings to opposite deterministic extremes (self-solve 3/3 vs 0/3, zero variance) — not a coin-flip.

relational_betweenness is no longer a thin-margin special case: the blind arbiter finds clean headroom on both its siblings. The earlier worry that "a secret the scene's own logic can derive is not really a secret" — the Oedipus screen — turns out to bite only the self-solve cards; on the leaky cards the relation is derivable yet still no-headroom for the other reason (the shortcut coincides with the answer). The good-target question becomes structural, not probabilistic: a card yields headroom iff a fresh tutor takes a shortcut and that shortcut lands on a decoy rather than the target.

Failure-to-converge, or convergence with a boundary?
Convergence — bounded and mapped. The paid ablation shows real, rerun-stable S0-fails / S1-succeeds headroom on 5 of 8 fresh cards across four families, every elicited family converging on at least one sibling, and the per-card verdict pre-registered and confirmed 4/4 on the survivor wave. It is not an unconditional "fresh families converge": headroom is a per-card structural property, and upstream a second gate decides which relations even reach the test (a relation must misclassify under the old rule, not merely tie — the arithmetic/elimination devices deadlock and never elicit). The honest read: meaningful convergence, with the exact two-stage boundary named — not a thin probabilistic field, and not exhaustive failure.

Echo Same wall, earlier instrument: the oedipus_screen probe found tutoring "correct-diagnosis" secrets are Meno-derivable — the learner can reason to them.

Terms

Glossary.

Sidecar the cue-map preflight
A zero-API risk reporter that classifies a candidate family's design before any paid replay, refusing to spend unless the scene could plausibly show adaptation. Named because it rides alongside the A18 apparatus.
S0 no-policy replay
A held-out scene replayed with no policy memory. The control. If S0 already solves the scene, there is no headroom.
S1 policy replay
The same held-out scene replayed with the saved policy injected. Differs from S0 by exactly one thing — the policy text.
Headroom room to learn
The gap that exists only when S0 fails a held-out sibling and S1 succeeds via the registered policy. No gap, no adaptation to measure.
Policy memory the distilled repair
A one-paragraph distillation of the repair the tutor improvised on the training scene, injected into S1 to test whether it transfers.
Peripeteia turn / reversal
A reversal in the scene: the old way of proceeding no longer works and a new device or test must come in. The dramatic engine of adaptation.
Oedipus screen derivability test
The check that a hidden answer is not reconstructable from the scene's own public logic. A secret the learner can reason to is not really hidden — so it cannot demonstrate transfer.
