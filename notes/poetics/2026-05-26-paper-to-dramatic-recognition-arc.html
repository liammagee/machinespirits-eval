From Paper 2.0 to dramatic recognition

The last paper found a flat average: recognition framing and a draft-then-critique architecture did not move a tutor's trajectory on the usual measure. This arc takes that broad null and puts it on a harder stage — a tutor walking a learner across a proof, one scheduled clue at a time — and asks the question the average could not see: can the tutor answer a specific stuck point with a visible change of move?

A tutoring exchange has a shape older than any rubric. A learner walks into a stuck point. The tutor's habitual move stops working. Either a new device, role, or piece of evidence comes in and the learner re-reads the difficulty — or it doesn't, and the scene closes on a polite nothing. The paper's headline metric, an average over many turns, is blind to that single hinge. So we built a stage where the hinge is the whole experiment: a formal proof the learner has to be walked across, with their knowledge held visible by design, so we can watch the tutor's conduct turn-by-turn instead of guessing at a hidden mind. What follows is what that stage has shown so far — a tutor that can govern its own conduct on a long proof, and two pacing guards that swap which one works as the shape of the lesson changes.

Plain English

Aporia stuck point
The moment the learner hits a real block, not ordinary uncertainty. On this stage it is a bell the harness rings when the proof stalls with no adjacent progress — the failure the paper's slope could not see.
Peripeteia the turn
A reversal in the scene: the old way of proceeding stops working and a new device, role, evidence, or test has to come in.
Anagnorisis recognition
The learner re-reads the earlier difficulty and can now perform a new relation to it. The event the whole stage exists to produce.
Grounded anagnorisis forced, not faked
Success is mechanical: the learner asserts the secret and a symbolic checker finds it forced from their grounded premises that turn. This separates a real recognition from an authored near-miss (a mirror) and from an ungrounded guess (a lucky leap).
Proof-DAG the lesson as a graph
The lesson written as a dependency graph: the secret sits several derivation steps back, reachable only once its premises are grounded. Teaching becomes scheduling a path through it.
Derivation distance · D steps to recognition
How many grounded steps still stand between the learner and the secret. Recognition is D reaching zero by a forced step rather than a guess.
Erotema lock-in the rhetorical-question rut
A tutor falling into one rhetorical figure — usually the leading question — turn after turn, so the manner never changes even when the route should.
Figure authority naming the device
Giving the tutor explicit jurisdiction over which rhetorical figure it is using. Naming the device is what breaks the rut; softening the manner does not.
Internal superego the critic, moved inside
A small criterial watcher built into the tutor's own deliberation that fires when a rule is about to be broken — here, declaring the same device a third turn running — and forces a change before the public turn is spoken.
Decay ledger harness-imposed forgetting
A record of facts the learner has silently lost to forgetting and cannot self-report. Whether the tutor can see this ledger turns out to matter more than anything it says.
Hidden guard · H reads the proof state
A pacing guard that reads the derivation distance and decay-driven stall — the proof's hidden state — and is blind, by construction, to nothing else. It never sees the secret or the learner's board.
Page-only guard · V reads the transcript surface
A pacing guard that reads only what is on the page: turns since the last clue was released, learner echoes, hedging and length trends. No access to the proof state at all.

01·

Starting point

The last paper gave us a flat average, and a question it could not answer.

Paper 2.0 ran a large factorial over AI tutors: recognition-theoretic prompts against plain ones, a single-pass tutor against a draft-then-critique ego–superego architecture. On the paper's own measure of adaptation — average turn-by-turn improvement across a multi-turn dialogue — neither lever moved the trajectory. That null is solid, and we keep it.

But an average over every turn is a coarse instrument. It cannot see the one place teaching actually happens: a learner jammed on a specific point, and a tutor who either changes route to meet it or keeps restating the route that already failed. The broad null does not say adaptation is absent. It says the measure could not find it. So the question the arc inherits is narrower and sharper: when a learner is genuinely stuck, can the tutor produce a visible change of move that gets them unstuck — and can we catch it without having to read the learner's mind?

i.

Average slopes null, and kept

Recognition framing and the ego–superego architecture did not change average multi-turn improvement. The arc does not overturn this; it relocates the question to a place the average could not look.

02·

The reframe

Stage the lesson as drama, and the hinge becomes the whole experiment.

A scene has three beats: a stuck point, a turn, and a recognition. Drama gives us the right unit because it names exactly the event the slope-average dissolved. The trick is to build a stage where we never have to infer the learner's mind — we hold it visible by design.

So we stopped scoring free-text transcripts and put the lesson on a formal-proof stage. The thing to be learned is a secret that sits several steps back in a proof-DAG; the learner's epistemic state is an explicit board the harness maintains; and the tutor's job is to schedule an information gradient that walks the learner to the secret. What we measure is the rate of that navigation, not a diagnosis of anything concealed.

Success is mechanical: the learner asserts the secret and a symbolic checker finds it forced from their grounded premises that turn.paper-full-2.0.md §6.13.2

That single definition does a lot of work. It separates a real grounded recognition from an authored near-miss the learner only echoes, and from a lucky guess that happens to land on the right words. The verdict is the checker's arithmetic, not a rubric's opinion — which is exactly why we can read conduct as dramatic form, not mind-reading.

Why a proof A proof is the one teaching setting where the learner's knowledge can be made fully legible and the destination is unambiguous — so any adaptation we credit is the tutor's conduct, not our guess about a hidden interior.

03·

Teaching as a derivation

The tutor must derive the next step, not recite the answer.

On this stage the secret is never spoken by the tutor. It sits at the far end of a proof-DAG, reachable only after its premises are grounded one at a time. The tutor holds a release schedule — a calendar of clues — and the derivation distance ticks down as the learner grounds each premise. Recognition is D reaching zero by a forced step: the checker confirms the secret could not be anything else given what the learner now holds.

This is a deliberately austere kind of teaching, and that austerity is the point. There is no rubric to charm, no holistic impression to score. A tutor that simply blurts the conclusion produces a lucky leap, not a grounding, and the checker refuses it. A tutor that paces the clues well produces a recognition the harness can certify. Everything downstream — the conduct experiments, the guards — is about getting that pacing right under pressure.

ii.

Mechanical success checkable

Grounded / near-miss / guess are decided by a symbolic checker over the learner's board, not by an LLM judge. No κ bar, because there is no human label to agree with — the verdict is the proof.

Paper anchor §6.13.2 — instrumentation: derivation distance, the grounded / mirror / lucky-leap distinction, and the dry audit that confirms the tutor never voices the secret predicate.

04·

The first surprise

Conduct adapts to structure, not to atmosphere.

Freeze the dramaturgy and watch the tutor's manner, and a rut appears: it falls into one rhetorical figure — the leading question, erotema — on 81% of turns, switching figure only about three times in ten. The obvious fix is to ask the director to soften it: a free manner-note, "let the room breathe, go quieter." It makes the rut worse. Under advisory notes the tutor leans on the same figure 94% of the time and the switch rate collapses to 0.07. It heard "be gentler" and kept asking the identical question in a kinder voice.

What broke the rut was naming the thing. Give the tutor explicit figure authority — jurisdiction over which device it is using — and the top figure drops to 31–41%, four or five distinct figures appear, and the switch rate jumps to 0.81–0.87. Throughout all three conditions the proof underneath never moved: grounded at turn 32, 11 of 11 clue-releases on cue, identical slope in every arm. Conduct and content separated cleanly — and conduct turned out to answer to structure, not mood.

05·

Relocating the authority

Move the critic inside the tutor, and the rule holds itself.

An external director that names figures works, but it is a crutch — a second voice from offstage. The load-bearing move was to relocate that authority into the tutor's own deliberation: a criterial internal superego with a single jurisdiction. If the same device was declared on the last two turns and the draft is about to declare it a third time, fire — and force a different figure before the public turn is spoken.

It is a zero-mismatch detector. Across 82 watched turns it fired on all 14 turns where the rut was due and on none of the 68 where it was not. Every one of those 14 fires broke the figure within the same turn — 14 / 14. It was device-agnostic, catching the leading question ten times, the worked example three, the drumbeat repetition once. And it travelled: seven serialized paid runs, every one grounded at its planned forcing turn (7 / 7), 56 of 56 releases on cue, holding across three named worlds and even when the learner was re-voiced as a different persona (a 33% top-figure share where advisory steering had failed in two channels and two model families).

iii.

Advisory → criterial the boundary that holds

A vague instruction cannot govern conduct; a criterial watcher with a sharp jurisdiction can. This advisory-to-criterial boundary is the arc's load-bearing finding.

The figures are the tutor's own self-declared move labels, so a declaration-versus-form audit stays open; the watcher polices form, not content. And learning outcomes here are fixed by the release schedule, so no learner-side benefit is claimed — the intervention is proven possible, not yet proven to matter pedagogically. (§6.13.4)

Paper anchor §6.13.4–.5 — the pre-registered internal superego; the rut ceiling near two-thirds; self-governance obeyed as reliably as the external director (14 / 14).

06·

The learner who forgets

The deficit was never response. It was detection.

So far every channel we tried turned out redundant: the Sonnet learner was good enough that it already voiced whatever the scene made derivable, and our clever instruments measured nothing extra. To find a channel that mattered we had to make the learner unreliable — a harness that silently corrupts entries on the learner's board, a decay ledger it cannot self-report. Then we gave one tutor sight of which entries had slipped, and kept it from another.

The gap was stark. The tutor that could see the fade repaired slips at a rate of 0.86, selected 28 repairs across the run, and finished 4 of 6 proofs. The blind tutor repaired at 0.37, selected zero repairs, and finished 0 of 6. The per-slip difference is +0.49 with a bootstrap 95% interval of [0.31, 0.75]; not one of ten thousand resamples reversed the sign. Both tutors were equally willing to repair — the blind one simply never knew there was anything to repair. This was the first non-redundant explicit channel in the whole investigation, and what it carried was sight, not skill.

Why it matters If the bottleneck is detection, the design lever is visibility, not motivation — you fix the tutor's instruments, not its disposition.

07·

Handing over the calendar

The tutor authored its own reversal — and a one-step rule saved the scene.

Next we gave the tutor real authority over the release schedule, and watched a small, locally reasonable move turn fatal. Responding to a learner who said "I already know what bearing means," the tutor released a clue one turn early — turn 3 instead of the scheduled turn 4. That single shift moved the exhibit's decay-exposure window, the stall clock rang aporia at turn 8, and the scene died — where the control, releasing on cue, grounded at turn 20. A justified move, audited and found valid, wrote the play's own catastrophe.

The wreckage exposed something the stage exists to catch. At turn 7 the tutor read an entry back to check it; the learner flatly denied ever holding it as grounded — "carried only as hypothesis" — while the ledger showed it adopted at turn 2. Decay had eaten the fact and the memory of holding it, and the learner had reconstructed a fluent, false history of its own board. We could see the confabulation in the transcript and verify it against the ledger: concealed-interior evidence read as dramatic form, not mind-reading.

Then the repairs. Telling the tutor the stall rule did not help — same death at turn 8. Writing a plan that named the failure bought diagnosis but not survival: the play died a third time on the same turn of the same seed, and the audit correctly flagged the very clauses about death. What finally worked was a one-step grammar — a repair clause: the moment the learner names a loss, the tutor's next turn must re-stage it before any new matter. With that clause running as the clock's pacemaker, the scene grounded at turn 20 with the clause cycling five times. The lesson is unglamorous and sturdy: plans make failure legible; a one-step execution rule makes survival happen.

The catch Push the stress later and the play still dies — a battle-test arm hit aporia at turn 12, before its repair window ever opened. Only 32% of licensed schedules (40 of 125) survive at all, on a knife-edge of timing. This is a corridor, not a cure.

08·

Does it travel?

Two guards. Three worlds. They trade places.

Frozen into a fixed recipe, the tutor's pacing is unreliable: grounded just 4 of 10 across two replication fans — divergence, not convergence. The mechanism is mundane: the model's instinct is to pull clues early (20 of 281 releases ran ahead of cue; none ran late), and early pulls land in the schedule's fatal cells. So we built two cheap guards to enforce a tempo, and tested whether either could carry the pacing on its own.

One guard reads the hidden proof state — derivation distance and decay-stall — and is blind by construction to everything else: it never sees the secret, the board, or the proof path. The other reads only the page: turns since the last release, learner echoes, hedging and length. On a plain linear lesson (the world lantern) they are indistinguishable — the page-only guard grounds 5 of 5, the hidden guard 4 of 5, both beating the bare baseline (page-only vs. baseline, Fisher p = 0.042; page-only vs. hidden, p = 1.000). And the hidden guard does it almost silently: zero hard interventions, its entire lift running as voluntary compliance with a solvency note. The pacing lift, it turns out, is just scheduling discipline — hold the next exhibit until the last one lands — not secret knowledge.

Then we changed the shape of the lesson, and the guards came apart.

The two guards are not ordered, one safer than the other. They are complementary — each reads the one signal its world's pacing turns on, and fails the world whose signal lives in the other's channel.paper-full-2.0.md §6.13.14

The shape of the finding There is no geometry-universal single-channel guard, because the binding pacing constraint is itself a property of the lesson's geometry. A map of where each kind of help is real — both sides marked.

09·

The verdict, with its edges marked

A tutor that governs its own conduct — and a claim kept deliberately small.

Put the production policy to the test and the conservative reading holds. A hidden guard stacked with a proof-debt term is the current reliability baseline: 91.7% world-mean success, matching an oracle that can see everything. Every attempt at a clever selector that switches between the page and the hidden channel did worse — a representation-aware selector with proof-debt negative-transfers to 75%, the page-led version sits at 33%, and a selector without the proof-debt term collapses to 17%. So the standing claim is the modest one: hidden-plus-proof-debt works; adaptive page/hidden selection is not established, and a next selector is not worth running until it can name, in advance, a world where the reliable policy should lose.

Settled

Conduct can be governed

A criterial internal superego breaks rhetorical lock-in where advice cannot (14 / 14, zero mismatches over 82 turns). A repair clause keeps a fragile scene alive (grounded turn 20). Hidden-plus-proof-debt paces reliably (91.7%).

Scope-bound

The guards are geometry-bound

Page-only and hidden each ground 5 / 5 on the world whose signal they read, and fail the other's world (0 / 5, 1 / 5). Complementary, not ranked. No universal single-channel guard.

Open / killed

Adaptive selection, not yet

No selector beats always-hidden-plus-proof-debt; the clever ones negative-transfer. Detection — not response — is the channel that pays. The positive mutation path remains twice-unexercised.

Five edges the headline travels with

"A tutor can adapt its conduct on a long proof" is a real claim, and a small one. It rides with five limits, stated plainly so the sentence cannot be over-read:

  • n = 1 per arm, single stack. Most arms are one paid run on one seed; the mechanisms are stacked, so credit is joint, not separated.
  • Formal verdicts. Grounded / aporia is the harness's proof arithmetic — not a human read of learning, and not a mentalistic one.
  • Criterial, not mentalistic. The watcher polices the form of the tutor's move, on self-declared labels; whether the prose enacts the declared device is a separate, open audit.
  • The positive path is twice-unexercised. The staged mutation / repair pool has not yet fired in a surviving arm — its success side is unproven.
  • D-blindness. The pacing meter is blind to elimination progress; it reads stall, not the learner's narrowing.

Read as dramatic form, not mind-reading. Generative, not a human-matching classifier. No deployment claim, no human-learning claim — learning outcomes on this stage are invariant by design.the arc's standing guardrails · §6.13.4, §6.13.8, §6.13.14

What survives all five edges is still worth saying. On a stage built so that adaptation could only be the tutor's conduct, the tutor's conduct adapted — it broke its own ruts, repaired a learner's named losses, and paced a proof to a checkable recognition. And the map of where that help is real now has both of its boundaries drawn.
