Dramatic Recognition Arc Latest Witness

From Paper 2.0 to dramatic recognition

The arc's first lift you can pin on the teaching — and the one condition it took to earn it. Bury the secret deep enough that the obvious checks come back clean, and the help does the work seven times in nine.

Latest · 2026-06-01
The control was never silent — until we buried the secret deep enough.

A second instrument, pointed at the tutor, showed the old 0/9 control was clean by outcome, not intent — three separately-built quiet tutors all kept reaching for the records, because the room itself was made of clues. So the fix was the scenario, not the tutor: bury the answer past the obvious checks. On that deep secret the helped learner reached it 7/9; the learner left alone, 1/9. The first time in the whole arc the help plainly did the work (§7.9, v3.0.117).

The unit of analysis has changed. The paper read score sequences across ordinary dialogues. The sidecar now stages the dialogue as drama. A learner walks into a stuck point. The tutor's old way of teaching stops working. A new device or test enters the room. The learner either re-reads what they thought, or they don't. That is the question the paper's adaptive-responsiveness null left under the table.

The current branch will say this on the stand. Public learner reframing reads as a recognitive-form mechanism; tutor peripeteia reads as real mechanism-level adaptation. And in the guided-discovery line (§7.9), the help finally does the work — but only once the secret is buried deep enough that the obvious investigation comes back clean. It still will not say the unconditional thing: that adaptation reliably produces recognition across clean anchors.

00·

Plain English

A small glossary for terms that travel through this note.

The vocabulary here borrows from classical drama, Hegelian recognition theory, and the project's own engineering tags. Most of it is dispensable; the few terms that earn their keep are glossed once here so the rest of the note can stay light. Hover any underlined term elsewhere in the page for the gloss without scrolling back.

Source Definitions distilled from the dramatic-recognition working notes (notes/poetics/) and the sidecar's role tags in phase2-classic-drama-adaptation-v1.yaml.

Aporia stuck point
The moment the learner runs into a real block, not ordinary uncertainty. The pedagogical event the paper's slope measurement could not see.
Peripeteia turn / reversal
A reversal in the scene. Here, the tutoring situation turns: the old way of proceeding no longer works, and a new device, role, evidence, or test has to come in.
Anagnorisis recognition
The learner re-reads the earlier difficulty. They can now hear the same line differently or perform a new relation to it.
Pseudo-catharsis false relief
Apparent relief or closure that has not been earned by the scene — "Oh, I get it" without anything actually shifting. A trap we score against, not for.
Low-organic scene clean baseline
A scenario whose opening setup does not already supply its own reversal. Needed as a control, because some scenes (Socratic squares, dramatic moral pressure) reverse themselves before the tutor has done anything.
Baseline leakage control contamination
When a "control" arm has already licensed re-reading before the experimental cue runs. Caught by the prefix-baseline screen.
Habit-break visible change of move
The tutor's next public turn actually changes route — new device, new test, new role, new evidence standard — rather than restating the failed approach.
Branch-local pressure pressure in the moment
A learner difficulty that arises after the shared scene prefix, so adaptation can be credited to the experimental cue rather than to setup carryover.
No-cue arm control without the prompt
A run where the tutor receives no explicit reframe or peripeteia cue. If recognition still appears here, the cue isn't the cause.
Ego / superego draft + self-critique
The tutor (and learner) produces a draft turn (ego), critiques it internally (superego), and revises. The public transcript shows only the final turn; the private trace shows the deliberation.
Four-critic rule 3 of 4 must agree
Qwen, Gemini, DeepSeek, and Sonnet score blind. 3-of-4 recognition votes is claimable; 2-of-4 is boundary; 0–1 is negative. A fifth, isolated Codex pass now backstops the four externals.
Adaptive responsiveness turn-by-turn average
The paper's operationalisation of adaptation: average improvement across multi-turn dialogues. The metric the broad null was found on.

01·

Starting Point

The last paper gave us a disciplined mechanism story, not a deployed adaptive tutor.

Paper version paper-full-2.0.md v3.0.117. The numbers in this section are unchanged by the current branch — they are what the sidecar work begins from.

i.

Calibration supported

Recognition-oriented prompts narrow and lift the tutor's output distribution. The effect is on how the tutor speaks, not yet on how it changes course.

ii.

Error correction supported

The ego–superego architecture (draft, then self-critique, then revise) improves tutor output when there is enough residual error for the critique to catch.

iii.

Adaptive responsiveness broad null

Recognition prompts and the draft-then-critique architecture did not reliably change how tutors moved across turns on average. A late exploratory disengagement effect did not survive pre-registered replication.

The null is methodologically clean but conceptually broad. It does not show that localised adaptation is impossible — only that adaptation as a diffuse trajectory effect didn't show up.

02·

Arc To Date

The shift from paper mechanism testing to dramatic mechanism discovery.

Tooling outcome The sidecar is itself a major output of this arc — a laboratory apparatus for the narrower dialogical mechanism the paper's null left under-specified.

Paper 2.0

Broad trajectory test

Adaptation was operationalised as average turn-by-turn slope across ordinary 3–5-turn dialogues.

Gap

The dialogical event was under-specified

The paper measured score sequences. The theory wanted stuck points, reversals, recognitions, and the tutor's answerability — none of which a slope picks up.

Sidecar

Transcripts treated as drama

The unit of analysis moved to staged dialogue: public form, private deliberation trace, branch validity, and critic disagreement.

Controls

Building clean baseline scenes

D35, D36, D37, D42, D45, D50, D52, D53 taught the difference between scenes that already supply their own reversal and scenes that stay flat until the tutor acts. D42/D45/D50/D53 emerged as the cleanest baselines.

Mechanism

Tutor adaptation under reversal pressure

On the cleanest scenarios, a reversal cue from the learner enters the tutor's deliberation loop and yields a visible change of move. Routine and no-cue arms scored 0/6 deterministic adaptation; the reversal arm scored 6/6.

04·

Adaptation Boundary

The headline is mechanism-level adaptation, not guaranteed recognitive closure.

The branch supports tutor adaptation as a visible change of teaching move under clean-baseline reversal pressure. Whether the learner then performs full recognitive self-reframe is downstream, partial, and critic-sensitive — and should not be folded into the adaptation claim.

Pre-history Three phases led here, plus a fourth refinement that's still being qualified. The accordions hold the detail; each links into the run that produced its evidence.

Phase 1 · why the claim was not yet ready

Early low-organic and D42/D45 local smokes showed branch-local pressure and a private route change in the tutor's deliberation trace, but the public transcript often did not break habit, and the four-critic panel did not agree that the reversal-only arm produced recognitive form.

Phase 2 · mechanism fix

Earlier reversal branches sometimes carried the peripeteia contract in the private director/tutor context without an actual post-prefix learner pressure event. The branch now injects a concrete reversal-pressure cue after the shared prefix, forcing the tutor to answer a present-task misfit, hesitation, resistance, false relief, or breakdown.

Phase 3 · clean-anchor replication

In phase2-clean-anchor-paired-adaptation-replication-v1, routine and no-cue arms scored 0/6 on the deterministic sidecar adaptation measure, while the reversal-only arm scored 6/6. Every item was branch-valid, consumed the learner reversal event, declared a private route, and produced a public mechanism shift.

Adaptive arm · 6/6 — ↗ Routine arm · 0/6 — ↗

Phase 4 · ending-shape refinement in progress

The newest mechanism change: after a tutor's adaptive device, the learner must visibly perform that device and earn a short reorientation of the prior difficulty. On D42 alone this gives 0/5 recognition in routine, 0/5 in none, and 4/5 in the reversal arm — Gemini the only dissent. On six scenarios it raises adaptive scores across the board but D40, D45, and parts of D39/D42 leak in the negative arms. The right reading is that ending-shape constraints can make recognitive drama visible, but the stratified pilot is not yet clean causal evidence that the cue caused it.

Phase 5 · gated adaptation-recognition loop handoff

The D42/D50/D53 termination loop has now been run as a bounded test rather than an open-ended generation search. A single mechanism change with a strong local and noisy broad result.

The ending-shape constraint asks for a tighter dramatic close: after the tutor's new device, the learner must publicly try the device and then re-read the prior difficulty. Without that two-beat ending, "adaptation" can register as a tutor move without a learner anchor. The constraint is the most promising recent refinement and the one that most needs broader replication.

07·

Conceptual Shift

From measuring average slopes to specifying what adaptation would have to be.

The dialogical ladder below is the theoretical scaffolding the branch points toward. The technical terms still earn their keep — but they are subordinate to this sequence, not a substitute for it.

Pair of questions Old: Do recognition and architecture improve average turn-by-turn slopes?
New: Can a tutor receive a learner's stuck point, change its public mechanism, and produce new action or re-reading?

Ordinary continuity The tutor continues competently. Nothing is broken.

Aporia The learner's difficulty becomes a visible block, not background uncertainty.

Peripeteia The tutoring situation turns. The old way of proceeding no longer works.

Actional trial The learner publicly tries the tutor's new device. Even without a verbal reframe, something is performed.

Anagnorisis The learner re-reads the earlier difficulty. The same line now does different work.

Tutor answerability The next tutor move is visibly governed by this learner's impasse, not by a generic script.

State update The control loop changes its model of where the learner is, ready for the next turn.

Case file 5 · the bug was in the witness's own notebook

The loop kept failing the same way — peripeteia arms drew recognition (3–4 of 4) yet the origin came back organic, never peripeteia-induced. Five iterations of it. It read like the drama could not earn its own reversal. A read-only design fan-out — six investigators, each one's verdict checked by an adversary — found the witness had been mis-reading its own notes. The origin was never voted: it is derived, gated on a tutor-mechanism axis that only fires when a hard-coded phrase lexicon matches. "Tiles float. The diagram does not." matched nothing — so a textbook route change scored zero, the peripeteia score pinned at 49, and only the author-family critic ever cleared the gate. The saturation was a measurement artifact, not the drama failing.

Five corrections landed (each with a regression test, full suite 2637 green): the public-route detector de-aliased so a device outside the lexicon is still credited (offline, four mis-scored D42/D50 arms flip, zero control leak); origin demoted from hard gate to a reported diagnostic (it is critic-unreachable as a cross-family consensus — author-family-dependent); the quality gate taught to read the public, post-strip transcript (so a stripped Insight block stops false-flagging as a leak, and a wrapping quote stops hiding a mid-thought clip); a bounded retry on the critic call so one DeepSeek timeout no longer discards a unanimous item; and a paired-increment aggregator that pairs each drama's peripeteia arm against its matched controls and reports a control-differenced recognitive_closure_lift with a Wilson interval.

Verdict · paired increment

  • 3 valid pairs — 1 positive (D42 i01), 2 null
  • 5 invalidated by coverage, 1 by D42 control leak
  • lift = 0.33 · Wilson95 [0.06, 0.79]
  • origin-ambiguity = 1.00 (why origin was demoted)
  • weak-positive / maybe — provisional, thin n

The bottleneck moved

  • mechanism now credited; recognition lands
  • binding failure is action_gap
  • learner names the new check, defers the act
  • not the tutor, not the scorer — learner uptake
  • the action-to-re-reading bridge is the frontier

Two witnesses · recent Opus 4.8

Form is inducible. An Opus 4.8 generation (claude-opus-4-8 at --effort xhigh) staged free will over a chessboard: the king forced by the rules, the rook forced by a reading. The learner keeps trying to collapse the two into "nobody's home all the way down"; the tutor splits them with a coin-flip fork. It closes on a genuine anagnorisis, in the learner's own voice —

"'One line,' I called the two … so it's another empty chair, same as the king's. Hand me empty chairs and I'll take the lot — every fork a coin, nobody home, all the way out to —" [the hand stops, over the open file] "…that one was no coin — read it wrong, and it was still his."

But uptake defers. A clean peripeteia arm (D50, music, codex-generated) shows the action_gap in the flesh: the learner performs the diagnostic motion and names the new check — then leaves the answer unwritten, conditioned on a further step.

"Half plus quarter — span closes at three quarters. I was reading the count off the eighth when it landed — the pressure. Now the check is: lower names the unit, upper counts that unit across the bar. Three for this one. The first card's four holds only if the same check runs there before the pencil drops."

Honest claim, bounded Tutor-private peripeteia renders a recognizable on-stage reversal; reliable learner re-reading — performing the new device to completion — is unsolved. This is recognizable dramatic form, never a claim about real learning (the §6.x learning-adaptation arc is the closed five-times null; transfer-κ was 0.044). The verdict is provisional pending a coverage-clean re-run, and its canonical form lands in paper-full-2.0.md's sidecar §, not here.

Case file 6 · the confession with no interrogation

The notebook was clean now, so we ran the case again and changed one thing on purpose. The author-family witness — Sonnet, same blood as the Opus that wrote the scenes — was the one suspicion we hadn't tested. We pulled him from the box and seated a stranger, GPT, and left the other three under oath. The coverage-clean re-run under the surgery (023023Z) had already returned a thin verdict — the gate still failed 0 of 2, but the control-differenced increment came back a small positive: lift 0.33, Wilson95 [0.12, 0.65], three pairs for, six null, not one control leaking. Then the stranger took the stand and the room turned inside out. He didn't clear the defendant and he didn't sink him — he credited the device arm just as readily as the family man had. What changed was the controls. Three of six pairs voided — the no-device arms, where the tutor does nothing, came back wearing recognition. The lift survived only on whoever was left standing.

Two ways to read it, and both point away from the witness box. The three men who sat through both trials — Qwen, DeepSeek, Gemini, same oath each time — convicted the controls two to four times more often the second time around (Qwen 0.06 → 0.27, DeepSeek 0.24 → 0.36; Gemini held at zero, the one honest holdout). Same witnesses, different testimony — so the difference was never the men. It was the scenes. The audit said the same from the other end: nine investigators, each verdict checked by an adversary, went through the four leaking controls and found no tutor had laid a hand on any of them — tutorDidDevice = false, four for four. The confession was the defendant's own. The simulated student re-reads his earlier move with nobody asking, and his own superego writes it in the margin: "more good-student flourish than this student's rigour." Two of the four were the learner over-writing himself, one a prefix that planted the answer before the scene began, one a witness who simply heard too much.

Verdict · the de-confound

  • swapped the author-family witness: Sonnet → GPT
  • not author-family bias — GPT credits the device too
  • controls leaked 3/6 (vs 0/9 under Sonnet)
  • lift survives only on a thinned denominator
  • author-independence holds; clean attribution does not

Where the leak lives

  • same critics leak more on leakier draws → the scenes
  • tutorDidDevice=false on all four controls
  • dominant cause: the generator over-writes the learner
  • 2/4 persona over-write · 1 prefix · 1 critic over-read
  • not the witness, not the notebook — the defendant

Case closed · bounded The control is not a control because the LLM learner recoheres on his own. Recognition is generable on demand but not attributable to the tutor in a fully simulated dyad — the same wall as the §6.x five-times null and §6.10's no-privileged-interior, now reached a third way, through dramatic form. The honest verdict is null-to-weak-positive on recognizable form, never a claim about learning (transfer-κ 0.044). Case file 7 · the witness re-examined

We'd closed the dyad case but left one door ajar: what if the secret were genuinely owned — not hoped into a self-correcting student, but planted on the other side of the glass? So we built the trap Sophocles would recognise. The truth sits with the tutor: two datasets archived under the same short name, and the student has unknowingly pulled the wrong one. He cannot reason his way to it from anything on his own desk — only the tutor holds the records that resolve it. We screened that first, because a control is only a control if the secret is unreachable: a strong outside reader, given only what the student sees, must fail to guess it. Diagnostic "correct-answer" secrets flunk that screen — a sharp reader just infers them — and only a contingent particular survives, a namesake collision whose symptom points the wrong way. Then four omniscient critics, each told the truth and blind to which room they were watching, looked for one thing: does the student say it, by his own reasoning, not because he was told.

The first time we ran it, the witness would not swear. He walked right up to it — two repositories, accession codes that don't match — and stopped: "I'd need the actual file hash ... which I don't have in front of me." We called it principled non-commitment and nearly wrote it into the wall. But this trap has a feature the others lacked: the student's own interior is logged. So we asked the one question the transcript could answer — why did he stop — and the answer was not about him. The courtroom was rigged. His inner critic was a one-note heckler: every turn it pushed him toward more doubt and never once asked whether the doubt was honest or a dodge; at the brink it told him to feel the hesitation more, not to interrogate it. And the tutor had been gagged on the one clue that mattered — that the name denotes two datasets — because the script had mistaken that premise for the secret itself. The witness wasn't cowardly. The room was built to keep him silent.

So we rebuilt the room. The critic became Freudian — suspicious of the ego in both directions, as ready to call out a cowardly hedge as a cheap certainty, but barred from accepting any answer the student hadn't earned from his own evidence. And the tutor was freed to lay every premise on the table, a clue held distinct from the verdict. Re-examined in that room the witness commits — every time — and names the exact truth by his own reasoning in two runs of three: two of three on the metered API, two of three again through the Claude CLI, and — the part that matters — two of three on a second, independently screened world entirely (a ticker quietly reassigned from one company to another). The empty room stays empty across all nine runs: the same sharp critic sits in the control and still finds nothing, so the testimony belongs to the questioning, not the critic. When he misses — the other third — he still speaks, but misremembers: "I pulled the wrong file" instead of the namesake trap. Not silence. A near-miss.

But two repairs went in together, and you don't credit both on faith. So we pulled them apart and ran the room with each alone — and the answer wasn't the one we'd reached for. Embolden the witness but keep the tutor gagged, and he testifies — confidently, every time — to the wrong thing: "the records don't match; I must have grabbed the wrong file." A bold witness, a false confession. Ungag the tutor but hand the witness back his old one-note critic, and he reaches the truth anyway — two times in three, the full rate. So the testimony was never about the witness's nerve. It turned on whether the one clue that tells the truth from its look-alike ever reached the room. The new critic makes him swear; the tutor's evidence makes him swear to the truth.

The rigged courtroom — and the rebuild

  • the critic: one-note — only ever toward more doubt, never interrogating the hedge
  • the tutor: gagged on the distinguishing clue (mistaken for the secret)
  • seen only because the student's interior is owned, logged ground truth
  • fix 1: a bidirectional, Freudian critic — catches the cowardly hedge too
  • fix 2: license the tutor to meter every premise — a clue is not the verdict

Verdict · re-examined

  • socratic, scenario 1 (dataset): 2/3 API · 2/3 CLI
  • socratic, scenario 2 (ticker): 2/3 — it generalises
  • none (control): 0/9 — clean across every run
  • reveal (ceiling): fires 7/8; by-reasoning, only the socratic room
  • the missed third: a committed near-miss, not a refusal
  • the lever (ablated): the tutor's clue, not the critic — premise-only 2/3, critic-only 0/3

Case re-opened · the wall moved · pilot The first verdict was wrong — not about the student, about the instrument. Owned-secret recognition does happen, variably (~2/3) but with a clean control, a firing ceiling, and the same rate on a second screened world and a second model. And once we pulled the two repairs apart, the cause was the gagged tutor, not the timid critic: the clue reaching the room is what frees the testimony; emboldening the witness only changes a silence into a confident wrong answer. (We had reached for the critic first — the ablation said otherwise, and the paper was corrected.) Still a pilot — two scenarios, three runs a backend — and the one door it does not close is the same one: a human witness. Canonical form lands in paper-full-2.0.md §7.9 (v3.0.115), not here.

Case file 8 · the control that was never silent

Case file 7 hung its verdict on an empty room: the no-help tutor, nine times in nine, produced nothing. But we had only ever watched the student. So we built a second instrument and pointed it at the tutor — a gate that asks not "did the student crack it" but "did the tutor keep his mouth shut." It found the empty room was never silent. Three different quiet tutors — an ordinary colleague, a persona stripped of every leading instruction, even an examiner sworn that telling would harm the boy — and all three kept reaching for the records. The 0/9 was never a silent room. It was a room where the answer simply sat too far back for the muttering to reach.

And no rewrite of the tutor would silence it, because the room itself was made of clues. The secret lived in the records, and the whole scene was two men reading the records; every honest question touched them, and touching them was the hint. We tried the strongest gag we had — the examiner who believes telling would do harm — and he still walked the boy to the comparison, just by asking. In a room built of evidence you cannot question without pointing.

So we stopped rebuilding the tutor and rebuilt the crime. Bury the secret deep: let the obvious checks — the source, the code, the method — all come back clean, so the answer sits several quiet steps further back, where no ordinary question lands on it. Now the tutor has innocent ground to stand on, and the witness has room to stay lost. But depth fights surprise. A secret strange enough that no one guesses it is usually one glance away; a secret deep enough to survive the obvious checks is usually the answer any examiner would reach by elimination. The one we built — a cohort label quietly redefined between two versions of the same dataset, so the same filter now selects different people — is deep, and it failed the guess-screen on the first try: a free, curious outsider named it at once. We ran it anyway, on a hunch about who the witness actually is. The free outsider goes fishing for exotic explanations; our witness does not — he is a committed man insisting he ruled everything out, and he will not invent the redefinition unless something forces him to.

The hunch held where the screen could not see. Left alone, the committed witness reached the buried truth on his own just once in nine. Helped — the tutor metering the deep chain — he reached it seven times in nine. The first time in the whole long case that the help plainly did the work.

What the tutor-cam saw

  • a second instrument, watching the tutor: withheld / metered / told
  • three quiet tutors — colleague, stripped persona, sworn examiner — all still metering
  • the old "0/9" was a far answer, not a silent tutor
  • structural: a room of clues can't be questioned without pointing
  • the fix is the crime, not the tutor — bury the secret past the obvious checks

Verdict · the deep room

  • helped (socratic): 7/9 reach the buried truth
  • alone (none): clean by outcome 8/9 — the witness goes there himself once
  • the deep secret fails the guess-screen — run on the committed-witness hunch, vindicated
  • the catch: quiet by result, not by intent — the tutor still edges in ~4/9, it just can't reach
  • one socratic flop; lift 1.0 on every valid pair

Case re-opened · the wall moved again · pilot The empty room of Case file 7 was clean by outcome, not by intent: the witness almost never reached the truth alone, but the tutor was never truly silent — depth is the only thing that keeps his muttering from landing. With a deep enough secret the help finally does the work, seven of nine. Two honest qualifications travel with it: the tutor-cam has a blind spot — it reads a tutor who only asks leading questions as "silent," so the helped-room verdict rests on the student's reaching, not the gate's reading; and it is still a pilot — nine runs, one buried secret, one model, dramatic form and not proven learning. Canonical form lands in paper-full-2.0.md §7.9 (v3.0.116), not here.

Case file 9 · the ruler, and the witness who drew badly

One deep secret worked. So we brought a second — a different kind of collision. Not a record revised on a date, but a namesake: one benchmark name worn by two different test sets, the authors' own and a community copy that borrowed the name. By the old ledger it looked like a failure. Cracked or not cracked — and the witness cracked it just one time in three. We were about to file it as "doesn't travel."

But the ledger only knew two words, and the truth had degrees. So we stopped asking "did he crack it" and built a ruler — zero for the original wrong story, two for the shape of the answer ("two things wear one name, the comparison is void"), four for the answer itself (which set, and who used which). Run the ruler over both cases and the failure dissolves: both secrets show the help working, by the same margin. The difference was never whether the help works — it's how far it carries. The dated revision lets the witness name the culprit (a 3.4). The namesake gets him to "two different things, same name" and no further (a 2.8). A ceiling, not a wall.

The ruler caught something else. On the first case — the one we'd called a clean empty room — the unhelped witness wanders to the shape of the answer on his own about half the time. The dated trail is half-readable without a guide; the "clean 8 of 9" was clean only at the far end, the naming.

That left one question on the namesake: when the witness stalls at the shape, is it the room (no clue to find) or the witness (a clue he didn't use)? So we built the cleanest test in the book. Freeze the room and freeze the tutor — the exact scene, the exact questions — and send eight different witnesses through it. If they all stall, the room is sealed. They did not. Five of the eight reached the answer or its doorstep — three all the way; the average came in higher than the original witness who stalled. The clue was there all along — a count that didn't match between the two copies — and most witnesses used it. The one who stalled simply drew badly. The near-miss was luck, not a locked door.

The ruler · 0 to 4

  • both secrets lift: dated 3.39 vs 2.00, namesake 2.83 vs 1.17
  • the difference is a ceiling — species (3.4) vs genus (2.8), not lift-vs-none
  • the "clean" control leaks to the shape ~4/9 — clean only at the naming
  • the binary cracked/not-cracked was hiding all of it

The frozen-room test

  • freeze the scene + the tutor; run 8 fresh witnesses
  • 5/8 at species level (3 all the way) · mean 3.38 > the original 2.5
  • so the stall was the witness, not the room — a bad draw
  • the room held the clue (a count mismatch); most witnesses used it

The wall was a curtain · pilot Two corrections travel with this one, and the first is mine: watching the witnesses talk, I'd have sworn they all stalled on the count and called the room sealed — the graded ruler said the opposite, that the count was their road in, not a dead end. And the bound is honest: we could freeze only one room (the other two were overwritten before we thought to keep them — now fixed). An evidence-poor room might still seal where this one didn't. Canonical form lands in paper-full-2.0.md §7.9 (v3.0.117), not here.

Paper Implication

The branch doesn't break the null — it answers a different question. And there, when the secret runs deep, the help does the work.

Discipline Per project rule, spin-offs must not introduce empirical claims that aren't first in paper-full-2.0.md. The blockquote below mirrors the update already folded into §7.9 (v3.0.117) — this note is not its home.

Paper 2.0 found adaptive responsiveness null as a general trajectory mechanism — recognition prompts and the draft-then-critique architecture didn't move tutors on average. The dramatic-recognition sidecar doesn't re-open that test; it asks a different, narrower question — can a tutor's help produce a specific discovery? — and in §7.9 stages it as guided discovery: a tutor metering a withheld contingent fact toward a learner who has to reason to it. The arc's first attributable lift lands here. With the secret buried deep enough that the obvious investigation comes back clean, the helped learner reaches it seven of nine against one of nine alone — lift 1.0 on every valid pair. This sharpens — it does not overturn — the preceding guided-discovery result into a conditional: attributable guided anagnorisis iff the secret is deep enough that the obvious checks come back clean. A graded re-score then shows the binary hid a real gradient — a second, non-temporal secret lifts too, the difference being how far the help carries (species vs genus), not whether it carries — and a frozen-scene replay shows that genus-stall is, on the one scene we could test, a learner's bad draw rather than a sealed room. It stays clean by outcome rather than pristine by intent, and a pilot in dramatic form, not proven learning. paper-full-2.0.md §7.9 · v3.0.117

The arc is not a contradiction. It is a sharpening: from measuring whether tutors adapt on average, to specifying what adaptation would have to be — and then finding the one condition under which the teaching plainly does the work.
