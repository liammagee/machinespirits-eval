How to build the ideal AI tutor

An evidence-ordered build sequence — what to put in, in what order, what to leave out, and what no one has shown yet. Drawn entirely from this project's findings, with every step cited to the paper.

The single highest-leverage move in building an AI tutor is a prompt-level one, and it is the same on every model tested: write the tutor's system prompt around an intersubjective-pedagogy stance — treat the learner as an active thinking subject and build on what this learner actually said before responding. That one change produces a very large, cross-model quality gain (judge-pooled d ≈ 1.63), and it works by lifting the floor — removing the catastrophic answers and making the tutor reliably adequate — not by raising the ceiling. Everything below is downstream of getting that right: add a critic where the model is weak enough to need it, match the scaffolding to the model, measure honestly, and resist the elaborations the data has already ruled out. Read this as a recipe, not a result; the results live in the paper.

  • supported  replicated across the three tested generation models and three independent judges.
  • model-dependent · bounded  real, but holds only within conditions (a capability range, a single-turn setting, a narrow scope) you must respect.
  • research-only · skip · null  either experimental and not yet settled, or something the data shows is not worth building.

One caveat sits over the whole page read first

Every quality number here is an LLM judge scoring a tutor that talked to a simulated learner. The mechanisms are about what the tutor produces, which is observable whoever the learner is — but whether better tutor output causes better human learning is untested, and is the headline open question (§8.1).

01·

Start at the prompt — the strongest, model-invariant lever

Before any architecture, get the stance into the system prompt.

The first three steps are all prompt-level, and the first one is the whole ballgame. None of it needs a second agent.

1

Write an intersubjective-pedagogy system prompt supported

Do. Instruct the tutor to treat the learner as an autonomous thinking subject and to engage the learner's specific contribution before responding. Encode three operational rules: (a) if the learner offers an interpretation, find what is valid in it before complicating or correcting; (b) if the learner is confused but engaged, pose a question rather than resolving prematurely; (c) if the learner rejects a suggestion or shows frustration, name what you got wrong and acknowledge the rupture before offering a corrected path. The reference text is Appendix A of the paper; the working implementation is tutor-ego-recognition.md and cells 5–8.

Why. This is the largest reliable effect in the entire study and it needs no extra agents: judge-pooled d ≈ 1.63 (up to 1.84–1.92 per model, unanimous across all 9 judge × run cells), replicating across five content domains (d = 1.45 math to 2.71 philosophy) and a peer-support coaching domain (1.57). It is floor-lifting: on DeepSeek the worst-response floor rises from 5.4 to about 22 while the ceiling moves only 52.5 → 71.0, and the biggest gains land on the weakest baseline dimensions (productive difficulty +1.33, elicitation +1.18). It works without a superego.

Caveat. This makes the tutor reliably adequate, not brilliant — do not promise excellent answers. The Sonnet judge runs ~20% hotter than the panel, so treat single-judge figures as an upper bound; the magnitude is a "very large" class, not a portable constant — re-measure per domain and model.

2

Ground it in any constructivist tradition — never behaviourist supported

Do. Author the stance against whichever named constructivist theory you can write most concretely — Vygotsky's zone of proximal development, Piaget's assimilation/accommodation, Kapur's productive failure, Chi's ICAP, VanLehn's step-level scaffolding, Graesser's affective tutoring. They are interchangeable. Do not build it around drill / reinforcement / mastery-gating / transmission framing, and do not spend effort chasing Hegelian "recognition" vocabulary as if the words were the lever.

Why. A length-matched constructivist prompt with all recognition wording stripped out scores indistinguishably from the recognition prompt (within-family pooled d = 0.14–0.19; A10 0.17, A10b 0.15; below the pre-registered |d| < 0.2 threshold). The active ingredient is the orientation, not the vocabulary. A length-matched behaviourist prompt scores d = 0.89 below a generic baseline — the between-family gap (d = 1.38) is roughly nine times the within-family residual. A careful prompt in the wrong family is worse than a short generic one.

Caveat. The behaviourist backfire is the strong-model statement; on a weaker model it softened to merely inert (d = −0.02). Safe floor: the wrong family is "at best inert." Cite the pooled within-family band, not a single judge's reading.

3

Let the stance drive real moves; never hard-mandate empathy markers model-dependent

Do. Have the tutor genuinely diagnose where the learner's thinking is and hand initiative back — often, in an open reply, via a question. Log question-marks-per-turn as a cheap live proxy that the stance is firing. Do not add rules like "always paraphrase the learner" or "always validate," and do not force the final turn of a dialogue to end on a question.

Why. A calibrated tutor asks far more questions (5.4× more, pooled 0.12 → 0.67 per turn; up to 19.8× on the weakest model), and question frequency mediates 42% of the first-turn and 91% of the holistic effect (Sobel p < .001). But you cannot fake it: within any one prompt condition, surface acknowledgement markers correlate negatively with quality (r = −0.18 to −0.38) — verbose empathy shows up more in weaker answers. The judge rewards substance, not costume.

Caveat. "End on a question" is a within-cell mediator only in single-turn replies (5/5 tested); in multi-turn dialogue it reverses sign (4/5), because ending the last turn on a question reads as leaving things unresolved. Question-asking is one channel of the stance, not the stance itself.

02·

Add a critic — then know when to remove it

A second agent that the first can refuse, used only where the model needs it.

The error-correction architecture is real and worth building — but on a capable model a good prompt makes it nearly redundant. Treat the prompt and the critic as substitutes, not an additive stack.

4

Build ego → superego → ego-revision, with the ego as final author supported

Do. Run one tutor turn as: the ego drafts; a bounded review loop (cap ≈ 2 rounds) where a superego returns {approved, interventionType, feedback, suggestedChanges}; on non-approval the ego revises. Give the ego final authority — the superego never writes learner-facing text, and even on "approved with suggestions" the ego does one last incorporation pass. Make the superego reject-by-default against named criteria (specificity, evidence, appropriateness, tone). Add two early-exit valves: approval, and a similarity-convergence check that stops the loop when a revision is ≥ ~0.8 similar to the prior draft. Mirror the same shape on the learner side.

Why. A critic catches pedagogical failures the tutor alone misses: under a bare prompt the superego adds +9.0 (DeepSeek), +15.0 (Haiku), +26.9 (Gemini Flash) points, with an 86.7% rejection rate and ~1.82 revision rounds per turn. The load-bearing part is genuine revision-receptivity, not just having a critic — which is why reject-by-default and the ego's retained authority matter.

Caveat. "We added a critic" is not error correction — measure rejection rate and round counts and confirm revisions change strategy. The cost is real: a 10-turn multi-agent dialogue is ~225,000 input tokens over 64 API calls.

5

Pick one primary mechanism by model capability model-dependent

Do. On a capable model (roughly Haiku-4.5-class or above), ship single-agent recognition and drop the superego to save its latency and tokens. On a weaker or cheaper model — or for emotionally intense moments — keep it.

Why. Once a strong recognition prompt is in place, the critic's base-prompt benefit collapses: the architecture delta goes from +9.0 / +15.0 / +26.9 (base) to +0.2 / −0.7 / +12.3 (recognition). A dedicated isolation run shows the superego adds d = 1.13 under base but d = 0.08 (n.s.) under recognition — calibration pre-empts ~88% of the critic's contribution, with a stable 15–17% additivity deficit. The residual stays large only on the weakest model (Gemini Flash +12.3), where base quality is low enough that calibration leaves failures uncorrected.

Caveat. This is calibrated on three at-least-moderately-capable models; it is robust in direction but judge-sensitive in magnitude. Re-run the substitution check whenever you change the base model — the very-weak and frontier tiers are untested.

03·

Fit the system to the model — and set the right expectation

Scaffolding is an inverted U; the win lands on the tutor, not (usually) the learner.

6

Match scaffolding weight to model capability model-dependent

Do. Author two prompt tiers and select by capability. Heavy (weak models): explicit step-by-step elicitation rules, diagnostic-probe algorithms, anti-patterns, worked examples. Light (strong models): a terse identity, one or two heuristics, and tonal permission to use abilities the model already has. Architect the prompt as a function of model → tier, swappable at deploy time; co-design the tutor and learner-role prompts together rather than editing one side alone.

Why. The peak of the scaffolding-vs-quality curve depends on the model. A weak 9B model more than doubled on a hard scenario with ~300 lines of rules (35.0 → 75.0, +114%); the same heavy treatment degraded a strong model (Haiku 90.0 → 73–85), which only improved with a light ~60-line edit (to 95.0). Transfer is asymmetrically destructive: a heavy prompt tuned on the weak model regressed the strong one to 62.2 — 27.8 points below its own untuned baseline (a "cognitive straitjacket").

Caveat. Small-N, single-scenario, two-model evidence — a clear direction, not a benchmarked law. The capability threshold is bracketed (between a 9B model and Haiku 4.5), not point-located. An automatic capability-keyed selector is motivated but unproven engineering. This is prompt-level scaffolding — do not confuse it with the learner-profiling "prosthesis" (see Skip), which does not help weak models.

7

You are building a better tutor; learner gains appear mainly on weak models bounded

Do. Instrument and reward tutor-side behaviour (elicitation, question-asking, building on input). Do not use a short-window simulated-learner score as your headline metric. If learner benefit is the goal, expect it mainly on weak/cheap models, and validate with real, capacity-limited novices on understanding and transfer — not satisfaction.

Why. Both supported mechanisms act on tutor production, not learner reception. On strong models the tutor effect is 7–12× the learner effect (tutor d ≈ 1.85 vs learner 0.16–0.25), and dialogue quality tracks the tutor almost perfectly (r > 0.99) — the recognition prompt is only in the tutor's context. But on a weaker model (Gemini Flash) recognition produces a large learner effect (d ≈ 1.2, ratio 1.6:1), because a weak base tutor produces output so poor the learner has nothing to engage until recognition rescues it. That is the strongest signal real learners may benefit.

Caveat. About half the 7–12× ratio is inflated by the content-weighted rubric (the holistic gap is ~56% smaller), though the direction survives. Do not claim short-session learning gains from a synthetic-learner uplift on a strong model.

04·

Build the observability spine

A per-turn rubric, symmetric traces, and a multi-judge panel.

8

Score every turn; log every step symmetrically; never trust one judge supported

Do. Score each tutor turn with a rubric whose dimensions map onto the prompt's rules (the v2.2 rubric: pedagogical craft 0.20; perception, elicitation, adaptive responsiveness, recognition quality 0.15 each; productive difficulty 0.10; epistemic integrity and content accuracy 0.05 each). Emit a structured trace for every step with symmetric labels on both sides (tutor context_input → ego/generate → superego/review → final_output, mirrored for the learner). Use a pre-registered multi-judge panel and report per-judge effect sizes.

Why. Mapping dimension names to prompt rules (Recognition Rule → recognition_quality; Productive Struggle → productive_difficulty) is what lets you verify the prompt does what it claims. All quality numbers are LLM-as-judge, which can reward surface markers and disagree in magnitude — direction is unanimous across three judges but means differ (Sonnet 1.88, GPT-5.4 1.56, Gemini Pro 1.44). A panel with per-judge reporting stops you optimising toward the judge that rewards empathy-as-costume.

Caveat. Despite eight named dimensions, one factor explains ~80.7% of variance (only content accuracy separates as a second facet). Treat the rubric as "overall quality + a content-accuracy modifier," not a panel of independently tunable skills.

05·

Adaptation — front-load turn 1; govern conduct with checks, not advice

The tutor does not "warm up." Get the first turn right.

9

Spend on turn 1; for a real adaptive rule, use a checkable trigger at the point of action bounded

Do. Get the first turn right rather than betting the tutor improves across the session. If you have a specific, checkable conduct rule (change tactic when the learner stalls; re-teach a forgotten item), encode it as a criterial, arithmetic trigger evaluated each turn with authority to revise the draft in place — e.g. "same rhetorical device on the last two turns and drafting a third → force a change this turn," or "if the learner names a loss, re-teach it next turn before any new content." Relocate the check into the tutor's own superego. Do not rely on soft guidance ("be adaptive," "pace yourself").

Why. Within a short session, adaptive responsiveness is a clean, well-powered null: recognition raises the level the tutor starts at, not the rate it improves (tutor slope d = 0.03, N=432 with 80% power for d ≥ 0.27; all factors d ≤ 0.15). One exploratory 10-turn effect failed pre-registered replication, and a memory-augmented self-rewriting critic also produced a slope null. By contrast, dispositional advice backfires (advisory notes worsened device lock-in to 94% vs 81% control), while a criterial within-turn watcher broke 14/14 due interventions with 0 mismatches over 82 turns. Gains come from new checkable structure at the point of action.

Caveat. A conduct watcher is a ceiling on degenerate repetition, not a source of invention — the disposition still lives in the base policy. All conduct-governance results are n = 1-per-arm descriptive verdicts on a single formal-proof drama stack, with formal (not human-learning) success criteria.

06·

The frontier — promising, not settled

Two architectures worth a prototype, clearly flagged research-only.

10

Optionally prototype id-director charisma and conduct-governance guards research-only

Do. For anti-routinising / charismatic teaching, prototype per-turn persona re-authoring (a back-stage "id" rewrites the tutor's whole prompt each turn) — but pick one specialisation (recognition or charisma; the maximally-both tutor is not achievable) and give the persona author ~600–1200 tokens, never terse. For reliable pacing in a structured curriculum, ship one robust obligation-tracking baseline (hidden state + an explicit unmet-prerequisite "proof-debt" graph), match the pacing signal to the task's structure, and keep conduct / ownership overlays as audit tools, not runtime authorities. Add a state-scramble placebo to your harness (permute the agent's internal state estimate; confirm performance collapses) to prove an "adaptive" controller actually depends on its state.

Why. The id-director topology yields clean design points (a recognition-tuned id wins recognition quality at 80.6; a charisma-tuned id wins charisma at 71.0; terse 200–400-token prompts fail both), and its charismatic lift is scenario-conditional (a 57-point gap on hard disclosures vs 4 on sympathetic ones). For conduct, a typed action-contract controller preserved correct strategy shifts (10/10) while raising judged quality (+21.3 tutor last-turn; +11.7 composite quality) on a held-out simulated suite, and the state-scramble placebo collapsed shift to 0/6 and 0/8 — proving genuine state-dependence. A static hidden+proof-debt baseline reached 91.7% world-mean success, matching the oracle, where a clever channel selector reached 75%.

Caveat. The id-director family is single-judge, single model-pair, outside the validated core. Every conduct-governance positive is simulated, single-stack, n = 1-per-arm, formally verified, with the destination fixed by the release schedule — none is evidence of human learning. There is provably no single-channel pacing guard safe across problem shapes, and intervention frequency does not predict success. Present all of this as experimental, not best practice.

07·

The skip list

What the data says is not worth building.

A blueprint earns its keep as much by what it leaves out. Each of these is a documented null or a backfire — listed so the budget goes elsewhere.

  • A multi-layer "Writing Pad" episodic memory. A controlled ablation flipped it off and the recognition effect did not shrink — if anything it was larger without the pad (d = 0.56 on vs 0.74 off; pad effect +3.2 in the wrong direction). The orientation lives in the prompt, not a memory architecture. §6.6.9
  • A bidirectional learner-profiling agent ("cognitive prosthesis"). Tested across six models over a ~26-point capability range: neutral on exactly one (d = 0.05) and substantially harmful on the other five (d = −1.29 to −2.59). Two equally weak models gave opposite results — low capability does not predict benefit. §6.6.10
  • Any feature whose value is the tutor visibly "learning the student" across a 3–5 turn session. Adaptive responsiveness is a clean, well-powered null (slope d = 0.03). Recognition sets the level, not the rate. Get turn 1 right instead. §6.3.2
  • Explicit Theory-of-Mind modules or richer learner-state schemas on a capable base model. Across multiple instruments these added no discrimination, and a richer schema sometimes did slightly worse. A capable model already infers learner state from context; bolting on a module re-encodes what it has. §6.8.5–.6 · §6.10
  • Hard-coded intersubjective surface markers (mandated paraphrase, "your X," "always validate") as a cheap stand-in for the stance. Within every condition, more markers correlate with worse answers (r = −0.18 to −0.38). The judge rewards substance, not costume. §6.1.8 · §7.10
  • Product logic that branches on "genuinely reasoned" vs "just complied," read from text. A pre-registered gate failed decisively (Cohen's κ = 0.084 vs a ≥0.60 bar); two careful annotators read the same revisions oppositely, and the label reduced to a length cue. It needs a counterfactual replay, not a finer reading. §6.12
  • An LLM "aha-moment" detector used as a proxy for whether a student learned. A dramatic-recognition instrument over-attributed recognition roughly threefold — fooled by the vocabulary of a breakthrough, not the fact of one. It scores the look of learning, not learning. §9 sidecar
  • Stacking the prompt and the critic and budgeting for additive gains on a capable model. They are substitutes: under a strong recognition prompt the critic is essentially neutral (Haiku −0.7 ≈ zero; DeepSeek +0.2). You mostly pay tokens and latency to rewrite already-good output. §6.4.1–.2

read me as a set of saved budgets. None of these is "impossible" — each is "shown not to carry the headline gain," so spend first principal elsewhere.

08·

What stays open

The unknowns a builder must keep in view.

  • Does better tutor output cause better human learning? Every result is on simulated learners. The mechanisms trace tutor production, which is observable whoever the learner is, but pedagogical value — understanding and transfer, not satisfaction — is untested. The weak-model learner effect (d ≈ 1.2) is the strongest hint that capacity-limited real learners might benefit. §8.1 · §6.5.1
  • Do the findings survive model swaps and capability extremes? Results are tied to specific model versions and three named judges. The supported prompt/architecture mechanisms should travel, but very weak and frontier tiers are untested — re-run the calibration and substitution checks on any new model. §8.3 · §6.6.5
  • Where exactly is the capability threshold that flips heavy scaffolding from helpful to harmful (and where a critic still earns its keep)? Both are bracketed, not point-located. Calibrate per model until finer sampling locates them. §7.8.3 · §9
  • Is the quality gain worth its cost in deployment? A 10-turn multi-agent dialogue runs ~225,000 input tokens over 64 calls. The architecture was built for observability, not deployment; the break-even is undetermined (single-agent recognition on a strong model is the favourable case). §8.7 · §4.2
  • Is there a form-versus-learning gap? Does a judge that rewards the look of good tutoring track actual learning, or reward costume? The aha-detector over-attributed ~3×, and the reasoned-vs-complied distinction was unannotatable (κ = 0.084). Human expert coding of a subsample is the outstanding validation. §8.2 · §6.12
  • Can adaptive conduct be governed generally, or only on a fixed-destination formal instrument? The bounded positives are simulated, single-stack, with formal success criteria and the destination fixed by the schedule, and no single-channel guard is safe across geometries. Generalisation to open-ended tutoring with real learners is unestablished. §6.12.3 · §6.13.15
