A Motivational Architecture for Conversational AGI

Anna Mikeda & Ben Goertzel · arXiv:2606.05411 · June 2026

Proposes a dialogue-native motivational homeostasis for conversational agents, recasting the OpenPsi/MetaMo motivational lineage so agents regulate competence, uncertainty reduction, affiliation, affinity, legitimacy, nurturing, and aesthetic coherence rather than bodily deficits. The agent's "sensorimotor loop" is linguistic; consequential actions are speech acts, tool invocations, and strategic silences.

Project relevance: The motivational drives—especially affiliation and aesthetic coherence—directly parallel recognition theory's relational ontology and may explain why recognition-enhanced prompts outperform baseline. Could inform how the tutor's ego models its own engagement state and why agents sometimes lapse into compliance rather than genuine Socratic challenge. The "legitimacy" drive maps neatly onto the superego's pedagogical oversight role.
Could accelerate current work: maps motivation-theory concepts onto the ego/superego architecture and provides vocabulary for explaining the recognition-prompt effect found in §6.3.
agent architecture motivation recognition theory ego/superego
Mikeda, A. & Goertzel, B. (2026). A Motivational Architecture for Conversational AGI. arXiv:2606.05411.

ReasoningFlow: Discourse Structures for Understanding LLM Reasoning Traces

Jinu Lee, Shivam Agarwal, Amruta Parulekar, Siddarth Madala, Dilek Hakkani-Tür & Julia Hockenmaier · arXiv:2606.05402 · 3 June 2026

Maps LLM reasoning traces to fine-grained directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) capturing backtracking, self-correction, and divergent reasoning paths. Validated against 1,260 traces (247.7k steps) spanning math, science, and argumentation tasks across five models; achieves high inter-annotator agreement in a 31-trace manual annotation corpus.

Project relevance: The DAG structure is a natural formalism for ego/superego deliberation traces: the superego's critique introduces a branch, and the ego's revision is a merge. Applying ReasoningFlow to the project's dialogue logs could replace or sharpen the current RevΔ/EgoSpec/AdaptΔ process measures in analyze-mechanism-traces.js.
Could directly enhance scripts/analyze-mechanism-traces.js — use DAG structure to quantify deliberation depth and backtrack frequency per cell.
discourse analysis reasoning traces deliberation graph structures
Lee, J., Agarwal, S., Parulekar, A., Madala, S., Hakkani-Tür, D. & Hockenmaier, J. (2026). ReasoningFlow: Discourse Structures for Understanding LLM Reasoning Traces. arXiv:2606.05402.

Agent Memory: Characterization and System Implications of Stateful Long-Horizon Workloads

Yasmine Omri, Ziyu Gan, Zachary Broveak, Robin Geens, Zexue He, Alex Pentland, Marian Verhelst, Tsachy Weissman & Thierry Tambe · arXiv:2606.06448 · 4 June 2026

First systems characterisation of agent memory, spanning flat retrieval, LLM-mediated extraction, consolidating fact stores, and agentic control flows. Analyses how agents must persistently store, retrieve, and update memory across sessions to sustain reasoning on long-horizon tasks.

Project relevance: Multi-turn tutoring dialogues are exactly the stateful long-horizon workloads characterised here. The taxonomy of memory architectures directly applies to the adaptive tutor (cells 110–113) and could inform how conversation history is managed across turns in the bilateral ego-superego loop.

Iterative Audit Convergence in LLM-Managed Multi-Agent Systems: A Case Study in Prompt Engineering Quality Assurance

Elias Calboreanu · arXiv:2605.12280 · May 2026

Empirical case study of Claude sub-agents auditing 7,150 lines of prompt specifications across a seven-lane multi-agent pipeline (AEGIS). Nine sequential audit rounds surfaced 51 prompt-specification consistency defects; the study derives a seven-category defect taxonomy and shows non-monotonic convergence consistent with cascading edits.

Project relevance: The AEGIS architecture (layered orchestration, coordinator+expert agent prompts) is structurally similar to the ego/superego prompt chain. The defect taxonomy—covering instruction conflicts, scope drift, role confusion, etc.—could be applied directly to audit the project's prompts/ directory, particularly the dialectical and superego variants (cells 22–33).

Ontology-Constrained Neural Reasoning in Enterprise Agentic Systems: A Neurosymbolic Architecture for Domain-Grounded AI Agents

arXiv:2604.00555 · April 2026 (v2)

Proposes a neurosymbolic architecture that constrains LLM generation with formal ontological graphs, ensuring domain-grounded reasoning in enterprise agents. Microsoft's Fabric IQ independently converged on the same pattern, providing industry-scale validation. The architecture preserves ontological validity while retaining the flexibility of neural generation.

Project relevance: The poetics rubric (config/evaluation-rubric-poetics.yaml) and the 6-dimension Aristotelian ontology are exactly the kind of domain graph this architecture targets. Constraining the critic LLM with an explicit poetics ontology graph could address the catastrophic inter-rater reliability failure (weighted κ ≈ 0.04 vs ≥0.60 target) by forcing the critic to reason from defined nodes rather than free-associating.
Potentially unblocking: the κ failure in poetics scoring may be addressable by ontology-grounded critic generation rather than free-form rubric prompting.

Rhetorical Questions in LLM Representations: A Linear Probing Study

arXiv:2604.14128 · April 2026

Investigates how LLMs internally represent rhetorical questions (asked to persuade or signal stance rather than seek information). Finds that rhetorical signals emerge early in layer progression and are most stably captured by last-token representations across multiple model families.

Project relevance: Tutoring dialogues are saturated with rhetorical questions as Socratic devices. Understanding that LLMs encode rhetorical intent differently from sincere questions—and that this signal is geometrically stable—suggests recognition-prompt design could explicitly target rhetorical framing to activate the right internal representations in the ego model.

Strategic Persuasion with Trait-Conditioned Multi-Agent Systems for Iterative Legal Argumentation

arXiv:2604.07028 · April 2026

Comprehensive empirical analysis of multi-agent argumentation where each agent's rhetorical strategy is conditioned on explicit personality traits. Evaluates 84 three-trait team configurations across 10 synthetic legal cases using DeepSeek-R1 and Gemini 2.5 Pro; quantifies how trait combinations affect argumentative dynamics and persuasion outcomes.

Project relevance: Trait-conditioning is the argumentative analogue of the project's learner persona modeling (services/learnerConfigLoader.js). The evaluation methodology—measuring which trait combos win arguments—is directly applicable to the dialectical superego cells (22–33) as a framework for understanding when adversary vs. advocate superego personas produce better learning outcomes.

The Presentation of Self in the Age of ChatGPT

Nils Klowait & Maria Erofeeva · Frontiers in Sociology, 2025 · DOI: 10.3389/fsoc.2025.1614473

Applies Goffman's dramaturgical sociology to AI-assisted professional work. Argues that LLMs handle backstage (invisible, routinised) tasks while humans maintain frontstage (visible, relational) performance, generating impression management dynamics that utopian/dystopian AI narratives both misread.

Project relevance: Goffman's frontstage/backstage distinction maps precisely onto the ego/superego architecture: the ego performs frontstage (visible learner-facing output) while the superego operates backstage (invisible deliberation). The impression management framework—and the gap between what is performed and what is actually processed—could yield new evaluation dimensions for the holistic and deliberation rubrics, and frames the poetics peripeteia/anagnorisis arc in sociological rather than purely Aristotelian terms.
dramaturgy Goffman impression management frontstage/backstage sociology
Klowait, N. & Erofeeva, M. (2025). The Presentation of Self in the Age of ChatGPT. Frontiers in Sociology. DOI: 10.3389/fsoc.2025.1614473.
