Agent Architecture

Multi-Agent Transactive Memory UNBLOCK

To Eun Kim, Xuhong He, Dishank Jain · arXiv 2606.19911 · June 18, 2026 · https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19911

Formalises population-level trajectory sharing between LLM agents by analogy with transactive memory in cognitive science: producer agents contribute interaction traces to a shared store; consumer agents retrieve and reuse them, avoiding re-discovery of known solutions. Extends RAG from individual context windows to an inter-agent knowledge commons, with efficiency gains across tool-use benchmarks.

Project relevance: The ego-superego critique traces already persist across runs to data/evaluations.db (the tutor_deliberation_* / learner_deliberation_* column families); MATM provides a principled framework for letting a population of tutor-cell runs treat those traces as a shared pedagogical memory — each new cell implicitly aware of what prior cells learned. The transactive-memory framing also maps onto Hegelian intersubjectivity — distributed recognition, knowledge constituted between agents rather than held individually — which could motivate a new §6.x discussion of what the cross-cell dialogue archive actually represents.

Sakana Fugu Technical Report: Training Scaffold-Aware Orchestrator LLMs WATCH

Sakana AI · arXiv 2606.21228 · June 2026 · https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.21228

Fugu orchestrator models are trained — not prompted — to dynamically compose and coordinate specialist LLM agents for a given task, achieving state-of-the-art results on SWE-Bench Pro, Terminal Bench, and LiveCodeBench. The headline finding is that scaffold-awareness must be learned into the orchestrator's weights, not injected at inference time by prompt.

Project relevance: Challenges the assumption that prompt-level superego instructions reliably steer ego behaviour. Fugu's trained orchestrator is the "what if the superego were fine-tuned rather than prompted?" counterfactual, most relevant to cells 13–14 (hardwired superego rules), cell 21 (dynamic prompt rewriting via Writing Pad), and the adaptive runner (cells 110–113), which already moves in this direction by externalising learner-state policy into programmatic constraints. Watch for implications on the current rubric v2.2 scoring of superego revision quality.
