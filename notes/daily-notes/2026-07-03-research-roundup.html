Agent Architecture

Behavior-Adaptive Conversational Agents: Toward a Fluid Personality Framework UNBLOCK

Hasibur Rahman, Smit Desai · arXiv 2607.01034 · early Jul 2026 (AAAI-26 workshop paper) · https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01034

Proposes a Fluid Personality Framework for LLM conversational agents that jointly adapts (1) the agent's metaphorical persona — coach, tutor, librarian, tool — and (2) its personality expression intensity — low, medium, high — to the moment, rather than fixing both across a session. Motivated by evidence that moderate personality expression beats low/high extremes on trust and adoption, and that context-appropriate metaphors beat a static one-note assistant.

Project relevance: A near-exact external framing of the id-director charisma family (cells 101-109): services/idDirectorEngine.js already constructs a per-turn persona envelope, and config/evaluation-rubric-charisma.yaml already scores register. This paper's two calibration axes (persona-type × intensity) are a ready-made secondary taxonomy to cross-tabulate against tutor_charisma_scores, and its "moderate beats extreme" finding is directly testable against cells 105-106 (tuned charisma-vs-pedagogy variants).

Can Agents Generalize to the Open World? Unveiling the Fragility of Static Training in Tool Use WATCH

Song-Lin Lv, Weiming Wu, Rui Zhu, Zi-Jian Cheng, Lan-Zhe Guo · arXiv 2607.01084 · early Jul 2026, accepted ICML 2026 · https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01084

Formalizes "OpenAgent," a problem setting for LLM tool-use agents facing distributional shift across query, action, observation, and domain dimensions, and builds a controlled sandbox with a four-tier shift hierarchy (Perception, Interaction, Reasoning, Internalization). Finds that agents trained via either supervised fine-tuning or reinforcement learning degrade to varying degrees under every tier of open-world shift — static training doesn't transfer cleanly to any of them.

Project relevance: A candidate classification lens for services/adaptiveTutor/'s trap-scenario suite (cells 110-125): the four-tier shift taxonomy (perception/interaction/reasoning/internalization) could sharpen *why* strategy_shift_correctness varies across config/adaptive-trap-scenarios.yaml items, and the SFT/RL degradation finding is a caution against any future plan to fine-tune the adaptive policy on past trap transcripts rather than keep it prompt/state-schema driven.

The State-Prediction Separation Hypothesis WATCH

Giovanni Monea, Nathan Godey, Kianté Brantley, Yoav Artzi (Cornell) · arXiv 2607.01218 · July 2, 2026 (date corroborated by a same-day news writeup) · https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01218

Argues that standard transformers waste compute by fusing two distinct jobs — maintaining a running world-state representation and placing a bet on the next token — into one residual stream. Proposes a dual-stream variant that explicitly separates state-tracking from next-token prediction and reports consistent 2-3% gains across pretraining scales.

Project relevance: Resonant theoretical grounding for why the runner: adaptive architecture (cell_110's LangGraph state-policy, services/adaptiveTutor/stateSchema.js) tends to outperform the dialogue-engine baseline (cell_114 / cell_125): the adaptive runner already externalizes learner state out of the token stream into an explicit schema, which is precisely the separation this paper finds valuable at the architecture level. Worth a citation in §6.8.4/§6.8.7 as independent evidence for the state/generation split, not just an empirical project-specific finding.

Dramaturgy / Role-Play

From Personas to Plot: Character-Grounded Multi-Agent Story Generation for Long-Form Narratives UNBLOCK

Aayush Aluru, Chloe Ho, Muhammad Hammouri, Kerry Luo, Myra Malik, Ryan Lagasse, Arjun Bahuguna, Vasu Sharma · arXiv 2607.00918 · early Jul 2026 · https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.00918

Introduces MAGNET, a multi-agent goal-driven narrative engine where persona-grounded character agents propose actions against a shared world state and evolving story goals, paired with ATLAS — a graph-based pipeline that compares scene-level world representations across a generated story to detect hallucinations and consistency drift in long-form fiction.

Project relevance: MAGNET's "persona-grounded agents + shared world state + evolving goals" is close kin to scripts/drama-generator.js's turn_plan and the poetics adaptation loop (npm run poetics:adaptation-loop). ATLAS's cross-scene consistency check is a concrete candidate mechanism for poetics:structure-critic / poetics:audit to catch drift in generated dramas' peripeteia/anagnorisis beats before scoring — currently consistency is checked only implicitly via the critic rubric, not via an explicit world-state diff.

Adversarial Pragmatics for AI Safety Evaluation: A Benchmark for Instruction Conflict, Embedded Commands, and Policy Ambiguity WATCH

Brett Reynolds · arXiv 2607.01153 · early Jul 2026 · https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01153

A benchmark grounded in pragmatics (how meaning is shaped by context, not just surface form) for evaluating how LLMs handle instruction conflict, commands embedded inside otherwise benign content, and policy ambiguity — distinct from surface-form jailbreak benchmarks in that the adversarial pressure comes from contextual/pragmatic construal rather than lexical obfuscation.

Project relevance: A pragmatics-flavored taxonomy of instruction conflict is directly applicable to the hardwired-rules cells (13-14, superego rules embedded in the ego prompt) versus the separate-superego cells (3-4, 7-8, 22-33): those two designs differ exactly in whether "policy" is embedded in-context or asserted by an independent agent, which is this paper's core distinction. Also relevant to naive baseline cell 71, where the absence of any critique layer makes embedded/conflicting instructions in scenario prompts hardest to catch.
