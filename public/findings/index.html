Recognition, pragmatics,
& the hidden structure
of LLM tutoring.

The recognition-theoretic prompt design works — but not for the reasons its Hegelian vocabulary advertises. After five passes of mechanism analysis on a four-cell matched-specificity corpus, the active ingredient turns out to be intersubjective pedagogy as a stance, not the master–slave dialectic specifically. A matched-pedagogical prompt grounded in Vygotsky and Piaget — with the Hegelian vocabulary actively suppressed — reproduces the recognition effect within d < 0.2. The single response-level mediator that survives every diagnostic check is small, surprising, and pragmatic: ending the tutor turn with a question.

d ≈1.38

§i.

Three findings, in plain language.

What the paper found, and what one might mistake it for.

The paper sets out to ask why recognition-enhanced tutor prompts produce large quality gains — and arrives, after a careful decomposition, at three findings each of which corrects an obvious but wrong reading of the data.

source Five-pass mechanism decomposition on the A10b 4-cell matched-specificity corpus, three-judge panel, n ≈ 191 per judge. Detail in paper §7.10–§7.10.1 and the methods companion §3.
i.

Recognition is one operationalisation of an intersubjective stance, not the only one.

Cell 5 loads a recognition-theoretic prompt with the explicit Hegelian vocabulary — mutuality, autonomy, Bildung, master–slave. Cell 95 loads a matched-specificity prompt grounded in Vygotsky's zone of proximal development, Piaget's assimilation–accommodation, Kapur's productive failure, Chi's ICAP, VanLehn's step-level scaffolding, and Graesser's affective-tutoring work — with the Hegelian vocabulary actively excluded by an authored block-list. The two cells score equivalently on the v2.2 rubric (~49 pooled).

What this means: the Hegelian content is decorative. The family-level stance — intersubjective pedagogy, the broad orientation that recognition theory and its constructivist descendants share — is what does the work.

One might still suspect the Hegelian vocabulary primes the generation model in some non-obvious way that the matched prompt accidentally inherits. The theory–vocabulary lexical analysis (D1 pass 1–2) rules this out: the embedding-channel similarity between cell 5 and cell 95 outputs is essentially uncorrelated with their Hegelian-lexicon densities.
ii.

"The recognition injection" can mean three different things — only one is load-bearing.

The §7.10 decomposition makes the distinction explicit. Most informal readings of the paper conflate the three.

Why "partially" for surface features? Because ends-with-question occurs in only 2–5% of intersubjective-cell responses; even if every such response scored 30 points higher than its peers, that base rate could not arithmetically explain the +14-point family effect. The feature is one observable channel of the underlying stance, not the stance itself.
iii.

Surface acknowledgement is a negative correlate, not the empathy signal it looks like.

The reflex operationalisation of "intersubjective tutor" is to mandate acknowledgement: quote the learner, paraphrase, use "your X" possessives, name what they said. This pushes the wrong way. Within most cells, broad-acknowledgement markers correlate with lower rubric scores — Pearson r = −0.18 in cell 5, −0.31 in cell 95, −0.38 in cell 1.

The pattern is consistent across the recognition cell, the matched-pedagogical cell, and even the generic baseline cell. Surface acknowledgement appears more often in weaker responses — surrogate moves that responses fall back on when substantive engagement is thin. The judges aren't fooled.

This is the most counterintuitive finding in the sequence and the one most likely to be misread. A naive reading of "intersubjective stance" as "more acknowledgement" would degrade tutor quality, not improve it. The substance the rubric rewards is not surface empathy but engaged engagement with the specific content of the learner's move.
§ii.

Why pooled correlations mislead.

Simpson's paradox, demonstrated on A10b.

Take the embedding similarity between each tutor response and a hand-authored intersubjective canonical (questions, scaffolding, inclusive framing). Plot it against the rubric score. Across all four cells, the correlation is positive — looks like a clean mediator candidate. Now stratify by cell. The two intersubjective cells reverse sign.

Toggle the regression mode below to see the reversal. Each point is a (simulated, paper-faithful) tutor response; clusters are positioned by the per-cell means and within-cell correlations reported in 7.10.

methods note Simulated 50-point clusters per cell, anchored to actual A10b means and within-cell Pearson r values (Sonnet judge, n ≈ 50/cell). The pooled regression's positive slope is driven entirely by cell_96 anchoring the lower-left.

The within-cell correlation is the diagnostic the methods paper proposes 8.6.1. Without it, you'd accept intersubjective-canonical similarity as a within-cell mediator on the strength of the pooled r alone. With it, you see that the pooled correlation is an artefact: cell 96 anchors the lower-left of the scatter, and the trend collapses (or reverses) whenever you control for cell.

on the rubric judges The pattern replicates across all three judges in the panel — Sonnet 4.6, Opus 4.7, GPT-5.2. Pooled r is positive in 3 of 3 judges; within-cell r is negative in cell 5 and cell 95 across all 3 judges. The Simpson reversal is a property of the data structure, not a Sonnet artefact. 7.10.1
§iii.

A portable diagnostic.

Three criteria for a within-cell mediator.

Methods paper §2 names the diagnostic. A response-level feature qualifies as a within-cell mediator candidate only when all three of the following pass.

  1. Large family contrast. |d| ≥ 0.5 between the family containing the recognition cell and a contrasting transmission family. The feature distinguishes the families. If it does not, it cannot mediate the family-level effect.
  2. Small within-family contrast. |d| < 0.5 between two cells inside the same family. Both family-member prompts elicit the feature; if only one does, the feature is a prompt marker rather than a family marker.
  3. Positive within-cell Pearson r in each family-member cell. With the prompt held constant, the feature still predicts higher scores. This is the criterion that rules out Simpson's-paradox false positives.
on the third criterion It is the existence of criterion 3 that makes the diagnostic do substantive work. Without it, a feature with significant pooled-r and large family contrast — the most common publishable shape — would pass through as a mediator. Criterion 3 catches the Simpson cases.

§v.

From paper to product.

Three ways the findings translate to chatbot design.

These recommendations are derived from paper findings rather than authored fresh. Each point cites the paper section it summarises.

on translation The intersubjective stance generalises across applications — D2 Path 1 found peer-support coaching gives the strongest within-cell mediator r = 0.739 in the entire D1 sequence. Same family-level pattern, different vocabulary fits the role.

tutor design

If you're building an LLM tutor

  1. Adopt an intersubjective stance. Recognition theory is one option; Vygotskian scaffolding is another. The vocabulary is interchangeable; the stance is what carries the effect. 7.9
  2. Don't operationalise the stance as surface acknowledgement. "Quote what the learner said," "use possessives," and paraphrase markers correlate negatively with rubric quality. Build for substance, not surface. 6.1.8
  3. End single-turn responses with a question when ceding initiative is contextually appropriate. The within-cell mediator that survived all five passes (r = +0.325 in cell 5, +0.392 in cell 95). Beware: in multi-turn dialogues, ending the final turn with a question reads as poor closure instead. 7.10.1

other applications

If you're building peer-support, customer service, or code review

  1. The family-level pattern is application-stable. Peer-support listener coaching reproduces the recognition effect at r = 0.739 — the strongest within-cell mediator in the D1 sequence, on a domain that's structurally not tutoring. 7.10.1
  2. Same intersubjective stance, different vocabulary. The vocabulary should fit the role: peer support draws on listening-as-skill traditions; code review on dialogic-critique traditions. The orientation transfers, the wording must be authored to context.
  3. Apply the §8.6.2 protocol before claiming your prompt design works. The same diagnostic that ruled out three of the four D1 candidate channels will rule out designer enthusiasm too.

evaluating your design

If you want to evaluate your own chatbot design

  1. Identify two prompt families you want to contrast: an "intersubjective" family (e.g., your designed prompt + a constructivist matched control) and a transmission family (a generic baseline + perhaps a behaviorist control).
  2. Compute candidate response-level features per row using the open-sourced analyse-d1-* scripts as a starting point. Author-specified regex sets and embedding similarity to canonical references both work.
  3. Run the three-criterion check on each candidate feature. Anything that fails criterion 3 (especially with a positive pooled-r) is a Simpson candidate. Hedge those claims. Pair with a PCA on rubric dimensions (8.6) to flag rubric over-specification.
