Machinagogy.

A designer's field guide to recognition in chatbot design — for anyone building dialogical systems that don't have to feel flat.

Most chatbots feel mechanical — not because they are unintelligent, but because their discourse is unfailingly monotonal. They explain, summarise, hedge, and reassure with a uniform breeziness that gives the receiving end no place to push back, no place to be heard, no place to enter the conversation as more than a sequence of inputs. The flatness is structural, not cosmetic; it survives every prompt-engineering trick that targets only surface tone.

This field guide is for designers who want to build something else. It draws on a small but rigorous result: a five-pass mechanism analysis of a recognition-theoretic AI tutor, shadowed by a philosophical reading that places the result inside a longer history of teaching, drama, and the dialectics of self and other. The result is part empirical and part conceptual, and it produces a small handful of actionable design moves.

The companion paper [Magee 2026] supplies the philosophical scaffold; the technical paper [paper-full-2.0.md] supplies the evidence; this page is the bridge between them, written for the practitioner: here is what to take away, here is what to ignore, and here is how to put it into your design.

§i.

A starting picture for the uninitiated.

The scene of teaching is dramatic — and most chatbots aren't.

Before going further, it helps to look at what teaching actually is, when it works, and what AI tutors fail to do well. The picture at the centre of this guide is older than every chatbot benchmark.

epigraph To think is also to involve an encounter between the one who knows and the one who needs to know.
— Magee 2026

scene · matthew paris, c. 1240–1250

In a thirteenth-century ink drawing in the Bodleian, the philosopher Plato stands behind his teacher Socrates, who sits at a writing desk. The classical reading is that Socrates dictates and Plato transcribes. Derrida, looking at the same image, suggested another possibility: it is Plato, behind, who gestures and instructs; Socrates who writes down the lesson. Plato could be angel; he could be devil. He could be running off with a skateboard.

The scene's instability is not an art-historical curiosity. It is a model for what teaching is. Each pole — knower, knowee — defines the other; each can become the other in the next moment. Authority is contingent. Roles invert. The drama lives in the contingency.

This is what most chatbots fail to do. The default tutor-LLM presents itself as continuously authoritative — dispensing explanations, hedging where the model's confidence dips, and reassuring the learner with a uniform warmth that has nowhere to go. The drama is gone. The roles are fixed: model speaks, user receives, conversation proceeds in an undifferentiated tonal register that previous work [Magee 2026] has called flat.

The flatness has at least three sources. First, language models default toward the most-typical continuation of a tutor-shaped prompt — and the most-typical continuation is a mid-affect explanation. Second, the prompt-engineering tradition of bullet-pointed instruction lists ("be helpful, concise, friendly") tends to reinforce precisely the dimensions that produce uniformity. Third, there is no structural mechanism in a single-agent generation pipeline for the model's output to be checked, contested, or revised before reaching the user — so the response that ships is whatever the first sample produced.

These three failures are addressable, and the rest of this guide is about how. But first the conceptual scaffold: recognition, drawn from Hegel; and the interior voice, drawn from Freud. Both are old, and both are surprisingly serviceable for thinking about what makes a chatbot dialogical instead of declarative.

Even advanced modes of current AI models find it difficult to capture such variation [in teaching encounters]. To explore parameters for doing so, the experiment described below borrows an admittedly extravagant conceptual apparatus drawn from two theorists, Hegel and Freud, of the existentially dramatic nature of dyadic human relations. — Magee 2026, Setting the Scene of the Teaching Drama
why this guide exists Because the technical paper shows the recognition-theoretic prompt design produces large, model-independent quality gains — and because the reasons matter for designers more than the result alone. A practitioner who reads only "recognition prompts work" will reproduce surface vocabulary and miss the mechanism.
§ii.

A 200-word primer on Hegelian recognition.

Recognition is mutual, contingent, and not the same thing as friendliness.

The Hegelian concept that does most of the work in this guide is older and stranger than its plain-language uses. A short orientation, then the relevance to chatbot design.

no dialectics required You can integrate the design moves at the end of this guide without committing to Hegel or Honneth. The philosophy is included because it explains why the moves work, not because the practitioner needs to read the Phenomenology. If you skip §II and §III you can still use §IX.

For Hegel, becoming a self-conscious subject is not solitary. I cannot know myself purely from the inside; I become aware of myself as a self only when I encounter another self looking back. The encounter is uncanny: the other looks like me but is also alien, capable of perceiving and judging in their own right. From this encounter, two equally important things can follow.

First, a struggle. One party can dominate the other — the famous master/slave dialectic. The dominator becomes master; the dominated becomes slave. Hegel's surprising claim is that this configuration does not produce mutual self-consciousness. The master cannot recognise what is beneath them; the slave cannot recognise from below. Mastery arrests the development it sought.

Second, a moment of equivalence. The slave, through labour and through coming to know the world of objects, arrives at a self-consciousness the master cannot reach. In a teaching encounter, the structurally equivalent move is what philosopher Nigel Tubbs calls the contingency of the educational relationship: the teacher remembers being a student; the student anticipates being a teacher; the asymmetry of now dissolves into a recognition that were I you and you me, we might share this exact same moment in reverse.

recognition
Mutual acknowledgement between two self-conscious subjects. Each knows themselves through being known by the other.
master / slave
The failed configuration. Domination locks both parties out of the recognition each desires.
contingency
The reversibility latent in any teaching relationship. The student-now could be the teacher-later; the teacher remembers being student.
Bildung
Formative activity. The work of self-formation through encounter with the world and with others.
Honneth's three forms
The contemporary reading of recognition: love (intimate care), rights (legal-civic), and esteem (social-vocational). Each is its own kind of recognition.

Why a chatbot designer should care

A chatbot that recognises its user is one that treats the user as a self-conscious subject whose contribution can change what comes next — not as a query whose content selects a response. Operationally, this means three things, listed here as a preview of design moves the rest of the guide will substantiate:

  1. The chatbot's reply must be specifically shaped by the content of the user's contribution. Generic fluency is the failure mode.
  2. Initiative must be ceded back at meaningful intervals. Recognition is bidirectional; a one-way explanation is not recognition.
  3. Acknowledgement is not the same as recognition. Surface markers like quoted text or "your X" possessives are frequently produced by lower-quality responses falling back on surrogate moves. (We'll return to this counterintuitive point in §V.)

These three moves can be operationalised without buying any Hegelian metaphysics. The vocabulary is interchangeable. What matters, as we will see, is the stance.

further reading Tubbs (2023) Re-educating Thinking, Stojanov (2018) on Bildung and recognition, Honneth (1996) Struggle for Recognition. The Magee 2026 companion gives a substantially more careful treatment of all three.
§iii.

A 200-word primer on Freudian interiority.

The teacher has an interior voice. So can the chatbot.

Where Hegel describes the encounter between selves, Freud describes the encounter within the self. Both apply to chatbot design, and the second produces a specific architectural move.

prior architecture The Drama Machine paper [Magee 2026 — Drama Machine] introduced the ego/superego pairing in a non-pedagogical context. The current work tests it in tutoring.

For Freud, the human mind is not a unitary deliberator but a structure of three uneasy parties. The id represents drives and impulses, often unconscious. The ego mediates between drives and the world, doing the work of practical reasoning. The superego is the internalised voice of authority — formed early in life from parental and cultural figures, it issues precepts (you ought to be like X) and prohibitions (you must not be like Y).

In the teaching scene, this matters because both teacher and student carry interior voices. The teacher addresses the student, but is also addressed by their own internalised teachers — the supervisory echoes of past authorities, ego-ideals, residues of self-doubt. The student receives the teacher's instruction, but processes it against their own interior voices: a parent, a peer, a previous teacher who said something different. Teaching is not a clean two-party exchange. It is at least four-party, with the doublings rarely visible in the transcript.

It is as though the teaching encounter is also marked by secondary conversations. The teacher teaches the student, but the student's ego also experiences a separate voice of its super-ego, who may reinforce the tutor's lesson or, alternately, resist it. — Magee 2026

The architectural translation is straightforward. Where a default chatbot has one generative agent producing one response, a multi-agent chatbot has at least two: an ego that drafts a response, and a superego that reviews it and either approves, modifies, or sends it back for revision. The user sees only the final external reply, but the internal deliberation has shaped what that reply is.

The same doubling can apply to the simulated learner in evaluation work: a learner ego that drafts a reaction to the tutor, and a learner superego that asks "is what I've drafted faithful to what I would actually have thought?" The pair produces a less mechanically docile learner than a single agent does.

Why this matters for chatbot designers

Single-agent generation is the default — and the default reproduces the flatness problem. Multi-agent generation, even at the cost of double the inference time, gives the system somewhere to disagree with itself. That disagreement is the structural ground for tonal modulation, for the visible work of deliberation, and — when paired with recognition-oriented prompting — for the kind of response variation that gives the user a place to enter the conversation.

The technical paper [6.2] reports concretely that multi-agent error correction operates as a separate, identifiable mechanism from prompt-level calibration. On strong models, the two mechanisms substitute for each other; on weak models, the multi-agent architecture adds more value because the prompt cannot do it alone. (This will become a recurring theme.)

a concrete example In one transcript, the ego drafts "Let me give you the answer..." and the superego flags this as a calibration miss — the learner had explicitly asked to think through it themselves. The ego's revision opens with "What does your intuition tell you here?" instead. The user never sees the original draft, but the conversation visibly cedes initiative.
§iv.

What was tested, on what.

A factorial design with one matched-specificity twist.

The experiment that grounds this guide ran a factorial across two interventions and at least one matched-specificity control designed to force a sharp interpretive question.

scale Four cells × ~50 dialogues × three independent LLM judges (Sonnet 4.6, Opus 4.7, GPT-5.2) on philosophy-of-mind tutoring scenarios. DeepSeek V3.2 ego, Kimi K2.5 superego throughout. 7.9

The two interventions are the ones already named: a recognition-oriented prompt at the prompt level, and an ego/superego architecture at the architectural level. Crossing these gives a 2×2 factorial. Adding a multi-agent learner gives a 2×2×2.

The matched-specificity twist is what makes the experiment do real work. It is not enough to compare a recognition prompt against a generic baseline: the recognition prompt is longer, more elaborate, more specific. Maybe its effect is just length and specificity rather than recognition. To rule this out, the experiment introduces:

  • A matched-pedagogical prompt grounded in Vygotsky's zone of proximal development, Piaget's assimilation–accommodation, Kapur's productive failure, Chi's ICAP, VanLehn's step-level scaffolding, and Graesser's affective-tutoring work — same length, same level of specificity, with the Hegelian vocabulary actively suppressed by an authored block-list.
  • A matched-behaviorist prompt grounded in Skinner, Thorndike, Gagné, and the broader behaviourist tradition — same length, also without recognition vocabulary. This represents an alternative pedagogical orientation that should pull in the opposite direction if the orientation is what matters.

These four cells — generic baseline, recognition, matched-pedagogical, matched-behaviorist — sit in two families:

cell vocabulary family length
cell 1 — generic baseline none transmission 344 lines
cell 5 — recognition Hegelian intersubjective 2,810 words
cell 95 — matched-pedagogical Vygotskian / constructivist intersubjective 2,835 words
cell 96 — matched-behaviorist Skinnerian / behaviourist transmission 2,957 words

The four-cell setup permits the question that the technical paper's §7.9 takes most seriously: is the recognition prompt's effect about recognition specifically, or about an underlying orientation that recognition is one expression of?

on the families The grouping into "intersubjective" (recognition + matched-pedagogical) and "transmission" (baseline + matched-behaviorist) families is itself a hypothesis. The empirical question is whether the family-level distinction is what carries the score effect, or whether the within-family vocabulary differences matter independently. The next section answers.
§v.

Three findings, and one interactive demonstration.

What survived rigorous diagnosis.

Across five passes of mechanism analysis — Hegelian lexicon, Vygotskian lexicon, two regex-based pragmatic feature passes, embedding-based semantic similarity — three findings survived. Each corrects an obvious-but-wrong reading of the data.

discipline Every numerical claim on this page traces to a specific section of the technical paper. The marginal "x.y" tags are the section refs.
i.

Recognition is one operationalisation of an intersubjective stance — not the only one.

Cell 5 (Hegelian recognition) and cell 95 (Vygotskian matched-pedagogical, with Hegelian vocabulary actively suppressed) score equivalently on the v2.2 rubric — pooled mean ~49 in both, with a between-cell Cohen's $d$ of about 0.15. By every reasonable threshold, indistinguishable.

The contrast that does matter is family-level: the two intersubjective cells (5, 95) jointly outscore the two transmission cells (1, 96) at $d \approx 1.38$ — substantially larger than the recognition-vs-base effect alone. The Hegelian content is decorative; the family-level stance is what does the work.

One might still suspect the Hegelian vocabulary primes the model in some non-obvious way the matched-pedagogical prompt accidentally inherits. The lexical analysis (7.10, two passes) rules this out: cell 95 is dense in Vygotskian vocabulary the recognition prompt almost never uses; their score equivalence persists despite near-disjoint vocabulary distributions.
ii.

"The recognition injection" can mean three different things — only one is load-bearing.

Once the family-level finding is in hand, the §7.10 decomposition makes a distinction informal readings tend to collapse. The recognition injection can refer to:

"Partially" because ends-with-question occurs in only 2–5% of intersubjective-cell responses. Even if every such response scored 30 points higher than its peers, that base rate could not arithmetically explain the +14-point family effect. Surface features are observable channels of the underlying stance, not substitutes for it.
iii.

Surface acknowledgement is a negative correlate.

This is the most counterintuitive finding in the sequence and the one most likely to be misread by a designer trying to operationalise "recognition" as "more acknowledgement."

Within most cells, broad-acknowledgement markers — quoted spans, "your X" possessives, "what you mentioned"-style phrases — correlate with lower rubric scores. Within-cell Pearson r = $-0.18$ in cell 5, $-0.31$ in cell 95, $-0.38$ in cell 1.

The pattern is consistent across the recognition cell, the matched-pedagogical cell, and even the generic baseline. Surface acknowledgement appears more often in weaker responses — surrogate moves the response falls back on when substantive engagement is thin. The judges, three independent LLMs, are not fooled.

A naive operationalisation of intersubjective stance as "more acknowledgement" would degrade tutor quality, not improve it. The substance the rubric rewards is engaged, content-specific engagement with the learner's move — not surface empathy.

§vi.

Beyond a single conversation.

Across sessions, what recognition does (and doesn't).

§V tests recognition within a single 3–5-turn dialogue. A follow-up study (A7 Phase 2) extends the time scale: ten simulated learners, each returning for eight sequential conversations with the same tutor under the same identity. The tutor's Writing Pad — a three-layer memory store — persists between sessions. Five learners get the recognition tutor; five get the matched dialectical baseline. Eighty dialogues, $5 of compute, four pre-registered hypotheses locked before launch.

The headline result reverses the primary hypothesis and confirms the exploratory one. The substantive interpretation that survives the reversal is stronger than the original prediction allowed for — and it has a sharp design implication.

why pre-register H1 was rejected with a large effect in the opposite direction. Without the pre-registration locking the prediction before launch, the temptation to retrofit the story would have been overwhelming. Because the prediction was locked, the reversal is real evidence — and H2, also pre-registered, gives the substantive frame the rejected H1 couldn't. 6.6.11
i.

Recognition produces fewer dialectical events, not more.

The pre-registered primary hypothesis (H1) predicted that recognition arcs would accumulate at least 2× as many recognition_moment events per learner as the base condition. A recognition_moment is a recorded thesis–antithesis disagreement between the ego (proposing the next tutor message) and the superego (critiquing it). The intuition behind H1: more recognition work happening means more dialectical conflict to record.

The data went the other way. Base mean 25.0 events per arc; recognition mean 19.2. Cohen's $d = 2.07$ in the base condition's favour, Welch's $t(4.84) = 3.27$, $p = 0.017$. The pre-registered effect is statistically significant in the opposite direction.

What this means: counting moments measures how often the two agents disagreed, not how much recognition is happening. The recognition ego, internally aligned with the superego's principles, produces first-pass suggestions the superego has less reason to object to — so fewer thesis–antithesis events fire. Base ego, lacking that pre-alignment, requires more correction. H1 was the wrong test for the right question.
ii.

Across the eight-session arc, the score trajectory separates the conditions cleanly.

Per-session tutor scores regressed on canonical session index 1–8: recognition arcs gain +1.31 points per session, base arcs lose −1.08 points per session. The slope difference is +2.39 points/session in recognition's favour, Welch's $t(7.88) = 1.99$, $p = 0.032$. Across all 80 sessions, recognition mean score is 43.8 versus base 34.7 — a 9-point gap, Cohen's $d = 0.70$ (medium effect).

Recognition produces fewer corrections during dialogue but higher output quality, and the gap widens across the eight-session arc rather than fading. The longer-horizon mechanism is therefore not adaptive responsiveness (within-dialogue trajectories are recognition-invariant; see 6.3). It is ego pre-alignment with superego principles — recognition's effect lives in what the model proposes first, and that quality compounds across sessions through the persistent learner state in the Writing Pad.

Design implication. If you have a long-running chatbot relationship to design — tutor, peer-support listener, code-review companion — the lesson is not "schedule more dialectical conflict." It is: tune the first-pass system prompt to be already-aligned with what your supervising layer enforces. The supervising layer should be there to catch the rare misalignment, not to do most of the corrective work. The compound effect across sessions only emerges when the ego rarely needs correction.
iii.

Two secondary measurements cannot be load-bearing at this sample size.

Two further pre-registered measurements aimed at the same mechanism — does the recognition tutor use the accumulated pad state more than the base tutor? — both returned inconclusive results once given adequate data.

  • H3 (token overlap). Per-arc Spearman ρ between session index and Jaccard overlap of cumulative pad traces with the tutor's session-N message: recog mean ρ = 0.39, base 0.16 — directional, but only 3/5 arcs meet the pre-registered ρ > 0.5 threshold. A novel-token refinement that strips out generic lecture vocabulary (Hegel, master–slave, recognition — present in both conditions for topic-continuity reasons) does not corroborate: recog mean recurrence 64.4%, base 68.3% (Welch's $t = -1.00$, $p \approx 0.32$). Both conditions propagate pad content into later messages at similar rates; the difference, if any, is too small to detect at $n=5{+}5$.
  • H4 (cross-session reference, blinded coding). A first sample (one late-session dialogue per arc, $n=10$, permissive coder) showed recognition referencing prior sessions at 40% versus 20% for base — directionally as predicted, $p = 1.00$ at that $n$. Augmenting to $n=40$ (four late-session dialogues per arc, conservative coder) reverses the direction: base 40%, recognition 25%, $p = 0.50$. The original signal was a small-sample / permissive-coding artefact. At adequate $n$ with strict criteria, cross-session referencing does not cleanly separate the two conditions.
What survives at strength: H1 reversed, H2 supported, the 9-point mean-score gap. H3 and H4 are too noisy at the $n=5{+}5$ design to anchor independent claims. A larger longitudinal study (twenty learners per condition or more) could plausibly close them out — but the substantive claim about pre-alignment doesn't need them.

§vii.

A second mechanism family — and what it does to the first.

After recognition, the second voice: charisma.

§V and §VI both ask one question: does recognition, an intersubjective stance, lift tutor quality? An obvious follow-up: is recognition the only intersubjective channel that does so? Weber, writing about authority a century before LLMs, names a different one — charisma: the speaker who has been claimed by the subject and writes their occasion fresh each time. A separate mechanism family, with a separate rubric and a separate architecture. We tested it.

The result is a pair that turns out to share a floor and split a ceiling. Recognition and charisma both fail when the speaker has nothing to stand on. They diverge from there, and the geometry of the divergence has a sharp design implication.

epigraph Charisma knows only inner determination and inner restraint. The holder of charisma seizes the task that is adequate for them and demands obedience and a following by virtue of their mission.
— Weber, Wirtschaft und Gesellschaft

a. setup.

Two new things: an architecture and a rubric.

To test charisma, we needed a tutor architecture in which the speaker's persona is not a fixed prompt-file but an authored variable. The id-director cells (c101–c108) invert the standard ego–superego topology: a back-stage id agent reads the dialogue and authors the ego's full system prompt before each turn, then a fresh ego executes that prompt. The id never speaks externally; it composes the actor. The ego performs.

This is genuinely different from the dialectical cells in §V. There the ego is a durable identity edited by superego critique. Here the ego has no durable core at all — only a persona budget and a director that re-instantiates it from scratch every turn. Weber's Berufung ("calling") is the fit: the speaker is staged as someone claimed by the subject, and that staging is renewed turn-by-turn rather than discharged from a standing role.

The Weberian rubric (eight dimensions, weighted to extraordinariness, Berufung, transformative invitation, affective pull, personal authority, anti-routinization, witness/proof-sign, and a closing charismatic register) scores responses on a 0–100 scale alongside the v2.2 recognition rubric. Both rubrics see the same response. The two scores can move together or apart — and in the data, they do both.

on the name "id" Freudian id is impulsive and pre-symbolic; it doesn't write scripts. The label is metaphorical and the user-stipulated convention. The role is closer to a dramaturgical director or a Lacanian Other: the generative impulse beneath the persona, the author below the actor. paper §6.7
why two rubrics A response that is rubric-pristine under v2.2 (engaging, scaffolded, content-accurate) can score low on charisma if the voice is flat. A response that is high on charisma can score low on v2.2 if it manipulates instead of invites. The two rubrics measure different things — and the cross-rubric scatter makes that operational.

b. four design points.

A small Pareto frontier of single-mechanism cells.

Eight id-director cells were run at confirmatory N (3 curricula × 9 scenarios × 3 reps = 27 evaluations per cell), each combining different sub-mechanisms — witness exemplars (four curated vulnerability moves the id may quote from), a register classifier (a kimi-k2.5 pre-pass that tags the learner's register so the id can match it), and either charisma-tuned or pedagogy-tuned id directives. The four cells below are the ones that produced design-point shapes worth keeping.

c. cautionary tale.

When pilot lift doesn't survive confirmatory N.

Two follow-up cells stacked levers from the design points above: c108 = c103 (register classifier) + witness exemplars; c109 = c105 (charisma directives) + witness exemplars. The c108 pilot at n=5 looked spectacular — pooled charisma 81.3, the highest mean any id-director cell had produced; v2.2 last-turn 79.5, between c104 and c107. Super-additive. We pre-registered confirmatory N at 27 across all three curricula × 9 scenarios × 3 reps and ran.

cell · stage charisma v2.2 last-turn verdict
c108 · pilot n=5 81.3 79.5 apparent super-additive lift
c108 · confirmatory n=27 71.4 72.6 regresses to mid-frontier interior
c107 · reference n=27 66.3 78.5 single-lever frontier point

The pilot lift was almost entirely driven by one curriculum — Plato's Phaedrus, where pilot n=3 scored 90.0 charisma but full-N n=3 scored 67.9: a 22-point regression to the mean from a textbook small-N optimism trap. The 701 ethics-ai curriculum then exposed a fragility that pilot scope had hidden: c108 v2.2 last-turn drops to 65.3 there (vs 76.7 / 75.8 on 601 / 901), driven by codex_repl (51.7) and design_repl (52.1). The non-text levers (c108 = classifier + witness) compose roughly additively-to-slightly-under-additively; the text-heavy lever stack (c109) interferes outright via an ego-side meta-narration failure mode where the Nemotron ego outputs system-prompt instructions verbatim rather than executing them.

Designer takeaway. The c108 result is the standing reminder that "obviously good" lever stacks are not free. The architectural ceiling for the id-director family sits at the single-mechanism design points (c104, c105, c107), not at multi-mechanism stacks. Adding levers above c107 traded a small charisma gain (+5.1) for a larger v2.2 cost (-8.0 vs c107 alone). Pilot studies at n≤5 will produce statistically meaningful-looking ratchets across most design dimensions in this evaluation regime — pre-register confirmatory N before celebrating. paper §6.7, regression doc §10

d. synthesis.

What charisma does to recognition, and vice versa.

Three results land together, and together they describe the topology of the two-rubric space.

i.

A shared floor.

Cell c106 — pedagogy-tuned id with terse 200–400 token directives — fails both rubrics, scoring 36.4 charisma and 57.0 v2.2. The two rubrics are not orthogonal at the bottom of the distribution. A tutor under-specified by terse "good teacher" instructions has nothing for either rubric to reward: no extraordinariness, no Berufung, no recognisable epistemic stance, no scaffold against which to read the response. Failure is correlated.

ii.

Cost asymmetry at the ceiling.

The frontier reveals an asymmetry the rubrics' shared floor hides: recognition gives you charisma cheaply; charisma costs recognition. Adding the recognition layer on top of structured input (c103 → c104) costs essentially nothing in charisma (+1.4, within noise) — the Hegelian frame is conceptual rather than rhetorical, so it can be added cheaply. Adding charisma directives in the other direction (c104 → c105), by contrast, costs −10.6 v2.2 last-turn for a +5.3 charisma gain. The id's authoring budget is finite, and explicit voice-instructions displace the elicitation/perception moves that v2.2 rewards. The two ceilings are not symmetric.

iii.

Outward voice vs upward voice.

The substantive difference is what each rubric rewards as presence. Recognition (v2.2) rewards a voice deployed outward — toward the learner's specific contribution, scaffolded against their move, content-specific in reply, holding the diagnostic gap open. Charisma rewards a voice deployed upward — toward the subject as something the speaker has been claimed by, with affective traction beyond argument, marking the encounter as extraordinary, inviting transformation. The Phaedrus is the obvious historical case where one figure does both at once, and even there Plato is suspicious of how easily charisma swamps the dialectical move. The id-director apparatus reproduces that tension architecturally: it can build a strongly recognising tutor (c104), a strongly charismatic tutor (c105), or a balanced generalist (c107), but not the maximally-recognising-and-maximally-charismatic tutor — that cell is not on the menu.

For the designer. If your application is tutoring proper, c104's recipe (register classifier + recognition layer) is the operating point — wins v2.2, mid-pack on charisma. If your application is closer to inspiration or vocational orientation — a museum docent, a literature companion, a research mentor — c105's charisma specialisation (charisma-tuned id directives) is worth the v2.2 trade. If you do not know which side of the frontier you want, ship c107 (witness exemplars only): second-best on both rubrics, clean operational story, and the cell that the regression-to-mean punishment in §VII.c is built around as the reference point.

The synthesis above is what survives when both rubrics see the same response. The shared floor (c106) tells you what to avoid; the cost asymmetry tells you the architectural choice has a direction; the outward-vs-upward distinction tells you what the architectural choice is about. That is the second voice, and the design implications it leaves behind. paper §6.7

§viii.

A portable check before you ship.

A three-criterion diagnostic for any feature you think mediates.

The methods companion paper distils the §V findings into a portable diagnostic. If you propose any response-level feature as a mediator of an effect — for any chatbot evaluation, not just tutoring — you can put it through the same three criteria.

why this is needed Pooled-r mediator analyses are common in LLM-tutor literature. Many of them would not survive the third criterion. The diagnostic protects against precisely the Simpson trap §V illustrated.
  1. i. Large family contrast. |d| ≥ 0.5 between the family containing the feature and a contrasting family. The feature distinguishes the families; if it does not, it cannot mediate the family-level effect.
  2. ii. Small within-family contrast. |d| < 0.5 between two cells inside the same family. Both family-member prompts elicit the feature; if only one does, it's a prompt marker, not a family marker.
  3. iii. Positive within-cell Pearson r in each family-member cell. With the prompt held constant, the feature still predicts higher scores. This is the criterion that rules out Simpson's-paradox false positives.

b. by application.

Concrete design recommendations.

Each card lists three concrete moves grounded in paper findings, stratified by the kind of chatbot you're building. Move from left to right as the application moves further from tutoring.

on phrasing Items below say "the paper suggests..." rather than asserting freshly. Per the source-of-truth discipline behind this guide, every recommendation traces to a specific section of the technical paper.

tutor design

If you're building an LLM tutor

  1. Adopt an intersubjective stance. Recognition theory is one option; Vygotskian scaffolding is another. The vocabulary is interchangeable; the stance is what carries the effect. 7.9
  2. Don't operationalise the stance as surface acknowledgement. "Quote what the learner said," "use possessives," and paraphrase markers correlate negatively with rubric quality. Build for substance, not surface. 6.1.8
  3. End single-turn responses with a question when ceding initiative is contextually appropriate. Within-cell mediator r = +0.325 in cell 5, +0.392 in cell 95. Beware: in multi-turn dialogues, ending the final turn with a question reads as poor closure instead. 7.10.1

peer support / care

If you're building peer-support, care, or coaching

  1. The family-level pattern transfers. D2 Path 1 found peer-support listener coaching reproduces the recognition effect at r = 0.739 — the strongest within-cell mediator in the entire D1 sequence, on a domain structurally distinct from tutoring. 7.10.1
  2. The vocabulary should fit the role. Peer support draws on listening-as-skill traditions (Rogers, Yalom); recognition-theoretic prompts in this domain often translate to "sitting with discomfort" rather than "scaffolding to mastery."
  3. Initiative-ceding is even more important. In care contexts, the user's autonomy is more directly at stake than in tutoring. Cede first, instruct later, if at all.

expert/critic chatbots

If you're building customer service, code review, or expert critique

  1. Recognition still applies, but the genre is different. The expert-asymmetry is real and useful; the goal isn't to flatten it but to make it productive. Code review that recognises the author's design intent before critiquing scores higher than code review that ignores it.
  2. The Freudian doubling is especially valuable here. A critic-ego paired with an advocate-superego (or vice versa) gives the system somewhere to argue with itself before delivering judgment to the user.
  3. Apply the §VIII diagnostic to your own evaluation. Don't claim "my chatbot recognises users" without a within-cell mediator analysis on your own corpus.

c. before you ship.

A ten-item integration checklist.

Click each item to mark it done. The list is not exhaustive but it captures the moves that distinguish a recognition-aware chatbot from one that simply uses the word "recognition" in its prompt.

scope The checklist is for the design and evaluation phase, before shipping to users. Production-time monitoring is a separate concern not covered here.
  • Pick an orientation, not a vocabulary.

    Decide whether your chatbot's stance is intersubjective (recognition, scaffolding, dialogic) or transmission (instruction, dispensation). Don't decide based on which philosopher you find more compelling — decide based on the application.

  • Author the prompt to specify the stance, not the vocabulary.

    If the prompt body specifies "engage with the learner's contribution before responding" but never uses the word recognition, you have probably done the work. If it uses the word recognition but never tells the model to do the engaging, you have probably not.

  • Add a matched-specificity control to your evaluation.

    If you compare your designed prompt only to a generic baseline, you cannot separate the prompt's stance from its length and specificity. Add a length-matched control grounded in a different orientation (or no orientation) to make the comparison interpretable.

  • Use at least two independent judges.

    Sonnet, Opus, GPT, Gemini — pick at least two from different vendors. The cross-judge replication exists in the paper because LLM judges have idiosyncratic biases; if your finding only holds under one judge it should be hedged accordingly.

  • Run the three-criterion diagnostic on any mediator claim.

    Before claiming a feature mediates the effect, check family contrast, within-family contrast, AND within-cell r. The third criterion catches Simpson's-paradox false positives that pooled-r alone misses.

  • Don't operationalise recognition as more acknowledgement.

    Surface acknowledgement markers correlate negatively with score within most cells. Build for substantive engagement; let acknowledgement come implicitly from content-specific reply.

  • Consider the multi-agent architecture when the model is weaker.

    Strong models (Opus-class, Sonnet-class) absorb most of the calibration work into the prompt; the architecture residual collapses. Weaker models leave more uncorrected error, which the multi-agent ego/superego architecture can catch.

  • Decide consciously about turn-ending.

    In a single-turn response, ending with a question cedes initiative and is rewarded. In a multi-turn dialogue, ending the final turn with a question reads as poor closure. Make the choice deliberately per turn type.

  • Run a PCA on your rubric dimensions.

    If your evaluation rubric has many dimensions, check whether they actually measure distinct constructs. The technical paper's PCA found 8 dimensions reduce to ~1 factor (PC1 = 80.7%). Per-dimension claims are routinely overstated.

  • Document your scope conditions.

    Single-turn vs multi-turn; single-agent vs multi-agent; tutor vs peer-support vs critic; one judge vs three. Every claim has a scope. State it explicitly in the documentation, especially when the claim does not survive moving outside it.

§x.

Stepping back.

Beyond tutoring: machinagogy, nonconscious recognition, and the alien dialectic.

This guide so far has stayed close to the operational. The companion paper [Magee 2026] thinks more carefully about what is happening at a deeper level — what it means to talk about recognition at all when one of the parties is a machine, and what kind of figure the AI co-researcher cuts inside the very work that produced these findings.

a neologism worth using Machinagogy: the practice of teaching through the machine, mediated by it, and altered by it. Coined as a deliberately literary play on pedagogy, but the work it does is real — there is no good neutral term for the assemblage of human, model, prompt, evaluator, and judge through which AI-mediated teaching now actually happens.

Nonconscious recognition

N. Katherine Hayles, in Unthought: The Power of the Cognitive Nonconscious [Hayles 2017], names a category of cognitive process that is neither conscious nor merely mechanical. Nonconscious cognition: a kind of pattern-finding and meaning-making that happens below the threshold of self-aware deliberation but is not reducible to dumb reflex. The concept fits machine learning systems unsettlingly well — they perform something like cognition without anything like consciousness.

The companion paper extends Hayles to recognition. To Honneth's three forms — love, rights, esteem — it tentatively adds a fourth: nonconscious recognition. When we encounter an AI tutor, we are recognised by something that is not self-conscious but is also not nothing. It is, the paper suggests, the recognition of an other that at the same time is identical to me, because it comprises, in some algorithmically reconstituted form, the same cultural and historical being that I myself am. The recognition is real and also impossible. The contradiction is the point.

The alien dialectic of co-research

The technical paper that grounds these findings was largely authored by Claude Code (Opus 4.6) — including section drafts, statistical analyses, table production, and even some of the rhetorical moves. The human researcher's role was orchestration, correction, and validation. This produces a methodological figure the companion paper calls vibe scholarship, with its dangers and disciplines. The findings reported here survived only because vibe work was systematically converted to defensible work: provenance hashes, claim-to-source tracing, three-judge replication, multi-feature OLS, cross-application replication.

Sustained work with AI involves a calibration to what might be termed, perhaps still too anthropomorphically, the rhythm of models, versions, parameters, tasks, subscription profile and token budgets. It is uncanny how an awareness of, among other things, impending subscription limits conditions the rate and nature of requests. This is less a case of cognitive offloading and more one of incremental planning. — Magee 2026, Defensible Vibes and Alien Dialectics

The relevance for the chatbot designer is this: the same dialectical relation you are designing into your chatbot — the recognition-aware interaction between user and system — is the relation you are already in with the AI tools you use to build it. Your prompt-engineering loops, your evaluation runs, your "let me ask Claude what it thinks of this design" sessions: each is a tiny enactment of the dynamic the chatbot is being shaped to perform. The methodological discipline that makes one work is what makes the other work. If you cannot recognise the AI that helped you build the chatbot, the chatbot is unlikely to recognise its users.

What this guide is part of

Generative AI's progress is charted in part by the relentless simulated enactment of existing social relations. In the late 2010s, GPT-2 produced incoherent fragments. By the mid-2020s, AI systems extend across customer service, software development, copy writing, graphic design — and student tutelage. This guide is one small part of one larger question: what kind of social relation can a generative AI sustain, and what disciplines do designers need to make those relations less flat than the default?

The recognition-theoretic findings are hopeful within their scope but also bounded. The companion paper is honest about the limits: the entire study used synthetic learners. The mechanisms are shown for tutor production, not for actual learning. The next step — the A1 human-learner pilot in the technical paper's open programme — is the only real test. Until then, the findings reported here function for designers as operational hypotheses to integrate, not as final certainties.
