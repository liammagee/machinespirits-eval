## How to Read a Study

Most people encounter studies through a headline, occasionally through an abstract, and rarely through the paper itself. This is unfortunate but not hopeless. With a short checklist and twenty minutes, a non-specialist reader can extract enough from almost any empirical paper to decide whether the headline is telling the truth. The checklist below is the one I use, and I will walk through it on a fictional example so the moves are concrete.

### The Checklist

First, the sample. Who was studied, how many of them, and how were they recruited? A study of eighty undergraduates at one university is not a study of human beings; it is a study of eighty undergraduates at one university. External validity — whether the result travels to other populations — depends on the sample looking like the population you care about.

Second, the outcome. What exactly was measured, and by whom? Self-reported happiness is not the same variable as a clinical depression score. Teacher-rated behavior is not the same variable as classroom observation. The construct the authors name and the construct they measured are sometimes far apart, and the gap often does the rhetorical work of the paper.

Third, the effect size. Statistical significance tells you the effect is probably not zero. It tells you almost nothing about whether the effect is important. Insist on an actual number: Cohen's d for continuous outcomes (0.2 small, 0.5 medium, 0.8 large), an odds ratio or relative risk for binary ones, a percentage-point change for rates. "The intervention significantly improved scores" is rhetoric. "The intervention improved scores by 0.12 standard deviations" is information.

Fourth, pre-registration. Was the analysis plan filed before the data were collected? If yes, the garden-of-forking-paths concern is substantially reduced. If no, the reported p-value is optimistic by an unknown amount.

Fifth, replication. Has anyone else found the same effect in a new sample? A single study, however clever, is one data point about the world. A meta-analysis of twenty studies in three countries is a different kind of evidence.

Sixth, funding and conflict of interest. Who paid for this, and who benefits if it concludes what it concludes? Funding bias is real but not deterministic — industry-funded studies can be excellent — so treat this as a prior to update on, not a verdict.

### A Worked Example

Consider a fictional paper, Smith et al. (2025), "A Fifteen-Minute Morning Journaling Intervention Significantly Improves Undergraduate Well-Being." Working the checklist: the sample is a hundred and twenty students at a single liberal arts college, recruited through a psychology subject pool. That caps external validity severely. The outcome is a self-reported seven-item well-being scale administered at baseline and after four weeks, by the same researchers who designed the intervention. That introduces demand characteristics. The effect size is reported as "significant at p < 0.03"; chasing it down in the results table, we find Cohen's d = 0.18, which is small and probably not clinically meaningful. The paper is not pre-registered. No replication is mentioned. Funding is from an internal university grant with no obvious conflict.

The honest summary is that Smith et al. found a small, potentially meaningful, potentially chance-level effect of journaling on self-reported well-being in a sample of psychology undergraduates at one school. The headline "Journaling Improves Well-Being" is not quite wrong, but it is considerably stronger than the evidence it is based on.

### What Skepticism Is and Isn't

Productive skepticism is not the posture of refusing to believe anything. It is the habit of asking, for any given claim, what the evidence would have to look like for the claim to be well-supported, and then checking whether the evidence actually looks that way. Most of the time the check is quick. Most of the time the answer is: partly, but not as confidently as the summary pretends. Living with "partly, but not as confidently" is the mature statistical attitude. It is also the only honest one.
