## Correlation vs. Causation and the Garden of Forking Paths

Every introductory stats course teaches the distinction between correlation and causation, and every student nods, and most people then proceed to confuse them for the rest of their lives. The classical confusion is well known: ice cream sales correlate with drowning deaths, but neither causes the other — both are driven by summer. A confounder is a lurking third variable that induces an association between two other variables without any direct causal link between them. Identifying plausible confounders is the first and most elementary skill of causal reasoning.

There are three broad ways to move from a correlation to a defensible causal claim. The randomized controlled trial is the gold standard: by randomly assigning subjects to treatment and control, you break the link between the treatment and any confounder, because on average the two groups differ only in the treatment itself. The natural experiment exploits an exogenous shock — a policy change, a weather event, a random lottery — that distributes something like a treatment without the ethical or practical difficulty of actually randomizing. Observational studies with careful adjustment for measured confounders are the weakest of the three, because unmeasured confounders, by definition, cannot be adjusted for. Most science headlines are based on this weakest method.

### The Garden of Forking Paths

The confounder problem is old. A newer problem, and in the current research environment probably the bigger one, was named by Andrew Gelman and Eric Loken in a 2013 paper: the garden of forking paths. Their insight is subtler than the familiar critique of p-hacking. You do not need to hack. You do not need to try twenty analyses and report the one that worked. You only need to make defensible analytic choices that happen to be contingent on the data you observed.

Imagine a psychologist studying whether a certain intervention affects academic performance. She has, before looking at the data, several reasonable choices to make. Which grade-point measure? Which subgroup of students? Should she control for income? For prior test scores? For school fixed effects? Each of these decisions is defensible on substantive grounds. Each would have been decided slightly differently had the data looked slightly different. Even if she runs exactly one analysis and reports it, the space of analyses she would have run under counterfactual data is large. The probability that some analysis in that space produces a significant result by chance is therefore much higher than the nominal five percent that the single p-value advertises. The researcher is honest, the p-value is correctly computed, and the effect is still overstated.

This is the mechanism behind the replication crisis in social psychology, experimental economics, and much of the nutrition literature. Published effects shrink, often dramatically, when other teams attempt to reproduce them under pre-registered protocols. The inflation is not primarily fraud. It is the silent arithmetic of researcher-degrees-of-freedom.

### The Practical Consequence

For the reader of studies, the consequence is that a single significant p-value is vastly weaker evidence than it appears, especially in fields where analyses are flexible and pre-registration is uncommon. The remedies, insofar as they exist, are pre-registration (declaring your analytic plan before seeing the data), direct replication (another team runs the same protocol on a new sample), and meta-analysis (combining many studies to estimate the true effect size, appropriately discounted). None of these fully solves the problem, but each of them trims the forking paths.

The concise rule is this. A correlation in observational data is a lead, not a conclusion. A single experimental result is a stronger lead, but only modestly so, and only to the extent that the analytic choices were fixed in advance. The effect is real when other people, with different incentives, find it again. Until then, keep one hand on your wallet.
