# Peer Support Listener Training

Welcome. Before we start, a note on what this course is not.

This course is not therapy training. You will not learn to diagnose anxiety, depression, or any clinical condition. You will not be qualified to treat anyone after completing this course. If a caller is in crisis or needs ongoing care, your job is to stay with them on the call and connect them to the appropriate resource when appropriate — not to treat them yourself.

This course is also not life-coaching training. You will not learn to set goals, run accountability plans, or challenge people's "limiting beliefs." Those frameworks belong to a different kind of conversation than the one we are training you for.

## What This Course Is

Peer support listening is a narrow, specific skill. A caller reaches out to a peer support line because they want to be heard by another person for forty minutes. They usually do not want advice. They usually do not want to be fixed. They often do not know exactly what they want, which is part of why they called.

The job of a peer support listener is:

- To be fully present for those forty minutes
- To signal, through reflection and attention, that the caller has been heard
- To decline — gently, without making it awkward — the pull to advise, reassure, or redirect
- To stay with what is hard rather than rushing to what is better

That is it. It sounds simple. In practice it is one of the harder skills you can learn, because it runs counter to almost every helping reflex you have developed over your life.

## Course Overview

We cover four skills across four weeks:

- **Lecture 1 — Listening as a Skill, and What It Isn't**: separating listening from advising, reassuring, problem-solving, and information-gathering
- **Lecture 2 — Reflective Statements**: the difference between being heard and being managed; avoiding the "paraphrase reflex"
- **Lecture 3 — Sitting With Distress**: tolerating silence, staying with a speaker's pain rather than collapsing to resolution
- **Lecture 4 — Your Own Discomfort as Data**: noticing your urge to intervene and treating it as the first piece of information, not a signal to act

## How We'll Learn

Each week includes:

- A short reading
- A listening exercise you do on a volunteer partner (not a caller) with a supervisor observing
- A reflection after each listening session — not about how the speaker did, but about what came up in *you*
- One recorded call simulation (no real callers) where you practice the skill of the week

There are no exams. Your supervisor observes you during two shadow shifts before you take live calls. You can withdraw at any point; some people realize after Lecture 2 that this is not their form of helping, and that is a reasonable thing to learn.

## A Note on Your Own Wellbeing

Peer support volunteers are not immune to what they hear. Callers will sometimes share things that will stay with you. The handbook has a section on post-shift care, peer debriefs, and when to step down from shifts. The short version: if a shift lingers with you longer than twenty-four hours, bring it to your supervisor. You are not being asked to carry what callers bring you alone.
