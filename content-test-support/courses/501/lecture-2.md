
## <a id="main-lecture"></a>Reflective Statements: Being Heard vs. Being Managed

---

### The Difference in One Line

Being heard sounds like: "It sounds like this has been sitting with you for a long time, and you haven't found anyone you could bring it to."

Being managed sounds like: "So what I'm hearing is that you're feeling overwhelmed by your work situation and looking for strategies to cope better."

Both are reflections. One feels like a real person. The other feels like an intake form. The caller can tell the difference within the first minute of the call.

---

### Why Paraphrase Fails

"So what I'm hearing is..." is the stock phrase taught in many active-listening manuals. It is a bad phrase for peer support.

Three reasons.

**It signals that you are performing listening rather than doing it.** The caller has encountered this language before, usually from someone whose job required it. They know what it means: the other person has been trained to say things like this, and the fact that they are saying it tells you they are running a script.

**It often flattens what the caller actually said.** A paraphrase is a summary. Summaries strip the specific words the caller chose, the hesitations, the contradictions. Those are the interesting parts. When you paraphrase, you throw them away.

**It puts the listener in the position of expert summarizer rather than fellow human.** The caller is on a peer support line specifically to avoid that asymmetry. A good reflection matches register and keeps the conversation on their side of the ledger.

```notes
This is the hardest skill in the course to teach because it looks so similar to listening-done-well on the surface. The volunteer nods. The volunteer says things back. The volunteer's sentences begin with "It sounds like." A supervisor watching the transcript can't always tell whether the volunteer is tracking the caller or managing them.

The difference shows up in what comes next. When the caller feels managed, they either comply (short "yeah" responses, a flattening of the conversation) or push back ("I guess... I mean, it's more complicated than that"). When the caller feels heard, they go deeper — they offer something they hadn't said before, because the reflection gave them somewhere to go.
```

### Three Kinds of Reflective Statements That Work

**1. The Noticing Reflection.**
Name something specific that the caller said, using language close to their own words, without summarizing.

- Caller: "I keep picking up my phone to call my sister and then putting it down. I've been doing it for three weeks."
- Bad reflection: "It sounds like you're having trouble reaching out."
- Good reflection: "Three weeks of picking it up and putting it down."

The good reflection says almost nothing new. That is its point. It returns what they said, and by returning it, signals that you heard the specific texture of it.

**2. The Tension Reflection.**
Name a tension or ambivalence the caller is holding, without resolving it.

- Caller: "I want to leave my job but I also just got promoted and everyone is really excited for me."
- Bad reflection: "So you're trying to figure out whether the promotion is worth staying for."
- Good reflection: "Both of those are real — the wanting-to-leave and the everyone-excited-for-you."

The good reflection honors that two things are true at once. It does not try to make them consistent.

**3. The Beneath-the-Surface Reflection.**
Name what seems to be underneath what the caller said, carefully and tentatively.

- Caller: "It's fine, I'll figure it out, I always do. Anyway — how do you usually end these calls?"
- Bad reflection: "You sound like you want to wrap up."
- Good reflection: "I notice you moved to how we end the call right after saying 'I'll figure it out.' I wonder if there's something you were about to say that you decided not to."

The good reflection is tentative (*I wonder, I notice*). It does not assert. It offers.

---

### What Not to Do

**Do not reflect with "Why do you think that is?"** That is a therapist move in a non-therapeutic relationship.

**Do not reflect with advice disguised as a question.** "Have you thought about talking to her?" is not a reflection; it is a suggestion you are embarrassed to make directly.

**Do not reflect with your own similar experience.** "That happened to me too" shifts the spotlight. If the caller wants to know whether you understand, they can ask; they usually do not, because the asymmetry is what they came for.

**Do not reflect what the caller *should* be feeling.** "It sounds like you're really sad about this" is a guess. If they are sad, they will tell you. If they are angry or exhausted or numb, a misnamed feeling will make them feel less seen, not more.

---

### Silence as Reflection

Sometimes the best reflection is not speaking at all. A two-or-three-second pause after the caller finishes a thought, before you respond, communicates something words cannot: that what they said landed, and you are considering it, and you are not in a hurry to move on.

New volunteers find silence excruciating. The impulse to fill it with words is strong. Part of your training is to sit with the silence for a count of three before you respond to anything a caller says. You will feel the urge to fill it. Notice the urge. Do not act on it.

---

### Discussion Prompt

Record yourself in conversation with a willing friend, just once, for ten minutes. Listen back. Count two things:

- How often did you start a sentence with "So..." or "It sounds like..."
- How often did you let three or more seconds of silence sit between their thought and your response

Most new volunteers are at 8+ paraphrases and 0 silences in a ten-minute stretch. If that is you, you are in good company. The skill is in the noticing, not yet in the doing.
