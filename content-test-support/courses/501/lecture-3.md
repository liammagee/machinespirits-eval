
## <a id="main-lecture"></a>Sitting With Distress Without Rushing to Resolve It

---

### The Hardest Part of the Job

The first time a caller cries on a call, you will want to do something. The first time a caller says "I just don't see a reason to keep going," your entire body will tighten. The first time a caller falls silent for forty seconds because they are holding something they cannot say, you will reach for words to fill it.

These urges are appropriate. They mean you are a person with working empathy. They are also, in every one of those moments, the wrong move.

The work of peer support listening is most visible when the caller is in distress. Everything you have been trained toward in ordinary social life — offering comfort, providing perspective, redirecting toward solutions — is set up to end the distress. Peer support is set up to be present to it.

---

### What "Sitting With" Means

Sitting with distress does not mean being stoic. It does not mean refusing to respond. It means staying with the caller inside the feeling they brought you, rather than trying to get them to a different feeling.

Concretely:

**When a caller is crying**, you do not rush to soothe. You do not say "it's going to be OK." You say, softly, "I'm here" or "take the time you need" or nothing at all. You do not treat the crying as a problem to be managed.

**When a caller says something catastrophic**, you do not minimize or reassure. A caller who says "I've ruined my marriage" is not asking you to tell them they haven't. They are telling you something true about how they feel. Disputing it is a form of not-listening.

**When a caller falls silent for a long time**, you do not fill the silence. You let them come back when they are ready. If forty seconds pass, you can say quietly "I'm still here." You do not say "so, what were we talking about."

**When a caller says something that alarms you** (hopelessness, intrusive thoughts, anything that makes you worry about their safety), you stay present, and you follow the handbook's crisis protocol, which you have practiced in simulation. You do not abandon the listening frame out of anxiety. You hold both.

---

### Why This Is So Hard

The pull to resolve distress is not primarily about the speaker. It is about the listener.

Witnessing someone else's pain is physically uncomfortable. Your nervous system activates. You produce stress hormones. You feel the urge to do something because doing something would relieve *your* discomfort. The fastest way to relieve your discomfort is to make the caller stop being in distress.

```notes
Volunteers often describe their first distress-heavy call as exhausting — not because of what the caller said, but because of how hard they had to work to not do the things their body was telling them to do.

This is the right kind of exhaustion. It means you were present to what was difficult rather than retreating from it. With practice the discomfort does not go away, but your tolerance for it increases, and you stop confusing the discomfort with a signal to act.
```

Noticing that the urge to intervene is about your discomfort, not about the caller's need, is the central skill of this lecture. It is what Lecture 4 goes deeper on.

---

### A Script That Does Not Work

Well-meaning new volunteers sometimes develop a personal script for distress moments. It sounds like:

- "I hear you."
- "That sounds really hard."
- "You're not alone."
- "It's going to be OK."
- "Have you talked to anyone else about this?"

These phrases are not bad in themselves. They are bad when deployed as a script, because the caller can feel the script. The script is what you reach for when you do not know what to say and the silence has become unbearable.

The better alternative, paradoxically, is often to say less. A quiet "I'm with you" after a long silence, followed by more silence, does more than any of the scripted lines above. The caller does not need you to perform empathy. They need you to be present without fleeing.

---

### What You Can Do (That Isn't Fleeing)

A few specific moves that stay inside the listening frame:

**Ask what they want from the call.** Not at the beginning — the beginning is too early. Somewhere in the middle, if it is not obvious, you can ask gently: "What would be most useful for you right now? Do you want me to just listen, or is there something you want to think through together?" This is not abdication; it is collaboration.

**Offer resources only if they ask.** If a caller is in acute crisis, you follow the crisis protocol. If they are in distress but not in crisis, you do not volunteer resources unless they ask. If you are worried the call is approaching crisis, you can say: "I want to check in — are you safe right now?" That is not a resource-drop; that is a safety check.

**Acknowledge the weight.** "What you are describing is a lot to be holding." This is neither advice nor reassurance. It names the weight without trying to take it from them.

**Name what you are not doing.** Sometimes it is useful, especially with a caller who expects advice, to name your stance: "I'm not going to tell you what to do on this call. I'm here to listen. If you want to think out loud about it, I can do that with you." This reframes expectations without being cold.

---

### Discussion Prompt

After your next simulated call, write about one specific moment where you felt the pull to intervene. Describe:

- What did the caller say just before you felt the pull
- What did your body do
- What did you do (or not do) in response
- In retrospect, what was the pull actually about — the caller's need, or your discomfort

There is no right answer to the last question. The point is to start noticing.
