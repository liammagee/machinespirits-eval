
## <a id="main-lecture"></a>Self-Awareness: Naming What You Feel

---

### Why Vocabulary Matters

- The feelings you can name, you can work with. The feelings you can't name, you just live inside.
- Most adults have a small active emotional vocabulary — maybe a dozen words they actually use on themselves.
- Expanding that vocabulary is the single most-tested intervention in the self-awareness literature.

You don't need to become a poet. You need to be able to tell the difference between "stressed" and "anxious" and "overwhelmed" and "disappointed," because they call for different responses.

```notes
When adults first try this exercise, they often protest: "I know the difference between stressed and anxious, I'm not a child." Then you ask them to describe the difference, and most can't. Stress has a trigger; anxiety often doesn't. Stress goes away when the trigger does; anxiety does not. Confusing them leads people to treat anxiety as a stress problem — "if I just had less to do, I'd be fine" — which never quite works.

The skill is not philosophical. It's a vocabulary skill. You get better at it by practising naming, not by reading about naming.
```

---

### The Basic Distinctions

A few pairs worth learning to tell apart:

**Stress vs anxiety**
- Stress has a specific cause ("the deadline Friday"). When the cause resolves, the stress lifts.
- Anxiety can exist without a specific cause. Sometimes it precedes a situation; sometimes it's about nothing identifiable.

**Overwhelm vs exhaustion**
- Overwhelm is a response to too many simultaneous demands. Resting does not fix it; triage does.
- Exhaustion is depletion. Triage does not fix it; rest does.

**Disappointment vs resentment**
- Disappointment is what you feel when an outcome was worse than you hoped. It fades.
- Resentment is what you feel when you believe someone wronged you and did not acknowledge it. Resentment does not fade on its own; it accumulates.

**Sad vs lonely vs grief**
- Sadness is a response to loss, small or large, present or remembered.
- Loneliness is a response to disconnection, even inside a room of people.
- Grief is the long-form of loss. It has its own timeline and structure.

```notes
A useful teaching move: ask students to describe a recent bad week. When they use a word like "stressed," ask a probing question: "what was the thing?" If they can name a thing, it's probably stress. If the thing wasn't actually that big, or if the feeling was there before the week started, it may be anxiety. Push gently.

Adults who are fluent in distress vocabulary but poor at positive vocabulary ("I'm fine" for everything above neutral) are also common. The positive side needs practice too — content, proud, relieved, pleased, grateful, curious are not interchangeable.
```

---

### The Body Is a Clue

Feelings live in the body before they land in your thoughts. A skill worth building: notice the body first, label second.

- Tight chest + shallow breathing → often anxiety
- Heavy limbs + slow speech → often sadness or exhaustion
- Jaw tension + clenched hands → often anger, sometimes frustration
- Racing thoughts + no body sensation → often rumination, not an emotion per se

The trick is to scan for 10 seconds before you answer "how am I?" — rather than reaching for the first word that fits.

```notes
The body scan takes practice. Adults who have spent years talking their way around their own states will find this slow at first. Rushing past "notice the body" is the most common skip. Ask students to pause for a full breath before naming.

Some students will report "I don't feel anything in my body." That is itself a notable report — it can indicate dissociation, overstimulation, or simply a lack of practice. Don't force a body sensation. The lack of one is information.
```

---

### The Check-In Exercise

Once or twice a day, ask yourself three questions:

1. **What am I feeling?** (Aim for two or three specific words. Not "fine." Not "stressed." Reach for granularity.)
2. **Where is it in my body?** (Or: is it not in my body?)
3. **What is it about?** (A specific trigger, a lingering situation, or nothing identifiable?)

Write the answers down if you can. You can use the notes app on your phone. The point is not the notes — the point is forcing yourself to actually answer the questions.

After two weeks of check-ins, you will notice patterns. Certain times of day. Certain triggers. Certain body signals. Those patterns are self-knowledge, and they are the beginning of self-management (next lecture).

```notes
The single most common failure mode: students do the check-in for three days, then forget. The skill is frail at first — it requires a cue.

Useful cues: tying the check-in to a routine event (morning coffee, walking home), an alarm on your phone (mid-morning, mid-afternoon), or a transition moment (between class and studying).

Students who hate the writing step can speak the answers into a voice memo. The medium doesn't matter; the naming does.
```

### Strengths Are Part of Self-Awareness Too

Self-awareness is not just about knowing what's hard. It also means knowing what you're good at, and what energizes you.

- Ask yourself regularly: *when am I at my best this semester?* What subject, what time of day, what kind of work?
- Notice the strengths you take for granted. "I'm just patient" is a strength, even if it feels like a personality fact.
- Notice what drains you vs what renews you. Two activities that look similar can have opposite effects.

Self-awareness is knowing your full shape — strengths, weaknesses, triggers, and renewers — not just your wounds.

```notes
Students coming from deficit-focused contexts (IEP meetings, therapy, academic probation) often have a much richer vocabulary for what's wrong than for what's strong. Part of the work is rebalancing.

A useful prompt: "list three things you did this week that worked, and try to name why they worked." Not "three things you're proud of" (that phrasing tends to invite modesty). Just: what worked, and why.

The why is the important part. "I finished my paper because I worked in the library instead of at home" is useful self-knowledge. "I finished my paper because I tried harder" is not — it's a moral judgement, and it doesn't transfer.
```

### Let's Practice!

**Question 1**: For each scenario, name the likely feeling with more specificity than a single-word answer.

a. A student has a test tomorrow and hasn't opened the textbook all week. Her heart rate is elevated when she thinks about it.

b. A student has been performing well all semester but feels flat and uninterested in everything for the past three days. No specific reason.

c. A student is waiting to hear back from a scholarship committee. The deadline for their decision was yesterday. He keeps refreshing his email.

d. A student spent a weekend helping a friend move, came home Sunday night, and could not focus on homework despite genuinely wanting to.

**Question 2**: A student says "I feel like shit." What two or three follow-up questions would help them get to a more specific naming?

**Question 3**: When is stress appropriate and when is anxiety appropriate as a label?

```notes
Question 1:
a. Probably stress (specific trigger: the test; specific delay: hasn't prepared) mixed with mild anxiety (body signal: elevated heart rate). Worth naming the delay — stress plus avoidance often compounds into a harder emotional state than stress alone.

b. Probably low mood / depression-adjacent / flat affect. Not anxiety (no elevated arousal). Not sadness with a cause. The "no specific reason" and the 3-day duration are the clues.

c. Anticipatory anxiety. Specific event (decision), but the event is outside his control. Checking email is a compulsive attempt to restore control.

d. Exhaustion — the physical kind. Not laziness, not failure of will. Naming it correctly matters because the response is different (rest, then restart) than for procrastination (examine the task, break it smaller).

Students who label all four as "stressed" are showing exactly why this exercise matters.

Question 2: good follow-ups:
- "What time did it start being this way?"
- "Where is it in your body?"
- "Is there a specific thing, or is it fuzzy?"
- "Is it the same as Tuesday's shit, or a different shit?" (Humor helps with this exercise; students often loosen up at a little levity.)

Questions that are LESS helpful at this stage:
- "What do you need?" (too early — they haven't named what they're feeling yet)
- "Have you tried X?" (solutioning without diagnosis)
- "Is it about your mom?" (putting a hypothesis in their mouth before they've looked)

Question 3: stress is the right label when there is a specific trigger AND the intensity roughly matches the trigger's importance. Anxiety is the right label when the trigger is absent, nonspecific, or when the intensity is out of proportion to the trigger.

"Out of proportion" is tricky — who decides? The usual rule: if the same trigger wouldn't distress most peers to the same intensity, the label is at least partially anxiety, not purely stress.

Note: the distinction is not about blaming anxiety. It's about intervention. Stress responds to problem-solving; anxiety often does not. Treating anxiety with a to-do list makes it worse.
```

## <a id="activity"></a>Activity: Two Weeks of Check-Ins

For the next two weeks, do the three-question check-in once per day. Pick the same time — before bed is a good one, because the day is done and you have material to examine.

Record your answers (notes app, journal, voice memo, whatever).

At the end of two weeks, read back. Look for:

- **Patterns by time** — certain days, certain hours, certain transitions that consistently show up as harder or easier
- **Recurring specific feelings** — words that keep coming up for you (not the obvious ones — the unexpected ones)
- **Body signatures** — locations in your body where your stress or exhaustion consistently lands
- **Positive patterns** — what activities, people, or conditions consistently preceded a lighter check-in

Write one paragraph of what you noticed. Not a judgement about whether the two weeks were good or bad — just observations about patterns.

```notes
The most common student failure on this exercise: forgetting. The check-in does not happen for most of the two weeks, and the student reports "I didn't really feel anything this week" — which is the absence of practice speaking, not the absence of feeling.

A scaffold: pair students for daily accountability. A one-sentence text exchange ("did your check-in today? 1 word summary") is enough. Makes the behavior sticky.

Another common failure: the check-in becomes performative. Students write what they think a good SEL student should write. You can spot this because the language is cliché-laden ("I'm working on being more present"). Push them: "what did you actually feel at 4pm on Thursday, specifically?"

When a student does the exercise honestly, the paragraph they write at the end is often surprising to them. "I didn't realize how much my mood is tied to sleep." "I didn't notice how often I'm anxious before class and relieved after class." That kind of pattern recognition is the point of the two weeks.

If a student's report is "nothing notable" — gently push to re-read. Often there's a pattern they dismissed as obvious. Obvious-to-them is still self-knowledge worth naming.
```
