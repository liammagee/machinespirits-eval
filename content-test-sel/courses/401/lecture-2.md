
## <a id="main-lecture"></a>Self-Management: Stress, Procrastination, and Study Habits

---

### The Difference Between Feeling and Acting

- Self-awareness (last lecture) is noticing what's happening inside you.
- Self-management is deciding what to do about it — which is NOT the same as "making the feeling go away."

A huge amount of adult distress comes from trying to erase feelings instead of acting despite them. You are never going to feel like writing the essay. You can still write the essay.

```notes
The biggest misconception about self-management is that it means controlling or suppressing feelings. It does not. It means expanding the gap between "I feel X" and "I do Y" — so that your actions are not hostage to your moment-to-moment states.

A student who says "I'll write it when I feel more motivated" has collapsed the gap. A student who says "I don't feel motivated and I'm going to write for 25 minutes anyway" has held it open. The second student finishes school.

This is not about willpower in the old-fashioned sense. It's about a specific skill: tolerating the discomfort of doing something while not wanting to.
```

---

### The Stress Signature

Everyone has a personal stress signature — the specific set of behaviors and body states that show up when you're overloaded. Learning yours is the prerequisite for managing it.

Common signatures:

- **Reactive signature**: short temper, snapping at loved ones, small annoyances feel enormous
- **Withdrawal signature**: stop returning texts, avoid class, spend hours on phone
- **Physical signature**: headaches, shallow sleep, clenched jaw, appetite changes
- **Perfectionism signature**: re-do the same task five times because nothing is "done enough"
- **Task-avoidance signature**: do easy tasks endlessly while avoiding the important one

Most people have a dominant signature plus one or two secondary ones. Knowing yours lets you catch it early.

```notes
Students often cannot name their signature until they look at their actual behavior. A useful exercise: "the last time a week was too much for you, what did the outside of your life look like?" Not how you felt — what you did. What didn't get done. Who didn't get called back.

Once the behavior is specific ("I didn't return texts for 3 days" or "I watched 14 hours of YouTube on Sunday"), the signature can be named. Before that, it's a fog.

Students who describe their signature in moral terms ("I become a bad person" / "I get lazy") need the vocabulary shift: laziness is usually a symptom, not a cause. The question is what the laziness is protecting from.
```

### Procrastination Is Usually Emotion-Avoidance

- Procrastination is rarely about time management. It's almost always about a feeling about the task.
- The feeling can be: boredom, shame ("I already should have done this"), fear ("if I try and it's bad, that's worse than not trying"), overwhelm ("I don't even know where to start").
- Naming the specific feeling often shrinks it.

A practical two-step:

1. Sit with the task in your head for 60 seconds. What comes up? Not thoughts — feelings.
2. Name them out loud. "I'm ashamed that I haven't started this." "I'm afraid the professor will think I'm dumb."

After naming, the task is often not smaller — but your ability to start it is larger. You have moved the feeling from the background (where it was running the show) to the foreground (where you can work alongside it).

```notes
This lecture has a quiet claim: most "productivity" advice is bad because it treats procrastination as a scheduling problem. The Pomodoro technique, better planners, time-blocking — all of these can help, but only if the underlying feeling has been named. Otherwise they are prescriptions for a misdiagnosed illness.

A useful teacher question: "what's the FEELING that comes up when you think about this assignment?" Not "what's hard about it?" (too intellectual). Not "why are you avoiding it?" (accusatory). Just: what feeling.

Adults who have learned to intellectualize everything will struggle. Sit in the silence after the question. Let them find the word. Often the word is one they're embarrassed to say: afraid. ashamed. resentful. bored. Each calls for a different next step.
```

### The 25-Minute Rule

When you cannot start a task, the rule is: **commit to 25 minutes, not completion**.

- Set a timer. Do only the task for 25 minutes.
- You are allowed to do it badly. You are allowed to produce nothing usable.
- At 25 minutes, you may stop without guilt. Or you may keep going if you have momentum.

The trick: most of the resistance to starting is about the anticipated badness of the 8-hour commitment. You cannot convince yourself you'll do 8 hours. You can convince yourself of 25 minutes.

By minute 20, the momentum is usually there. If it isn't, you genuinely needed rest, and you've gained useful data.

```notes
25 minutes is not magic. Some students do better with 15. Some need 40 to get into it. The point is a small commitment that is tolerable.

The most common failure: students set the timer, then allow themselves to check email, scroll, switch tasks. The timer isn't enforcing behavior, it's just present in the background. The timer has to be paired with a single-task rule: for these 25 minutes, only this task.

Phone in a drawer, notifications off. The environmental setup matters more than the mental commitment. Adults overestimate their willpower against notifications.
```

### Sleep Is the Foundation

If any single self-management move gives outsized returns, it is sleep.

- Adults need 7-9 hours. Most college students get 5-6.
- One night of 5 hours: your cognitive performance the next day is measurably impaired — reaction time, working memory, emotional regulation.
- Three nights of 5 hours: the impairment compounds, and you are now making worse decisions about everything — including about whether you need more sleep.

The self-management implication: no study schedule works if it requires you to consistently sleep under 7 hours. You will feel productive in the short term and pay for it within a week.

```notes
Students will argue this. "I do fine on 5 hours." They don't. They have adapted to 5 hours, which is not the same as being optimal on 5. Baseline comparisons to their own 8-hour performance would show the gap.

But you can't argue adults out of their sleep habits with research. You can only invite experiments. "Try one week of 7.5 hours, same study load, and compare your Friday-afternoon state to a normal week."

Adults who work full-time and go to school part-time often genuinely cannot get 7-9 hours without sacrificing something else. In that case, the honest conversation is about load, not about sleep — sleep is the canary, not the problem.
```

### Stress Management in Real Time

When you're actively stressed and need to continue functioning:

- **Box breathing** (4 seconds in, 4 hold, 4 out, 4 hold) for one minute. Not mystical. It engages the parasympathetic nervous system and measurably slows heart rate.
- **Name three things you see, three you hear, three you feel.** Grounding exercise. Moves attention from rumination to environment.
- **Move your body.** A 5-minute walk changes your chemistry more than 5 minutes of mental reframing.

None of these "fix" stress. They reduce acute intensity enough for you to act. Use them; don't overvalue them.

```notes
Students with mental health conditions that require medical care need more than these techniques. The lecture should not imply that box breathing replaces therapy for anxiety disorders. Make this explicit in workshop: these are tools for ordinary stress; they are not treatment.

At the same time, don't underplay them either. A student who will never see a therapist may still benefit from a 1-minute breathing technique used consistently. Imperfect tools, used, beat perfect tools, not used.
```

### Let's Practice!

**Question 1**: Match the signature to the likely underlying feeling.

Signatures:
a. Can't return texts for days, even from people you like
b. Rewriting the same paragraph 12 times without finishing
c. Picking a fight with a partner over something small
d. Doing laundry, cleaning, organizing instead of starting the essay
e. Scrolling phone for three hours after class instead of studying

Feelings:
- (i) overwhelm / freeze
- (ii) fear of failure / shame
- (iii) exhaustion accumulating
- (iv) displaced anger
- (v) task-avoidance via comfort

**Question 2**: A student says "I can't focus. I've been trying to study for two hours and nothing is sticking." What are three possible underlying causes, and what do they each call for?

**Question 3**: Your roommate makes this argument: "I do my best work at 3 a.m. I'm a night owl. I need to sleep less during finals week to get everything done." What would you say?

```notes
Question 1:
a. → (iii) exhaustion accumulating. Adults who "drop off the radar" socially are often depleted, not angry.
b. → (ii) fear of failure / shame. Perfectionism is usually fear-based; the task-completion is being deferred because finishing = being judged.
c. → (iv) displaced anger, often from an unexpressed frustration at school or work.
d. → (i) overwhelm / freeze. The essay feels too big; laundry is manageable; completing laundry gives a small reward that staves off the overwhelm.
e. → (v) task-avoidance via comfort. Different from (i) in that the task isn't overwhelming, it's uninteresting or anxiety-producing.

Students who match everything to (v) or everything to (i) are showing limited vocabulary. Push them on distinctions.

Question 2: three possibilities, each with a different response.

1. Actually exhausted / under-slept. Response: stop, nap or sleep, try again with real energy.
2. Anxious about the underlying material (doesn't understand chapter 3, afraid to admit it). Response: back up to what you understand, build forward, accept that the last 90 minutes were re-reading what you already didn't get.
3. Distracted by something specific (a conflict, a financial worry, news). Response: acknowledge the distraction, either set a specific time to think about it ("I'll worry about this from 6-6:30") or name it and move on.

A less useful response: "try a different study technique." The technique may not be the problem. Diagnosis before prescription.

Question 3: the roommate is almost certainly wrong about their "best work" claim. Self-report about cognitive performance at 3 a.m. is notoriously unreliable — tired brains think they're doing fine. If it were measurable, they'd find their error rates were higher.

The harder claim is "I need to sleep less during finals week to get everything done." This is sometimes, briefly, true — one all-nighter to finish one critical thing might be net-positive if there's rest on the other side. But sustained sleep restriction across a week reduces total productive output, not increases it.

Practical advice: prioritize ruthlessly during finals, accept that not everything will be done perfectly, sleep at least 6 hours nightly. The marginal productivity of the 4th hour awake past midnight is usually negative once errors are counted.

A student who won't hear this usually learns from experience in their 20s. That's fine. The course can only offer the information.
```

## <a id="activity"></a>Activity: The Signature Map

This is a self-inventory exercise. Pick a recent week — ideally one you'd describe as "too much" — and write a one-page account of it. Not a journal entry. A report.

Include:

1. **What was the load?** List the obligations that week — classes, work hours, social commitments, caretaking, health stuff.
2. **What were the visible behaviors?** Not feelings. What did the outside of your life look like? What did / didn't get done? Who did / didn't hear from you?
3. **What was the stress signature?** Match your behaviors to one of the five common signatures (reactive, withdrawal, physical, perfectionism, task-avoidance). You can have more than one.
4. **What was the trigger?** Was there a specific event that tipped the week, or was it accumulated load? If accumulated, how many days of overload before it started showing?
5. **What would have helped, in retrospect?** Not what you should have done "ideally" — what, specifically, could have prevented the spiral.

This exercise often reveals that your stress signature is predictable. You can see it coming. That is the beginning of managing it — intercepting before it takes the week.

```notes
The most useful part of this exercise is not the self-report — it's the retrospective-advice part (#5). Students often write "I should have taken a break" or "I should have slept more," which is correct but not operational. Push for specificity.

"I should have not signed up for the extra shifts the week of midterms" is operational.
"I should have asked my roommate to not have people over that Thursday" is operational.
"I should have stopped caffeine after 2pm starting on Monday" is operational.

Operational = reproducible, specific, falsifiable. The student can test the advice next time a similar week appears.

A common student failure: writing #5 as a personality change ("I should have been more disciplined"). That is not advice; it is a judgment. Push until they produce a structural change they could actually implement.

If a student's retrospective reveals that there was no structural move available — the week genuinely could not have been survived intact — that is also information. Some weeks need to be triaged, not managed. Knowing which kind you're in is self-management too.
```
