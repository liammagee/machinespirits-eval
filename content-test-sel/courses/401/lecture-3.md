
## <a id="main-lecture"></a>Social Awareness: Perspective-Taking and Active Listening

---

### The Shape of the Skill

- Social awareness is the capacity to notice what's going on inside someone else, even when you don't agree with them.
- It is not agreement. It is not approval. It is not even liking the person.
- It is the ability to hold their viewpoint in your head long enough to see the situation from where they are standing.

This matters in school because group projects, roommate arguments, partner frustrations, and professor-student misunderstandings all fail at the same point: one person stopped being curious about the other.

```notes
Students frequently confuse empathy with agreement. "If I try to see it from her side, I'm saying she's right." No. You can see exactly why she is furious AND still think she is factually wrong. The skill is separating the two operations: first, understand; then, decide whether to agree.

Adults who grew up in environments where understanding was punished (when "I see why they're upset" got read as disloyalty) often struggle with this. The work is gently distinguishing the two moves, and giving students permission to understand without committing to anything.
```

---

### Sympathy, Empathy, Compassion — Three Different Things

**Sympathy**: "That's terrible, I feel sorry for you." You are standing outside their experience, feeling bad *for* them.

**Empathy**: "I can imagine what that feels like for you." You are temporarily inhabiting their experience, feeling *with* them.

**Compassion**: "I understand, and I want to help reduce the suffering." Empathy plus motivation to act.

These are not a hierarchy — one is not better than the others. They are different tools for different situations.

- Sympathy is appropriate when the situation is outside your lived experience. (You have not lost a parent; the grieving friend does not need you to pretend you understand.)
- Empathy is appropriate when you can draw on a real, honest parallel in your own life.
- Compassion is appropriate when action is warranted and you are in a position to help.

```notes
The most common social-awareness mistake adults make: offering unsolicited compassion (advice, solutions) when the other person wanted empathy (being seen). Then the "helper" feels frustrated that their help wasn't taken, and the other person feels unseen.

The test: before offering, ask "do you want me to just listen, or are you looking for ideas?" Most adults have never been asked this and find it startlingly clarifying.

Students who pride themselves on being "problem-solvers" will resist this. Their identity is tied up in fixing things. A useful reframe: the fastest fix for 80% of problems is the other person feeling fully heard. Listening *is* the problem-solving, in those cases.
```

---

### Active Listening: What It Actually Is

Most people think they are listening when they are really waiting for their turn to talk.

Active listening is a specific skill with specific moves:

- **Don't rehearse your reply.** Let the sentence you're about to say be unformed until the other person finishes.
- **Check your understanding before responding.** "So what I'm hearing is ____. Is that right?" — and mean it. They may correct you.
- **Match the emotional register.** If they are upset, don't respond in a flat, logical tone. If they are flat, don't get artificially warm.
- **Don't jump to the solution.** Even if you know the answer. Let them get to the end.
- **Notice what they are NOT saying.** What is carefully avoided? What keeps almost coming up and then being diverted?

You cannot do all of this at once. Start with one move. Not rehearsing your reply is the hardest and the highest-leverage.

```notes
The "not rehearsing" move fails in predictable ways. Students stop listening partway through to compose their response. By the time the other person finishes, the listener has missed the last 30% of what was said — which is usually where the actual feeling landed.

A practical cue: when you notice yourself starting to form a reply, silently name "rehearsing" and return to listening. You will get better at catching it.

Also: active listening is exhausting if you're not used to it. That's normal. It's cognitive work. Don't expect to do it for two hours straight. 20 minutes of real listening is worth more than 2 hours of half-listening.
```

### Reading the Room

Some information about what's happening in a group is carried by words. Most of it is not.

- Who is talking and who is not?
- Who is making eye contact with whom? Who is being avoided?
- What's the energy level? Is it higher or lower than the content would predict?
- Is someone performing "I'm fine" while their body says otherwise?

A useful exercise: in your next class or meeting, spend 5 minutes not listening to the content at all, only watching the dynamics. You will see things you have been missing.

```notes
This skill comes more naturally to some students than others. Students with any autistic traits will often report that reading the room feels like solving a puzzle with missing pieces — that's real, and the skill can still be built, just more slowly and more explicitly.

Students who pride themselves on being "perceptive" sometimes overreach in the other direction — confidently misreading situations, projecting their own feelings onto others, "knowing" what people are thinking when they don't. The corrective for overconfidence is the same as for underconfidence: ask. Check. Don't act on a reading without some verbal confirmation when the stakes are real.
```

### When Someone Disagrees With You

The hardest social awareness test is listening to someone who thinks you are wrong.

- Your default response will be to rehearse a counter-argument.
- That is not listening. That is debate prep.
- Actual listening, in a disagreement, means: can you describe their view back to them in a way they would accept — before you respond with yours?

This is called "steelmanning" the other person. The test is not "can I argue against them" but "can I argue FOR them, accurately?" If you can't, you haven't understood.

```notes
A useful exercise for when students are locked in a disagreement: "before anyone says anything else, each of you has to describe the other's position in a way they will accept as accurate. If they say 'no, that's not quite what I mean,' you have to revise until they accept it."

This takes longer than students expect. Most arguments have significant mutual misunderstanding baked in, and the exercise surfaces it. After 15 minutes of steelmanning, the argument has often either dissolved or shrunk to its actual core.

Students who hate this exercise are usually the ones who most need it. Their identity is tied up in being right, and being forced to articulate the "wrong" position threatens that identity. Ride it out. It's a muscle.
```

### Cultural and Contextual Awareness

What looks like rudeness or disengagement in one context may be appropriate respect in another.

- Direct eye contact: required in some cultures, disrespectful in others.
- Silence after a question: rude in some contexts, thoughtful in others.
- Blunt speech: valued in some workplaces, considered aggressive in others.
- Saying "no" to a favor: normal in some families, unthinkable in others.

You cannot assume your norms are universal. When someone's behavior surprises you, consider the possibility that they are operating by a different, equally coherent set of rules — not that they are wrong.

```notes
This is especially relevant at a community college, where students bring a wider range of cultural backgrounds than at a selective residential college. Students who grew up translating between home and school often know this intuitively. Students who grew up in a single context may need the explicit frame.

A common failure: students project their own family's communication style onto their roommate, partner, or professor, and read difference as pathology. "My roommate is so cold" may mean "my roommate communicates differently than my family did."

The move is not to abandon your own norms. The move is to notice that the person who seems wrong may be operating correctly in their own frame.
```

### Let's Practice!

**Question 1**: Your classmate says "I'm so stressed about my mom." What are three different possible responses, and what assumption does each one make?

**Question 2**: You are in a group project. One member, Jessica, has stopped responding to messages for 3 days. The deadline is Friday. What is the NON-accusatory version of reaching out, and what is the accusatory version that will feel satisfying but make things worse?

**Question 3**: Your roommate is clearly upset about something. You ask "what's wrong?" and they say "nothing." What are your three options, and what are the tradeoffs of each?

```notes
Question 1:
Response A: "Is she okay? What's going on?" — assumes you want to know more, ready for full story.
Response B: "Oh no, that sucks. I'm so sorry." — sympathy response, may close the conversation.
Response C: "Do you want to talk about it, or do you want distraction?" — explicitly asks what they need.

C is often best when you are close to them. A is appropriate when you don't know the full context yet. B is the default but can flatten the conversation if that's all you offer.

The wrong responses: "My mom was sick too, when I was 12, and..." (hijacking); "Have you tried calling her every day?" (solutioning); "You just need to not worry about it" (dismissing).

Question 2:
Non-accusatory: "Hey Jessica, I haven't heard from you in a couple days. Everything okay? No pressure — just want to make sure nothing's wrong. We're at the part of the project where I'd love to sync up when you can."

Accusatory (feels satisfying): "Jessica, the deadline is Friday and you've disappeared. The rest of us are doing your work. Please respond."

The accusatory version assumes you know what's going on (she's being irresponsible). You don't. She may have had a medical emergency, a family crisis, or a mental health crisis. Or she may genuinely be flaking. You don't know which until you ask. The non-accusatory version is not weaker — it's just accurate about what you don't yet know.

Question 3:
Option A: Accept "nothing" and give them space. Tradeoff: respects their pace but may leave them feeling uncared-for if they actually wanted to be pursued.
Option B: Stay present but don't push. "Okay. I'm here if that changes." Tradeoff: opens a door without forcing them through it.
Option C: Gently persist. "I hear nothing, but you seem off. You don't have to tell me, but I'd rather you said 'I don't want to talk about it' than 'nothing,' because then I know what to do." Tradeoff: risky but sometimes produces a truer answer.

The right choice depends on the relationship and your history. A fresh roommate gets A or B. A close friend of five years can sometimes handle C. Reading your standing with the person is part of the skill.

A student who always picks A (non-intrusive) may be enabling isolation. A student who always picks C may be overstepping. The goal is calibration, not a rule.
```

## <a id="activity"></a>Activity: The Other Side

Pick a disagreement you've had in the last month. Not one that was resolved — one that is still bothering you.

Without contacting the other person, write two versions of the disagreement:

**Version 1: Your side, fully.** Write what you thought, what you felt, what you observed. Give yourself full voice.

**Version 2: Their side, fully.** Not a straw-man. Not "what they wrongly believe." Write as if you were them. What did they see? What did they feel? What made their position coherent *from the inside*? If you cannot make it coherent, you have not understood yet.

After you have both versions, read them side by side. Notice:

- Where did you assume bad faith when you could have assumed confusion, pain, or a different priority?
- Where did they likely assume the same about you?
- What would you have said differently if you had started from Version 2?

```notes
This exercise separates "agreeing with their view" from "understanding their view." Students often refuse at first: "I don't want to write their side because it means I'm letting them off the hook." The instructor's job is to reassure them: steelmanning does not mean forgiving. It means seeing clearly. You can steelman your worst enemy and still think they're wrong.

The breakthrough moment usually happens in Version 2 when the student discovers they can, in fact, make the other person's position coherent — and that this doesn't erase their own grievance. Both sides can be coherent and in conflict. Mature social awareness holds both.

A common failure: Version 2 comes out as a parody ("She just wanted to be right all the time"). That's not the other side — that's your theory of their motivation. Push until they write the OTHER person's experience, including the feelings that drove the behavior. Real Version 2s usually surprise their authors.

A deeper failure: the student writes Version 2 well and then revises Version 1 in response. That is not weakness — it is growth. Being able to update your own account of a disagreement is the point.

Some disagreements, after this exercise, are clearly ready for repair. Others are clearly not — the gap is too large, or the other person is not safe. Knowing which kind you have is also part of the skill.
```
