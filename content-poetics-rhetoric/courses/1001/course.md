# Poiesis and Rhetoric

Welcome to *Poiesis and Rhetoric*. This course studies the vocabulary of made
language — the terms the Greek and Roman traditions developed for how a piece of
language is built and what it does to the people who receive it.

## Course Overview

We begin with the Greeks and the question of *poiesis* — making. Aristotle's
*Poetics* is short, unfinished, and decisive: it asks what a tragedy is made of,
how its parts fit, and why a well-made plot moves us. From there we cross to Rome
and to *rhetoric* — the systematic art of persuasion that took the figure as its
unit of study and handed the Renaissance its catalogue of several hundred named
devices.

The wager of the course is that these are not two subjects but one. The plot
that turns on a reversal and the sentence that turns on an antithesis are doing a
related thing: articulating language at a joint so that an audience feels the
turn. We study:

- *Poiesis* and *mimesis* — making, and representation as the genus of the arts
- The six parts of tragedy and the architecture of plot
- *Hamartia*, *catharsis*, and wonder — the affective end of drama
- The passage from Greek poetics to Roman rhetoric, by way of the three appeals
- The five canons, the three styles, and the parts of an oration
- The tropes, the schemes, and the figures of thought

## Structure

The course runs over eight lectures, each introducing a band of the vocabulary
and building on the last.

1. **Mimesis and Poiesis** — making vs. action; mimesis as genus; the six parts of tragedy
2. **The Architecture of Plot** — *mythos*, reversal (*peripeteia*), recognition (*anagnorisis*), suffering (*pathos*); complication and unravelling; the unities
3. **Hamartia, Catharsis, Wonder** — the flaw that drives the change of fortune, and the affective end the plot is for
4. **From Poetics to Rhetoric** — the three appeals (*ethos*, *pathos*, *logos*) and the turn to Rome
5. **The Five Canons** — *inventio*, *dispositio*, *elocutio*, *memoria*, *pronuntiatio*
6. **Tropes** — metaphor, metonymy, synecdoche, irony, and their relatives
7. **Schemes** — figures of arrangement and sound: anaphora, antithesis, chiasmus, asyndeton, isocolon, climax
8. **Figures of Thought — and the Reflexive Turn** — rhetorical question, apostrophe, prosopopoeia, praeteritio; and a reading of the tutor's own moves as figures

## A Note on Method

The terms in this course are also the terms a companion system uses to *stage*
tutoring as drama. Where a lecture names a figure, the system's ontology declares
it as a formal class. You can read the course on its own, as classical poetics
and rhetoric; or you can read it as the glossary of a machine that tries to make
recognition happen in a conversation. Lecture 8 reads it both ways at once.
