### The third kind of figure

- Recall the figures split by *level*:
  - **trope** — turns the *meaning* of a word;
  - **scheme** — turns the *arrangement* of words;
  - **figure of thought** (*figura sententiae*) — turns the *idea*, or the speaker's relation to it.
- The test (Quintilian IX.1): a figure of thought **survives paraphrase**. Reword it, change every word, and the figure is still there — because it lives in the *conception*, not the diction.
- A rhetorical question stays a rhetorical question in any words; a chiasmus dies the moment you reorder it. That is the line between the families.

```notes
Quintilian draws the line we need at the start of Book IX, and it is the cleanest test in the whole subject. The figures of *speech* — the schemes — reside in the form of the words: change the words and you destroy the figure. The figures of *thought* reside in the conception, the *mens*, the mind behind the words: "even if the words be changed, the figure of thought remains." 

So here is the operational test. Take a candidate figure and paraphrase it — say the same thing in entirely different words. If the figure vanishes, it was a figure of *speech*: a chiasmus reordered is no longer a chiasmus, an anaphora un-repeated is gone. If the figure *survives* the paraphrase, it was a figure of *thought*: a rhetorical question is still a rhetorical question however you phrase it, because what makes it a figure is the *stance* — asking without seeking an answer — not any particular arrangement of words. 

That is why the figures of thought are the subtlest and, in a sense, the most powerful of the three families. They are not effects on the surface of the language; they are postures of the mind — a feigned question, a turning-aside to address the absent, the lending of a voice to what cannot speak, the pretense of passing over what one is busily naming. They shade, at their edge, into pure strategy, into the management of the whole relation between the one who speaks and the ones who listen.
```

### The rhetorical question (and its answer)

- **Rhetorical question** (Greek *erotema*; Latin *interrogatio*): a question asked **not to get information** but to assert, to press, to make the audience supply the answer themselves.
  - "Was I not right?" "Who could disagree?" "How long, Catiline, will you abuse our patience?" (*Quo usque tandem…*)
- Its dialogic cousin, **hypophora**: you **raise** the question *and answer it yourself* — "You ask what I propose? I propose this."
- Both make the listener an *active party*: the rhetorical question hands them the conclusion to draw; hypophora voices the question already forming in their mind.

```notes
The rhetorical question is the most familiar figure of thought, and a perfect illustration of the paraphrase test: it is a question in *form* that is an assertion in *force*, and that gap between form and force survives any rewording. Cicero opens the First Catilinarian with one of the most famous in the language — "Quo usque tandem abutere, Catilina, patientia nostra?", "How long, Catiline, will you go on abusing our patience?" He is not requesting a duration. He is making the Senate *feel* the patience already exhausted. The figure works by handing the audience the conclusion and letting them complete it, which lodges it more firmly than a flat statement ever could — a thing concluded for oneself is believed harder than a thing merely heard.

Its close relative is *hypophora* (or *anthypophora*): the speaker poses the question *and then answers it themselves*. "You ask what I bring against him? I bring this, and this." Where the bare rhetorical question trusts the audience to answer, hypophora voices the question the audience is already forming and then satisfies it — it stages a little dialogue inside a monologue. I single these two out, more than the others, because they are the figures of thought a *teacher* lives on. They are how a single speaker makes a listener into a participant. Hold that thought; it returns when we turn the lecture on teaching.
```

### Apostrophe — the turn aside

- **Apostrophe** (Greek *apostrophē*, "a turning away"): the speaker **breaks off** to address someone or something **not able to answer** — the absent, the dead, an abstraction, an object.
  - "O Death, where is thy sting?" "Milton! thou shouldst be living at this hour." "O eloquent, just and mighty Death!"
- The name is, once more, a **turn**: the orator wheels away from the audience to address another — and in doing so makes the audience *overhear*.
- Its force is intensity: the address to the un-addressable signals feeling too great for ordinary statement.

```notes
*Apostrophe* — the word means "a turning away" — is the figure in which the speaker abruptly breaks off from the audience and turns to address someone or something that cannot, in the ordinary sense, be spoken to: a dead person, an absent one, a city, an abstraction, Death itself. Paul: "O death, where is thy sting? O grave, where is thy victory?" Wordsworth: "Milton! thou shouldst be living at this hour." Raleigh's great address: "O eloquent, just and mighty Death!"

Notice that the name belongs to the family of *turns* we keep meeting — *apostrophē* is a turning-away, just as *peripeteia* is the plot's turn, *tropos* the word's turn, *chiasmus* the sentence's turn. Here the turn is in the direction of address: the speaker pivots off the line to the audience and onto an impossible interlocutor. And the paradox of the figure is that this turning-away is *for* the audience — they are meant to overhear it. The address to what cannot answer is a sign of feeling that has overflowed ordinary speech, and the audience, watching the speaker swept past them toward the absent, is moved by the overflow. It is a figure of pure intensity, and it survives any paraphrase, because it is the *stance* — the turn toward the un-addressable — that constitutes it.
```

### Prosopopoeia — lending a voice

- **Prosopopoeia** / **personification**: giving **voice or person** to what has none — the dead, the absent, an abstraction, a thing — making it *speak* or *act* as a person.
  - The Laws of Athens rise and argue with Socrates (Plato, *Crito*); "Justice herself would say…"; a country, a virtue, a city given a speaking part.
- More than apostrophe: apostrophe *addresses* the silent; prosopopoeia *makes it answer*.
- It is, in miniature, what a **poet** does — *mimesis* of an agent — and so it folds back toward Lecture 1.

```notes
*Prosopopoeia* — the Greek is built from *prosopon*, "face" or "mask," the same root as the dramatic *persona* — goes one step past apostrophe. Apostrophe *addresses* the absent or the abstract; prosopopoeia *gives them a voice* and lets them speak or act in their own person. Quintilian treats it as one of the boldest of all figures: by it "we display the inner thoughts of our adversaries as though they were talking to themselves," and we "bring down the gods from heaven and raise the dead," and give speech to "cities and peoples."

The greatest philosophical example is in Plato's *Crito*: when Socrates wants to argue that he must not escape, he does not argue in his own voice — he *personifies the Laws of Athens*, has them rise up and stand before him and reason with him, "Tell us, Socrates, what are you about?" An abstraction is given a face and a voice and made a character in the scene. 

And here you should feel the floor of the course tilt toward its beginning. To give a voice to what has none, to make an abstraction into a speaking agent — that is, in miniature, exactly what Aristotle said the poet does: *mimesis*, the representation of agents acting. Prosopopoeia is a figure of *rhetoric* that is also a seed of *drama*. The two halves of the course meet inside this one device.
```

### Praeteritio — saying by not saying

- **Praeteritio** (also *occupatio*, *paralipsis*): emphasising something by **professing to pass over it**.
  - "I will not mention my opponent's debts, nor his three divorces, nor the matter of the missing funds…"
  - "It goes without saying that…" (and then saying it).
- The figure is a small, knowing **irony of stance**: the speaker names precisely by refusing to name, and the audience is in on it.
- It is the cleanest proof of the paraphrase test: reword it however you like — the *move* (mention-by-disavowal) is what survives.

```notes
*Praeteritio* — the Latin means "a passing over"; the Greek is *paralipsis*, "a leaving aside" — is the figure of mentioning something by announcing that you will *not* mention it. "I shall say nothing of his bankruptcies; I pass over in silence the scandal of last winter; I will not even allude to the company he keeps." Every item in the list is delivered with full force *under cover of being omitted*. The audience hears all of it, and hears, too, the speaker's show of restraint — which flatters everyone as being too high-minded to dwell on such things, while dwelling on them precisely.

It is a figure of *thought* in the purest sense, and the best possible illustration of Quintilian's paraphrase test: there is no particular wording that makes a praeteritio. "I will not mention," "I pass over," "it goes without saying," "far be it from me to bring up" — any of them, in any language, performs the same *move*. The figure lives entirely in the *stance*: the gap between the professed passing-over and the actual naming. It is, you will notice, a species of irony — saying one thing (I will not mention this) and meaning its opposite (attend closely to this) — which is why the four master tropes reach all the way up here, into the figures of thought. Irony is not just a trope; it is a posture available at every level.
```

### The vocabulary folds back: thought, *dianoia*, and rhetoric

- The figures of thought are the rhetorical descendants of a term from **Lecture 1**: *dianoia*, **thought**, the *third part of tragedy* — "reasoning, proof and refutation, saying what is pertinent."
- Aristotle says so himself: on *dianoia*, "we may assume what is said in the *Rhetoric*, for this inquiry belongs more properly to that study" (*Poetics* ch. 19). He **hands thought to rhetoric** with his own hand.
- So the companion ontology records the kinship: `ms:FigureOfThought` carries a `seeAlso` to `ms:Dianoia`. The last figures of the course point back at the third part of the first lecture.

```notes
Before we turn the lecture on teaching, let me close one specific loop, because it is exact and it is Aristotle's own. In Lecture 1 we named the six parts of tragedy, and the third was *dianoia* — thought — which Aristotle defined as the power of saying what is pertinent and possible, the element of reasoning and proof and refutation *within* the drama. And when he reaches *dianoia* in chapter 19 of the *Poetics*, he does a remarkable thing: he refuses to treat it, and sends the reader away. "Concerning Thought, we may assume what is said in the *Rhetoric*, for the inquiry belongs more properly to that study." He hands *thought*, with his own hand, across from poetics to rhetoric.

The figures of thought are what is waiting on the other side of that handoff. The *figura sententiae* — the figure of the *sententia*, the thought — is the rhetorical tradition's catalogue of exactly the thing Aristotle declined to treat in the *Poetics* and pointed toward the *Rhetoric* to find. So the last family of figures in the course is the lineal descendant of the third part of the drama in the first lecture. The companion ontology records this with a single edge: the class for the figures of thought carries a *seeAlso* link to the class for *dianoia*. It is the tidiest possible demonstration of the course's wager — that poetics and rhetoric are one subject, joined at the joints, and that the joins are real enough to draw as lines in a graph.
```

### The reflexive turn — the tutor's moves as figures

- Now turn the whole vocabulary on the tutoring dialogue itself. The tutor is a **maker** (*poietes*) working in pure dialogue, and the tutor's moves *are* figures:
  - the Socratic question that presses without informing — **rhetorical question** (*erotema*);
  - "You may ask why this works. Here is why." — **hypophora**;
  - "Not *adding* the parts, but *combining* them" — **antithesis**;
  - "Think of the fraction as a *recipe*…" — **metaphor**;
  - "I'll grant the easy reading, but…" — a figured **concession**;
  - the takeaway phrased as a ringing three — **tricolon** doing the learner's *memoria*.
- And the whole exchange has **dramatic** form: the learner's misconception is the *hamartia*; the worked dialogue is *desis* and *lysis*; the moment of seeing-differently is **anagnorisis** at a **peripeteia**.
- **Caution.** All of this names *form*. A dialogue can have flawless figural and dramatic shape and carry **no real understanding**. The ontology records that a recognition-*shaped* move occurred — never that a mind actually changed. We classify the drama; we do not read the interior.

```notes
Here is the turn the whole course has been building toward. Take everything we have assembled and aim it at the tutoring dialogue, and the tutor resolves into a *maker* — a *poietes* working in the stripped-down medium Aristotle said still carries the full force of the art: pure dialogue, all plot and thought and diction, no spectacle at all. And the tutor's individual moves turn out to *be* the figures we have spent eight lectures naming.

The Socratic question that does not want information but wants the learner to arrive — that is *erotema*, the rhetorical question, the figure that hands the listener the conclusion to draw. The tutor who says "you might wonder why this step is legal; here is why" is performing *hypophora*, voicing the learner's forming question and answering it. The correction "you are not *adding* two separate numbers, you are *combining* two parts of one whole" is an *antithesis*, the scheme of distinction, carrying the right conception against the wrong one in a single balanced frame. Every illuminating "think of it as…" is a *metaphor*, the master trope, the transfer that lets the learner cross from the known to the unknown — and every one carries the metaphor's standing risk, that the picture misleads (the "orbiting" electron we worried about in Lecture 6). The good takeaway phrased as a tricolon — "find the common denominator, add the numerators, simplify" — is a *scheme* doing the learner's *memoria*, building the insight into a shape the mind can keep.

And above the level of the single move, the *whole* exchange has the form of a drama, exactly as Lecture 1 promised. The learner's misconception is the *hamartia*, the flaw the action works through; the back-and-forth is the *desis* and *lysis*, the tying and untying of the knot; and the moment the learner sees it differently is the *anagnorisis*, the recognition, arriving at the *peripeteia*, the turn — the change from ignorance to knowledge that the whole arrangement was *for*. The tutor is a dramatist of recognition, and the figures are the instruments.

Now the caution, and it is the most important sentence in the course, so I will state it without any softening. *Everything I have just said names form.* The drama-ontology and the figure-classes describe the *shape* of what was said: that a recognition-shaped move was made, that an antithesis was deployed, that the dialogue had the arc of a complex plot. None of it — not one class, not one edge in the graph — tells you that a mind actually changed. A dialogue can have flawless figural and dramatic form and leave the learner exactly as confused as before; the catharsis can be perfectly staged and land on no one. The companion system reads the *text* and classifies its *form*. It does not, and we must never claim it does, read the interior — it does not detect understanding, or belief, or the real movement of a mind from not-knowing to knowing. It detects the *figure* of that movement. Keeping the figure and the fact apart is the whole discipline; collapsing them is the one error the entire course is built to prevent.
```

### Closing the loop — the turn at every level

- One motif has recurred at every scale of made language. It is the **turn**:
  - **peripeteia** — the turn of the *plot* (Lecture 2);
  - **tropos** — the turn of the *word's meaning* (Lecture 6);
  - **chiasmus** — the turn of the *sentence's syntax* (Lecture 7);
  - **anagnorisis** — the turn from *ignorance to knowledge* (Lectures 2–3).
- They are one figure at four scales — and the companion ontology even draws the line, with `ms:Chiasmus seeAlso ms:Peripeteia`.
- The course itself has the shape of a return: it began at **poiesis** (making) and ends at a **maker** — the tutor — read through the whole made vocabulary. A chiasmus eight lectures long.

```notes
Let me gather the motif that has been surfacing since Lecture 2, because naming it is the right way to end. One word has kept returning, in different costumes, at every level of made language: the *turn*. At the level of the whole plot, it is *peripeteia* — the action wheeling round to its opposite. At the level of the single word, it is *tropos* — the literal root of "trope," a word swerving from its proper sense. At the level of the sentence, it is *chiasmus* — the syntax folding back on itself in a mirror. And at the level of the mind, it is *anagnorisis* — the turn from ignorance to knowledge, recognition itself.

These are not four coincidentally similar things. They are one figure realised at four scales — the turn that articulates made language wherever it is jointed, from the arc of a tragedy down to the crossing of a single clause and inward to the pivot of a learner's understanding. The companion ontology even commits the kinship to a line you can read: the class for chiasmus carries a *seeAlso* to the class for peripeteia — the syntactic turn pointed straight at the dramatic one.

And the course itself has performed the figure it studied. We began, in Lecture 1, with *poiesis* — making — and with *mimesis*, the representation of agents acting. We have ended with an agent — the tutor — making, and representing, and being read in turn through the very vocabulary of making we set out to learn. Out to the figures, and back to the maker: the course is a chiasmus eight lectures long, and you have just felt it close. 

What you are left holding is a glossary — the working terms of made language, Greek and Roman, drama and rhetoric, joined. You can read it as classical poetics and rhetoric, which it is. Or you can read it as the grammar of a machine that tries to stage recognition in a conversation — and now you can read that machine critically, because you know the difference, which it cannot draw for itself, between a recognition and the figure of one. That difference is the course's parting gift, and its parting warning. Thank you.
```

### Where this leaves us

- You now hold the full vocabulary of **made language**: making and representation; the parts and arc of drama; the appeals; the five canons; tropes, schemes, and figures of thought.
- Two readings of it remain live, as they have all along:
  - as **classical poetics and rhetoric** — the terms in their own right;
  - as the **glossary of a system** that stages tutoring as a drama, which you can now read *critically*.
- The parting discipline: keep the **figure** and the **fact** apart. A recognition-*shaped* dialogue is not a proof that anyone recognised anything. The form is real and nameable; the uptake is not ours to read.

```notes
There is no next lecture, so instead of looking ahead, let me say where this leaves you. You hold a complete vocabulary now — the terms the Greek and Roman traditions worked out for how a piece of language is built and what it does to those who receive it, from the largest unit (the whole made action) to the smallest (the crossing of a clause), and now including the figures pitched at the level of the idea itself.

And you hold it as a *double* instrument, which is what I most wanted you to carry away. Read one way, it is the classical inheritance, valuable on its own terms — you can now read a tragedy, an oration, a poem, a sentence, and name what it is doing and why it works. Read the other way, it is the glossary of the system that surrounds this course, which represents a tutoring session as a small drama and files the tutor's moves as figures — and you can now read *that* system critically, which is the more important skill, because you know the seam it is built on. The system can recognise the *figure* of recognition with real precision. It cannot tell you whether a mind changed. You can keep those two apart; it cannot. Carrying that distinction carefully — honouring the form without mistaking it for the fact — is the whole of what I hoped to teach. Go and read everything as made.
```
