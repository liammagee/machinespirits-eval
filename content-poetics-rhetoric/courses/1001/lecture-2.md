### The "whole" action

- A plot imitates *one action*, and that action must be a **whole** — with a beginning, a middle, and an end (ch. 7). Aristotle means this technically:
  - a **beginning** does not necessarily follow from anything else, but something naturally follows it;
  - an **end** necessarily or usually follows from what came before, and has nothing after it;
  - a **middle** follows something and is followed by something.
- Trivial-sounding, decisive in use: it rules out plots that merely start, or merely stop.

```notes
"A whole," Aristotle says, "is that which has a beginning, a middle, and an end." You may smile — what else would it have? But read his definitions and the smile fades, because each term is defined by its place in a chain of necessity.

A beginning is "that which does not itself follow anything by causal necessity, but after which something naturally is or comes to be." It is where the causal chain *starts* for the purposes of this action — not preceded, within the work, by the thing that made it happen.

An end is the reverse: "that which itself naturally follows some other thing, either by necessity, or as a rule, but has nothing following it." It is where the chain comes to rest.

And a middle "follows something as some other thing follows it." It is doubly bound — caused and causing.

What this rules out is more interesting than what it permits. A drama that simply *begins* in the middle of things and *stops* without resolution is, by this definition, not a whole. A well-made plot is not a slice cut arbitrarily from the flow of events; it is a complete causal arc, sealed at both ends. This is why you can feel a botched ending as a violation and not merely a disappointment — it leaves the chain dangling, and Aristotle has told you that an end is precisely the link with nothing after it.
```

### Magnitude and order

- Beauty, Aristotle says, depends on **magnitude and order** (ch. 7). A plot must be a *size* — large enough for a change of fortune to unfold, small enough to be held in view.
- His image is the living organism: a creature too tiny to see, or too vast to take in at a glance, is not beautiful, because its unity cannot be perceived.
- The working limit: a length "which can be easily embraced by the memory."

```notes
Having defined the whole, Aristotle asks about its size, and gives one of his loveliest analogies. A beautiful object, whether a living creature or anything composed of parts, must not only have its parts in order but also be of a certain magnitude — "for beauty depends on magnitude and order."

He pushes it both ways. A very tiny creature cannot be beautiful to us, because our view of it "is confused, the object being seen in an almost imperceptible moment of time." But neither can an immense one — say, a creature a thousand miles long — "for then the unity and sense of the whole is lost for the beholder." You cannot take it in.

The plot is like the animal. It needs room enough for the change from bad fortune to good, or good to bad, to develop "in the sequence of events according to the law of probability or necessity." And it needs a ceiling, which he names with a wonderfully practical criterion: a magnitude "which can be easily embraced by the memory." Long enough to turn; short enough to hold in mind as one thing.

Notice that "held in one view" is the same demand he made of the very small and very large creature. Unity is something *perceived*. A plot that overflows the memory has, for the spectator, no shape — and shape is where the meaning lives.
```

### Unity of action — the one true unity

- Unity of plot does **not** mean unity of the *hero* (ch. 8). One man's life contains countless events that form no single action.
- It means one action, complete, whose parts are "so closely connected that the transposal or withdrawal of any one of them will disjoin and dislocate the whole."
- This is the genuine Aristotelian unity — and, we will see, the *only* one he actually held.

```notes
Now Aristotle corrects a mistake, and the correction is the heart of the lecture. Some people think, he says, that a plot is unified if it is about one person — a single hero. This is wrong. "Infinitely various are the incidents in one man's life which cannot be reduced to unity." Homer understood this: in composing the *Odyssey* he did *not* put in everything that ever happened to Odysseus — the wound on Parnassus, the feigned madness at the muster — because those events had no necessary or probable connection to *this* action, the homecoming.

So unity is unity of *action*, not of agent. And Aristotle gives a beautiful test for it, one worth memorising: the structural parts of the plot must be "so closely connected that the transposal or withdrawal of any one of them will disjoin and dislocate the whole. For a thing whose presence or absence makes no visible difference is not an organic part of the whole."

That is a stringent standard. It says: if you can lift an episode out and nothing breaks, the episode was never part of the action — it was decoration. A unified plot has no removable scenes. Every incident earns its place by being load-bearing.

I am flagging this one in particular because it is the unity Aristotle actually argued for — unity of *action*. Hold that, because in a few minutes two impostor unities are going to show up claiming his authority, and we are going to turn them away at the door.
```

### Probability and necessity — poetry vs. history

- The poet's job is not to report what happened, but **what may happen** — "what is possible according to the law of probability or necessity" (ch. 9).
- Hence the famous claim: **poetry is more philosophical than history.** History tells particulars (what Alcibiades did); poetry tells universals (what such a person *would* probably do).
- The enemy is the **episodic** plot — episodes following one another "without probable or necessary sequence." Aristotle calls it the worst kind.

```notes
Chapter 9 contains the single most quoted sentence in the *Poetics*, and it follows directly from the demand for unity. If every part must connect by necessity or probability, then the poet's material is not *the actual* but *the possible*.

"It is not the function of the poet to relate what has happened, but what may happen — what is possible according to the law of probability or necessity." The historian and the poet differ not because one writes in verse and the other in prose — you could put Herodotus into verse and he would still be a historian. They differ because "the one describes the thing that has been, and the other a kind of thing that might be."

And then the claim that elevated poetry for two thousand years: "Poetry, therefore, is a more philosophical and a higher thing than history: for poetry tends to express the universal, history the particular." The universal — how a certain kind of person will, by probability or necessity, speak or act — is exactly what a well-built plot exhibits. History is stuck with what *did* happen, even when what happened was a meaningless coincidence.

The shadow side of this is the *episodic* plot, which he names the worst of all. "I call a plot episodic in which the episodes or acts succeed one another without probable or necessary sequence." A string of "and then, and then, and then." It fails not because it is eventful but because its events are merely sequential, not consequential. The cure is always the same: bind the incidents by necessity or probability, so that each arises *because of* what came before.
```

### Simple and complex plots

- Aristotle sorts plots into two kinds (ch. 10):
  - a **simple plot** — the change of fortune happens *without* reversal or recognition;
  - a **complex plot** — the change *is accompanied by* reversal (*peripeteia*), recognition (*anagnorisis*), or both.
- Complex plots are the higher achievement, because reversal and recognition are how a plot produces wonder and the tragic emotions.

```notes
With unity and consequence in hand, Aristotle draws the distinction that organises the rest of the discussion. Actions, and the plots that imitate them, are either *simple* or *complex*.

"An action which is one and continuous… I call simple, when the change of fortune takes place without reversal of the situation and without recognition." Things go from good to bad, or bad to good, in a straight line — sad or happy, but unturned.

"A complex action is one in which the change is accompanied by such reversal, or by recognition, or by both." Here the change of fortune is produced *through* a swerve or a discovery.

The two kinds are not equal in Aristotle's eyes. The complex plot is the harder and finer thing, because reversal and recognition are precisely the devices by which tragedy achieves its proper pleasure — the shiver of the surprising-yet-inevitable, the pity and fear of the recognition that comes too late. A simple plot can be moving; a complex one can do something a simple one cannot. So we had better understand its parts, which is the next slide.
```

### The three parts of the plot

- A complex plot has **three working parts** (ch. 11):
  - **Peripeteia** — *reversal*: a change by which the action veers round to its opposite, by probability or necessity.
  - **Anagnorisis** — *recognition*: a change from ignorance to knowledge, producing love or hate.
  - **Pathos** — *the scene of suffering*: a destructive or painful action — death, agony, wounds.
- The finest stroke: when reversal and recognition **coincide**, as in the *Oedipus*. That conjunction is the summit of the dramatic art.

```notes
Here are the three parts, and they are the engine room of the whole *Poetics*.

*Peripeteia*, reversal: "a change by which the action veers round to its opposite, subject always to our rule of probability or necessity." The crucial word is *opposite*. Not just a setback — a swing to the contrary of what was intended or expected. Aristotle's example is the messenger who comes to free Oedipus from fear about his mother and, by revealing who he is, produces the very opposite effect.

*Anagnorisis*, recognition: "a change from ignorance to knowledge, producing love or hate between the persons destined by the poet for good or bad fortune." The finest recognition is of *persons* — who someone really is — but Aristotle allows recognition of facts and deeds too. Recall the seed we planted last lecture: the small pleasure of recognising what a picture depicts is the same shape as this, the great recognition on which a life turns.

*Pathos*, the scene of suffering: "a destructive or painful action, such as deaths on the stage, bodily agony, wounds, and the like." This is the third part, and the most physical — the felt cost of the action.

And then the sentence that names the peak of the art. The two parts that turn upon surprise — reversal and recognition — are most powerful when they fall *together*: recognition "is best when it coincides with a reversal of the situation, as in the *Oedipus*." When the discovery *is* the reversal — when learning the truth is the very stroke that overturns the situation — tragedy does the most it can do. We will see this conjunction again as the gold standard the whole system is reaching for.

One note on names, since we will need it in Lecture 4. This *pathos* — the scene of suffering — is not the same as the rhetorical *pathos*, the appeal to the audience's emotions, that we meet when we cross to rhetoric. Same Greek word, two jobs. The system's ontology keeps them apart: the tragic one is `ms:Pathos`; the rhetorical appeal will get its own name later.
```

### Complication and unravelling

- Every plot divides into two phases (ch. 18):
  - **Desis** — the *complication*, or tying of the knot: from the start of the action to the turning-point.
  - **Lysis** — the *unravelling*, or denouement: from the turning-point to the end.
- The turning-point is the hinge. Everything before ties the knot; everything after unties it.

```notes
The last structural idea is the simplest to state and the most useful to hold. In chapter 18 Aristotle says that every tragedy falls into two parts, and he borrows a metaphor from rope and weaving.

The first is *desis* — the complication, literally the *tying*. It runs "from the beginning of the action to the part which marks the turning-point to good or bad fortune." This is the building of the knot: the circumstances, the entanglements, the pressures that accumulate.

The second is *lysis* — the unravelling, the *loosening* or denouement. It runs "from the beginning of the change to the end." Once the action turns, the knot comes undone, and we watch the consequences play out to rest.

Between them sits the turning-point — and you will notice that the turning-point is where the *peripeteia* lives. The reversal is the hinge between the tying and the untying. So the parts of the plot and the phases of the plot are two views of the same structure: *desis* builds toward the reversal, the reversal turns the action, *lysis* carries it home, and somewhere in the turn the recognition lands.

This is the cleanest map of dramatic shape we have, and it is the one that transfers most directly to a conversation. A tutoring exchange has a tying — the building of a misunderstanding under pressure — and an untying — what follows once the learner's view turns over. The hinge between them is the moment of recognition.
```

### The "three unities," untangled

- The neoclassical tradition speaks of **three unities** — of action, time, and place — and credits them to Aristotle. Only one is his.
- **Unity of action** — genuinely Aristotle's (ch. 8), as we saw.
- **Unity of time** — Aristotle merely *observes* (ch. 5) that tragedy "tends to confine itself to a single revolution of the sun." A passing remark, not a rule.
- **Unity of place** — *not in Aristotle at all.*
- The three-unity doctrine was codified by **Castelvetro (1570)** and hardened by French neoclassicism. A lesson in how a tradition rewrites its founder.

```notes
Now we keep the promise from last week and turn two impostors away at the door.

If you have encountered the *Poetics* secondhand, you have probably met "the three unities" — action, time, and place — presented as the iron law of classical drama, handed down by Aristotle. It is one of the most durable misattributions in the history of criticism.

Unity of *action* is real and is his. We spent a whole slide on it: one action, whole, with no removable parts. That is chapter 8, argued for explicitly.

Unity of *time* is a different matter. The only thing Aristotle says is an observation, not a prescription. In chapter 5, comparing tragedy to epic, he notes that tragedy "endeavours, as far as possible, to confine itself to a single revolution of the sun, or but slightly to exceed this limit." He is describing a tendency of the tragedies he knew, contrasting their compactness with epic's sprawl. He does not lay it down as a requirement, and he attaches no weight to it.

Unity of *place* — that the action occupy a single location — is not in the *Poetics* at all. You will not find it. It was inferred later, by analogy, to keep company with the other two.

So where did "the three unities" come from? The codification is usually credited to the Italian critic Lodovico Castelvetro, whose 1570 commentary on the *Poetics* spelled out all three as rules, and the doctrine was then enforced with great severity by French neoclassical critics in the next century — it is behind the famous quarrel over Corneille's *Le Cid*. A tendency Aristotle mentioned in passing became, two thousand years on, a law issued in his name.

The lesson is larger than this one case. Traditions do not merely transmit their founders; they *rewrite* them, projecting later needs backward and signing the founder's name. Reading the *Poetics* directly, rather than through the neoclassical filter, is itself an exercise in recognition — a small *anagnorisis* about a text you thought you knew. The system's ontology marks the distinction explicitly: it records which unity is Aristotle's and which two are not.
```

### The reflexive turn — a lesson as a complex plot

- Read a tutoring exchange dramatically and the architecture transfers whole:
  - the learner's misconception accumulates under pressure — the **complication** (*desis*);
  - the exchange turns when the learner's view flips — the **reversal** (*peripeteia*), ideally *coincident* with the learner naming what they had missed (*anagnorisis*);
  - what follows works the insight through to rest — the **unravelling** (*lysis*).
- A lesson with that shape is, in the ontology's terms, a **complex plot** (`ms:ComplexPlot`) — not a simple change of state but one accomplished *through* a recognition.

```notes
Let me close by making the transfer explicit, now that we have all the parts.

Take an ordinary good lesson and look at its shape. There is a tying: the learner arrives with a misconception, and as the conversation proceeds the misconception is put under pressure — examples that don't fit, questions it can't answer, a contradiction surfacing. That accumulation is the *desis*, the complication.

Then, if the lesson works, there is a turn. The learner's framing flips to its opposite — the thing they were sure of becomes the thing they were wrong about. That swerve is a *peripeteia*. And the finest version, exactly as in the *Oedipus*, is when the turn and the discovery are the *same stroke*: when the learner, in the act of seeing the truth, reverses their own position — "oh, I see, I was adding the denominators because I was treating the fraction as two separate whole numbers." Reversal and recognition coincident. That is the summit Aristotle named, transposed into a classroom.

And then the *lysis*: the working-through, the consolidation, the new understanding carried to rest.

A lesson with this shape is what the system calls a complex plot — `ms:ComplexPlot`, a *mythos* that contains a reversal or a recognition, as opposed to a simple change of state. The whole apparatus we built today — whole action, necessity, the three parts, complication and unravelling — is the grammar the machine uses to ask whether a given conversation was a complex plot or merely an episodic one. Next lecture we look at *why* it should be tragic at all: at the flaw that drives the change, and the clarification it is for.
```
