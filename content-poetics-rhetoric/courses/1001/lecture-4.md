
## <a id="main-lecture"></a>Lecture 4 — From Poetics to Rhetoric

---

### Where we are

- We have finished the Greek arc of *poiesis*: making, the six parts, the architecture of plot, and the affective end (catharsis, wonder).
- Today we cross a bridge — from the made *drama* to the persuasive *speech* — on a span Aristotle built himself.
- The crossing point is his **three appeals**: **ethos** (character), **pathos** (emotion), **logos** (argument). From here the course turns Roman.

```notes
For three lectures we have studied *poiesis* — making — and its highest form, tragedy. Today we change subject, but not as sharply as you might think, because the man who wrote the *Poetics* also wrote the *Rhetoric*, and the two books speak to each other across his desk.

The *Poetics* asks how a maker builds an artefact — a plot — that does something to an audience. The *Rhetoric* asks how a speaker builds an argument that does something to an audience. Same final cause — an effect on the people receiving the work — different means. And there is a literal seam between them: the *Poetics* devotes its later chapters to *lexis*, diction, the fourth of our six parts; and the *Rhetoric*'s third book is also about *lexis* — style and delivery. Aristotle himself cross-refers. So we are not leaping to a new country; we are walking across a bridge.

The bridge's keystone is one of the most influential triads in the history of thought: the three means of persuasion. Today we lay them down carefully, because everything Roman that follows — the canons, the figures, the whole apparatus — is built on top of them. And we finally cash out a promise from Lecture 2 about a treacherous homonym.
```

---

### One author, two arts

- Aristotle wrote both the *Poetics* and the *Rhetoric*; they share a vocabulary (*lexis*, audience, the parts of a made thing) and a method (analysis by cause and part).
- **Rhetoric**, defined (*Rhet.* I.2): "the faculty of observing in any given case the available means of persuasion."
- Note the definition: rhetoric is not persuasion itself, but the *capacity to see what is persuadable* in a situation — an analytic art, like poetics.

```notes
Start with the definition, because it is exact and often misremembered. Aristotle does not define rhetoric as "the art of persuading," still less as "the art of winning." He defines it as "the faculty of observing in any given case the available means of persuasion." It is a power of *observation* — of seeing, in a particular situation, what the persuasive resources are. You can possess the art fully and still lose, the way a physician can possess medicine fully and still lose a patient; the art is in the seeing, not in the guaranteed result.

This matters for two reasons. First, it makes rhetoric a *neutral* art, a tool — which is exactly why it has always been suspect, from Plato's attack in the *Gorgias* onward. The same faculty that lets you see the available means of persuasion in a just cause lets you see them in an unjust one. Second, it makes rhetoric *analytic*, like the *Poetics*: both books take a complex human practice and break it into causes and parts so that it can be studied and taught. Aristotle is doing the same kind of thing in both — anatomising an art. That shared method is why the two books fit together as the front and back of a single study of how made language acts on an audience.
```

---

### The three appeals

- Persuasion through speech, Aristotle says, has exactly **three** sources — the three *pisteis*, or proofs (*Rhet.* I.2):
  - **Ethos** — the personal *character* of the speaker, as the speech presents it;
  - **Pathos** — the *emotional state* the speech puts the audience into;
  - **Logos** — the *argument* itself, the proof or apparent proof in the words.
- These are not optional styles. They are the three places persuasion can come *from*: the speaker, the hearer, and the speech.

```notes
Here is the triad, in Aristotle's own division. "Of the modes of persuasion furnished by the spoken word there are three kinds. The first kind depends on the personal character of the speaker; the second on putting the audience into a certain frame of mind; the third on the proof, or apparent proof, provided by the words of the speech itself."

The elegance is that the three are *exhaustive by location*. A persuasive speech is a transaction among three things and only three: a speaker, an audience, and a message. So persuasion can be sourced from exactly three places — from who is speaking (ethos), from how the listener is feeling (pathos), or from what is actually said (logos). There is nowhere else for it to come from. That is why the triad has survived twenty-four centuries: it is not a list someone happened to draw up, it is a partition of a space.

Let us take each in turn, because each has a precise content that the loose modern usage ("ethos = credibility, pathos = emotion, logos = logic") flattens.
```

---

### Ethos — persuasion by character

- **Ethos**: we believe a speaker we find credible. Its parts (*Rhet.* II.1): good sense (*phronesis*), virtue (*aretē*), and goodwill toward the audience (*eunoia*).
- Aristotle's strong claim: ethos should be produced **by the speech itself**, not by the speaker's prior reputation — "the most effective means of persuasion he possesses."
- Note *eunoia* — goodwill, wishing the audience well. It is the relational hinge we will return to.

```notes
Ethos is persuasion through the character the speaker projects. We extend more belief, and more readily, to someone we judge to be credible — and Aristotle breaks credibility into three components in Book II. *Phronesis*: practical good sense, the judgment to know what is so and what to do. *Aretē*: virtue, moral character, the disposition not to mislead. And *eunoia*: goodwill — actually wishing your audience well, being on their side.

His strong and characteristic claim is that this credibility must be *earned within the speech*. "This kind of persuasion, like the others, should be achieved by what the speaker says, not by what people think of his character before he begins to speak." Prior reputation is not, for Aristotle, rhetorical ethos; the art is in *constructing* a credible character through the speaking itself. He calls ethos "the most effective means of persuasion" the speaker has.

I want to flag *eunoia* — goodwill — and leave a marker on it, because it is the component that will matter most when we turn the apparatus on tutoring. Goodwill is not a technique for getting your way; it is the orientation of genuinely wanting the good of the person you are addressing. Hold that; recognition theory and the whole tutoring project turn on it.
```

### Pathos — persuasion by emotion (and a homonym to defuse)

- **Pathos**: putting the audience into the emotional state in which they are most persuadable. *Rhetoric* Book II is a systematic *psychology of the emotions* — anger, fear, pity, confidence, shame — each with its causes.
- **The homonym, at last.** This rhetorical *pathos* (audience-emotion) is **not** the tragic *pathos* (the scene of suffering, the third part of the plot, Lecture 2). Same Greek word, two jobs.
- The ontology keeps them apart by name: the appeal is `ms:PathosAppeal`; the tragic part is `ms:Pathos`.

```notes
Pathos is persuasion through the audience's emotions — "awakening emotion in the audience so as to induce them to make the judgment desired." What is striking is that Aristotle does not treat this as a dark art of manipulation; he treats it as a science. The whole of Book II of the *Rhetoric* is a systematic psychology of the emotions, taken one at a time — anger and calm, friendship and enmity, fear and confidence, shame, kindness, pity, indignation, envy. For each he asks the same three questions: what state of mind are people in when they feel it, whom do they feel it toward, and on what grounds? It is arguably the first systematic treatise on the emotions in the Western tradition, written as a manual for a speaker who needs to know how feeling actually works.

And now the promise from Lecture 2 comes due. We have now met *pathos* twice in this course, meaning two different things. In the *Poetics*, *pathos* is the scene of suffering — the third part of the plot, the destructive or painful action, deaths and wounds. In the *Rhetoric*, *pathos* is an appeal — the arousal of emotion *in the audience* as a means of persuasion. Same word; one names a component of the represented action, the other names an effect aimed at the receiver.

This is exactly the kind of collision that makes a formal vocabulary worth building, because a formal vocabulary cannot tolerate a pun. So the companion ontology splits them by name. The tragic part is `ms:Pathos` (Lecture 2's slice). The rhetorical appeal is `ms:PathosAppeal`. Two classes, two URIs, no collision — and the same discipline applies to ethos, which is also a homonym, as the next slide shows.
```

### Logos — persuasion by the argument

- **Logos**: the proof in the words themselves. Its instruments (*Rhet.* I.2):
  - the **enthymeme** — the *rhetorical syllogism*, an argument from probabilities and signs, often with a premise left unstated for the audience to supply;
  - the **example** (*paradeigma*) — rhetorical induction, persuasion from cases.
- Aristotle calls the enthymeme "the **body** of persuasion" — logos is the substance, not the ornament.

```notes
Logos is persuasion through the argument itself — "we persuade by the proof, or apparent proof, provided by the words of the speech." And here Aristotle is emphatic about priority: the enthymeme, he says, is "the body of proof," the very substance of rhetorical persuasion. Everything else — the character, the emotion — works on top of an actual argument. Logos is not one flavour among three; it is the load-bearing one.

It has two instruments. The *enthymeme* is the rhetorical counterpart of the syllogism — but a syllogism fitted to public speech. It argues from what is *probable* and from *signs*, not from necessary first principles, because human affairs are not certain. And it characteristically *leaves a premise unspoken*, for the audience to supply in their own heads — which is part of its persuasive power, because a conclusion you complete yourself feels like your own. The *example*, *paradeigma*, is the inductive instrument: persuasion by precedent and parallel case, reasoning from the particular to the particular by way of an implied general.

Keep the enthymeme in view. An argument that deliberately leaves a step for the listener to take is, structurally, very close to what a good teacher does — and very close to the recognition we have been circling since Lecture 1. We will make that connection explicit at the end.
```

### The three genres of oratory

- Aristotle sorts speeches by their audience and their time (*Rhet.* I.3):
  - **Deliberative** (political) — addressed to a decision; about the **future**; the question is the *expedient* vs. the harmful;
  - **Forensic** (judicial) — addressed to a court; about the **past**; the question is the *just* vs. the unjust;
  - **Epideictic** (ceremonial) — addressed to spectators; about the **present**; the question is the *honourable* vs. the base (praise and blame).
- Each genre weights the appeals differently — a useful reminder that *which* appeal leads depends on the situation.

```notes
Aristotle's last great division is by *kind of occasion*, and it falls out of asking who the audience is and what they are being asked to judge. There are, he says, three.

*Deliberative* oratory is the speech of the assembly — the political speech that urges a course of action or warns against one. Its audience is a decision-maker; its time is the *future* (what shall we do?); and its governing question is the *expedient* and the harmful.

*Forensic* or judicial oratory is the speech of the law court — accusation and defence. Its audience is a judge of things already done; its time is the *past* (what happened, and was it just?); and its governing question is the *just* and the unjust.

*Epideictic* or ceremonial oratory is the speech of display — the funeral oration, the panegyric, the speech of praise or blame. Its audience is, in effect, a spectator judging the speaker's skill; its time is the *present*; and its governing question is the *honourable* and the base.

The reason this matters for us is that it shows the three appeals are not used in fixed proportion. A forensic speech leans hard on logos and on forensic ethos; an epideictic speech may be almost all pathos and amplification. Which appeal leads is a function of the occasion. A tutor, too, is in different "genres" at different moments — sometimes deliberating about what to do next, sometimes judging an answer already given, sometimes simply praising an effort — and the balance of appeals shifts accordingly.
```

### The reflexive turn — the tutor as persuader

- A tutoring turn is a small act of rhetoric. The three appeals map directly, and the ontology names them as `ms:RhetoricalAppeal`s:
  - **ethos** (`ms:EthosAppeal`) — the tutor's credibility *and goodwill* (*eunoia*): being visibly on the learner's side;
  - **pathos** (`ms:PathosAppeal`) — reading and addressing the learner's emotional and motivational state;
  - **logos** (`ms:LogosAppeal`) — the actual explanation, and especially the **enthymeme** that leaves the last step for the learner to take.
- The recognition connection: *eunoia* (goodwill) is where rhetoric touches the ethics of recognition — wanting the learner's good, not merely the correct answer.
- **Caution, again.** This classifies the *form* of the turn (does it deploy ethos / pathos / logos?), not whether persuasion or learning truly occurred.

```notes
Turn the triad on the tutor. Every tutoring move is a small persuasive act: it is trying to bring a learner to see something, and it has exactly the three resources Aristotle named.

There is the tutor's *ethos* — the credibility the turn projects, and above all the *eunoia*, the goodwill: the sense, conveyed by the speaking itself, that the tutor is on the learner's side and wants their understanding, not their submission. There is *pathos* — the turn's attention to where the learner actually is emotionally: frustrated, overconfident, discouraged, curious — and its work to put them in a state where they can take the next step. And there is *logos* — the explanation itself, the argument. The best tutorial logos is often an *enthymeme*: it lays out the premises and stops just short of the conclusion, leaving the learner to supply the last step — so that the insight, when it comes, feels like theirs. That is the recognition we have been pursuing, dressed now in rhetorical clothes.

Notice where the deepest connection lies. Of the three components of ethos, *eunoia* — goodwill — is the one that opens onto ethics, and onto the recognition theory the whole surrounding project is built on. A merely competent tutor has phronesis and aretē; a tutor who *recognises* the learner has eunoia, wants their flourishing, treats them as an end. The companion system files all three appeals under `ms:RhetoricalAppeal`, with `ms:EthosAppeal`, `ms:PathosAppeal`, and `ms:LogosAppeal` as its kinds — and the suffix on each is doing real work, holding them apart from the tragic `ms:Ethos` and `ms:Pathos` of the earlier lectures.

And the standing caution. To say a turn "uses pathos" is to classify its *form* — that it does emotional work — not to certify that the learner was actually moved, or actually learned. We read the rhetoric of the transcript. Whether it worked is a different question, and not one the form can answer.
```

### Looking ahead

- We have the Greek foundation of persuasion — the three appeals and the three genres.
- **Next lecture — The Five Canons.** Rome systematises rhetoric into five stages of producing a speech: *inventio*, *dispositio*, *elocutio*, *memoria*, *pronuntiatio*. The course turns Roman, and a **new ontology module** (`rhetoric-core.ttl`) opens to hold it.
- Carry forward: the three appeals (ethos / pathos / logos), and especially the **enthymeme** and *eunoia*.

```notes
We now have the Greek bedrock of rhetoric: the definition (the faculty of seeing the available means of persuasion), the three appeals, and the three genres. Everything from here is Roman — the great systematisers, Cicero and Quintilian and the author of the *Rhetorica ad Herennium*, who took Aristotle's analysis and turned it into a complete pedagogy: a sequence of stages for producing a speech, a doctrine of styles, and an enormous catalogue of named figures.

Next lecture we lay down the frame that organises all of it: the five canons of rhetoric — invention, arrangement, style, memory, and delivery — the five things an orator does, in order, to get from a blank mind to a delivered speech. This is also where the course's ontology grows a second module. Until now every class we have declared has gone into `poetics-core.ttl`, the Greek file. The Roman material gets its own file, `rhetoric-core.ttl` — mirroring, in the ontology, the very crossing we made today.

Read for next time: the opening of the *Rhetorica ad Herennium* Book I on the five *officia oratoris*, and, if you can, Quintilian on the same. Carry forward the three appeals — and keep the enthymeme and *eunoia* especially close; they are the threads that tie this Roman machinery back to recognition.
```
