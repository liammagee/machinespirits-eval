
## <a id="main-lecture"></a>Lecture 6 — Tropes

---

### Where we are

- We are inside the third canon, **elocutio** (style), under its virtue **ornatus** (ornament) — the home of the figures.
- The figures fall into two great families: **tropes**, which turn a word's *meaning*; and **schemes**, which turn the *arrangement* of words. Today: tropes.
- A *trope* (Greek *tropos*, "a turn") swerves a word away from its literal sense — and that swerve is kin to the *peripeteia*, the turn of a plot.

```notes
We have spent five lectures getting to this room, and now we are in it. Recall the map: the third canon is elocutio, style; within elocutio the third virtue is ornatus, ornament; and under ornatus live the figures. The figures are the most concrete legacy of the whole tradition — the named, teachable devices that the Renaissance counted in the hundreds — and they are what this course is finally about.

They divide into two families, and the division is the most important conceptual distinction in the back half of the course, so fix it now. A *trope* changes the *meaning* of a word — it turns the word from its literal sense to another. A *scheme* changes the *arrangement* of words — their order, their sound, their syntax — without altering their literal meaning. Metaphor is a trope: "she is a lion" turns the meaning of "lion." Antithesis is a scheme: "ask not what your country can do for you, but what you can do for your country" arranges words in a balanced opposition, but every word keeps its plain meaning. Tropes today; schemes next lecture.

And hear the word itself: *tropos*, a turn. A trope is a *turning* of sense — and we have met a turn before, the *peripeteia*, the reversal that swings a plot to its opposite. This is not a coincidence of vocabulary. The same deep idea — meaning made by a swerve — runs through both the drama and the figure, and the last lecture is going to make that folding-back its explicit subject. For now, keep the kinship in your ear: a plot turns, a word turns, and both turns make meaning.
```

### What is a trope?

- **Quintilian's definition** (*Inst.* VIII.6.1): a trope is "the artistic alteration of a word or phrase from its proper meaning to another."
- The key word is *artistic* (*cum virtute*) — a trope is a *deliberate* turn for effect, not a mistake.
- Tropes are not decoration laid on plain meaning; they are often the *only* way to say a thing — abstractions become graspable only when turned into something concrete.

```notes
Quintilian gives the classic definition: a trope is "the artistic alteration of a word or phrase from its proper signification to another" — *a propria significatione in aliam cum virtute mutatio*. Three things in that definition repay attention.

First, *alteration of meaning*: the word is moved from its *proper* sense — its literal, dictionary sense — to another. This is what makes it a trope and not a scheme. Second, *cum virtute*, "with artistic merit," deliberately, for effect: a trope is a chosen turn, not a slip of the tongue. If you call a table's support a "leg" by mistake, that is an error; if a poet does it knowingly, exploiting the transfer, that is a trope. Third — and this is Quintilian's own emphasis — the turn is from the proper sense *to another*, which presupposes there *is* a proper sense being departed from.

But here is the deep point, and it is why tropes are not mere ornament. Very often the "proper" literal word does not exist, or cannot reach the thing. How do you speak of time? You turn a spatial word: time *passes*, the future *lies ahead*, we *look back* on the past. How do you speak of understanding? You turn a word of sight: "I *see* what you mean." These are tropes — turns of physical words onto non-physical things — and there is frequently no literal alternative. So the trope is not a pretty alternative to plain speech; it is, for whole regions of experience, the *only* speech there is. That is why this family matters more than its reputation as "ornament" suggests, and why the master of it, Aristotle said, is the master of language.
```

### Metaphor — the master trope

- **Metaphor** (Aristotle, *Poetics* 1457b): "the application of a word that belongs to another thing" — *transference*. His four kinds: genus→species, species→genus, species→species, and **by analogy** (A:B :: C:D).
- The analogy form is the deepest: "the evening of life" (old age : life :: evening : day).
- Aristotle's verdict (*Poetics* ch. 22): "the greatest thing by far is to be a master of metaphor… it is the mark of genius, for to make good metaphors implies an eye for resemblances."

```notes
Metaphor is the master trope — the one all the others are, in some accounts, variations of. And we return here to the Greek arc, because Aristotle's best discussion of metaphor is in the *Poetics* itself, chapters 21 and 22, among the parts of *lexis*.

His definition is *transference*: "metaphor is the application of an alien name by transference" — *epiphora*, a carrying-over. A name that belongs to one thing is carried over to another. And he sorts the transfer into four kinds. From *genus to species*: "here stands my ship" — standing is the genus, lying at anchor the species. From *species to genus*: "ten thousand noble deeds" for "many." From *species to species*. And the fourth and richest, *by analogy* — a proportional transfer of the form A is to B as C is to D. As old age is to life, so evening is to the day; therefore you may call old age "the evening of life," or call evening "the old age of the day." The proportion licenses the transfer.

This analogical metaphor is the deep one, because it works by *perceiving a structure shared between two different domains* — and that perception is exactly what Aristotle prizes. In chapter 22 he delivers his famous verdict: "the greatest thing by far is to be a master of metaphor. It is the one thing that cannot be learnt from others; and it is also a sign of genius, since a good metaphor implies an intuitive perception of the similarity in dissimilars." Read that against Lecture 1, where we said the small pleasure of recognising a likeness is the seed of the great recognition. Metaphor is that pleasure made into an art: the eye for resemblance, the perception of sameness across difference. The master of metaphor is a master of recognition.
```

### Metonymy — the turn by association

- **Metonymy** (Greek "change of name"): substituting a term for something *associated* with it — by contiguity, not resemblance.
- "The **crown** decided" (symbol → institution); "the **White House** said" (place → occupant); "I read **Shakespeare**" (author → works); "the **bottle** ruined him" (container → contents).
- Where metaphor leaps across domains on a likeness, metonymy slides *within* a domain along a real connection.

```notes
Metonymy — the word means "change of name" — is the trope of association. You name a thing not by a word for something it *resembles* (that would be metaphor) but by a word for something it is *connected to*, something contiguous to it in the world. The crown for the monarchy; the bottle for drink; the White House for the presidency; the stage for the theatrical profession; "Downing Street" for the British government; "reading Shakespeare" for reading his plays.

The contrast with metaphor is the thing to hold. Metaphor works by *resemblance* and leaps *across* domains: "the ship plows the sea" carries the word from farming to sailing on the strength of a likeness. Metonymy works by *real connection* and slides *within* a domain: "the crown" for the monarch travels along an actual link — the monarch wears the crown — not a resemblance. One is a jump on a likeness; the other a slide along a contiguity. Kenneth Burke glossed metonymy as *reduction* — using a tangible, particular sign to stand for something larger or more abstract it is bound up with. The crown is tangible; the institution of monarchy is not; the trope lets the tangible stand in.
```

### Synecdoche — the part and the whole

- **Synecdoche** ("taking together"): naming a thing by a *part* of it, or naming a part by the *whole*.
- *Part for whole*: "all **hands** on deck"; "nice **wheels**"; "give us this day our daily **bread**."
- *Whole for part*: "**England** won the match" (the team); "the **world** treated him badly" (some people).
- Often counted a species of metonymy — the association is specifically that of part to whole.

```notes
Synecdoche — the name means "taking together" — is the trope of part and whole. You name the whole by one of its parts, or a part by its whole. "All hands on deck" — hands for sailors, part for whole. "Nice wheels" — wheels for car. "The hired hand," "lend me your ears," "daily bread" for food in general. And the reverse, whole for part: "England won" when it was eleven players; "the city voted" when it was some of its citizens.

Many ancient and modern theorists treat synecdoche as a special case of metonymy, and you can see why: part-and-whole is one particular kind of real-world association, alongside container-and-contained, cause-and-effect, symbol-and-thing. So if metonymy is the genus of association-tropes, synecdoche is the species whose association is specifically *part to whole*. Burke linked it to *representation* — the part that genuinely *represents* the whole, as a representative stands for the constituency. We will let it stand as its own trope, because the part-whole relation is distinctive and important enough to name, but keep its kinship to metonymy in mind: both are turns along a real connection, not a resemblance.
```

### Irony — the turn against itself

- **Irony** (*eirōneia*): saying one thing and meaning another — typically the *opposite*. "A *fine* mess you've made."
- The turn here is the sharpest: the literal sense and the intended sense *diverge*, and the listener must perceive the gap.
- Irony scales: from a single barbed word, to *Socratic irony* (feigned ignorance), to *dramatic irony* (the audience knows what the character does not) — the bridge back to the drama.

```notes
Irony is the trope of divergence, and the most demanding, because it asks the listener to perceive that the speaker means the *contrary* of what the words say. "Oh, that's just wonderful," when it is a disaster. The surface says one thing; the intent is its opposite; and the whole effect lives in the gap between them, which the listener must leap.

What is striking is how irony scales. At the smallest, it is a single word turned against itself — sarcasm's barb. Larger, it is *Socratic irony*: the sustained pose of ignorance Socrates adopts, professing to know nothing while exposing that his interlocutors know less — irony as a whole philosophical method. Larger still, it is *dramatic irony*, which carries us straight back to the first half of the course: the situation in which the audience knows what the character does not, so that the character's words carry a second meaning, visible to us and invisible to them. Oedipus vowing to hunt down the murderer of Laius — not knowing he is hunting himself — is the supreme example, and you will notice it is built on the same machinery as *anagnorisis*: a gap between what is known and what is not, waiting to close.

So irony is the trope where the figure and the drama most obviously rejoin. The turn of the ironic word and the turn of the tragic recognition are the same shape — a divergence between surface and truth that the work eventually forces us to see. Hold that for the final lecture, where the whole vocabulary folds back on itself. And note one practical thing for tutoring: irony is risky pedagogy, because it *requires* the listener to already share enough ground to perceive the gap. Used with a learner who takes it literally, the ironic turn does not turn — it simply misinforms.
```

### The further tropes

- The family is larger. A quick tour of the most useful relatives:
  - **Catachresis** (*abusio*) — the forced or "misused" metaphor that fills a gap where no literal word exists: the *leg* of a table, the *mouth* of a river, the *face* of a cliff;
  - **Hyperbole** — overstatement for effect: "I've told you a thousand times";
  - **Litotes** — understatement by negation: "not bad at all"; "no small feat";
  - **Allegory** — sustained metaphor: a whole narrative that means another (the cave, the pilgrimage);
  - **Antonomasia** — an epithet for a name: "the Bard," "the Philosopher," "the Iron Lady."

```notes
The trope family is large, and the Renaissance handbooks list dozens, but a handful of relatives earn their keep and you should know them by name.

*Catachresis*, the Romans' *abusio* or "misuse," is the trope that fills a lexical gap. When a thing has no proper word, we force a borrowed one onto it: the *leg* of a table, the *mouth* of a river, the *arm* of a chair, the *face* of a cliff, the *hands* of a clock. These are dead metaphors that became the only available words — catachresis is metaphor pressed into service out of necessity, and it shows how the language quietly runs on frozen tropes we no longer feel as turns.

*Hyperbole* is overstatement — "a thousand times," "an ocean of tears" — exaggeration the listener is meant to discount and feel at once. *Litotes* is its opposite, understatement by negation: "not bad," "no small achievement," "she was not unmoved." The double negative affirms by denying the contrary, and English is peculiarly fond of it. *Allegory* is metaphor extended into a whole structure — a narrative whose every element means something else, like Plato's cave or Bunyan's pilgrimage; Quintilian indeed called allegory a continued metaphor. And *antonomasia* substitutes an epithet for a proper name: "the Bard" for Shakespeare, "the Philosopher" for Aristotle (as the medievals called him), "the Iron Lady" for Thatcher.

You do not need to memorise the whole catalogue. You need to feel the principle they share: each is a *turn of sense*, a word moved off its literal mark for an effect that the literal could not achieve.
```

### The four master tropes

- Giambattista Vico, and later Kenneth Burke ("The Four Master Tropes"), argued the whole family reduces to **four** fundamental turns, each a basic relation of thought:
  - **Metaphor** — resemblance;
  - **Metonymy** — association / reduction;
  - **Synecdoche** — part–whole / representation;
  - **Irony** — opposition / dialectic.
- On this view the tropes are not mere word-games but the elementary moves of the mind — the four ways meaning can be turned.

```notes
Let me close the survey with a unifying idea, because it lifts the tropes from a list to be memorised into something deeper. The eighteenth-century philosopher Giambattista Vico, in the *New Science*, proposed that beneath the sprawling catalogue lie just *four* master tropes, and the twentieth-century critic Kenneth Burke gave the idea its famous modern form in an essay called "The Four Master Tropes."

The four are the ones we have just studied: metaphor, metonymy, synecdoche, irony. And the claim is that each corresponds to a basic operation of *thought*, not merely of language. Metaphor is *resemblance* — perceiving one thing in terms of another. Metonymy is *reduction* — rendering the intangible through a tangible sign bound up with it. Synecdoche is *representation* — letting a part genuinely stand for a whole. Irony is *dialectic* — holding a thing together with its opposite. On this view the four tropes are the elementary ways the mind can *turn* a meaning: by likeness, by association, by part-for-whole, by opposition. There is nowhere else for a turn of sense to go.

Whether or not you buy the strong claim that these four exhaust thought, the frame is useful: it tells you that when you reach for a trope you are reaching for one of a small number of fundamental cognitive moves. The companion ontology records the distinction — it marks metaphor, metonymy, synecdoche, and irony as *master* tropes among the rest, the way Lecture 2's ontology marked which of the three unities was genuinely Aristotle's.
```

### The reflexive turn — the tutor's tropes

- The tutor's single most powerful instrument is a trope: the **analogy** — metaphor by proportion (this new thing is to its domain as that familiar thing is to yours).
- Explanation often *is* trope-work: turning an abstraction into something concrete the learner can hold ("electricity flows *like* water in a pipe").
- The risk mirrors the power: a metaphor that turns the *wrong* way installs a misconception (the "*orbiting*" electron). A trope is a bid that can misfire.
- The ontology files these under `ms:Trope` (a kind of `ms:Figure`, within `ms:Elocutio`). **Caution**: this classifies *which* figure a turn uses — not whether it landed.

```notes
Turn the tropes on the tutor, and one of them turns out to be the central instrument of teaching itself. The analogy — which is just Aristotle's fourth kind of metaphor, the proportional one — is arguably the most powerful single move a teacher has. "An atom is like a tiny solar system." "The cell membrane is like a security gate." "Resistance is to current as a narrow pipe is to water." Each takes a structure the learner already commands and carries it over to a structure they do not — A is to B as C is to D — and lets the familiar proportion illuminate the strange one. This is metaphor by analogy, the turn Aristotle called the mark of genius, working as pedagogy.

In fact a great deal of explanation simply *is* trope-work: the turning of an abstraction into something concrete enough to be held in the hand of the mind. We cannot point to electricity, so we say it *flows*; we cannot see a force, so we draw it as an *arrow*. The tutor lives by the turn of sense.

But the risk is the exact shadow of the power, and this is the lesson to carry. A metaphor that turns the *wrong* way does not fail harmlessly — it installs a misconception that can outlast the lesson. The "orbiting" electron, modelled on the planet, makes students expect it to spiral inward and radiate; generations have had to unlearn the very analogy that first let them grasp the atom. The trope is a *bid*: it offers a resemblance, and if the resemblance is false in the part that matters, the learner inherits the falsehood along with the insight. So the tutor's tropes are double-edged, and choosing them well is a real part of the art.

The companion ontology files all of this under `ms:Trope`, a kind of `ms:Figure` living within `ms:Elocutio` — with metaphor, metonymy, synecdoche, and irony marked as the masters. And the standing caution holds with special force here: to record that a turn *used a metaphor* is to classify its form. Whether the metaphor illuminated or misled — whether the bid was taken up, and taken up rightly — is exactly what the form cannot tell you. That question belongs to the learner's mind, which we do not read.
```

### Looking ahead

- Tropes turn *meaning*. Next we turn *form*.
- **Next lecture — Schemes.** Figures of arrangement and sound: anaphora, antithesis, chiasmus, asyndeton, isocolon, climax — where the words keep their literal meaning but their *order* does the work.
- Carry forward: the trope/scheme distinction, and the master tropes (metaphor especially) as instruments of explanation.

```notes
We have done the first family. Tropes turn the *meaning* of a word — by resemblance, association, part-whole, or opposition — and they include the single most important device a teacher owns, the analogy. Next lecture we cross to the other family, the *schemes*, where the turn is not of meaning but of *form*: the arrangement, the syntax, the sound of the words, with every word keeping its plain sense.

If tropes are turns of sense, schemes are turns of order — and they are, if anything, more immediately teachable, because they are patterns you can see on the page: a repetition at the head of clauses, a balanced opposition, a mirror-image reversal. They are the figures that give prose its rhythm and its memorability, and they are why some sentences lodge in the mind for a lifetime.

Read for next time: *Rhetorica ad Herennium* Book IV on the figures of diction, and Lanham's *Handlist* entries for anaphora, antithesis, and chiasmus. Carry forward the trope/scheme distinction above all — next lecture depends on it — and keep the analogy in mind as the tutor's master trope.
```
