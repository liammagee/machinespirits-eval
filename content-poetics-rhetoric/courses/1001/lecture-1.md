
## <a id="main-lecture"></a>Lecture 1 — Mimesis and Poiesis

### What this course is about

- The vocabulary of *made* language: how a piece of language is built, and what it does to those who receive it.
- We start with the Greeks and the word *poiesis* — making — and Aristotle's short, decisive book, the *Poetics*.
- Today: two foundational ideas (poiesis, mimesis) and the six parts a tragedy is made of.
- A reflexive note to keep in your pocket: these are also the parts the surrounding tutoring system uses to stage a lesson as a drama. We return to that in Lecture 8.

```notes
Welcome. This first lecture lays two cornerstones and then builds one wall on top of them.

The two cornerstones are a pair of Greek words. The first is *poiesis* — making, or production. It is the root of our word "poetry," but for the Greeks it was wider than verse: it named a whole stance toward the world, the stance of someone bringing a thing into being. The second is *mimesis* — representation, or imitation — which Aristotle takes to be the common root of all the poetic arts.

The wall we build on top of them is Aristotle's analysis, in chapter 6 of the *Poetics*, of the six parts that every tragedy is made of. By the end of the hour you should be able to name all six, rank them the way Aristotle ranks them, and say why he puts plot at the top and spectacle at the bottom.

One orienting remark before we start. This course has a double life. You can take it straight, as an introduction to classical poetics and rhetoric — and that is a complete and worthwhile thing to do. But the vocabulary we are learning is also the working vocabulary of the system that may well be tutoring you through it. The terms we name in lectures are declared, elsewhere, as formal categories that a machine uses to stage a conversation as a small drama with a turn and a recognition in it. I will flag those connections lightly as we go, and pull them together at the end. For now, keep the straight reading in front and the reflexive one in your pocket.
```

### Poiesis: the standpoint of the maker

- *Poiesis* — making, production. Aristotle sets it beside two other ways of being occupied with the world.
- **Praxis** — *doing* / action, whose end is the doing itself (acting well, living well).
- **Theoria** — *contemplation*, the disinterested beholding of what is.
- *Poiesis* is the odd one: its end lies *outside* the activity, in the thing made. And it is governed by **techne** — craft, the knowledge of how to bring something into being that might not have been.

```notes
Aristotle is a great maker of distinctions, and one of his most useful is a three-way split in how a human being can be occupied. He draws it most sharply in the *Nicomachean Ethics*, Book VI, but it stands behind everything in the *Poetics*.

There is *theoria* — contemplation. This is the activity of the scientist or the philosopher beholding what cannot be otherwise: the eternal truths, the way things necessarily are. Its end is simply the seeing.

There is *praxis* — doing, or action, in the rich sense. The end of *praxis* is internal to it: the point of acting courageously is to act courageously, the point of living well is the living well. You do not act in order to manufacture some separate product called "a good life"; the acting *is* the life.

And then there is *poiesis* — making. Here the end is external. The point of building a house is the house; once it stands, the building stops. The maker's knowledge is *techne*, craft — and Aristotle gives *techne* a beautiful definition: it is concerned with bringing into being something "that is capable of either being or not being," something whose origin is in the maker and not in the thing itself. The carpenter is the origin of the table; the table does not make itself.

Why does this matter for poetry? Because it tells us where to look. If poetry is a kind of *poiesis*, then to understand it we examine the thing made — its parts, its construction, the craft of its assembly — and not, in the first instance, the inner states of the poet or the audience. Aristotle will be relentlessly an analyst of the made object. That is the standpoint of the whole book, and it is worth marking, because it is also the standpoint a literary critic takes, and — to put the reflexive note in once — the standpoint a system takes when it judges a tutoring transcript as an artifact rather than guessing at what is going on inside the learner's head.
```

### Mimesis: representation as the genus

- Aristotle opens the *Poetics* by placing all the poetic arts under one genus: they are all forms of **mimesis** — imitation, representation (ch. 1).
- The kinds differ by **medium** (rhythm, language, melody), **object** (people better than us, like us, worse than us), and **manner** (narrated or enacted).
- Mimesis is rooted in our nature (ch. 4): we are the most imitative of animals, and we *take pleasure* in recognising what an imitation represents — "ah, that is so-and-so."
- Hold onto that phrase. The pleasure of recognition in mimesis is the distant seed of *anagnorisis*, the recognition-scene we meet in Lecture 2.

```notes
The very first move of the *Poetics* is to gather a scattered field under a single heading. Epic, tragedy, comedy, dithyramb, even music for flute and lyre — Aristotle says they are "all, in their general conception, modes of *mimesis*." Imitation, or representation. That is the genus; the individual arts are its species.

He then sorts the species by three questions, which are worth memorising because they recur throughout the tradition. *In what medium* does the imitation work — rhythm, language, melody, or some combination? *What objects* does it imitate — and here Aristotle's answer is people in action, represented as better than we are, or much as we are, or worse than we are. (That last axis quietly separates tragedy from comedy: tragedy shows people better than average, comedy worse.) And *in what manner* — does the poet narrate, telling us about the action, or does the poet enact it, putting living agents in front of us? Tragedy is enacted; epic is narrated.

Then, in chapter 4, Aristotle asks where the whole impulse comes from, and gives two causes rooted in human nature. First, imitation is natural to us from childhood — we are, he says, the most imitative of all creatures, and we learn our first lessons by imitating. Second, and this is the line to carry with you: everyone delights in works of imitation. We enjoy looking at a faithful picture even of something that would disgust us in life — a corpse, a repellent animal — and Aristotle explains the pleasure as a pleasure of *learning*: we look, and we infer, and we say "ah, *that* is the thing." The pleasure is in the recognising.

I want to plant a flag on that sentence. The small, almost casual pleasure of recognising what a picture depicts is the same shape, writ small, as the great recognition-scenes that Aristotle will later call the finest device a tragedy can have — the moment Oedipus learns who he is. *Anagnorisis*, recognition, runs from the bottom of the *Poetics* to the top. We meet it properly next lecture; for now, just notice that mimesis and recognition are bound together from the first pages.
```

### The definition of tragedy

- In chapter 6 Aristotle gives the famous compressed definition:

> "Tragedy, then, is an imitation of an action that is serious, complete, and of a certain magnitude; in language embellished with each kind of artistic ornament, the several kinds being found in separate parts of the play; in the form of action, not of narrative; through pity and fear effecting the proper purgation of these emotions."
>
> — *Poetics* 1449b (Butcher trans.)

- Almost every term in that sentence is a thesis. "Of an action" — not of persons. "Complete, and of a certain magnitude" — it has a shape and a size. "In the form of action, not of narrative" — it is enacted. "Through pity and fear effecting the purgation" — *catharsis*, the affective end, the subject of Lecture 3.

```notes
This single sentence is the seed crystal of the entire book; the rest of the *Poetics* unpacks it. Read it slowly and you find that nearly every phrase is a claim you could argue about.

"An imitation of an action" — *mimesis praxeos*. Note the object: an action, not a person. Aristotle is about to insist that tragedy imitates not character but action and life, and we will see why in a moment.

"Serious, complete, and of a certain magnitude." Serious — *spoudaios* — distinguishes tragedy from comedy. Complete — it has a beginning, a middle, and an end, which sounds trivial until you realise Aristotle means it technically: a beginning is what does not follow necessarily from anything else, an end is what necessarily or usually follows from what came before and has nothing after it. And "of a certain magnitude" — a size, large enough for a change of fortune to unfold and small enough to be held in view. A drama, like an animal, must be a size the eye can take in.

"In language embellished with each kind of artistic ornament, the several kinds being found in separate parts" — this is Aristotle telling us that the verbal and musical adornments are distributed across the work: spoken parts, sung parts.

"In the form of action, not of narrative" — *drama*, the enacted form, as against epic, which is told.

And then the ending: "through pity and fear effecting the proper purgation of these emotions." *Catharsis*. This is the most argued-over word in the history of criticism, and we give it a lecture of its own. For today, simply register that Aristotle defines tragedy partly by its *effect on the audience* — by what it accomplishes in the spectator. The end of the made thing lies, in part, outside the made thing, in us. That is a remarkable claim to find inside a definition, and it will matter enormously when we get to rhetoric, whose whole reason for being is its effect on a listener.
```

### The six parts of tragedy

- From the definition Aristotle derives **six parts** (*merē*) that every tragedy must have (ch. 6):

| Greek | Part | What it is |
|-------|------|-----------|
| *mythos* | **Plot** | the arrangement of the incidents |
| *ēthos* | **Character** | the moral qualities of the agents, shown in their choices |
| *dianoia* | **Thought** | the reasoning: proof, refutation, the saying of what is pertinent |
| *lexis* | **Diction** | the expression of meaning in words |
| *melos* | **Song** | the musical element, "the greatest of the embellishments" |
| *opsis* | **Spectacle** | the visual, staged dimension |

- Three concern the *objects* of imitation (plot, character, thought); two the *medium* (diction, song); one the *manner* (spectacle). Nothing is left over.

```notes
Here is the wall we have been building toward. Aristotle says that since tragedy is the imitation of an action, and the action implies agents, and agents have both character and thought — and since the imitation is carried in a medium and presented in a manner — it follows that every tragedy must have exactly six parts. He is fond of this kind of derivation: the parts are not a list he happened to assemble, they are forced by the analysis of what tragedy is.

Let me name them with their Greek, because the Greek words travel with us for the rest of the course and indeed into the system's own categories.

*Mythos* — plot. Not "myth" in our sense, but the *arrangement of the incidents*, the structure of the events. This is the one we will spend the most time on.

*Ēthos* — character. The moral quality of the agents, which Aristotle says is revealed precisely in what they *choose* — in the kind of thing they go for or avoid when the choice is not obvious. Character, for Aristotle, is shown in decision under pressure.

*Dianoia* — thought. The intellectual content: the agents' reasoning, the arguments they make, proving and refuting, arousing emotion by speech, maximising or minimising things. Where character is shown in choice, thought is shown in what is *said* to demonstrate a point.

*Lexis* — diction. The expression of meaning through language; the choice and arrangement of words. When we reach rhetoric, *lexis* is the bridge — the Romans call this department *elocutio*, style, and the figures live there.

*Melos* — song, melody. The sung portions, the music of the chorus.

*Opsis* — spectacle. Everything that meets the eye: the staging, the costumes, the visual effect.

A tidy way to remember the grouping: three of the six concern the *objects* of imitation — plot, character, thought, the things being represented. Two concern the *medium* — diction and song, the materials of representation. And one, spectacle, concerns the *manner* of presentation. The six parts are just the three axes of mimesis from chapter 1 — medium, object, manner — read off a single staged work.
```

### Why plot is the soul

- Aristotle ranks the six, and the ranking is itself an argument. **Plot is first** — "the first principle and, as it were, the soul of a tragedy; character holds the second place."
- His reason: tragedy imitates **action and life**, not quality. We are what we do. "Character gives us qualities, but it is in our actions — what we do — that we are happy or the reverse."
- A tragedy with no character is still possible; a tragedy with no plot, no arrangement of incidents, is not a tragedy at all.

```notes
Now Aristotle does something more than list — he ranks, and the ranking is one of the most consequential judgments in the history of criticism.

Plot, he says, is first: "the first principle, and, as it were, the soul of a tragedy; character holds the second place." That phrase, "the soul of a tragedy," is doing real work. The soul, for Aristotle, is the form that makes a living thing the thing it is. To call plot the soul of tragedy is to say that the *structure of the events* is what makes a tragedy a tragedy — everything else is body.

Why? Because, he argues, tragedy is an imitation not of persons but of action and life, of happiness and misery — and happiness and misery reside in action. "Character gives us qualities, but it is in our actions — what we do — that we are happy or the reverse." We do not act in order to portray character; rather, character comes in as a by-product of the action. Reverse the priority and you misunderstand the art.

He drives it home with a pair of thought-experiments. You could have a tragedy without character — a string of well-arranged incidents in which the agents are barely sketched — and it would still grip us, because the arrangement carries the emotional power, the reversals and recognitions live in the plot. But a series of vivid character-sketches with no action, no structure of events, would not be a tragedy at all. Plot is necessary; character, strictly, is not. Hence the ranking. He even says that the reversal and the recognition — the two most powerful means by which tragedy moves us — are *parts of the plot*. The machinery of the deepest emotional effect is structural. We will take that claim apart next lecture.
```

### The ranking, top to bottom

- The full order Aristotle gives: **plot** > **character** > **thought** > **diction** > **song** > **spectacle**.
- *Diction* and *thought* are the means by which the agents speak and reason; *song* is "the greatest of the embellishments."
- *Spectacle* comes last — "of all the parts, the least artistic, and connected least with the art of poetry." The power of a tragedy, Aristotle insists, should be felt even without performance, even in the reading.

```notes
Let me give you the whole ladder, because the bottom rungs are as revealing as the top.

After plot and character comes *thought* — third — the power of saying what can be said and what is appropriate to the occasion. Fourth is *diction*, the expression in words. Aristotle's reason for ranking these where he does is consistent: the closer a part is to the *structure of the action*, the higher it ranks; the closer to mere surface, the lower.

Then song, "the greatest of the embellishments" — chief among the adornments, but an adornment nonetheless.

And last, spectacle. Here Aristotle is almost dismissive, and it is worth dwelling on because it cuts against modern intuition. *Opsis*, he says, is emotionally powerful but "the least artistic, and connected least with the art of poetry." His argument is striking: the force of a tragedy should be felt even apart from performance and actors — you should feel the dread of the Oedipus story merely on *hearing the plot unfold*, without seeing a thing. Spectacle that does the work the plot should be doing is, for Aristotle, a kind of cheat; it produces an effect that the structure has not earned. He goes further: the spectacular depends more on the property-master than on the poet.

This is a deep and deliberately provocative position. It says the essence of the dramatic art is portable — it survives translation out of the theatre and onto the page, into the bare telling. That portability is exactly what lets a tradition built on staged tragedy be carried over, centuries later, into a system that has no stage at all — only text, only transcript. If the power lives in the arrangement of incidents and not in the spectacle, then a tutoring conversation, which is all *mythos* and no *opsis*, can in principle have dramatic form. Hold that thought.
```

### From parts to effects — and the reflexive turn

- The six parts are the *material* of drama. The *effects* a well-made plot produces — reversal, recognition, the earned clarification — are what the rest of the course tracks.
- Two of those effects, Aristotle says, are **parts of the plot** itself: *peripeteia* (reversal) and *anagnorisis* (recognition). That is Lecture 2.
- Reflexive note: this same six-part scheme is declared, formally, in the system's ontology — `ms:Mythos`, `ms:Ethos`, `ms:Dianoia`, `ms:Lexis`, `ms:Melos`, `ms:Opsis`, under `ms:TragicPart`, ranked exactly as Aristotle ranks them. The tutor's job, read dramatically, is to build a *mythos* — an arrangement of conversational incidents — in which a recognition can land.

```notes
Let me close by separating two things that are easy to run together, and then making the reflexive connection explicit for the first time.

We have spent today on the six *parts* — the material a tragedy is made of. The rest of the course is largely about *effects* — what a well-built plot does. The single most important thing Aristotle says on the seam between them is this: the two devices by which tragedy produces its strongest effects, reversal and recognition, are not separate from plot — they are *parts of the plot*. *Peripeteia*, the swing of the action to its opposite, and *anagnorisis*, the change from ignorance to knowledge — both are structural. That is why he could call plot the soul. Next lecture is devoted to them.

Now the reflexive turn, stated plainly. The system around this course represents a tutoring session as a small staged drama, and it does so using exactly the vocabulary we have begun to learn. The six parts we named today are written into its ontology as formal categories — there is a class for plot, a class for character, for thought, for diction, for song, for spectacle, all grouped under a single parent, "tragic part," and carrying Aristotle's ranking as a number you can read off. When the system asks whether a tutoring exchange had dramatic form, it is asking a version of Aristotle's question: was there a *mythos* here — an arrangement of incidents — and did a recognition occur within it?

Read this way, the tutor is a kind of dramatist working in a medium of pure dialogue: all plot and thought and diction, no spectacle at all — which is precisely the stripped-down case Aristotle says still carries the full force of the art. The learner's misunderstanding is the flaw the plot works through; the moment the learner sees it differently is the recognition the whole arrangement was for. We are, in other words, learning the grammar of a machine that tries to make recognition happen in a conversation. Lecture 8 returns to read the tutor's individual moves as figures. Until then, keep both readings live.
```
