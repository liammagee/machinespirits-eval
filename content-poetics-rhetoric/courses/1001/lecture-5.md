
## <a id="main-lecture"></a>Lecture 5 — The Five Canons

---

### Where we are

- We have the Greek foundation: *poiesis* and the drama, and Aristotle's three appeals.
- Now the course turns **Roman**. Where the Greeks analysed, the Romans *systematised* — they built rhetoric into a complete pedagogy.
- Today's frame is the master scheme of that pedagogy: the **five canons** — the five tasks an orator performs, in order, to get from a blank mind to a delivered speech.

```notes
We cross into Rome now, and the character of the material changes. Aristotle gave us a brilliant analysis — the appeals, the genres — but the *Rhetoric* is a philosopher's book, exploratory and uneven. The Romans wanted something else: a curriculum. They were training young men to speak in the law courts and the senate, and they needed a system you could teach, drill, and examine. So they took the art apart into stages and built the most durable educational scheme in Western history.

That scheme is the five canons — in Latin the *officia oratoris*, the offices or duties of the orator. They are five things you do, roughly in sequence, to produce a speech: *inventio*, finding your material; *dispositio*, arranging it; *elocutio*, putting it into words; *memoria*, committing it to memory; and *pronuntiatio*, delivering it. You find the texts in Cicero's youthful *De Inventione*, in the anonymous *Rhetorica ad Herennium*, and in Quintilian's vast *Institutio Oratoria*, the great teacher's summa.

Today is the frame; the next three lectures fill in its third compartment. Because *elocutio* — style — is where the figures live, and the figures are the heart of this course. So treat the five canons as a map, and watch for the box marked *elocutio*: that is the door we walk through next lecture.
```

### The five canons

- The orator's five tasks (*Rhet. ad Herennium* I.2; Cicero, *De Inventione* I.7):
  1. **Inventio** — *invention*: finding the available arguments and material;
  2. **Dispositio** — *arrangement*: ordering the material into a structure;
  3. **Elocutio** — *style*: putting the material into words (← where the **figures** live);
  4. **Memoria** — *memory*: fixing the speech in the mind;
  5. **Pronuntiatio** (*actio*) — *delivery*: voice and gesture before the audience.
- The order is a workflow: you cannot arrange what you have not found, or deliver what you have not remembered.

```notes
Here are the five, and the first thing to see is that they are a *process*, not a classification. They describe the orator's working day, from first thought to final word. You begin by *finding* what you have to say — inventio. You then *arrange* it — dispositio — deciding what comes first, what last, what is proved and what refuted. You then *word* it — elocutio — choosing the diction, the rhythm, the figures. You then *memorise* it — memoria. And finally you *deliver* it — pronuntiatio, also called actio — standing up and using your voice and body on the audience.

The sequence is not arbitrary; each stage depends on the last. There is nothing to arrange until invention has found it; nothing to style until arrangement has ordered it; nothing to memorise until style has fixed the words; nothing to deliver until memory holds it. This is why "canon" is a slightly misleading English word — these are not rules but *stages*, the Latin *partes* or *officia*, the jointed phases of a single craft.

And notice the third stage especially, because it is our destination. *Elocutio*, style, is the Roman name for what Aristotle called *lexis* — the fourth of the six parts of tragedy, the verbal texture of the made thing. The two traditions meet exactly here. Everything we will study in Lectures 6, 7, and 8 — every trope, every scheme, every figure of thought — is a subdivision of this one canon. Let us walk through all five, then come back and open that door.
```

### Inventio — finding the matter

- **Inventio** (ch. I.7, *De Inventione*): "the discovery of valid or seemingly valid arguments to render one's cause plausible."
- Its tools:
  - the **topics** (*loci*, Greek *topoi*) — the "places" where arguments are found: definition, comparison, cause and effect, opposites, authority…
  - **status** (*stasis*) theory — locating the precise point at issue: *did it happen?* (fact); *what is it?* (definition); *how serious?* (quality); *is this the right venue?* (jurisdiction).
- Inventio is where the three appeals are *sourced* — it finds the logos, and the matter of ethos and pathos too.

```notes
Inventio is the finding of material — Cicero defines it as "the discovery of valid or seemingly valid arguments to render one's cause plausible." Note that "seemingly valid" again: like Aristotle, the Romans are clear-eyed that rhetoric deals in the persuasive, which overlaps with but is not identical to the true.

It has two great instruments. The first is the doctrine of *topics* — in Latin *loci*, in Greek *topoi*, literally "places." The metaphor is spatial: arguments are imagined as residing in standard locations, and the topics are the addresses you visit to find them. Is there an argument from *definition*? from *comparison*? from *cause and effect*? from *contraries*, from *authority*, from *consequences*? The topics are a checklist for systematically searching the space of possible arguments — a heuristic, in the old sense, an art of discovery.

The second instrument is *status* theory, also called *stasis* — the doctrine of the issue. Before you can argue a case you must find the *point on which it turns*. Did the act happen at all? That is an issue of fact, of conjecture. It happened, but what is it — was it murder or self-defence? That is an issue of definition. It was murder, but how culpable, given the circumstances? An issue of quality. Or perhaps the whole matter is being heard in the wrong court — an issue of jurisdiction. Locating the *status* tells you what kind of fight you are actually in, and therefore what arguments are worth finding. It is, if you like, the orator's diagnosis.
```

### Dispositio — arranging the matter

- **Dispositio** — *arrangement*: ordering the found material so it lands. Cicero: "the distribution of arguments in the proper order."
- This canon governs the **parts of an oration** — the standard structure into which a speech is poured (next slide).
- Arrangement is strategic, not mechanical: you may *withhold* your strongest point, *front-load* goodwill, *bury* a weakness. Order is itself persuasion.

```notes
Dispositio is arrangement — "the distribution of arguments in the proper order," in Cicero's phrase. If invention fills the storehouse, arrangement decides the sequence in which the goods are brought out, and that sequence is itself a rhetorical weapon. A famous strategic question of dispositio: where do you put your strongest argument? At the front, to establish dominance? At the end, to leave the final impression? Buried in the middle, between two lesser ones, so a weak point does not stand exposed? The Romans called the strongest-first-and-last arrangement the *Nestorian* order, after the way Nestor drew up his troops in the *Iliad* — veterans at the front and rear, the doubtful in the middle.

But the main content of dispositio is the doctrine of the *parts of an oration* — the standard skeleton of a speech, which every Roman schoolboy learned and which still, two thousand years later, underlies how we are taught to structure an argument. That skeleton is the next slide, and one of its parts will jump out at you as a tutoring move in disguise.
```

### The parts of an oration

- The classic structure (*De Inventione* I.19; *Ad Herennium* I.3):
  - **Exordium** — *introduction*: win the audience's goodwill, attention, and *teachability* (*benevolum, attentum, docilem*);
  - **Narratio** — *statement of facts*: the story of the case;
  - **Divisio** / *partitio* — *division*: what is agreed, what is in dispute, and a forecast of the argument;
  - **Confirmatio** — *proof*: the positive arguments;
  - **Confutatio** / *refutatio* — *refutation*: the demolition of the opponent's case;
  - **Peroratio** — *conclusion*: recapitulation, and a final appeal to the emotions.
- Note the exordium's aim of making the hearer *docilis* — teachable, ready to receive. That is a pedagogical goal at the head of every speech.

```notes
Here is the skeleton. The *exordium* is the opening, and its task is precisely defined: to render the audience *benevolum, attentum, docilem* — well-disposed toward you, attentive to what you say, and *docilis*, teachable, ready to receive instruction. I want to dwell on that third word, because it is startling to find it here. The very first task of any speech, the Romans say, is to put the listener into a state of *teachability*. Every tutor will recognise the goal; we will come back to it.

The *narratio* is the statement of facts — the narrative of what happened, told with an eye to your case. The *divisio* or *partitio* divides the ground: it states what both sides agree on and what is genuinely in dispute, and forecasts the order of what is to come (the ancient ancestor of "in this paper I will argue three things"). The *confirmatio* is the heart: your positive proof, your arguments marshalled. The *confutatio* or *refutatio* is the demolition of the other side. And the *peroratio* is the conclusion, which does two jobs: it *recapitulates* — gathers the argument into a memorable summary — and it makes a final, heightened appeal to the emotions, the place where pathos is licensed to run highest.

This structure is astonishingly durable. The five-paragraph essay, the legal brief, the structure of a scientific paper, the shape of a good lecture — all are descendants of these parts. And the exordium's *docilem* will return at the end of the course as a clue to what a tutor's opening move is really doing.
```

### Elocutio — putting it into words (the door to the figures)

- **Elocutio** — *style*: the verbal realisation of the matter. This is Aristotle's *lexis*, Romanised — the two traditions meet here.
- It carries three sub-doctrines:
  - the **three styles** (*genera dicendi*): the **plain** (to teach/prove), the **middle** (to please), the **grand** (to move);
  - the **virtues of style** (Theophrastus's four): *correctness*, *clarity*, *ornament* (*ornatus*), and *propriety*;
  - under *ornatus* live the **figures** — the tropes, the schemes, and the figures of thought.
- The whole back half of the course is one box in this diagram.

```notes
Now we open the door. *Elocutio* is style — the canon that turns arranged matter into actual words. And here the two halves of the course click together, because *elocutio* is simply the Latin for what Aristotle called *lexis*, the fourth of the six parts of tragedy, the verbal texture. The Greek dramatist and the Roman orator are working the same material from two directions; this is the canon where poetics and rhetoric are revealed to be one study. (The companion ontology marks the identity directly: the rhetoric module's `ms:Elocutio` carries a `seeAlso` link to the poetics module's `ms:Lexis`.)

Elocutio has three sub-doctrines. First, the *three styles*, the *genera dicendi*: the **plain** style, low and clear, whose job is to *teach* and *prove*; the **middle** style, smooth and pleasant, whose job is to *please*; and the **grand** style, weighty and ornate, whose job is to *move*. Notice that the three styles map onto three of our verbs from the appeals — to teach (logos), to please, to move (pathos). A master orator commands all three and modulates between them.

Second, the *virtues of style* — the four qualities good style must have, a doctrine going back to Aristotle's pupil Theophrastus: *correctness* (good grammar, "good Latin"), *clarity* (being understood), *ornament* (*ornatus*, distinction and beauty), and *propriety* (fitting the words to the matter and the audience).

And it is under the third virtue, *ornatus* — ornament — that the figures live. Every metaphor, every antithesis, every rhetorical question we will study is, in the Roman scheme, an instance of *ornatus*, a resource of stylistic distinction, filed under the third of the five canons. That single box — elocutio's ornatus — is what Lectures 6, 7, and 8 unpack. The companion ontology mirrors this exactly: the figure classes we add in the next three lectures all hang off `ms:Elocutio` by a `withinCanon` link.
```

### Memoria — fixing it in the mind

- **Memoria** — *memory*: holding the speech for delivery without notes.
- The **art of memory**: the *method of loci* — place vivid images (*imagines*) along a remembered architectural path (*loci*), then "walk" it to recall the speech in order.
- The *Rhetorica ad Herennium* (Book III) is the oldest surviving description; the technique runs through the Middle Ages and Renaissance (Frances Yates, *The Art of Memory*).

```notes
The fourth canon, *memoria*, has fallen out of modern rhetoric — we have notes, slides, teleprompters — but to the Romans it was indispensable and, characteristically, they turned it into a technique. The *art of memory* is one of the strangest and most beautiful inventions of antiquity, and the oldest surviving account of it is in the *Rhetorica ad Herennium*, Book III.

The method is this. You fix in your mind a familiar architecture — a house, a street, a temple — as a sequence of distinct *places*, *loci*. Then you convert the points of your speech into vivid, striking *images*, *imagines* — the more grotesque, active, and emotionally charged the better, because the bland is forgettable and the bizarre sticks. You then *place* each image in one of your loci, in order. To deliver the speech, you simply walk through the building in your mind, and at each place the image is waiting to remind you of the next point. The "memory palace." We still say "in the first place," "in the second place" — fossils of this art in everyday speech.

Frances Yates's great book *The Art of Memory* traces how this technique, born as a rhetorical canon, became over the centuries a whole tradition of thought, entangled with magic, mysticism, and the architecture of knowledge itself. For us it is enough to see that even *memory* the Romans refused to leave to nature; they made it an art with a method.
```

### Pronuntiatio — delivering it

- **Pronuntiatio**, also *actio* — *delivery*: the management of **voice** (*vox*) and **gesture** (*gestus*) before the audience.
- Asked the most important part of oratory, **Demosthenes** is said to have answered "delivery"; the second? "delivery"; the third? "delivery."
- Delivery is where the speech becomes an *act* — the same words, badly delivered, persuade no one.

```notes
The fifth and last canon is *pronuntiatio*, delivery, also called *actio* — action, performance. It governs the orator's *voice* and *body*: pitch, pace, volume, pause; stance, gesture, facial expression. It is the canon that turns a memorised text into a living act in a room.

The Romans rated it astonishingly highly. Cicero reports the famous anecdote: Demosthenes, the greatest of the Greek orators, was asked what was the first part of oratory, and answered "delivery" — *hypokrisis*. The second? "Delivery." The third? "Delivery." The word *hypokrisis*, by the way, is also the word for an actor's playing of a part — the root of our "hypocrisy" — which tells you how close delivery brought the orator to the actor, and why some philosophers were nervous about the whole business.

The point beneath the anecdote is serious: the *same words* succeed or fail on delivery. A devastating argument muttered to the floor persuades no one; a thin argument delivered with conviction and timing can carry a room. Delivery is where elocutio's words become pronuntiatio's act — and it closes the arc from the silent finding of arguments to their public, embodied performance.
```

### The reflexive turn — a tutoring turn runs the canons

- A tutor's turn is itself a tiny passage through the five canons:
  - **inventio** — choosing *which* explanation, example, or question to deploy;
  - **dispositio** — ordering it (and the *exordium*'s aim — making the learner *docilis*, teachable — is the tutor's perennial first task);
  - **elocutio** — *how* it is phrased: the figures the tutor reaches for (next three lectures);
  - **memoria** & **pronuntiatio** — holding the thread of the dialogue, and the "delivery" of tone even in text.
- The ontology grows a second module here: `config/ontology/rhetoric-core.ttl`, opened to mirror the course's crossing into Rome. Its `ms:Elocutio` links by `seeAlso` to the poetics `ms:Lexis`, and the figures to come will hang off it by `withinCanon`.

```notes
Turn the five canons on the tutor, and they describe a single move with surprising precision. A good tutoring turn begins with *inventio*: of all the things that could be said, which argument, which example, which question will move *this* learner from where they are? It proceeds through *dispositio*: in what order — and note that the exordium's ancient task, making the listener *docilis*, teachable, is the tutor's standing first job. Before you can teach anything you must bring the learner into a state of readiness to receive it; the Romans put that requirement at the head of every speech, and it belongs at the head of every lesson.

Then *elocutio* — the actual phrasing, which is where the figures we are about to study come into play: the well-chosen metaphor that makes an abstraction graspable, the antithesis that sharpens a contrast, the rhetorical question that hands the learner the next step. And even in a text-based tutor there is something of *memoria* — holding the thread of the whole dialogue, not just the last turn — and of *pronuntiatio* — the "delivery" carried by tone, emphasis, and pacing even on the page.

This lecture is also where the companion ontology grows its second module. And it is stitched to the Greek file at the seam we found today: `ms:Elocutio` carries a `seeAlso` to `ms:Lexis`, recording in the ontology the very identity — style is the meeting-point of poetics and rhetoric — that this lecture turned on.
```
