
## <a id="main-lecture"></a>Lecture 3 — Hamartia, Catharsis, Wonder

---

### Where we are

- Last lecture: how a plot is *built* — whole action, necessity, the three parts (reversal, recognition, suffering), complication and unravelling.
- We know the *grammar* of plot. We do not yet know what it is *for*.
- Today: the flaw that drives the change of fortune (**hamartia**); the emotions a tragedy works on (**pity** and **fear**); the end it works them toward (**catharsis**); and the affect of the surprising-yet-inevitable (**wonder**).

```notes
We have spent two lectures on structure. We can now say, with Aristotle, what makes an action whole, what binds its parts, and how a complex plot turns on a reversal and a recognition. But structure is a means, not an end. A perfectly built plot is built *for* something — it is meant to do something to the people who watch it.

Today we ask what that something is. Three of Aristotle's most famous and most argued-over terms are the answer. *Hamartia* — the error or flaw that sets the tragic change in motion. *Catharsis* — the affective end named in the very definition of tragedy, and never explained in the surviving text. And *to thaumaston* — wonder, the marvellous, the proper pleasure of a plot whose events surprise us and yet, on reflection, had to be.

These are not tidy concepts. Two of them — hamartia and catharsis — are among the most contested words in the history of criticism, and I will not pretend to settle debates that have run for four centuries. What I will do is show you the textual ground each interpretation stands on, so that you know what is Aristotle and what is later building. At the end, as always, we turn the apparatus on ourselves and ask what the affective end of a *lesson* might be.
```

### The best kind of protagonist

- Aristotle asks who the change of fortune should happen *to* (ch. 13). He reasons by elimination:
  - **not** a wholly good man falling into misery — that is merely *shocking* (*miaron*), and arouses neither pity nor fear;
  - **not** a wicked man rising to fortune — untragic in every way;
  - **not** an utter villain falling — just, perhaps, but it moves neither pity nor fear.
- What is left: a man **like us**, "highly renowned and prosperous," who falls "not by vice or depravity, but by some error" — *hamartia*.

```notes
Chapter 13 is a chain of eliminations, and the conclusion is the famous "tragic middle." Aristotle is asking a structural question: given that the best plots are complex and end in a change of fortune, *whose* fall will produce the tragic emotions?

He rules out three figures. A perfectly virtuous man passing from happiness to misery — this does not arouse pity or fear, it merely shocks us; Aristotle uses the word *miaron*, "repugnant," "polluted." A bad man passing from misery to happiness — the least tragic of all, satisfying none of our moral or emotional demands. And a thoroughly wicked man falling from happiness to misery — this may satisfy our sense of justice, but justice is not pity, and a villain getting his deserts frightens no one who is not a villain.

What remains is the figure "who is not eminently good and just, yet whose misfortune is brought about not by vice or depravity, but by some error or frailty." He is "highly renowned and prosperous — a personage like Oedipus, Thyestes, or other illustrious men of such families." Notice the two requirements working together. He must be *elevated* — renowned, prosperous — so that the fall is a real fall, from a height. And he must be *like us* — flawed, fallible, not a paragon — so that we can fear for ourselves in watching him. The whole emotional machinery of tragedy is calibrated by this single choice of protagonist.
```

---

### Hamartia — the contested word

- *Hamartia* (ch. 13) is the hinge of the tragic plot, and its meaning is disputed across a spectrum:
  - a **mistake of fact** — acting in ignorance of some crucial circumstance (Oedipus not knowing whom he kills and marries);
  - an **error of judgment** — a misjudgement that is intellectual, not moral;
  - a **moral flaw** — the later "tragic flaw" reading (hubris, etc.), popular but narrower than Aristotle's word.
- The Greek root is *hamartanein*, "to miss the mark" (an archery metaphor). Aristotle's term is **broad**: it need not be a sin.

```notes
*Hamartia* comes from *hamartanein*, to miss the mark — an archer's word. That image matters, because it makes the basic sense an *error* rather than a *vice*: the arrow that goes wide is not wicked, it is mistaken.

The interpretations fan out from there. At one end, *hamartia* is a simple mistake of fact — you act on the world as you believe it to be, and you are wrong about a crucial particular. Oedipus is the paradigm: everything he does to escape the oracle is reasonable given what he knows, and catastrophic given what he does not. He misses the mark not because he is bad but because he is ignorant of who he is.

In the middle, it is an error of judgment — still primarily intellectual, a misreading of a situation, but with the agent more implicated.

At the far end is the reading you have probably met in school: the "tragic flaw," a moral defect of character — pride, ambition, jealousy — that brings the hero down. This reading has the weight of tradition behind it, and it fits some plays. But it is narrower than Aristotle's word, and it imports a moralism the Greek does not require. Aristotle is careful to say the misfortune comes "not by vice or depravity." If hamartia were simply moral vice, that sentence would contradict itself.

The fair reading is that Aristotle's term spans the range, and different tragedies sit at different points on it. What the term *does*, structurally, is locate the cause of the change of fortune in the protagonist himself, neither in sheer external accident nor in deliberate wickedness. That is what makes the fall *tragic* rather than merely sad or merely deserved.
```

### Pity and fear

- The two tragic emotions are precisely paired (ch. 13):
  - **pity** (*eleos*) is aroused by *undeserved* misfortune;
  - **fear** (*phobos*) is aroused by the misfortune of one *like ourselves*.
- The in-between protagonist is engineered to produce *both at once*: undeserved enough to pity, near enough to us to fear for ourselves.

```notes
"Pity is aroused by unmerited misfortune, fear by the misfortune of a man like ourselves." That single sentence explains the whole design of the previous slide.

Pity requires that the suffering be, in some measure, *undeserved* — which is why the villain's fall does not move us to pity. Fear requires that the sufferer be *like us* — which is why the perfect saint's fall does not frighten us; we cannot see ourselves in him. The tragic middle — the good-but-flawed, elevated-but-fallible figure — is the unique solution that satisfies both conditions simultaneously. His misfortune is disproportionate to his error, so we pity him; and he is enough like us that, watching, we feel the cold breath of *there but for fortune*.

This is worth dwelling on because it shows how tightly Aristotle's concepts interlock. The choice of protagonist (ch. 13), the two emotions (ch. 13), and the end those emotions serve (catharsis, ch. 6) are not separate doctrines. They are one mechanism described from three angles. And the emotions are not incidental side-effects — they are the *material* that the tragic plot operates on, the raw feeling that catharsis will do something to.
```

### Catharsis — the word at the end of the definition

- The definition of tragedy (ch. 6) ends on a term Aristotle never explains in the surviving text: tragedy effects, "through pity and fear, the **catharsis** of such emotions."
- Three great readings:
  - **Purgation** (medical) — a homeopathic discharge: the emotions are roused so as to be relieved (Jacob Bernays, 1857).
  - **Purification** (moral / religious) — the emotions are refined, cleansed of excess, brought to a right measure.
  - **Clarification** (intellectual) — *katharsis* as a cognitive clearing-up: we come to *understand* pity and fear, and the events that warrant them (Else; Golden; Nussbaum).

```notes
Here is the great crux. The definition of tragedy in chapter 6 is one long sentence, and its final clause says that tragedy, "through pity and fear," effects the *catharsis* "of such emotions." And then — in the text we have — Aristotle never tells us what catharsis means. The *Poetics* is unfinished; the promised second book, on comedy, is lost; and the discussion that might have defined catharsis is simply not there. So the most famous word in the book is also its largest hole, and the history of the *Poetics* is in large part a history of filling it.

Three readings have the most weight. The first is *purgation*, given its decisive modern form by Jacob Bernays in 1857. *Katharsis* is a medical word — the discharge of something from the body — and on this reading tragedy works homeopathically: it rouses pity and fear precisely in order to discharge them, leaving the spectator relieved, lightened, drained of excess feeling. The pleasure of tragedy is the pleasure of release.

The second is *purification*: not a discharge but a refinement. The emotions are not expelled but *cleansed* — brought from a crude or excessive state to a measured, appropriate one. On this reading tragedy is a moral education of the feelings: it teaches us to feel pity and fear at the right things, in the right amount.

The third, which has gained ground in the last century, is *clarification* — an intellectual reading. *Katharsis* is a clearing-up, a cognitive purification: through the structured arousal of pity and fear, and the recognition the plot turns on, we come to *understand* something — about the events, about the kind of person to whom such things happen, about the relation of action and suffering. Gerald Else, Leon Golden, and Martha Nussbaum are associated with versions of this view. Its great attraction is that it ties catharsis to the rest of the *Poetics* — to recognition, to the universals that poetry expresses — rather than leaving it as a free-floating medical metaphor.

I am not going to adjudicate. What I want you to carry away is that all three are *readings of a silence*, each anchored to a different sense of a single Greek word, and that your choice among them is really a choice about what you think tragedy is *for*: relief, moral schooling, or understanding.
```

### Pity and fear from structure, not spectacle

- Aristotle insists the tragic emotions arise from the **plot itself**, not from stage effects (ch. 14): the *opsis*-driven shudder is the inferior way.
- The richest case: a terrible deed *about to be done in ignorance*, the recognition arriving in time to prevent it — or, devastatingly, too late.
- Recognition is therefore not decoration. It is the device by which the plot delivers pity and fear *and* understanding at the same stroke.

```notes
Chapter 14 makes a claim that follows from everything we have said and that we will need at the end of the lecture. The tragic pleasure — pity and fear — should be produced "from the inner structure of the piece," from the *arrangement of the incidents*, and not from spectacle. A playwright who gets the shudder out of monstrous costumes and stage blood is, Aristotle says, doing the lesser thing. The true tragedian gets it out of the *plot* — so that merely hearing the story told, without seeing it staged, would make you "thrill with horror and melt to pity." (Recall the ranking from Lecture 1: *opsis*, spectacle, came last among the six parts. This is why.)

He then sorts the ways a terrible deed can be related to knowledge. The deed can be done knowingly; or done in ignorance and the bond discovered afterwards; or — the case he most admires — *about to be done in ignorance*, with the recognition arriving just in time to prevent it. The structure that produces the deepest emotion is the one where knowledge and action are racing each other: where what the character *learns* bears directly on what they are about to *do*.

This is the deep reason recognition matters so much to Aristotle, and the reason it matters to us. *Anagnorisis* is not a clever twist bolted onto the plot for surprise. It is the joint at which the plot delivers its two payloads together — the emotional one (pity and fear) and the cognitive one (a passage from ignorance to knowledge). Hold that thought; it is the bridge to the reflexive turn.
```

### Wonder — *to thaumaston*

- Tragedy aims at **wonder** (*to thaumaston*) — the marvellous, the astonishing (chs. 9, 24).
- "The wonderful is pleasing." Wonder is the affective signature of a plot working *well*.
- Epic admits *more* of the wonderful than tragedy, because it can include the improbable off-stage (ch. 24) — but tragedy needs its measure of wonder too.

```notes
The third term is the most pleasant and the least disputed: *to thaumaston*, the wonderful, the marvellous. Aristotle says plainly, "the wonderful is pleasing," and offers a charming proof: everyone, in telling a story, adds something of their own, because they know their listeners enjoy the marvel. Wonder is woven into why we tell tales at all.

In chapter 24, comparing tragedy and epic, he observes that epic affords *more* scope for the wonderful, because the improbable — the chief source of the marvellous — can be placed off-stage, out of sight, where we do not scrutinise it. (Achilles chasing Hector while the whole army stands still would look absurd on a stage; in narrated epic it passes, and thrills.) Tragedy is more constrained, because we are watching; but it still aims at wonder, and gets it by other means.

What are those means? Chiefly the complex plot — the reversal and the recognition. The swerve we did not see coming, the discovery that turns everything, the truth that lands like a blow: these are tragedy's licensed sources of wonder. Which brings us to the precise mechanism, and the loveliest example in the book.
```

### Surprise *and* inevitability

- The deepest wonder comes from events that are *both* (ch. 9):
  - **contrary to expectation** — they surprise us; and
  - **on account of one another** — they follow as cause and effect.
- Aristotle's example: the statue of **Mitys** at Argos, which fell on the very man who had murdered Mitys, killing him as he watched a festival. "Such events seem not to be due to mere chance."
- This is the form the system names **surprising-yet-inevitable** — and it is the source of the tragic wonder.

```notes
Here is the single most important sentence about wonder, and one of the most important in the whole book. Tragic effect, Aristotle says, "is best produced when the events come on us by surprise; and the effect is heightened when, at the same time, they follow as cause and effect. The tragic wonder will then be greater than if they happened of themselves or by accident; for even coincidences are most striking when they have an air of design."

Read that carefully, because it is a paradox stated as a recipe. Pure surprise is cheap — anything can surprise you. Pure inevitability is dull — if you saw it coming, where is the wonder? The summit is the *conjunction*: events that surprise us *and yet*, the moment they arrive, reveal themselves as having been necessary all along. Surprising in prospect, inevitable in retrospect. You did not see it coming; and now that it has come, you see it could not have been otherwise.

His example is unforgettable. "The statue of Mitys at Argos fell upon his murderer while he was a spectator at a festival, and killed him. Such events seem not to be due to mere chance." A coincidence — but one with such an *air of design* that it reads as justice, as meaning, as the world rhyming. That "air of design" is the whole secret. The artist's job is to make the necessary feel surprising and the surprising feel necessary.

This is one of the six dimensions the companion system actually scores a drama on, under the name *surprising-yet-inevitable*. And — this is the point — it is the affective core of a good *recognition*. When the learner's insight lands with that double character, surprising and then obviously-true, the conversation has produced the tragic wonder. That is the bridge to today's reflexive turn.
```

### The reflexive turn — the affective end of a lesson

- Map the three terms onto a tutoring exchange — *as drama*, which is all the instrument reads:
  - the learner's **misconception** is the *hamartia* — not a moral failing, a sincere missing-of-the-mark that drives the whole exchange (the system's `ms:HamartiaIntegration`: the misconception as mainspring);
  - the resolution that *lands*, that clears something up, is the analogue of **catharsis** on the clarification reading (`ms:Catharsis` — "earned clarification that lands");
  - the good insight that is surprising-then-obvious carries **wonder** (`ms:SurpriseInevitability` → `ms:Thaumaston`).
- **Caution.** This reads *dramatic form*, not minds. A transcript can have the shape of catharsis without anyone having actually learned. The instrument classifies the drama; it does not certify the cognition.

```notes
Now the transfer — and a warning attached to it.

Take the three terms in turn. The learner's misconception is the natural analogue of *hamartia*. This is exactly why the archery metaphor matters: a misconception is not a vice, it is a missing of the mark — a reasonable belief, sincerely held, that happens to be wrong, and that drives everything the learner then does. The system already names the dramatic form in which that misconception becomes the engine of the exchange: `ms:HamartiaIntegration`, "the misconception as the mainspring of the plot." A good lesson, like a good tragedy, puts the flaw at the centre and lets the action flow from it.

The resolution maps to catharsis — and notice that of the three readings, the *clarification* reading is the one that transfers. A lesson is not aiming to purge the learner of emotion, nor mainly to refine their feelings; it is aiming to *clear something up*. The system's gloss on `ms:Catharsis` is precisely "earned clarification that lands for the audience." That is the clarification reading of catharsis, transposed to a classroom.

And the quality of a really good insight is wonder — the surprising-yet-inevitable. "Oh — of course." Surprising the instant before, obvious the instant after. That is `ms:SurpriseInevitability` producing `ms:Thaumaston`, and it is the felt signature of a recognition that has truly landed.

Now the warning, and it is the most important sentence in this lecture. *All of this is dramatic form.* The instrument reads the shape of the transcript — does it have a flaw at the centre, a clarification that lands, a surprise that resolves into necessity? A transcript can have every one of these features and still involve no actual learning at all: the learner can perform the recognition, say "oh I see," produce the cathartic line, and have understood nothing. The drama can be staged. So when we say a lesson "achieved catharsis," we mean it had the *form* of catharsis — not that a mind was changed. Keeping that distinction sharp is a condition of using this vocabulary without fooling ourselves. We are literary critics of the transcript, not readers of the learner's mind.
```

### Looking ahead

- We have finished the Greek arc: making (*poiesis*), the parts, the architecture of plot, and the affective end.
- **Next lecture — From Poetics to Rhetoric.** We cross from the made *drama* to the persuasive *speech*, by way of Aristotle's own bridge: the three appeals — **ethos**, **pathos**, **logos**.
- A naming note for next time: rhetorical *pathos* (the appeal to the audience's emotion) is **not** the tragic *pathos* (the scene of suffering) we met in Lecture 2. Same word, different jobs — and the ontology keeps them apart.

```notes
That completes the Greek arc of the course. We began with *poiesis*, making, and *mimesis*, representation; we named and ranked the six parts of tragedy; we opened the plot and traced its architecture; and today we asked what the whole apparatus is *for* — the flaw that drives it, the emotions it works on, the clarification and wonder it aims at.

Next lecture we cross a bridge that Aristotle himself built, because the same man who wrote the *Poetics* wrote the *Rhetoric*. We move from the made thing — the drama — to the persuasive act — the speech — and we make the crossing on his three famous appeals: *ethos*, the speaker's character; *pathos*, the audience's emotion; *logos*, the argument itself. From there the course turns Roman, and the figure becomes our unit of study.

One thing to fix in memory before then, because it is a trap. The *pathos* of rhetoric — persuasion through the audience's feelings — is not the *pathos* of tragedy, the scene of suffering, that we met as the third part of the plot. Greek reused the word; we must not. When we build the rhetorical appeals into the ontology next time, they get their own names — `ms:PathosAppeal`, not `ms:Pathos` — precisely so the homonym never collapses. Read, for next time, Book I of the *Rhetoric* on the three appeals.
```
