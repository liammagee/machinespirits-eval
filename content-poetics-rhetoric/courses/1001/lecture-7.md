
## <a id="main-lecture"></a>Lecture 7 — Schemes

---

### Where we are

- Last lecture: **tropes** — turns of *meaning*, a word swerved from its literal sense (metaphor, metonymy, synecdoche, irony).
- Today: **schemes** — turns of *form*, the *arrangement* and *sound* of words, with every word keeping its plain meaning.
- A scheme (Greek *schēma*, "shape") is a pattern you can almost *see* on the page: a repetition, a balance, a mirror, an omission.

```notes
Hold the distinction from last lecture firmly, because today is its other half. A trope changes what a word *means* — "she is a lion" turns the sense of "lion." A scheme leaves every word meaning exactly what it ordinarily means, and instead arranges the words into a *pattern* — by repeating them, balancing them, mirroring them, omitting the expected connective, or playing on their sound. The Greek word is *schēma*, shape or form; a scheme is a *figure* in the most literal sense, a shape made of words.

The practical difference is that schemes are more *visible* than tropes. A metaphor you have to interpret; a scheme you can often spot by its silhouette — the same word marching down the front of three clauses, two halves balanced against each other, a phrase that reverses itself in the middle. This visibility is exactly why schemes are the great engines of *memorability*. The sentences you know by heart — "I came, I saw, I conquered"; "ask not what your country can do for you"; "of the people, by the people, for the people" — are almost all carried by schemes. The pattern is what makes them stick, and that, we will see at the end, ties the schemes directly to the fourth canon, *memoria*.

We will take them in four groups: schemes of *repetition*, schemes of *balance*, schemes of *omission and excess*, and schemes of *sound and climax*. The companion ontology uses the same four groups as intermediate classes under `ms:Scheme`.
```

### What is a scheme?

- A **scheme** is a figure of *arrangement*: a deliberate departure from the ordinary order or pattern of words, made for effect, with literal meaning intact.
- The contrast with the trope is exact:
  - **trope** = a turn of *sense* (the word changes meaning);
  - **scheme** = a turn of *order* (the words change position or pattern).
- Schemes work on the *body* of language — its rhythm, symmetry, repetition — the way music works on sound.

```notes
Let us be precise about the definition, because the trope/scheme line is the organising distinction of the whole back half of the course, and it is easy to blur. A scheme is a deliberate patterning of language — a departure from the plain, expected arrangement of words — undertaken for effect, in which the words retain their ordinary meanings. The deviation is in the *form*, not the *sense*.

Take "ask not what your country can do for you, but what you can do for your country." Every single word there is used in its plain, literal meaning. There is no metaphor, no turn of sense anywhere in it. What gives the sentence its power is entirely a matter of *arrangement*: the second half reverses the order of the first. That reversal is a scheme — chiasmus — and it does its work without touching the meaning of any word. Compare "she is a lion," where the entire effect depends on *not* meaning "lion" literally. That is the line between the families: schemes rearrange, tropes re-mean.

Because schemes work on the physical body of language — its rhythm, its symmetry, its repetitions and silences — they are close kin to music and to verse. They are how prose acquires shape in time. And they are, of the two families, the more *learnable*, because a pattern can be named, pointed to, and copied in a way that the intuitive leap of a good metaphor cannot. That makes them especially useful to a teacher, as we will see.
```

---

### Schemes of repetition

- The first family repeats words in patterned positions:
  - **Anaphora** — repetition at the *start* of successive clauses: "*We shall fight* on the beaches, *we shall fight* on the landing grounds…";
  - **Epistrophe** — repetition at the *end*: "of the people, by the people, *for the people*" pattern;
  - **Anadiplosis** — the *last* word of one clause opens the next: "fear leads to anger; *anger* leads to hate";
  - **Polyptoton** — repetition of one *root* in different forms: "the *Greeks* are strong, and skilful to their strength, / fierce to their skill, and to their *fierceness* valiant."

```notes
The first and largest group repeats words in disciplined positions, and position is everything — the same repetition at the head, the tail, or the joint of clauses produces quite different effects.

*Anaphora* repeats a word or phrase at the *beginning* of successive clauses. Churchill: "we shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields and in the streets." The drumbeat at the front of each clause builds insistence, accumulation, resolve. *Epistrophe* (also called epiphora) does the reverse, repeating at the *end*: the pattern of Lincoln's "of the people, by the people, for the people," where the recurring tail rings like a bell. Combine the two — repeat at both head and tail — and you have *symploce*.

*Anadiplosis* repeats the *last* word of one clause at the *start* of the next, threading the clauses together: "fear leads to anger; anger leads to hate; hate leads to suffering." Each clause hands its final word to the next like a baton, and the chain has a logic of inevitability built into its very form. *Polyptoton* repeats not a whole word but a *root*, in different grammatical forms — "strong / strength," "fierce / fierceness," "judge not, that ye be not judged" — ringing changes on a single stem so the idea turns over without quite repeating. Notice that all four leave meaning untouched; the effect is pure patterning of recurrence.
```

### Schemes of balance — antithesis and parallelism

- The second family sets clauses in *symmetry*:
  - **Parallelism** — successive clauses of matching grammatical structure;
  - **Isocolon** — parallel clauses of *equal length* (and often rhythm): "veni, vidi, vici";
  - **Antithesis** — parallel structure carrying *opposed* ideas: "many are called, but few are chosen"; "to err is human, to forgive divine."
- Balance makes contrast *legible*: the symmetry of the form lets the opposition of the content land cleanly.

```notes
The second family is the family of *symmetry*, and it culminates in one of the most useful figures in the language, antithesis.

*Parallelism* is the base: successive clauses built to the same grammatical pattern, so the ear recognises the repetition of *shape*. *Isocolon* tightens it — parallel members of roughly *equal length and rhythm*, like Caesar's "veni, vidi, vici," three two-syllable verbs in lockstep. The equality of the members gives a sense of control, completeness, mastery.

And then *antithesis*, which pours *opposed* content into a *balanced* form. "Many are called, but few are chosen." "To err is human, to forgive divine." "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times." The power of antithesis is that the symmetry of the structure makes the *opposition* of the ideas instantly legible — the parallel form is a frame that holds the two contraries side by side so the contrast snaps into focus. This is why antithesis is the natural figure of *distinction* and *definition*: whenever you need to say "this, not that," antithesis gives you the shape to say it sharply. For a teacher drawing a line between a right and a wrong conception, it is close to indispensable, and we will return to it.
```

### Chiasmus — the crossing

- **Chiasmus** — the *mirror reversal*: the second half inverts the order of the first (the ABBA pattern). Named for the Greek letter **chi** (Χ), an *X*, a crossing.
- "Fair is foul, and foul is fair." "Ask not what your country can do for you — ask what you can do for your country." (When the very same words return reversed, it is *antimetabole*.)
- The chiasmus is, literally, *a turn*: the sentence reverses on itself — the same shape as the *peripeteia* of Lecture 2. The vocabulary folds back.

```notes
Chiasmus deserves its own slide, partly because it is the most satisfying of all the schemes, and partly because it brings the course full circle in a way I want you to feel.

A chiasmus reverses the order of the elements in the second half relative to the first: an ABBA pattern. "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" — adjective-noun, noun-adjective, mirrored across the comma. The name comes from the Greek letter *chi*, written Χ — an X, a crossing — because if you draw lines connecting the matched elements, they cross. When the *identical words* come back in reverse order, as in Kennedy's "ask not what your country can do for you — ask what you can do for your country," the figure has its own name, *antimetabole*, but the shape is the same: a structure that walks out and then walks back, inverting itself at the centre.

Now hear the word. A chiasmus is, structurally, *a reversal* — the sentence turns around a hinge and comes back as its own mirror image. And we have spent this whole course on reversal: the *peripeteia*, the turn by which a plot veers round to its opposite; the *tropos*, the turn by which a word swerves from its sense. The chiasmus is that same turn, executed at the level of *syntax* — the sentence performing, in its very shape, the reversal that a plot performs in its action. This is the folding-back the final lecture is about: peripeteia, trope, chiasmus, recognition — the turn appears at every level of made language, from the arc of a tragedy to the crossing of a single sentence. The companion ontology even records the kinship, with a `seeAlso` link from `ms:Chiasmus` to `ms:Peripeteia` — the syntactic turn pointed at the dramatic one.
```

### Schemes of omission and excess

- The third family departs from the *expected number* of words or connectives:
  - **Asyndeton** — *omitting* conjunctions: "veni, vidi, vici"; "government of the people, by the people, for the people";
  - **Polysyndeton** — *piling up* conjunctions: "and the earth, and the sea, and the sky";
  - **Ellipsis** — omitting a word the listener readily supplies;
  - **Zeugma** — one word governing two others, often with a swerve: "he lost his coat and his temper."
- Asyndeton speeds and compresses; polysyndeton slows and accumulates. The presence or absence of little words changes the *pace* of thought.

```notes
The third family plays with the *quantity* of words — especially the small connective words — departing from the expected number in either direction.

*Asyndeton* omits the conjunctions you would expect. "Veni, vidi, vici" — not "veni et vidi et vici." The missing "ands" make the clauses rush, clipped and headlong, conveying speed, decisiveness, energy. *Polysyndeton* does the opposite, deliberately *multiplying* conjunctions: "and the earth, and the sea, and the sky, and all that in them is." The piled "ands" slow the sentence to a stately, accumulating march, each item given full weight. Notice that these are pure *scheme* effects — the words mean exactly what they mean; only the connective tissue, present or absent, changes, and with it the entire pace and feel of the thought. Pace is a meaning the arrangement carries that no single word holds.

*Ellipsis* omits a word that the listener can easily supply from context — "the spirit is willing, but the flesh [is] weak" — trusting the reader to complete the pattern, which (as with the enthymeme's missing premise) draws them in. *Zeugma* yokes two objects under a single governing word, often with a delicious shift between literal and figurative: "he lost his coat and his temper," where "lost" governs both but means something different for each. Zeugma sits right on the trope/scheme border — the governing word does double duty of *sense* even as the figure is one of *arrangement* — which is a useful reminder that our two families are clean categories drawn over a messier reality.
```

### Climax, the tricolon, and the schemes of sound

- Two last patterns of order, then the schemes of sound:
  - **Climax** (*gradatio*) — mounting by degrees, each step rising from the last (often via anadiplosis): "…patience, experience; and experience, hope";
  - **Tricolon** — a series of *three* parallel members (the "rule of three"), often with the third longest: "of the people, by the people, for the people";
  - **Sound**: **alliteration** (repeated initial consonants), **assonance** (repeated vowels), **homoioteleuton** (similar endings), **paronomasia** (the pun — wordplay on similar sound).
- These are the schemes that make a phrase *ring* and *lodge* — the sound-shape that memory grips.

```notes
Two final schemes of arrangement, then the family of sound.

*Climax*, in the rhetorical sense — the Latin name is *gradatio*, "a staircase" — is an arrangement in *mounting degrees*, each member stronger than the last, often built by anadiplosis so the chain also threads through its repeated words. Paul: "tribulation worketh patience; and patience, experience; and experience, hope." The form climbs, and the meaning climbs with it. (Our ordinary use of "climax" for the peak itself is a later narrowing of this older sense of the whole ascending series.)

The *tricolon* is the series of *three* — three parallel members — and it is so deeply grooved into our ears that we call it simply "the rule of three." "Friends, Romans, countrymen." "Government of the people, by the people, for the people." "Life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness." Three is the smallest number that establishes a pattern *and* completes it; two feels like a mere pair, four begins to sprawl, but three sets up an expectation and satisfies it in the same breath. Often the third member is the longest — the *tricolon crescens* — so the series swells to its close.

Finally, the schemes of *sound*, which pattern the phonemes themselves. *Alliteration* repeats initial consonants ("blow, bugle, blow"); *assonance* repeats vowel sounds; *homoioteleuton* gives successive words or clauses similar endings, the prose cousin of rhyme; and *paronomasia* is the pun, the play on words of similar sound but different sense — which, like zeugma, leans toward the tropes, since it trades on a collision of meanings. All of these work on the ear, and the ear is the gate of memory. Which is exactly where we turn the lecture back on teaching.
```

### The reflexive turn — the tutor's schemes and the grip of memory

- Schemes are the teacher's instruments of *structure* and *retention*:
  - **parallelism** makes the shape of an explanation visible ("*first*… *then*… *finally*…");
  - **antithesis** sharpens the line between a right and a wrong conception ("not *adding* the parts, but *combining* them");
  - the **tricolon** and **repetition** make a key point *lodge*;
  - **climax** builds a sequence of ideas to the one that matters.
- This is where schemes meet the fourth canon, **memoria**: the patterned phrase is the one the learner *keeps*. Form is a memory aid.
- The ontology files these under `ms:Scheme` (a kind of `ms:Figure`). **Caution**: a memorable phrasing is not an understood one — the scheme classifies the *form*, not the uptake.

```notes
Turn the schemes on teaching, and they reveal themselves as instruments of *structure* and *memory* — the two things a learner most needs and most easily loses.

Parallelism makes the architecture of an explanation *visible*: "first we find a common denominator, then we add the numerators, finally we simplify" — the matching grammatical shape tells the learner these are three steps of one procedure, ranked and ordered. Antithesis sharpens the crucial lines: almost every misconception is best dislodged by a clean "not this, but that" — "you are not adding two separate numbers, you are combining two parts of one whole" — and antithesis is the scheme built precisely to carry that contrast. The tricolon and plain repetition make a key point *stick*; a principle stated once is heard, a principle stated as a rhythmic three is remembered. And climax lets you build a chain of ideas so that they mount toward the one that matters, the form itself signalling what is most important.

But the deepest connection is to the fourth canon, *memoria*, and it closes a loop we opened two lectures ago. The schemes are *mnemonic*. The reason "of the people, by the people, for the people" survives in millions of memories, while a hundred plainer phrasings of the same idea are forgotten, is that the *pattern* gives the mind something to grip. The Romans built memory palaces of vivid images; the schemes build memory into the *shape of the sentence itself*. So when a tutor phrases the key takeaway as a balanced antithesis or a ringing tricolon, they are not merely decorating — they are doing the learner's *memoria* for them, packaging the insight in a form that the mind can hold.

And the standing caution, sharpened for this family. A memorable sentence is not the same as an understood one. A learner can carry away a perfectly ringing phrase — "you can't divide by zero" — and have no idea why; the scheme has done its mnemonic work while the comprehension stayed empty. So when the ontology files a turn under `ms:Scheme`, it records that the form was used. Whether the form carried real understanding into a mind that kept it is, once again, the question the form cannot answer, and which we do not pretend to read.
```

### Looking ahead

- We have both families of *word*-figures: tropes (turns of meaning) and schemes (turns of arrangement).
- **Next lecture — Figures of Thought, and the Reflexive Turn.** Figures that operate not on the word or its order but on the *idea*: rhetorical question, apostrophe, prosopopoeia, praeteritio. Then the finale: reading the tutor's *own* moves as figures, and closing the loop the whole course has been drawing.
- Carry forward: the trope/scheme distinction, and the kinship of *turns* — peripeteia, trope, chiasmus — that we keep meeting.

```notes
We now command both families of figures that operate on *words*: the tropes, which turn a word's meaning, and the schemes, which turn the arrangement of words. Together they are the *figurae verborum*, the figures of diction — the level at which most of the Renaissance handbooks did most of their counting.

But there is a third kind of figure, pitched at a different level — not the word, not the order of words, but the *idea*, the *thought*, the relation between the speaker and what is being said. These are the *figurae sententiae*, the figures of thought: the rhetorical question that is not really a question, the address to someone absent or dead, the giving of a voice to an abstraction, the "I will not even mention" that mentions precisely by refusing to. Those are next lecture's first business.

And then the course closes its own circle. Having built the whole vocabulary — poiesis and the drama, the appeals, the canons, the tropes, the schemes — we turn it, in the final lecture, on the thing that has been quietly using it all along: the tutoring dialogue itself, read as a drama, and the tutor's moves read as figures. The folding-back we have felt at every turn — peripeteia, tropos, chiasmus — becomes, at last, the explicit subject. Read for next time: Lanham on the figures of thought, and re-read your own notes from Lecture 1, because we are going back to the beginning.
```
