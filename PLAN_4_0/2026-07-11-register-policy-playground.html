Filter & switch: when should one reading change the strategy?

A context-free simulation of the assessment → strategy loop: a noisy scalar signal, a filter that estimates level and trend, and a switching policy with temperature and hysteresis.

Nothing here knows about tutoring. There is a hidden quantity x(t) you would like to track, a per-turn observation of it corrupted by noise, a filter that turns the observation stream into an estimate of where the signal is (level) and where it is going (trend), and a policy that maps that estimate to one of four abstract strategies. Every question about register-shifting — should a derivative smooth over several responses before the strategy moves? what does temperature do? why does the system chatter? — becomes visible as geometry: a point moving through a colored plane.

The model in one pass

Signal. A hidden latent x(t) ∈ [0,1] evolves over 48 turns (choose its shape with the presets). Each turn emits one observation s(t) = x(t) + noise. One preset injects a single misread — an observation far from the latent — the abstract version of a terse reply scored as disengagement.

Filter. Three estimators of (level L, trend T): raw uses the last observation and the last one-step difference; windowed uses a k-point mean and the least-squares slope over the same window (a weighted moving average of the per-step changes); EWMA uses exponentially weighted averages of level and change with memory α.

Policy. Fixed linear map from (L, T) to four strategy logits — Steady · Advance · Repair · Disrupt — then softmax at temperature τ, then either argmax or a seeded sample. A hysteresis term adds h to the incumbent strategy's logit: positive h makes the current strategy sticky; negative h penalizes repeating it (the anti-repetition regime).

The phase plane below colors each point of (level, trend) space by the policy's choice given the current incumbent, so hysteresis is visible as the incumbent's region growing (h > 0) or shrinking (h < 0) in place.

Five experiments worth running

1 · The misread

Preset Misread spike plants one wildly wrong observation in an otherwise steady signal — the abstract "yeah" scored as disengagement.

Try: filter = raw → the policy whiplashes through Repair and Advance off one bad reading. Windowed k=5 still flinches into Repair for a turn (a least-squares slope weights its endpoints heavily); the default k=7 shrinks that to a single brief flinch, and k=7 with hysteresis h≈+0.3 removes it entirely. Averaging shrinks the damage; averaging plus stickiness erases it.

2 · Smoothing buys calm, costs lag

Preset Step drop: the latent genuinely falls at turn 20. The filter that ignored the misread now delays the legitimate strategy change.

Try: sweep window k from 2 → 9 and watch false switches collapse; then raise noise σ and watch the detection-lag cost appear at the large windows. There is no filter that is both instant and immune; there is only a chosen point on this tradeoff.

3 · Chatter and hysteresis

Preset Oscillation swings the signal fast enough that the estimate keeps crossing policy boundaries; the strategy toggles every few turns.

Try: windowed k=5, h = 0, and note the switch count. h = +0.5 grows the incumbent's region in place and trims the one-turn toggles. Now set h = −0.45 — the current runtime's anti-repetition regime — and switching gets strictly worse: the incumbent's own region shrinks under the point standing in it.

4 · Temperature is boundary blur

With sampling on, τ sets how far from the boundary the policy still gambles.

Try: preset Plateau, choice = sample, τ = 0.1 vs τ = 0.9. At high τ the plane's colors smear together and strategies switch with no signal cause at all — exploration indistinguishable from noise unless you log the draw.

5 · Slope vs velocity

Preset Ramp & surge: quiet, then a genuine climb of +0.07 per turn — the same size as one noise step. A one-step difference cannot tell the surge from noise; a windowed slope integrates until the trend is undeniable.

Try: raw vs windowed at σ = 0.08–0.12. The raw trajectory whips vertically in the phase plane, clipping the Advance region by luck; the windowed one drifts coherently into it and stays.

6 · What this playground omits

One channel only. The runtime tracks three (learner-field, DAG coverage, risk) and its most interesting flags — coerciveProgress, fieldOnlyDrift — are divergences between channels, a 2-signal generalization of this plane. It also omits the efficacy feedback loop that re-weights strategies by observed outcome.
